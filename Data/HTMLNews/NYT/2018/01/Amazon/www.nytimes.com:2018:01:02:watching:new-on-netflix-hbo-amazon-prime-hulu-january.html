The Best TV Shows and Movies New to Netflix, Amazon and More in January

by Monica Castillo

Jan. 2, 2018UPDATED June 8, 2018

From left: “Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets,” “Strictly Ballroom” and “Spaceballs.”Warner Bros. Pictures; Miramax; MGM

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation website. Sign up for our thrice-weekly newsletter here.

Every month, subscription streaming services add a new batch of movies and TV shows to their libraries. Here are the titles we think are most interesting for January, broken down by service and release date. Streaming services occasionally change schedules without giving notice.

New to Netflix

From left, Bill Paxton, Kevin Bacon and Tom Hanks in “Apollo 13.”Universal Pictures
‘Apollo 13’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

In June, it was announced that Ron Howard was replacing Phil Lord and Christopher Miller as the director of the coming “Star Wars” prequel about young Han Solo. But before Howard took on that story from a galaxy far, far away, he directed a space drama situated much closer to home. This 1995 movie follows the events of the doomed Apollo 13 and its crew’s desperate attempt to make it back to earth. Not even Tom Hanks’s calming presence as one of the astronauts can soothe my stress when watching this movie.
—

Kirsten Dunst, left, and Gabrielle Union in “Bring It On.”Ken Jacques/Universal Studios
‘Bring It On’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Kirsten Dunst stars in this 2000 movie as a spunky cheerleading captain who thinks she’s cursed. It’s the only reason her character can think of when she discovers her champion squad has a problematic history of stealing routines from a poorer team in South Central Los Angeles (led by Gabrielle Union in one of her earliest films roles). “Bring It On” isn’t just the best cheerleading movie of all time. It’s one of the best sports comedies. And now, I have the “Brr, It’s Cold in Here” cheer stuck in my head.
—

Marlon Brando in “The Godfather.”Paramount Pictures

‘The Godfather’ trilogy
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

The first and second entries of the “Godfather” trilogy are essential entries in the American movie canon. They have many unforgettable lines, shadowy cinematography that makes almost every character look ominous and great performances from Marlon Brando, Al Pacino, Diane Keaton, John Cazale, James Caan and Robert De Niro.
—

Kate Hudson and Matthew McConaughey in “How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days.”Michael Gibson/Paramount Pictures
‘How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Remember when Matthew McConaughey was the “it” guy for almost every romantic comedy in the early 2000s? I sure do. And “How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days” is likely one of the better ones of the bunch. Here, he’s an ad exec who needs to prove that he understands romance when he comes across a magazine writer (Kate Hudson) who, unbeknown to him, is on assignment to get a guy to dump her within 10 days. Don’t you just love it when two unlikely characters played by really attractive people are the ones who find love?
—

Kirsten Dunst, center, in “Marie Antoinette.”Leigh Johnson/Columbia Pictures
‘Marie Antoinette’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Sofia Coppola delivers this imaginative spin on the story of the last pre-Revolution queen of France. As Marie Antoinette, Kirsten Dunst wears frilly dresses, prances through opulent rooms and luxuriates in riches in a naïve girlish way. Anyone coming to this movie for a period-perfect recreation of Antoinette’s story may be disappointed. The movie is more interested in the mood and inner life of the queen than in historical accuracy.
—

Tara Morice and Paul Mercurio in “Strictly Ballroom.”Miramax Films
‘Strictly Ballroom’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

There’s so much to love about this ostentatiously silly Australian film. Underneath the ridiculous ’90s hairdos, sparkly costumes and silly dance routines, there’s a lovely story about two unlikely partners — one a champion dancer, the other an untested and clumsy newcomer — who upend the stuffy ballroom scene with their unorthodox style. This was the film that launched Baz Luhrmann’s career, so take note, fans of “Romeo + Juliet” and “Moulin Rouge.”
—

Jim Carrey in “The Truman Show.”Paramount Pictures
‘The Truman Show’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

This 1998 movie features one of Jim Carrey’s best performances. (I have a soft spot, though, for Carrey’s role in the sappy “The Majestic.”) In it, Carrey combines his manic energy with a sensitive side audiences hadn’t really seen when it was released. But “The Truman Show” is also an interesting look at the rise of reality TV and how people cannibalize one another’s experiences for entertainment.
—

Vera Farmiga in “The Conjuring.”Michael Tackett/Warner Bros. Pictures
‘The Conjuring’
Starts streaming: Jan. 8

Director James Wan (“Saw”) channels the spirit of 1970s horror films like “The Exorcist” and “The Amityville Horror” to create a mythology around a real-life married pair of paranormal investigators. The premise may feel familiar, but Vera Farmiga and Patrick Wilson do wonders with their roles as the ghost-hunting couple.
—

Other movies of interest: “Batman” (Jan. 1), “Breakfast at Tiffany’s” (Jan 1), “Caddyshack” (Jan. 1), the “Lethal Weapon” series (Jan. 1), “Like Water for Chocolate” (Jan. 1), “The Shawshank Redemption” (Jan. 1) and “Dallas Buyers Club” (Jan. 16).

TV series of interest: “Lovesick” Season 3 (Jan. 1), “Comedians in Cars Getting Coffee” (Jan. 5) and “Episodes” Seasons 1-5 (Jan. 6).

New Netflix Original TV Series

From left, Lily Tomlin, Jane Fonda and Lisa Kudrow in “Grace and Frankie.”Melissa Moseley/Netflix
‘Grace and Frankie’ Season 4
Starts streaming: Jan. 19

It’s time for TV fans to start paying more attention to Netflix’s sweet and stinging sitcom “Grace and Frankie,” which by the end of January 2018 will have four seasons and more than 50 episodes available — an impressive run for any show. The series’s co-creator Marta Kauffman made her reputation with the widely beloved “Friends,” and for this season of “Grace and Frankie” she’s bringing along her old pal Lisa Kudrow, who joins the showbiz legends Jane Fonda and Lily Tomlin in this continuing story about two aging women looking to reinvent themselves.
—

A scene from “Dirty Money.”Netflix
‘Dirty Money’
Starts streaming: Jan. 26

Alex Gibney is an impassioned and prolific activist documentarian, and one assumes he can’t possibly find the time to research and shoot a feature film about every social ill that ignites him. So it makes sense that he would shift some of his production company’s resources into a TV series, helping similarly passionate directors make a difference. The short documentaries presented under Gibney’s “Dirty Money” umbrella pursue blood-boiling tales of corporate malfeasance and corruption, with episodes tackling pharmaceutical price-gouging, the banking practices of drug cartels, Donald Trump’s shady business history and more.
—

A scene from “One Day at a Time.”Netflix
‘One Day at a Time’ Season 2
Starts streaming: Jan. 26

This reimagined revival of the long-running sitcom from producer Norman Lear landed on multiple “Best of 2017” lists, making the prospect of new “One Day at a Time” episodes one of the year’s most-anticipated TV events. Season 1 left off with the Alvarez family’s embracing its eldest daughter, Elena, after she came out as gay, while Penelope was struggling to overcome her busy schedule as a single mother and nurse, along with the chronic injury she suffered in the military. Expect more poignant and pertinent complications in Season 2, as well as more scene-stealing moments for Rita Moreno as Penelope’s Cuban immigrant mother.
—

New Netflix Original Movies

Jack Black, left, and Jason Schwartzman in “The Polka King.”Netflix
‘The Polka King’
Starts streaming: Jan. 12

Based on the true story of Jan Lewan — who is also the subject of a 2009 documentary — “The Polka King” stars Jack Black as Lewan, a popular Pennsylvania polka bandleader who takes advantage of his mostly older fan base to raise money in a shady investment scheme. The film finally arrives on Netflix this month, almost exactly one year after it was well received in its debut at the Sundance Film Festival.
—

Piercey Dalton and Dylan Minnette in “The Open House.”Netflix
‘The Open House’
Starts streaming: Jan. 19

Over the past year or so, Netflix has been quietly assembling a healthy library of original horror films, calling on fledgling creative teams and emerging genre auteurs to share their original visions. For “The Open House,” the novice writer-director team of Matt Angel and Suzanne Coote collaborate with Dylan Minnette, the star of Netflix’s “13 Reasons Why,” for a supernatural thriller about a teenager and his mother, who encounter menacing spirits in their new home.
—

Will Forte, left, and Domhnall Gleeson in “A Futile and Stupid Gesture.”John P. Fleenor/Netflix
‘A Futile and Stupid Gesture’
Starts streaming: Jan. 26

A hit list of notable comic actors pay homage to the influentially anarchic humor magazine “National Lampoon” in this movie about the troubled genius of its high-living writer, editor and co-founder Doug Kenney. Will Forte plays Kenney, Joel McHale plays Chevy Chase, Seth Green is Christopher Guest, Paul Scheer is Paul Shaffer, and a host of other comedians play the likes of Bill Murray, John Belushi, Harold Ramis and Dan Aykroyd. The “Wet Hot American Summer” director David Wain — who has more than a little experience with corralling brilliantly funny folk — directs what looks to be a raucous film about the tricky business of making people laugh.
—

New to Amazon Prime

Robert Redford in “All Is Lost.”Richard Foreman/Roadside Attractions
‘All Is Lost’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Robert Redford plays a man all alone at sea. We never learn his name or who he is, we only see his struggle to survive after his boat is damaged by a wayward shipping container.
—

Philip Seymour Hoffman in “Capote.”Attila Dory/Sony Pictures Classics
‘Capote’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Philip Seymour Hoffman won an Academy Award for his portrayal in this film of the eccentric author Truman Capote. This movie covers the years Capote researched and wrote his landmark best-seller “In Cold Blood.”
—

Jake Gyllenhaal and Chloë Sevigny in “Zodiac.”Merrick Morton/Paramount Pictures
‘Zodiac’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Before Robert Downey Jr. became one of the world’s biggest movie stars in “Iron Man,” he enjoyed a bit of a renaissance in movies like “Kiss Kiss Bang Bang” and this David Fincher film. In it, Downey and Jake Gyllenhaal play two San Francisco newsmen trying to solve the case of the Zodiac killer. Mark Ruffalo and Anthony Edwards play the detectives on the case. The movie is a fascinating study of obsession — and of the idea that some questions just never get answered.
—

A scene from “A Ghost Story.”A24
‘A Ghost Story’
Starts streaming: Jan. 7

This movie is not spooky — not even close. This ghost story is sad and existential. After a young married man dies, he returns to haunt his home and brood as his wife (and time) move on.
—

Also of interest: “Requiem for a Dream” (Jan. 1), “Reservoir Dogs” (Jan. 1) and “Thelma & Louise” (Jan. 1).

New to Hulu

Wesley Snipes, left, Alfre Woodard in “Down in the Delta.” Ben Mark Holzberg/Miramax Films
‘Down in the Delta’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

The poet Maya Angelou directed one movie and it’s a great one. Starring Alfre Woodard, “Down in the Delta” follows an exasperated and struggling young mother whose own mother forces her to spend a summer with relatives in the Mississippi Delta, in hopes that it will straighten out the family’s problems. Woodard is amazing in just about every movie I’ve seen her in, and this one is no different. Although Angelou didn’t write the story, her sensitive and observational touch is all over the movie.
—

Geena Davis in “A League of Their Own.”MGM
‘A League of Their Own’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

This movie is a reliable choice if you’re looking for a pick-me-up, with all the heartbreak and good cheer of a classic sports movie. Geena Davis stars alongside Tom Hanks, Madonna and Rosie O’Donnell in this fictionalized retelling of the inaugural season of the All-American Girls Professional Baseball League. Remember, “There’s no crying in baseball!”
—

Hayley Mills playing twins in “The Parent Trap.”Disney

‘The Parent Trap’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

The original live-action Disney movie “The Parent Trap,” from 1961, starred Hayley Mills in the dual role of twin sisters who try to get their divorced parents back together. In this 1998 remake, the mischievous twins are played by Lindsay Lohan, and they come up with a similar scheme. Now, you can watch both versions back to back as a double feature. Only one of them, however, has an adorably catchy song, and that’s the original.
—

Maggie Gyllenhaal in “Secretary.”Lions Gate Films
‘Secretary’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

Before her role in HBO’s “The Deuce” as a porn industry pioneer, Maggie Gyllenhaal took on kink taboos in this cult favorite from 2002. In this role, she plays a submissive woman recovering from a breakdown who starts a sadomasochistic relationship with her boss (James Spader). Interestingly, Spader’s character is named Mr. Grey, like the main love interest in the “Fifty Shades of Grey” series. I much prefer Spader and Gyllenhaal’s darkly comic relationship.
—

John Candy, left, and Bill Pullman in “Spaceballs.”MGM
‘Spaceballs’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

In a decade far, far away (kidding, it’s just the ’80s), Mel Brooks skewered the super-popular “Star Wars” films with this silly, pun-filled story about a kidnapped princess, a helmet-wearing villain, an unlikely hero and his tall, furry co-pilot. It’s hard for me to pick a favorite joke from this movie, but the one that comes to mind first is the not-so-subtle jab at “Star Wars” merchandising, the “Spaceballs” version of which is peddled by Yogurt, Brooks’s spoof of Yoda.
—

Michael Fassbender as the title character in “Frank.”Magnolia Pictures
‘Frank’
Starts streaming: Jan. 8

You’ve probably seen Michael Fassbender in one of his many serious roles, but his performance in this 2014 dark comedy is odder than usual. For most of the movie, his face is concealed by a big, bizarre papier-mâché head that his character, a bandleader named Frank, wears at all times.
—

Tilda Swinton in “We Need to Talk About Kevin.”Nicole Rivelli/Oscilloscope
‘We Need to Talk About Kevin’
Starts streaming: Jan. 15

I’ve yet to see a bad movie starring Tilda Swinton, and if there is one, don’t tell me about it. “We Need to Talk About Kevin” was one of the first movies that made me fall in love with her intense but understated acting style. In this tragic drama, she plays a mother who struggles to connect with her demanding and disturbed child (Ezra Miller). In many scenes, Swinton conveys her character’s conflicted emotions without saying a word. Her performance is so good, it’s haunting.
—

Elizabeth Olsen, left, and Aubrey Plaza in “Ingrid Goes West.”Neon
‘Ingrid Goes West’
Starts streaming: Jan. 22

A black satire of social media obsession, this movie follows a troubled young woman, Ingrid (Aubrey Plaza), as she develops an unhealthy fixation on a boho chic Instagram celebrity (Elizabeth Olsen). Ingrid then plots to become this Insta-famous stranger’s best friend in real life by moving to her neighborhood in Los Angeles and inserting herself into her life. This is a very dark comedy, but one with several funny performances from Plaza, Olsen and O’Shea Jackson Jr., who plays Ingrid’s Batman-obsessed landlord.
—

Also of interest: “Bill & Ted’s Excellent Adventure” (Jan. 1), “Capote” (Jan. 1), “The Karate Kid” (Jan. 1), “Punch Drunk Love” (Jan. 1), “Requiem for a Dream” (Jan. 1), “Reservoir Dogs” (Jan. 1), “Saturday Night Fever” (Jan. 1), “Sliding Doors” (Jan. 1), “Thelma & Louise” (Jan. 1), “Total Recall” (Jan. 1), “Zodiac” (Jan. 1), “Coherence” (Jan. 15), “The Diving Bell and the Butterfly” (Jan. 15), “Meek’s Cutoff” (Jan. 15), “Wendy and Lucy” (Jan. 15) and “All Dogs Go to Heaven” (Jan. 31).

New to HBO

Zooey Deschanel and Joseph Gordon-Levitt in “(500) Days of Summer.”Chuck Zlotnick/Fox Searchlight Pictures
‘(500) Days of Summer’
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

I’m semi-afraid to revisit this movie. I’m not sure how well it has aged. It’s possible Joseph Gordon-Levitt’s sad sack character with attachment issues will get on my nerves. And it’s likely I’ll want to tell Zooey Deschanel’s character that there are a lot of guys out there who like the Smiths, and that she should probably avoid dating in the workplace.
—

Emma Watson and Daniel Radcliffe in “Harry Potter and the Prisoner of Azkaban.”Murray Close/Warner Bros.

The Harry Potter movies
Starts streaming: Jan. 1

You could watch “Fantastic Beasts and Where to Find Them” (please don’t), or you could binge the eight original Harry Potter movies instead. I recommend the latter. If you’re a fan, the originals may spur memories of watching them for the first time. Did you wait in line all night for a midnight screening? Did you grumble about how much the later movies deviated from the books, or dress up as your favorite character? I know I wasn’t the only one in those days wearing a wizard’s robe to the movie theater.
—

Also of interest: “Back to the Future” (Jan. 1), “Holes” (Jan. 1), “The Hours” (Jan. 1), “National Lampoon’s Animal House” (Jan. 1), “Three Kings” (Jan. 1) and “Sideways” (Jan. 1).
