    There’s a Data Crackdown Coming.
    Why It’s Good for Customers and Business.
    Like tremors generated by shifting tectonic plates, a rumbling of change is shaking the world of data privacy.
    Like tremors generated by shifting tectonic plates, a rumbling of change is shaking the world of data privacy.

    new rules are causing businesses to rethink the way they collect, store and process customer data. But for the most progressive companies, it doesn’t stop there. Regulations also have the potential to herald a new era of information technology reform.

    The clarion call for businesses to rethink their approaches to protecting customer data is being heeded by the European Union’s General Data Privacy Regulation (G.D.P.R.), a set of guidelines that establishes a means for individuals to control their private data. Set to take effect on May 25, 2018, the rules specify data privacy rights, such as the right to have a company forget your data, and the right to export and move personal information from one company to another. Violators face stiff fines — up to 20 million euros or 4 percent of global revenue, whichever is higher.

    “G.D.P.R. is about the change of the culture of processing data,” said Eva Skornickova, a G.D.P.R. compliance consultant based in Prague. “Companies think all your personal data belongs to them. But it belongs to us as individuals. It is a part of our personal life. We should decide who is going to license our data and what they are going to do with it.”

    It may seem to some that the European Union reforms — and other data privacy protections popping up worldwide — are yet another regulatory burden for which companies must absorb the cost. But that doesn’t have to be the case. Data privacy compliance can provide a distinct competitive advantage and business opportunity, Skornickova and other experts say.

    By choosing the right tools to protect customer privacy, companies can reshape their data practices in ways that reap cascading benefits. Compliance provides a way to win customer trust. And tackling inventory and discovery in a strategic manner can improve firms’ agility and efficiency.

    Trust Wins

    01

    Customers who understand how their data is being used will reward firms with trust and loyalty.

    in the data economy, trust is currency.In exchange for more personalized experiences or enhanced health and well-being, customers are willing to part with a great deal of personal information. Because of this, more than ever, the relationship between company and customer must be built on trust. When trust is broken, the negative reaction from customers can be staggering, as Yahoo!, Equifax and other firms with huge data breaches have learned.

    “G.D.P.R. compliance is not about the big, costly exercise,” Skornickova said. “It’s about your reputation. Microsoft, for instance, is investing heavily to ensure Microsoft 365 and its cloud tools are compliant with G.D.P.R., because this is a huge business advantage. That’s being competitive.”

    PricewaterhouseCoopers recently surveyed its clients and found that 64 percent plan to deploy Internet of Things-based solutions and 63 percent will roll out advanced predictive analytics over the next year. Data privacy and trust are critical to customers’ acceptance of these new technologies, said Jay Cline, a privacy leader with PwC who helps clients comply with data privacy and security regulations.

    Companies think all your personal data belongs to them. But it belongs to us as individuals, it is a part of our personal life.
    Eva Skornickova, G.D.P.R. compliance consultant

    “If your predictive analytics is too accurate, if things are predicting my next move too much, the word people use is ‘creepy,’” Cline said. “IoT includes things like connected cars and smart homes, smart appliances, things that talk to each other. There’s a group of people who think this is cool. They’re going to buy those no matter what. But there are just as many people skeptical of where all this data goes, and they’re concerned and want to know more about how their data is going to be safe.”

    Customers also want to know when their data is shared, if it’s retained for an appropriate period of time and that it’s secured throughout its use, Cline said. “Consumers trust that the collection of their data is proportionate to the promise of its use,” he said. Companies that demonstrate value so that customers opt in will have an advantage as technology evolves.

    The Silver Lining of Compliance

    02

    In the post-G.D.P.R. world, companies that don’t work to maintain a clear 360-degree view of consumer data will get left behind.

    because the cost of data storage has plummeted over the years, piling up information has been easy, and some companies have grown complacent about maintaining data quality and consistency. After years of neglect, data quality has become an issue. Data that’s out-of-date — email addresses or phone numbers, for example — create both compliance and security risks and weaken business initiatives.

    In 2016, the Harvard Business Review reported that poor data quality cost the United States economy $3.1 trillion per year, mostly in misspent marketing dollars and wasted staff time. Among British executives surveyed in February 2017, 91 percent described their organization as “plagued with data quality issues.” The same report estimated that British businesses lost 20 percent of customers annually because of poor data.

    And security is equally important. A survey conducted by independent technology market research specialist Vanson Bourne found that more than three quarters of Americans said they would avoid companies with a number of data breaches. Globally, the average data breach now costs a firm $3.62 million; in the U.S., the figure is over $7 million, according to research by IBM and the Ponemon Institute.

    Compliance with G.D.P.R. and other data privacy regulations will require most firms to engage in long-overdue data cleansing. Businesses will have to gain a much clearer understanding of the data they collect and its purpose. “Then you have to identify the data which you really don’t need,” Skornickova said. “The principle of data minimization will be simple, and regulatory authorities will go after that. They want to see you are only processing data you need for the activities you are doing.”

    This is a compliance issue that drives business innovation.
    Jay Cline, PriceWaterhouseCoopers U.S. Privacy Leader

    G.D.P.R.’s right to be forgotten, for example, elevates the importance of high data quality. Because (with limited exceptions) companies will be required to delete certain personal data on demand of a data subject, they must achieve a clear view of the personal data they process or store on behalf of their customers.

    “We’ve seen surveys that indicate one-third of U.K. customers plan to exercise their right to be forgotten as soon as G.D.P.R. goes live in May,” said Cline.

    Suppose a company has 10 customers named Joe Smith, Cline said. If one of them exercises his right to be forgotten or to edit his data, the company has to be sure it’s acting on data from the proper Joe Smith — or else they’ve created another privacy issue.

    “There’s a reward for high-quality data, but the cost of inaccurate data is also higher,” Cline said.

    Data cleansing and restructuring can result in more robust governance models, with clear and consistent classification and labeling, which makes data secure and easier to access.

    Accelerating Data-Driven Performance

    03

    The road to compliance can lead to a reliable, accurate inventory and transparent mapping of all data, which helps fuel innovation.

    a well-planned internal compliance process should also include something business units clamor for: a reliable, accurate inventory and transparent mapping of all the data the company controls and what can be done with it.

    “Companies think they have good knowledge of their data architecture, but taking that and mapping it to a business process has proven a much harder thing than just a physical I.T. transfer,” said Chris Babel, the chief executive of TrustArc.

    Data privacy compliance brings the information technology department, legal teams, operations, marketing and the C-suite to the same table to inventory data assets and relationships across the enterprise. Nomenclature and formats can be more uniformly structured, allowing the business faster, more agile access to the information that really matters.

    Say a business team dreams up a mobile app idea to better serve customers. With a clear understanding of where databases lie, how data is classified, what its labels mean, which pieces of information will be used from each field, which geographic region the data comes from and, finally, what the ultimate data profile or product will be, design engineers and privacy teams can work together to ensure that data resources are auto-approved for that app and others like it.

    Outside of the information technology department, many firms lack a clear awareness of what data they collect, where it’s stored and how it’s accessed, Babel said. But mature firms are using the regulations and other privacy dictates to map their assets, because, Babel said, “that’s where mature companies truly enable their business to develop processes much more quickly.”

    Cline’s experience with PwC squares with Babel’s. Despite the thirst for customer information, Cline said, only a few businesses truly understand how to use data to its full potential. “We’ve done a lot of data inventories for clients,” he said. “Often, we’re the first ones to show them how they make money with data. That changes the conversation to a business strategy conversation. This is a compliance issue that drives business innovation.”

    Choosing

    Compliance

    most companies are somewhere along the road toward G.D.P.R. compliance, although additional data privacy regulations are being seen around the globe. As firms struggle to meet new rules, they’d be well served to work with platforms and partners who have already met compliance standards.

    Working with a cloud partner that is G.D.P.R.-compliant will be critical in coming months, because today, almost all data moves through the cloud to a distributed workforce and mobile apps. Because data moves from onsite platforms across cloud services, devices and apps, data protection needs to persistently stick with the data, no matter where it goes. Yet personal data must be visible and easily accessible to the people from whom it was collected.

    Firms should also use tools that allow them to discover where sensitive or personal data exists and securely classify, label and archive that information. They should regularly test their compliance status to ensure their business units are doing their best to comply with existing and looming regulations.

    “This is really a global set of changes as different countries are thinking about how data can be used,” Babel said. “Consumers have a new avenue to display their displeasure with you and interact with you. You have to manage, monitor and use it as a way to build trust. You need to start with security and the legal issues of privacy, and then figure out how you use data to power business without violating that trust.”
