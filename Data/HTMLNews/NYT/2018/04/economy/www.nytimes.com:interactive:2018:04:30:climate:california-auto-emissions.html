    California Is Ready for a Fight Over Tailpipe Emissions. Here’s Why.

    By NADJA POPOVICH APRIL 30, 2018

    California and a coalition of 16 other states and the District of Columbia sued the Trump administration on Tuesday over its plan to roll back greenhouse gas emissions standards for cars, trucks and S.U.V.s.

    The move comes days after reports that the Environmental Protection Agency and Transportation Department plan to dramatically weaken federal auto emissions rules. California, which holds a waiver under the Clean Air Act that allows it to set its own standards for vehicle exhaust pollution, has pledged to stick with stricter Obama-era targets.

    Here are three things to know about the standards standoff.

    California needs to lower vehicle emissions if it wants to achieve its ambitious climate goals

    California’s tailpipe regulations are a key component of its broader climate plan to cut emissions 40 percent below 1990 levels by 2030.

    Transportation is the largest source of greenhouse gas releases for the state. In 2015, the most recent data available, the transportation sector accounted for 39 percent of California’s overall emissions, compared to 27 percent nationally. Emissions from passenger cars alone topped 115 million metric tons of carbon dioxide, more than total releases from any other sector.

    To bring that number down, California needs to get cleaner cars on the road.

    California’s auto emissions standards were the basis for national rules

    The state, which has fought smog and toxic air pollution for decades, first proposed limiting greenhouse gas emissions from automobiles in 2002, but was denied a waiver by the Bush administration to enforce its own standards.

    In 2009, the Obama administration granted California’s request and, in one of its first major climate policy achievements, negotiated a deal between the auto industry, the E.P.A., the Transportation Department and California officials to bring federal standards in line with the state’s rules.

    The national standards, extended in 2012, aimed to roughly double the average fuel economy of new passenger cars and trucks to bring greenhouse gas emissions across the fleet down by half by 2025 compared to 2010 levels. But the Trump administration announced this month that those targets were “too high” and “didn’t comport with reality.”

    California’s own clean air agency came to a different conclusion last year, arguing that the standards remained appropriate. More than a dozen other states follow California’s stricter auto emissions rules.

    In a draft proposal, described to The New York Times last week, the Environmental Protection Agency and Department of Transportation outlined eight options for loosening the Obama-era standards. The agencies’ preferred option would freeze current auto emissions and fuel economy targets at levels now set for model year 2021. The draft also challenges California's ability to set greenhouse gas emissions standards for vehicles under the Clean Air Act.

    On Tuesday, California, joined by 16 states and the District of Columbia, filed suit, arguing that the E.P.A. was “arbitrary and capricious” in its decision to roll back the rules.

    “My message to the E.P.A. and administrator Pruitt is simple: Do your job. Regulate carbon pollution from vehicles, defend the clean car standards, don’t tear them up,” Xavier Becerra, California’s attorney general, said in a press conference.

    The waiver battle could have repercussions for another California auto rule

    Under a separate but related program, California requires auto manufacturers to sell an increasing number of low- and no-emissions vehicles each year. In that program, by 2025 about 8 percent of new cars sold in the state would need to be zero-emission vehicles, including plug-in hybrid, electric and hydrogen fuel cell models, according to the Union of Concerned Scientists.

    These sales quotas have played a crucial role in pushing automakers to develop and market more electric cars, experts say. The state also provides consumer incentives, like rebates and access to high-occupancy vehicle lanes for residents who drive electric cars, in support of the state’s broader goal: to get 5 million zero-emissions vehicles on the road by 2030. (Currently, there are about 370,000.)

    California is the largest zero-emission vehicle market in the country. Electric and plug-in hybrid vehicles made up 3.6 percent of the state’s overall car sales in 2016, and rose to nearly 5 percent in 2017.

    Nine other states have adopted similar zero-emission vehicle sales requirements, based on the California program.

    But California’s power to enforce sales targets for zero-emissions vehicles is tied up with its power to set independent tailpipe standards, said Ann E. Carlson, a professor of environmental law at the University of California.

    By imposing a sales requirement for zero emission cars, California is “telling manufacturers they can’t have anything coming out of the tailpipes,” Prof. Carlson said.

    In December, industry groups criticized California’s zero emission sales quota during a House Panel hearing on fuel economy and emissions standards, calling the mandate onerous and costly.

    The rule has not yet come under public scrutiny by the Trump administration, but the E.P.A. administrator, Scott Pruitt, has said, “it is in America's best interest to have a national standard.”

    Brad Plumer contributed reporting.

    Correction: April 30, 2018

    A chart with an earlier version of this article showing the number of zero-emission vehicles registered in the United States misstated the time frame. The data was based on new-vehicle registrations from 2010 through 2017, not from 2010 to 2016.

    Correction: May 1, 2018

    An earlier version of this article misstated the number of states that joined California in a suit against the Trump administration. Sixteen states and the District of Columbia joined California, not 17 states.
