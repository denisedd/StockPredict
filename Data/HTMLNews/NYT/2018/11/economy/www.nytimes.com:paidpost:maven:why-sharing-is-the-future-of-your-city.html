    Why Sharing Is the Future of Your City

    Life has changed in many cities over recent years, as an astonishing number of people worldwide embrace a new lifestyle based on sharing: They sleep on strangers’ couches when traveling the globe. They drive cars or bikes at any time without ever owning one. They wear all the right clothes, yet their closets never fill up. They earn extra income while they sleep.

    ___

    “People want to increase their experiences, not possessions.”

    Julia Steyn, Vice President of Urban Mobility and Maven at General Motors

    “The sharing economy puts a value on making underutilized assets available to a community,” says Alex Stephany, author of “The Business of Sharing” and the chief executive of Beam, a sharing platform that uses crowdfunding to provide vocational training for homeless people. “It gives us more from less.”

    Sharing isn’t limited to physical goods; it extends to talent, storage, experiences, almost anything you can imagine. You can hire a personal trainer to come to your house, for example, secure a sitar player for your next dinner party, rent a neighbor’s boat for a weekend or exchange professional services such as accounting or graphic design. PricewaterhouseCoopers estimates that revenue from the sharing economy will increase to $335 billion in 2025 from around $14 billion in 2014.

    With more people moving to cities, embracing minimalism and connecting with like-minded people via smartphone apps, the sharing economy is affecting almost every aspect of our lives — and improving our society in surprising ways. Like our first-grade teachers taught us: Sharing is good.

    Room rental start-ups have drastically altered the hospitality industry. The ease with which one can now rent rooms helps many people offset their living costs — something true for both renters, who can score lower rates than from traditional hotels, and owners, who make extra income by sharing their homes with others from around the world.

    “People don’t want to spend half their paycheck on housing and a car these days,” says Julia Steyn, the vice president of urban mobility and Maven at General Motors. “They want to increase their experiences, not possessions.”

    Many people appreciate the connection that often comes with sharing someone’s home. In a response to the sharing economy boom, hotel chains are now adding personalized, quirky touches to their previously standardized rooms. “Hotels are realizing they can borrow some tricks from these folks and make it a more authentic experience,” Stephany says.

    The sharing economy has also revolutionized our relationship with cars. Today, only 78 percent of young drivers own or lease cars, as opposed to 91 percent of the previous generation of drivers. Sharing is providing people with a new type of transportation freedom. “The new generation has a very different approach,” Steyn says. “They value access over ownership.”

    Car-sharing apps such as Maven allow members to easily find and rent cars, and they enable car owners to earn extra income by renting their car during times when it would normally just be parked. Considering that the majority of cars sit unused 95 percent of time, this type of sharing can help free up parking spots and increase the efficiency of cities.

    Transportation sharing can also have a positive impact on the environment. “In the long run, sharing will likely lower the average person’s ecological footprint,” says Arun Sundararajan, professor of business at New York University and author of “The Sharing Economy.” Fewer cars are being used for more people, maximizing efficiency and minimizing traffic.

    ___

    “The sharing economy puts a value on making underutilized assets available to a community.”

    Alex Stephany, author of “The Business of Sharing” and the chief executive of Beam

    When shared cars run on electricity rather than gas, air quality can improve. “Maven has about 1,000 electric Chevrolet Bolt EVs available for sharing,” says Alex Keros, the smart cities chief at Maven. “This creates environmental benefits for the community and also introduces more drivers to electric vehicles.”

    By having access to a wide variety of transportation options and being able to try out vehicles before they commit to one, more people are enjoying greater privileges and flexibility with car sharing. “It’s increasing quality of life,” Sundararajan says.

    Around 43 percent of U.S. adults familiar with the sharing economy say they find ownership burdensome, PricewaterhouseCoopers reports. Why should every person on your block own a lawnmower when each person would need to use it for only a few hours a month? The same goes for clothing. Why spend a lot of money on a suit or dress that you’ll wear only once every two months, when you could just rent one and not have to store it during downtime?

    ___

    “Sharing is in all of us. That’s how we’re wired.”

    Alex Keros, smart cities chief at Maven

    Sharing has had a large impact on the fashion industry. Consumers are eagerly signing up with clothing rental startups that allow them to try designers whose pieces they could otherwise not afford, wear a different on-trend outfit every day, and reduce the number of clothes they buy and store in their closets.

    “The fashion-sharing economy is connected with a new values system of sustainable and socially responsible consumerism,” says Julie Gilhart, a fashion consultant in New York. “The up-and-coming generation understands this and believes it is the future; just look at the growth of the resale and renting market. It extends the life cycle of fashion.”

    Whether it’s done at home or on the road, sharing fosters a sense of community. When you’re using your neighbor’s car to take your child to the doctor, for example, you’re connected to that neighbor in a new way.

    “The sharing economy improves lives, because it is about being more efficient with our assets and also about establishing community,” says Keros. “Sharing is in all of us. That’s how we’re wired.”
