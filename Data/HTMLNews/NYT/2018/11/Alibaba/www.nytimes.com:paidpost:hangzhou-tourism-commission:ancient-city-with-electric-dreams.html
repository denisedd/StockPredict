    HANGZHOU:

    Ancient City With Electric Dreams

    HANGZHOU:

    Ancient City
    With Electric Dreams

    With the force of thousands of years of Chinese culture and art behind it, Hangzhou is poised to catapult into the future.

    With the force of thousands of years of Chinese culture and art behind it, Hangzhou is poised to catapult into the future.

    Governed by the renowned Tang dynasty poet Bai Juyi in the ninth century, Hangzhou is currently home to two Unesco World Heritage Sites — the West Lake and the Grand Canal — with the Liangzhu archaeological site expected to join their company next year. Meanwhile, the city is planning to increase the number of Unesco World Heritage Sites in the near future, preparing applications for the imperial city of the Southern Song dynasty, the Kuahuqiao site, the Qiantang River seawall and the Xixi National Wetland Park.

    At the same time, Hangzhou is the headquarters of the multinational conglomerate Alibaba. This new Hangzhou balances on a knife’s edge, deeply intriguing to the discerning visitor beholding a newly assertive China.

    Water, Lifeblood of the City

    Willows line the Grand Canal, the commercial artery constructed to link Hangzhou to Beijing over a thousand years ago.

    A quick turn off Xiaohe Street adjacent to the Canal, and craft museums and trendy shops give way to narrow alleyways and quiet courtyards enveloping Qing-era houses. Pockets of the past live on at the historic block of Wuliuxiang, where Ling Xi rents out a pre-Communist-era room to visitors eager to taste a bygone era. “It’s very Chinese and historical,” she says.

    The modern-day central business district, the Qianjiang New Area, is 10 minutes away by app-hailed taxi. Nearly a decade old, the downtown cityscape has been reshaped with numerous futuristic structures. Its crown jewel is the Wave Cultural Center, a giant LED-studded balcony jutting into the Qiantang River. The unparalleled view announces Qianjiang as the contemporary counterpart of the Grand Canal, places of different eras in which commerce and culture have intersected along the waterfront.

    The Changing Face of the Tea Business

    The West Lake is Hangzhou’s other Unesco World Heritage Site and a particularly intense object of Chinese cultural obsession. Cultivated in the 11th century by another poet-governor, Su Dongpo, and originally restricted to the upper classes, the lake has become a more common space over the years. Lush private villas have been turned into hotels, and previously restricted areas have become free to enter.

    Off its eastern shore stands a much younger enterprise, the Qingteng teahouse. Its owners, Chen Yuqing and Mao Xiaoyu, opened it in 1996. “[We started it] not to make money, but really for fun, and now 20 years have gone by!” Mao says, seeming slightly surprised herself. Pouring out cups of fragrant puerh tea, they proudly emphasize the localness of Qingteng.

    Their tea is sourced from plantations such as Meijiawu in the west, always with terroir in mind. At Meijiawu, a favorable combination of soil and rain produces world-famous West Lake Dragon Well tea leaves. Today, Qingteng is a 900-seater cornerstone of Hangzhou, showing how the deeply traditional business has adjusted to changing times.

    In Art, East Meets West, Old Meets New

    Wieslaw Borkowski moved from Poland to study Chinese painting here at the China Academy of Art, graduating in 2015. The Japanese anime series Dragon Ball first introduced him to East Asian culture and figure drawing. He later found it was based on the 16th-century novel “Journey to the West.” “China turned out to be the right choice after all,” he says. Here, Borkowski goes by his Chinese name, Bai Wei.

    By allowing his Western worldview to influence his artistic direction, his approach differs from that of most traditional Chinese painters. “Chinese art is about how to follow the proper techniques,” he says, “which is perhaps why they are not so hung up about imitation.” Bai Wei’s work, including pieces like “Dialogue Between Apsara and Angel,” has been well received by the city, having been exhibited at the library of the Civic Center, another landmark in Qianjiang.

    This hybrid sensibility is in evidence at the David Chipperfield-designed Liangzhu Culture Museum in the north of the city, which marries the best of contemporary and global architectural design with select classical Chinese influences.

    Alibaba’s Widespread Influence

    Riding the technology wave equally effortlessly, Bai Wei is also artistic director of the VR company FXG, whose CEO spoke at the Alibaba Cloud Conference in 2017. Alibaba’s presence in Hangzhou is outsized and found in unexpected places, such as the Xixi National Wetland Park, which was properly conserved only in 2003.

    The first such project in China, it now features 2,800 acres of creeks and ponds teeming with wildlife and vegetation. The tranquility of its emerald-green waterways is in stark contrast to the fact that at one point Xixi “used to be home to pig farmers and silk weavers,” says Lu Weili, a local guide.

    Amid ripe persimmons and the thick smell of osmanthus, two white-gloved volunteers pick up litter from the ground. They are the Alibaba employees He Zhen and Wen Du, who are on a mission to help keep Xixi trash-free. He says the company encourages employees to give back to society: “We want to show others that they too can help keep the park clean.”

    Chen Min’s Hangzhou stool.

    Farther south, Chen Min is an industrial designer who also feels Alibaba’s influence. His studio’s proximity to the company’s Cloud Town, which hosts Alibaba’s Cloud Conference, means he has seen an exhibition center rise out of the ground in just four months. Nonetheless, Hangzhou is relatively peaceful. “Although Shanghai is more well known, being here means I work undisturbed,” says the Hangzhou native. “I’ve taken the best ideas and experiences from studying in Europe, and now I want to give back.”

    Chen’s design approach is a deep synthesis of tradition and modernity, recognizing contemporary sensibilities in Song-dynasty ceramics and Qing-dynasty lacquerware. One of his most striking creations is the Hangzhou stool, made of elegantly curved layers of bamboo sheets and inspired by the six bridges of the Su Causeway, another scenic spot by the West Lake. The thoughtful design earned him a 2018 Loewe Foundation Craft Prize nomination.

    Home Away From Home

    Today, the city’s historical richness and its creative and technological possibilities are fueling a confident young generation of makers. As Bai Wei reflects, upon recently traveling from Poland to Hangzhou, “It felt like I was leaving one home to return to another.” In a city whose streets are swept using tree branches, whose taxi-rides are paid by mobile phone, this feeling is doubly rich.
