    Dilemma

    Imagine a contractor you’ve hired to make repairs on your home isn’t doing quality work. What’s more, you’ve paid him $2,500 up front, as a 50-percent deposit on the job. After confronting him with your concerns, he promises to fix the problems. After a couple of weeks, it’s clear he’s still doing shoddy work. You want to fire him, but you know you’ll lose the $2,500 deposit.

    Dilemma
    Question

    Should you keep the contractor and continue to press for quality work? Or should you fire him and find someone else, knowing you’d sacrifice the $2,500 deposit?

    If your instinct is to keep working with your contractor, given the $2,500 you’ve already invested, you might want to think again. “It’s reasonable to want to get your money’s worth, says Mark Riepe, senior vice president of the Schwab Center for Financial Research. “But once the money has been spent — and you can’t get it back — it shouldn’t influence your decision about what to do next.”

    Economists have a term for this behavioral quirk: the sunk cost fallacy. In 2002, the Nobel Prize in Economics was awarded to Daniel Kahneman for combining insights from the worlds of psychology and economics to show that this sort of loss aversion has enormous implications for financial decision-making.

    For regular investors, too, this bias can exert a powerful pull on personal and investing decisions, Riepe says. “Once someone has put time and money going down a given path, they’re loath to turn around, even when it becomes increasingly obvious there are better options.”

    When it comes to investing, your instinct may be to stick to the plan in the hope of breaking even. After all, there is the potential to recoup losses if your investment gains value. But holding onto a losing position also potentially exposes you to more risk. And there’s some ego involved here, too. “If you have to sell a stock and realize a loss, that’s tantamount to admitting you’re wrong” Riepe says.

    To train yourself to see past these blind spots, shift your perspective. “If an outsider were to come in with fresh eyes, would they make the same choices?” asks Riepe, who says that some soul searching might also help. “Ask yourself, ‘Is my decision influenced by the fact that I’ve already invested?’”

    Next, when you get a portfolio statement, consider all your holdings in order to evaluate any missed opportunities. “You might say, ‘What else am I not doing because I’ve got my money tied up in these investments?’”

    The upside: Once you cut your losses, whether it’s firing a bad contractor or selling losing stocks, you’re open for new opportunities — and perhaps a better chance at reaping some gains and overcoming the loss after all.
