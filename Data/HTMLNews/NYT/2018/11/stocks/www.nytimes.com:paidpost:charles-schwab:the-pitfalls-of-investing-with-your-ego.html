    Dilemma

    You’ve decided to buy a large number of shares in one of your favorite tech companies. You’ve followed the company for years, and you’re confident that the stock is poised for long-term growth. The only question is how much of your portfolio you should invest.

    Dilemma
    Question

    You have $50,000 worth of other stocks you’re willing to sell for this, but you could invest more if you sold off other, less promising pieces of your portfolio. What do you do?

    Just invest the $50,000, for now.

    Confidence is a positive quality, but overconfidence — a behavioral bias that’s hard to spot — can work against you as an investor, says Mark Riepe, senior vice president of the Schwab Center for Financial Research. Investors pride themselves on their confidence, and as a result, “many people overrate their ability to pick stocks,” Riepe says.

    Overconfidence can create a number of hurdles for investors, starting with risk exposure.

    In this case, your faith in the tech company’s performance has led you to invest your original $50,000 and sell off some of your other stocks to invest even more, leaving you with a potentially bigger stake in equities (and in tech stocks) than might be ideal for your investment goals. “When concentrated portfolios work well, that’s wonderful,” Riepe says. “But the downside is that you’re exposed to more risk if something goes wrong.”

    It’s hard to accurately forecast the future, and overconfidence will only add unnecessary risk to the equation. “Things can morph in lots of different directions,” says Riepe, so you're going to end up being unprepared for the future as it actually plays out.” In short, overconfidence can amount to missing opportunities. “You can end up taking on more risk than you realized,” he says.

    When does confidence tip into overconfidence? Again, it can be tricky to spot. You don’t want to lack conviction in your investing choices. But hubris also can lead you to make swift decisions without enough information.

    What can investors do to remain self-assured but not reckless?

    “Be your own devil’s advocate,” Riepe says. If you’re 100 percent certain about a financial choice, try to argue yourself out of it so that you see the downside.

    Getting a second opinion, of course, can be a helpful reality check. And keeping your confidence under control could ultimately help you come out ahead.
