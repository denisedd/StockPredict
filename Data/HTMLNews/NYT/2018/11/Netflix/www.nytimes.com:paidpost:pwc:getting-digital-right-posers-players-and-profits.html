    Getting Digital Right: Posers, Players and Profits

    What does it mean to be digital? Think back to a decade or two ago. It meant being on the web. Then it meant adopting new shiny IT. No more. Digital is a way of doing business. Its being innovative. Quick. Adaptive. Busting hierarchies. Using technology to drive change--and profits.

    But how well do companies harness and profit from “being digital”? More successful companies bear similar hallmarks: they define digital as a way of doing business, they have a mindset that embraces innovation they work with, less bureaucratic decision-making, and they put experience high on the priority list. They’re transcending a product or service and becoming an experience, a name that rolls off the tongue, according to research from PwC’s decade-plus research on Digital IQ.

    While plenty of companies believe they’re digital players, their strategies, priorities, workforce and investments tell a different story. But to those who dive in full-throttle, there’s reward: financial success. Just 8% of the 2,200 respondents to PwC’s 2018 Digital IQ survey fall into that group of top financial performers--defined as companies with 3 years or more of profit growth and revenue increases of 5% or more and an expectation of similar revenue growth over the next three years.

    What makes the 8% great?

    What separates the companies—across industries and countries—that are financial superstars from the rest? For starters, top performers have embraced a forward-looking definition of digital. They are twice as likely their lower-performing peers to define digital as a reflection of a mindset that embraces constant innovation, less-bureaucratic decision-making, and the integration of technology, as a tool that helps everyone work smarter. They’ve long since blown through the murky, discovery stage—unlike their peers whose definition of digital is much more likely to center on technology and IT.

    Sound like the company you work for? Probably not. But, there’s a path to success and the 8% can show the way.

    Top performers are also gaining a competitive edge by living that definition out, taking an approach to digital that integrates business strategy, customer and employee experience. They are aware of disruption, deliberately work collaboratively and use appropriate technology to execute against overall corporate strategy. They are also more likely to promote innovation and employ workforces that are more digitally savvy and are led by executives with more digital acumen than their peers--in short, they’ve transformed into digital workforces.

    It’s the combination of these crucial elements that lead to much more than just financial success for companies in the 8%. They’re doing what we all want to: creating and delivering better products and services, fostering happier, more savvy employees, wowing customers, really getting tech right--for the company, for employees, for customers--and living a way of working that makes it happen.

    Silos or synthesis?

    If you knew you needed to transport your entire workforce across the continent, you wouldn’t build an airplane off in a side building, at one-tenth the scale you need, a side project delegated to a handful of people who ‘get’ or just want to “experiment” with planes.

    Yet, that’s exactly what many companies do with digital—silo a small project off in the corner in the form of an innovation group or a digital subset, forgotten on another floor or another P&L statement and not a part of overall strategy or success of the company. Who runs that digital thing? Oh, right, that guy. We should invite him to a meeting now and then. Sound familiar?

    Top-performing companies are far more likely to involve the executives responsible for executing on digital strategies in their higher-level corporate strategy efforts at twice the rate of their less-successful peers—although only about half explicitly incorporate digital strategy into overall corporate strategy. Digital capabilities are the single-most driver of top-line growth—and failure to include digital executives in strategy expresses a commitment to failure.

    Digital is everything

    Top performers are putting their money where their mouth—and strategy—sits. A full 59% of top performers are investing 10% or more of their revenue in digital efforts—and one-in-six of them are investing 20%-to-25%. Compare that to their less successful peers: just 29% are investing at least 10% in their digital efforts, according to PwC’s 2018 Digital IQ survey. And they are more likely to see those investments pay off more than expected off in critical areas. Among them: creating better customer experiences, innovating products and improving talent retention and recruitment—key elements of taking a company from a product-and-service world into a platform that ascends want and turns into need.

    That, in turn, is translating into better profits, better decision making, enhanced brand and bigger cost savings.

    Leaders or laggards at the top?

    High organizational digital IQ flows from the top, with senior leaders modeling the skills and behaviors they need from the digital workforce. While there’s no difference between top performers and the rest when it comes to having a CEO that champions digital (72% say they do), there’s a gap in know-how: 54% of top-performers say their leadership is digitally savvy and helps the workforce think in new ways, according to PwC’s 2018 Digital IQ survey. That leaves plenty of room for improvement, but compared with just 41% of less successful peers, it’s a world of difference.

    For many executives across industries, there’s a strong need to actively improve their digital know-how across functions. To bridge the gap, some companies look outside their industry--in particular, in areas that are core to delivering on the promises of digital. Experience is one key area: 91% of top performers have an executive in charge of employee experience—with about the same number committed to the idea that employee experience is critical to business performance. Just 70% of their peers say the same. The gap is similar for customer experience. It matters. People say they will pay an average of 16% more for superior customer service. And one in three people say they would walk away from a brand they love after just one bad experience.

    Even digitally-savvy Apple struggled to meet customers’ desire for in-store tech-enabled but still-human human experiences. It looked outside to help transform its stores into thriving town squares under the aegis of Angela Ahrendts—the former Burberry CEO who’d restored that brand’s luxury roots with strategic cuts to anything creating noise around the brand.

    Disruptable or indestructible?

    The next wave of digital disruption is coming, but it might not be seismic. Yet, many companies ignore the threat because they believe they’re less likely to take a death blow. That could be true, but we’ve moved to a rapid-pace, always-changing business environment and these days disruption is often much more iterative.

    It’s that incremental disruption that can be a company-killer. Imagine if Netflix had ignored the burgeoning fascination with streaming and insisted on sticking with mail-and-return movie rentals, or dismissed the idea of owned content as a fad and stuck with movie streaming. A full 64% of top financial performers do view disruption as a serious threat; only 27% of posers say the same, according to PwC’s 2018 Digital IQ survey. That belief guides digital investment and prioritization as well as strategy and workforce upskilling.

    Top performers are more than twice as likely to expect their digital investments to help combat disruption or help them disrupt their own or other industries. And they are finding success in doing so.

    Being digital isn’t something extra. It’s not a nice to have. It’s now. It’s everything. It’s having a mindset that embraces constant innovation. It’s flatter, data-guided decision making. It’s developing a workforce and culture that hones the types of skills and behaviors that speed innovation and bolster experience. The companies that have embraced this “it’s all digital” approach offer a roadmap of sorts for those who are playing catch up. It’s not too late to take control and win in the digital world.

    The hallmarks of a truly digital company help drive results.
