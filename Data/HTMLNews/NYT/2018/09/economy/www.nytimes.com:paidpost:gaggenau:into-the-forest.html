    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY GAGGENAU

    Into the Forest

    Gaggenau, a centuries-old German appliance brand, creates a magical dining experience in downtown Los Angeles. Take a look inside.

    By the time the sun sets and the first guests step into the cool darkness of the pop-up Gaggenau Restaurant 1683, every detail has been precisely put in place, to fairytale effect. Craggy fir trees soar toward the roof. A blue-white luminescence cuts through tree shadows and fog, reflecting off drifts of pristine snow. And all around the woodsy scene, a dozen chefs, split among six immaculate cooking stations, diligently prep for the upcoming service.

    Five days earlier, the architect Hendrik Müller had flown in to help execute the ephemeral dining experience on the top floor of a commercial building in downtown Los Angeles. For Müller, who runs the architectural firm Einszu33 in Munich, it was the culmination of five months of meticulous planning and design. The final installment demanded a team of 50 working long hours to build out the space, which also included a giant cuckoo clock flanked by models in traditional Black Forest garb, and dark timber framing each custom kitchen station. In total, Restaurant 1683 would encompass three incredible nights of eating, drinking and celebration.

    Müller cited childhood memories of the Black Forest and works by the director David Lynch as inspiration. This spring with Hollywood nearby, Müller chose a more theatrical spin: winter. But how exactly does dinner in a storybook penthouse high above Los Angeles evince Gaggenau, a 335-year-old luxury home appliance company?

    Gaggenau was started in 1683 as a nail forge by the Margrave of Baden to kickstart the economy in the German territory after central Europe’s devastating Thirty Years’ War. Its location in the Black Forest was key, providing ample energy to smelt iron. Over centuries Gaggenau evolved, producing everything from agricultural tools to bicycles. Post World War II, a German middle class emerged, and the cooking enthusiast Georg von Blanquet took over in 1956. The company began designing sophisticated, simple-to-use appliances for the growing trend of built-in kitchens — including cooktops, ovens and ventilation geared to the needs of professional chefs.

    Gaggenau has since transformed into a luxury appliance brand, one coveted by culinary enthusiasts committed to creating only the most refined home spaces. Skilled craftspeople have used their artisanship for centuries to transform raw metal into practical tools, and more recently alongside technological advances, to finish each product.

    I have a two-word saying: ‘slow innovation.’ It’s not disruption. It’s to know what we do, to know what people expect from us and to make it last.
    — Sven Schnee, Head of Global Gaggenau

    Two years ago, the chef Daniel Humm of New York’s Eleven Madison Park helmed the inaugural Gaggenau pop-up in the Chelsea arts district, which marked the launch of this extraordinary series. This spring, Humm took 30 staff members to help create the 48-person multicourse dinners at the Los Angeles event. “In New York we were just blown away. That’s why we’re here again,” he said.

    In Los Angeles, invite-only guests include industry members, tastemakers and celebrities. As they settle into seats at their cooking stations, Humm spoons dollops of hollandaise sauce over his signature eggs Benedict, the brunch staple reimagined in a kaleidoscope-like presentation of diced ham, asparagus and caviar.

    Humm has a select number of brand partners. So why Gaggenau? “I love the product, I love the aesthetic, I love the heritage. It’s very close to where I’m from,” says Humm, who grew up in Switzerland near the German border. “But then it’s also about the chemistry between the people behind it. Very few times do you get to work with a group of people who are creative and don’t cut corners, who are pushing the boundaries. It was a true collaboration.”

    The precision is remarkable, as is the reliability. And beyond that, all of the equipment is stunning, a work of art, really.
    — Chef Daniel Humm, Eleven Madison Park

    In our modern, disposable culture in which so many products are designed to become quickly obsolete or fall out of fashion, Gaggenau continues to defy every trend. The company has lasted for centuries and stands for craftsmanship and quality worth preserving.
