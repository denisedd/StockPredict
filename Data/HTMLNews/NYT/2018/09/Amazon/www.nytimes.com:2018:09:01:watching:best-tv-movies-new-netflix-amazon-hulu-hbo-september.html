The Best TV Shows and Movies New to Netflix, Amazon and More in September

by Jennifer Vineyard

Sep. 1, 2018UPDATED Sep. 4, 2018

From left: Emma Stone in “Maniac;” Doug Jones, as the Asset, with Sally Hawkins in “The Shape of Water;” Leonardo DiCaprio in “What’s Eating Gilbert Grape.”Netflix; 20th Century Fox; Paramount, via Everett Collection

Every month, subscription streaming services add a new batch of movies and TV shows to their libraries. Here are the titles we think are most interesting for September, broken down by service and release date. Streaming services occasionally change schedules without giving notice.

TV New to Netflix

Victoria Yeates in Season 7 of “Call the Midwife.”Laura Radford/Neal Street Productions

‘Call the Midwife’ Season 7
Starts streaming: Sept. 10
Save it to your Watchlist.

Scruffy East London in the early 1960s: Pregnancy and childbirth come with all the age-old complications, as well as some period-specific ones, as the charming nurses and nuns of Nonnatus House make the rounds and confront social issues of the day. Comfort viewing.
—

A scene from Season 2 of “American Vandal.”Netflix

‘American Vandal’ Season 2
Starts streaming: Sept. 14
Save it to your Watchlist.

The first season of “American Vandal” came out of nowhere last fall, becoming one of the year’s most acclaimed new series. Season 2 will continue to parody true-crime documentaries, with the high school filmmakers investigating a fresh bit of disgusting juvenile mayhem involving a laxative-spiked batch of Catholic school lemonade.
—

A scene from Season 5 of “BoJack Horseman.”Netflix

‘BoJack Horseman’ Season 5
Starts streaming: Sept. 14
Save it to your Watchlist.

This acclaimed adult animated series became even more mature in Season 4, often pushing its absurdist humor into the background in order to focus on the core issues faced by its characters: aging, loneliness and wanting to have some meaningful impact on the world. Creator Raphael Bob-Waksberg has been tight-lipped about the coming season, but judging by the way the last one ended, it looks as if the show may get back to being more of a showbiz spoof.
—

Jane Fonda and Norm Macdonald in “Norm Macdonald Has a Show.”Netflix

‘Norm Macdonald Has a Show’
Starts streaming: Sept. 14
Save it to your Watchlist.

The dry-witted comedian Norm Macdonald has long been one of TV’s best talk show guests, cracking up the likes of David Letterman and Conan O’Brien with his long-winded shaggy dog stories and inappropriate remarks. His comic style in recent years has been more conceptual, sometimes testing the audience’s tolerance for “jokes” that may not even be intended as such. It will be interesting to see how Macdonald brings that sensibility to the other side of the chat-show desk.
—

Evan Peters, center, in “American Horror Story: Cult.”Frank Ockenfels/FX

‘American Horror Story’: Cult’
Starts streaming: Sept. 18
Save it to your Watchlist.

“American Horror Story: Apocalypse” not ghastly enough? This is its ultraviolent cousin. Evan Peters plays a candidate (and cult leader) with a specialty in stirring up female rage. Sarah Paulson is his opponent (and target), a “snowflake” who suffers postelection anxiety disorder (among other awful things). The story is sometimes ludicrous and messy, just like politics.
—

Jonah Hill and Emma Stone in "Maniac."Michele K. Short/Netflix

‘Maniac’ (Limited Series)
Starts streaming: Sept. 21
Save it to your Watchlist.

Emma Stone and Jonah Hill appear on screen together for the first time since “Superbad” (2007) in an adaptation of a darkly comic, science-fiction-tinged Norwegian TV series. Directed by Cary Fukunaga (who worked wonders with “True Detective” Season 1), with a script by Patrick Somerville (“The Leftovers”), “Maniac” also stars Justin Theroux and Sally Field, in a story about lost souls who take part in a drug trial, then find out that the panacea they’ve been promised is messing with their perception of reality.
—

Also of interest: “Once Upon a Time” Season 7 (Sept. 6), “Atypical” Season 2 (September 7), Marvel’s Iron Fist” Season 2 (Sept. 7), “The Blacklist” Season 5 (Sept. 12), “Ingobernable” Season 2 (Sept. 14), and “The Walking Dead” Season 8 (Sept. 23).

Movies New to Netflix

From left, Judd Nelson, Emilio Estevez, Ally Sheedy, Molly Ringwald and Anthony Michael Hall in “The Breakfast Club.”Universal Pictures, via Everett Collection

‘The Breakfast Club’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Five period-perfect students from the 1980s (Judd Nelson, Molly Ringwald, Emilio Estevez, Ally Sheedy, Anthony Michael Hall) are stuck in Saturday detention together, and they pass the time tearing down each other’s facades with condescending eye rolls, snappy dialogue and a little marijuana. Nelson’s defiant Bender is a classic jerk, but we’d be neo-maxi-zoom-dweebies if we didn’t admit he has the best lines.
—

Andie MacDowell and Bill Murray in “Groundhog Day.”Columbia Pictures

‘Groundhog Day’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Why is “Groundhog Day” considered one of the best movie comedies? Most of the credit goes to Bill Murray, who gives perhaps his funniest and most soulful performance, as a weatherman trapped in a time-loop in Punxsutawney, Penn. But what also makes the film special is the way writer-director Harold Ramis and his co-writer Danny Rubin waste little time jumping into their premise and then proceed to have fun with it, coming up with dozens of clever ideas for what a person might do if he kept reliving the same day.
—

Al Pacino in “Scarface.”Universal Pictures

‘Scarface’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

This is the Brian De Palma movie that launched a handful of catchphrases, many bad Al Pacino impressions and countless movie posters in dorm rooms across the country. Written by Oliver Stone, the film follows the ruthless Cuban gangster Tony Montana as he climbs his way up from being a low-level hood to ruling a slice of the Miami drug scene in the decadent ’80s. In his perverted sense of the American dream, nothing is off limits.
—

Lupita Nyong'o, left, and Letitia Wright in "Black Panther."Matt Kennedy/Marvel — Disney

‘Black Panther’
Starts streaming: Sept. 4
Save it to your Watchlist.

All hail the women of Wakanda! The fierce warrior Okoye (Danai Gurira), the spy Nakia (Lupita Nyong’o) and the lovable tech wizard Shuri (Letitia Wright), sister of the titular superhero (Chadwick Boseman), are the secret weapons of this groundbreaking Marvel flick.
—

Ben Mendelsohn and Edie Falco in “The Land of Steady Habits.”Alison Rosa/Netflix

‘The Land of Steady Habits’
Starts streaming: Sept. 14
Save it to your Watchlist.

The writer-director Nicole Holofcener — one of the smartest and funniest American filmmakers of the past 20 years — makes a rare foray into literary adaptation, turning Ted Thompson’s acclaimed debut novel “The Land of Steady Habits” into a movie. Ben Mendelsohn plays an upper-middle-class Connecticut suburbanite who has worked for decades to be able to coast through retirement, but ends up unsatisfied. Edie Falco, Connie Britton, Thomas Mann and Bill Camp also star, in what should be another witty and affecting Holofcener film about the spiritual unease of the financially comfortable.
—

Michael Cera, top, and Satya Bhabha in “Scott Pilgrim vs. the World.”Universal Pictures

‘Scott Pilgrim vs. the World’
Starts streaming: Sept. 16
Save it to your Watchlist.

Coolly received on its release, this action comedy from Edgar Wright is a hilarious riff on the underdog story. Our hero, Scott Pilgrim (Michael Cera), falls in love with the perfect cool girl, Ramona Flowers (Mary Elizabeth Winstead), but there’s a catch: He has to defeat her seven evil exes in video game-style fights so they can have a real chance at love.
—

A scene from “The Witch.”A24

‘The Witch’
Starts streaming: Sept. 17
Save it to your Watchlist.

In 17th-century Massachusetts, a family exiled from a local community tries to make a go of it on the edge of a forest. But misery befalls them. Is a witch to blame? (Probably. The movie is called “The Witch.”) One of the best horror movies in recent memory, this should nonetheless be watched only if you’re patient and adventurous. It’s slow, and everyone speaks in 17th-century English — a film more full of dread than frights.
—

Sanaa Lathan in “Nappily Ever After.”Tina Rowden/Netflix

‘Nappily Ever After’
Starts streaming: Sept. 21
Save it to your Watchlist.

Hairstyles have power in this romantic comedy, which stars Sanaa Lathan as a glamorous professional woman who gets so fed up with her boyfriend’s fear of commitment that she shaves her head. Based on a Trisha R. Thomas novel, “Nappily Ever After” takes aim at the black beauty industry and at the time and money women spend each week to conform to expectations. Haifaa al-Mansour directs, whose coming-of-age film “Wadjda” was a critical hit and minor art-house sensation a few years ago.
—

Jeffrey Wright in “Hold the Dark.”David Bukach/Netflix

‘Hold the Dark’
Starts streaming: Sept. 28
Save it to your Watchlist.

With just two films — “Blue Ruin” (2013) and “Green Room” (2015) — director Jeremy Saulnier has built a reputation as a modern master of suspense. His latest film is an adaptation of William Giraldi’s novel “Hold the Dark,” about a wolf expert (played by Jeffrey Wright) called to an icy, tiny Alaskan village to investigate the abduction of the young son of a local couple (Riley Keough and Alexander Skarsgard). If the movie stays true to the book, expect something grim and gripping.
—

Also of interest: “Bruce Almighty” (Sept. 1), “The Cider House Rules” (Sept. 1), “The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy” (Sept. 1), “Nacho Libre” (Sept. 1), “Two Weeks Notice” (Sept. 1), “Unforgiven” (Sept. 1), “Lilo & Stitch” (Sept. 2), “City of Joy” (Sept. 7), “The Angel” (Sept. 14), “Quincy” (Sept. 21), “Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl” (Sept. 25) and “A Wrinkle in Time” (Sept. 25).

New to Amazon Prime Video

John Travolta in “Blow Out.”Filmways Pictures

‘Blow Out’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

In this Brian De Palma thriller from 1981, John Travolta plays a very different character from his better-known roles of the time — like his disco-driven dancer in “Saturday Night Fever” or his singing-and-dancing greaser in “Grease.” Here, his role is somber and serious: a sound man trying to solve a murder he inadvertently recorded before the killer (John Lithgow) returns to finish off a witness (Nancy Allen).
—

Jack Nicholson and Faye Dunaway in “Chinatown.”Paramount Pictures

‘Chinatown’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

With its meaty script by Robert Towne, “Chinatown” is a 1974 update to the film noir genre, set in sunny 1937 Los Angeles amid the California water wars. Jack Nicholson plays Jake Gittes, a reasonably honest private investigator; Faye Dunaway plays Evelyn Mulwray, a strange, nervous woman he becomes professionally involved with. The movie is just about perfect in every particular, with a bleak and jolting conclusion that shined a light on powerful men who get away with rape and murder. Its director, Roman Polanski, fled the country a few years later after pleading guilty to statutory rape.
—

From left, Taraji P. Henson, Paula Jai Parker, Terrence Howard and Taryn Manning in “Hustle & Flow.”John Singleton/Crunk Pictures

‘Hustle & Flow’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

DJay (Terrence Howard) is a pimp who aspires to be more than what life has set out for him: He wants to be a rap star. To record a demo, DJay assembles a makeshift crew, but to make a real splash, though, he must fall back on the misogyny and violence that defined his first career. The single DJay records (actually recorded by Three 6 Mafia) won the Academy Award for best original song, the first time a rap group ever won that prize.
—

Cuba Gooding Jr., left, and Tom Cruise in “Jerry Maguire.”Columbia Pictures

‘Jerry Maguire’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

When the sports agent Jerry Maguire (Tom Cruise) writes a mission statement proclaiming the importance of personal relationships over profits, the “cynical world” he decries strikes back. Jerry doesn’t actually know how love and friendships work, but the great beauty of Cameron Crowe’s movie is watching him learn. Lessons begin with his sole client (played by Cuba Gooding Jr., who won an Oscar for his performance); a sweet single mom who becomes his wife (Renée Zellweger); and her seriously cute kid (Jonathan Lipnicki).
—

Campbell Scott, left, and Jesse Eisenberg in “Roger Dodger.”Peter Figetakis/Artisan Entertainment

‘Roger Dodger’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Roger (a wonderfully glib Campbell Scott) fancies himself a pickup artist — and because he has an unflagging gift of gab, he can be quite persuasive. When his sweet but awkward teenage nephew (Jesse Eisenberg) approaches him for pointers, Roger takes him out for a master class in seduction. Waiting for the inevitable backfire is a large part of the fun.
—

Juliette Lewis and Johnny Depp in “What’s Eating Gilbert Grape.”Paramount Pictures, via Everett Collection

‘What’s Eating Gilbert Grape’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Johnny Depp and Leonardo DiCaprio have possibly never been better than in this hugely affecting movie from 1993. Depp’s Gilbert is a young man trapped in a going-nowhere life, burdened with taking care of his morbidly obese mother (Darlene Cates) — who, at 500 pounds, is putting serious stress on the foundations of their house — and his developmentally disabled brother (DiCaprio), who keeps wandering off into trouble. Director Lasse Hallstrom presents none of these people as objects of pity — he’s on their side, and very soon so are we.
—

Also of interest: “The Amityville Horror” (Sept. 1), “Bandits” (Sept. 1), “Dressed to Kill” (Sept. 1), “A Field in England” (Sept. 1), “Ghostbusters” (Sept. 1), “Gutland” (Sept. 1), “The Longest Yard” (Sept. 1), “Miami Vice” (Sept. 1), “Primal Fear” (Sept. 1), “The Score” (Sept. 1), “Sleepless in Seattle” (Sept. 1), “Smokey and the Bandit” (Sept. 1), “There Will Be Blood” (Sept. 1), “Crescent” (Sept. 4), “Stronger” (Sept. 8), “On Chesil Beach” (Sept. 15), “Baby Mama” (Sept. 16), “The Good Shepherd” (Sept. 16) and “King Lear” (Sept. 28).

New to Hulu

Nicolas Cage as twin brothers in “Adaptation.”Columbia Pictures

‘Adaptation’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

This multilayered, self-referential adaptation of Susan Orlean’s nonfiction book “The Orchid Thief” tells the complex story of a successful but beleaguered screenwriter (a mopey Nicolas Cage), his clueless twin brother (a manic Nicolas Cage), a journalist (Meryl Streep) and a Floridian misfit (Chris Cooper). How they fit together is fascinating — directed by Spike Jonze and written by Charlie Kaufman (who collaborated on “Being John Malkovich”), the film is partly about struggling to adapt “The Orchid Thief” — and the performances and script hold many surprises.
—

Alexandre Rodrigues and Alice Braga in “City of God.”Miramax Films

‘City of God’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Set away from Rio de Janeiro’s sandy beaches and tourist destinations, “City of God” is about a Brazilian favela that gives this lively but disturbing movie its title. The events of this ferocious drama rush by over the course of two decades, as Rocket (Alexandre Rodrigues) seeks to escape the slums, gun-toting children and ruthless gangs of his neighborhood by becoming a photographer.
—

Gwyneth Paltrow, left, and Toni Collette in “Emma.”Mary Evans/Miramax Films, via Everett Collection

‘Emma’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Jane Austen’s “Emma” has inspired wayward matchmakers like the ever-stylish Cher in “Clueless,” but this 1996 adaptation sticks closer to its 19th-century source, with Gwyneth Paltrow as the titular young woman who thinks she has a knack for matching up her friends. Toni Collette, Alan Cumming and Ewan McGregor co-star.
—

Jeff Goldblum in “The Fly.”20th Century Fox

‘The Fly’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

This remake of a low-budget horror classic is a far more disturbing — and emotionally affecting — picture than the 1958 original. Jeff Goldblum gives one of his most powerful performances as scientist Seth Brundle, who in a teleportation experiment accidentally fuses his body with that of a housefly. That’s pretty much the premise of the first film, but director David Cronenberg turns it into a penetrating assessment of the fragility of life and the hideousness of disease. Goldblum is simply amazing, and a certain contingent also rates this as his sexiest role. We assume they mean pre-Brundlefly.
—

Jason Schwartzman in “Rushmore.”Touchstone Pictures

‘Rushmore’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Have you met Max Fischer? You really should. With his nerdy black-rimmed glasses, his braces, and his monomaniacal approach to … well, everything, Max is one of Wes Anderson’s most indelible characters. Actor Jason Schwartzman had never been to an audition or even read a script before he tried out for the part of this prep school prodigy, and yet he’s the perfect embodiment of Max’s intense drive. Equally inspired is the casting of Max’s world-weary mentor and romantic rival, who’s played unforgettably by Bill Murray. Sounds unlikely, right? Just go with it.
—

Samuel L. Jackson in “Unbreakable.”Frank Masi/Touchstone Pictures

‘Unbreakable’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

What better time to revisit the first installment in director M. Night Shyamalan’s brilliant sci-fi trilogy. (This movie was followed by “Split,” from 2016; “Glass” is scheduled for January.) David Dunn (Bruce Willis) survives a horrific train crash that kills everyone aboard but him. Elijah Price (Samuel L. Jackson), who suffers from a rare bone disease, tries to convince Dunn that they are twinned opposites, and that Dunn might actually be a superhero. (Price’s other ideas are much more sinister.) It took a while before this 2009 film was seen as the wholly original take on comic book origin stories it is.
—

Sean Penn in “The First.”Paul Schrimaldi/Hulu

‘The First’ Season 1
Starts streaming: Sept. 14
Save it to your Watchlist.

In this new show created by Beau Willimon (“House of Cards”), a flight crew prepares for the launch of the first mission to send humans to Mars. What could possibly go wrong? Surely you’re not wondering. It’s good to see Sean Penn in a meaty role, and while the series is a slow burn, patience will definitely be repaid.
—

New to HBO

The title character in “Corpse Bride.”Warner Bros.

‘Corpse Bride’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

Nothing brings the dead back to life like stop-motion animation. Tim Burton, a loyalist to this painstaking technique, gives us a decaying bride with a hole in her cheek and a maggot popping her eyeball out — but it’s cute somehow. In fact, compared with the lives we live here “Upstairs,” things seem positively cheery down in the land of the dead. Are those singing skeletons we hear?
—

Michelle Pfeiffer and Jeff Bridges in “The Fabulous Baker Boys.”20th Century Fox

‘The Fabulous Baker Boys’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

In 1989, Michelle Pfeiffer knocked everybody out not only with her acting but also with her voice. Hired to spruce up a moldy cocktail-lounge duo featuring the Baker brothers (played by the brothers Jeff and Beau Bridges), Pfeiffer’s Susie Diamond brings the aging act alive — and lights up the movie with a now-famous rendition of “Makin’ Whoopee,” performed while slithering atop a piano. There’s a lot more to the movie, but Pfeiffer is its centerpiece.
—

Deborah Kerr and Yul Brynner in “The King and I.”20th Century Fox

‘The King and I’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1
Save it to your Watchlist.

The true story of a 19th-century British governess in the court of the king of Siam was the basis of a 1944 best-selling book, then a hit Broadway musical, which in turn spawned this Oscar-winning 1956 movie. The picture plays a little strangely today: The lack of Thai performers in the several Siamese roles reflects a cinematic convention of the time (Yul Brynner, who played the king, was Russian). More abrasive, perhaps, are some antique ideas about East and West. Still, there’s the glorious Rodgers and Hammerstein score, which features a number of songs (“Hello, Young Lovers,” “Getting To Know You,” “I Whistle a Happy Tune”) that have become standards.
—

Sally Hawkins in “The Shape of Water.”Kerry Hayes/20th Century Fox

‘The Shape of Water’
Starts streaming: Sept. 22
Save it to your Watchlist.

This Oscar-winning fantasy from Guillermo del Toro is a magical romance starring Sally Hawkins and creature specialist Doug Jones. She’s a mute, mousy custodial worker, he’s a misunderstood merman who likes to eat cats, and soon enough, these two crazy kids fall in love and figure out the intricacies of interspecies sex. (One of the film’s best moments is Hawkins’s explanation of the mechanics to a surprised friend.) An underwater embrace captured in a long shot of the lovers floating in a stream of light is priceless.
—

Vicky Krieps and Daniel Day-Lewis in “Phantom Thread.”Laurie Sparham/Focus Features

‘Phantom Thread’
Starts streaming: Sept. 29
Save it to your Watchlist.

Although uncredited on the script, Daniel Day-Lewis spent a number of years working with the writer-director Paul Thomas Anderson on shaping the difficult character he plays here, an obsessive couturier named Reynolds Woodcock. Day-Lewis prepared for the role in his own obsessive way — by learning to sew (those are real scars on his fingertips) and by replicating a vintage Balenciaga dress by trial and error. But something about performing this part unsettled the actor, and Day-Lewis announced his retirement afterward. Will that last? Who’s to say? His fans — an obsessive bunch themselves — can unravel this film in the meantime.
—

Also of interest: “About Time” (Sept. 1), “Analyze This” (Sept. 1), “The Brothers McMullen” (Sept. 1), “Funny People” (Sept. 1), “Goodfellas” (Sept. 1), “Miss Sherlock” Season 1 premiere (Sept. 1), “Ronin” (Sept. 1), “Sherlock Holmes” (Sept. 1), “Shrek 2” (Sept. 1), “The Greatest Showman” (Sept. 8), “The Deuce” Season 2 premiere (Sept. 9) and “Arli$$” Seasons 1-7 (Sept. 14).
