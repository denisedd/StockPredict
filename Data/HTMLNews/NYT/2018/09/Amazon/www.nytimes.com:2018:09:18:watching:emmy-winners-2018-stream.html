Where to Stream the 2018 Emmy Winners

by Scott Tobias

Sep. 18, 2018UPDATED Sep. 22, 2018

Clockwise from top left: “Game of Thrones,” “The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel” and “RuPaul’s Drag Race.”HBO; Amazon; VH1

In an Emmys ceremony in which last year’s two big winners for drama and comedy, “The Handmaid’s Tale” and “Atlanta,” came up empty, there were plenty of surprises to go around Monday night. These included several newcomers like “The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel” and “Barry,” as well as a few longtime critical favorites like “The Americans” and “RuPaul’s Drag Race,” which finally got the attention they deserved.

Subscription services like HBO, Netflix and Amazon came out on top, which is good news for anyone who wants to catch up from home. But all the major winners from cable networks like FX and VH-1 are all available to stream as well. Here’s a rundown of what they are and where to find them.

A scene from ”Game of Thrones.”HBO

TV Show

Game of Thrones

Major wins: drama series, supporting actor in a drama series (Peter Dinklage)

Leaving George R.R. Martin and his pokey typewriter behind, the seventh season of “Game of Thrones” stepped on the gas, partly because creators David Benioff and D.B. Weiss were liberated from Martin’s novels and partly because it has only one six-episode season remaining to wrap things up. That meant incident and lots of it: Daenerys (Emilia Clarke) and her dragons leading a scorching attack on the Lannisters, Arya Stark (Maisie Williams) taking revenge for the Red Wedding, and a long-awaited confrontation with the White Walkers. The patient world-building phase of the show is over — it’s payoff time now.

Rachel Brosnahan, left, and Alex Borstein in "The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel."Amazon

TV Show

The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel

Major wins: supporting actress in a comedy series (Alex Borstein), writing for a comedy series (Amy Sherman-Palladino), directing for a comedy series (Amy Sherman-Palladino), lead actress in a comedy series (Rachel Brosnahan), comedy series

From the very first episode of her long-running TV show “Gilmore Girls,” it was clear that creator Amy Sherman-Palladino’s voice was unusually stylized, turning a fictional New England town into a utopia of screwball patter and nonstop quips. The big winner among comedies Monday night, taking five Primetime Emmys, “The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel” accommodates Sherman-Palladino’s sensibility so perfectly that it feels like the show she was born to make: a gorgeous vision of 1958 New York City that accommodates her borscht belt wit. As “Midge” Maisel, Rachel Brosnahan bristles with confidence and style as a character who tries to be the perfect ’50s housewife but eventually opts to be the next Lenny Bruce instead.

Matthew Rhys and Keri Russell in “The Americans.”FX

TV Show

The Americans

Major wins: writing for a drama series (Joe Weisberg and Joel Fields), lead actor for a drama series (Matthew Rhys)

The final season of “The Americans” reckons with the thawing of the Cold War and its consequences for Soviet spies Philip (Matthew Rhys) and Elizabeth (Keri Russell), who risked their lives for a cause that brought mostly pain and disillusionment in return. While there’s an air of inevitability that hangs over the season, showrunners Joe Weisberg and Joel Fields make the “how” of it thrilling and unexpected, tying their characters’ fate to that of two superpowers coming to terms with decades of conflict. “The Americans” also serves as a prime example of how satisfying a show can be when its makers know well in advance when it’s going to end.

Danny Sapani and Claire Foy in “The Crown.”Alex Bailey/Netflix

TV Show

The Crown

Major wins: directing for a drama series (Stephen Daldry), lead actress in a drama series (Claire Foy)

This series from Peter Morgan about Queen Elizabeth II strikes like a velvet hammer, luxuriating in the impeccable period fineries of the monarchy while noting Elizabeth’s shrewd, occasionally devastating shows of power. With the next season jumping ahead and replacing the entire cast, now is the time to savor Claire Foy’s turn as Elizabeth, who spends Season 2 seeing the royal family through the late 1950s and early 1960s, when the English government was dealt its share of humbling moments. Amid failures like Britain’s quick withdrawal from Egypt and the Profumo affair, Elizabeth also confronts serious crises at home, as her marriage to Philip (Matt Smith) hits the rocks.

Bill Hader in “Barry.”HBO

TV Show

Barry

Major wins: supporting actor in a comedy series (Henry Winkler), lead actor in a comedy series (Bill Hader)

A contract killer takes an acting class on the side. Sounds funny, right? Yes and no. To some extent, Bill Hader and Alec Berg’s terrific new series does capitalize on the cognitive dissonance that comes from Hader’s hit man faking his way through acting exercises led by an earnest never-was in the Los Angeles theater scene (Henry Winkler). Yet “Barry” grew darker and more emotionally rich as it progressed, shifting into a twisty tragicomedy about an ex-Marine who wants to turn his life around, but can’t so easily transcend the horrors he’s perpetuated. “Grosse Pointe Blank”: The TV Show this is not.

Darren Criss in “The Assassination of Gianni Versace: American Crime Story.” Jeff Daly/FX

TV Show

The Assassination of Gianni Versace: American Crime Story

Major wins: directing for a limited series, movie or dramatic special (Ryan Murphy), lead actor in a limited series or movie (Darren Criss), limited series

Ryan Murphy’s follow-up to “The People v. O.J. Simpson” continues the same scrupulous approach to the true-crime genre, although the story of the fashion designer Gianni Versace and his killer, Andrew Cunanan, leaves far more room for speculation about their private lives and motivations. The series starts at the end, with Versace (Edgar Ramírez) murdered outside his home in Miami Beach in 1997. Moving backward in time through Cunanan’s killing spree, “The Assassination of Gianni Versace” spends the bulk of its time on Cunanan (Darren Criss), a con man and killer whose exploits carry a lot of insight into attitudes about homosexuality in “Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell”-era America and their toxic consequences.

Regina King in “Seven Seconds.”JoJo Whilden/Netflix

TV Show

Seven Seconds

Major wins: lead actress in a limited series or movie (Regina King)

The 2013 Russian action thriller “The Major” covered the fallout from a police major who strikes and kills a child with his car while rushing to the hospital, where his wife is in labor. From the bones of that 99-minute feature, the creator of “The Killing,” Veena Sud successfully expands the premise to a 10-episode limited series and adds a racial component that speaks to contemporary tensions between the police and African-Americans. She also gets a powerhouse performance from Regina King, who plays the mother of a slain black teenager from Jersey City, seeking justice against a police unit that is closing rank around one of its own.

Jeff Daniels in “Godless.”Ursula Coyote/Netflix

TV Show

Godless

Major wins: supporting actress in a limited series or movie (Merritt Wever), supporting actor in a limited series or movie (Jeff Daniels)

The genre maestro Scott Frank (“Out of Sight”) spent years trying to get “Godless” made as a movie before he was encouraged to expand his script into a network series, which is how it landed at Netflix. Over its seven-episode sprawl, Frank mixes-and-matches seemingly incompatible elements: classic shoot-’em-ups like “Rio Bravo”; a sadistic outlaw (Jeff Daniels) who’s cut from the same cloth as Judge Holden in Cormac McCarthy’s “Blood Meridian”; and a town populated and run mostly by widows, including the dead mayor’s wife (Merritt Wever), who literally steps into her husband’s pants. That something-for-everyone approach risks incoherence, but Frank affirms the elasticity of the Western genre, which can accommodate many tones at once.

Thandie Newton in "Westworld."HBO

TV Show

Westworld

Major wins: supporting actress in a drama series (Thandie Newton)

In its second season, this sci-fi Western from Lisa Joy and Jonathan Nolan pushed its philosophical puzzle-making to the edge of coherence, adding multiple timelines, multiple android clones and multiple theme parks to an already busy agenda. But the host rebellion that started the previous season added thrilling new wrinkles to Season 2, which forced the androids to confront difficult choices over what to do with their newfound freedom. As Maeve, Thandie Newton became the unlikely heart of the show, transforming herself from the vengeful host of Season 1 to a mother pursuing an elusive picture of family.

A scene from “RuPaul’s Drag Race.”VH1

TV Show

RuPaul’s Drag Race

Major wins: reality competition series, reality host (RuPaul)

It took 10 seasons for “RuPaul’s Drag Race” to finally win the Emmy for reality competition series, but the show has quietly gained in popularity as it has aged. Most reality shows of its kind separate hosts, judges and mentors, but RuPaul assumes all three roles, presiding over contestants like a towering, benevolent deity. The 10th season brought in a murderer’s row of guest judges — Christina Aguilera, Courtney Love, Kate Upton and Lena Dunham, among others — and included challenges like creating looks based on climate change, portraying Cher from various phases of her career and the annual “Snatch Game” impersonations. (Maya Angelou!) But mostly, the show continues to serve as a joyous affirmation of the queer experience, in glorious Technicolor.

Jesse Plemons, right, in “Black Mirror.”Netflix

TV Show

Black Mirror

Major wins: writing for a limited series, movie or drama (Charlie Brooker and William Bridges)

In its fourth season, this anthology series from Charlie Brooker about our inglorious technological future — in short, it’s bad — explored a comprehensive monitoring system for fretful parents, a device that visualizes eyewitness memories and a world overrun by robotic guard dogs. Fans and critics were most impressed by the first episode, the Emmy-winning “U.S.S. Callister,” about the co-founder of a multiplayer online game (Jesse Plemons) who clones his co-workers’ DNA and creates a “Star Trek”-like game-within-the-game to exert a cruel authority over them. A virtual mutiny follows, but the main draw is the allusions to “Star Trek,” which are realized with a playfulness that often eludes the show.

A scene from “Wild Wild Country.”Netflix

TV Show

Wild Wild Country

Major wins: documentary or nonfiction series

The question of religious liberty — who gets it, how far it extends and what rights it might supersede — is eternally relevant in America. Amid the glut of true-crime documentary series, “Wild Wild Country” stands out for unearthing a case that demonstrates the privileges that some forms of worship in America enjoy over others, and the resistance that can arise toward immigrants who import their beliefs. Over six episodes, each just over an hour long, the show looks into the movement that surrounded Bhagwan Shree Rajneesh, an Indian guru who brought his followers to a barren patch of land in Wasco County, Ore., where their practices drew intense scrutiny from the locals and the government. And that only begins to describe a story with more wild turns than the Bhagwan had Rolls Royces.

A scene from "Rick and Morty."Adult Swim

TV Show

Rick and Morty

Major wins: animated program

Here’s what happens in “Pickle Rick,” the “Rick and Morty” episode that earned the show its first Emmy nomination and win for outstanding animated program: The alcoholic mad scientist Rick turns himself into a sentient pickle in order to get out of family counseling and then gets carried off on an adventure that has him slaying rats and 32 armed guards in an exoskeleton made of improvised cybernetics and rat parts; meanwhile, his 14-year-old grandson, Morty, joins his mother and sister in a session run by a therapist who also specializes in treating coprophagia (Susan Sarandon). And that’s just a typical 22-minute assault on the senses from creators Justin Roiland and Dan Harmon, who are now three seasons into a show that combines multidimensional science fiction, existential dread and domestic discord in an absurdist, hyperkinetic blitz.
