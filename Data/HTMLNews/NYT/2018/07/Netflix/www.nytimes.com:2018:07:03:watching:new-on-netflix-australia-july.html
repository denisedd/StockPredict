The Best Movies and TV Shows New to Netflix Australia in July

by Noel Murray

July 3, 2018UPDATED Nov. 9, 2018

From left, scenes from “White Fang” and “The Rocky Horror Picture Show.”Big Beach; 20th Century Fox

Every month, Netflix Australia adds a new batch of movies and TV shows to its library. Here are the titles we think are most interesting for July, broken down by release date. Netflix occasionally changes its schedule without giving notice.

TV Series New to Netflix

‘Suits’ Season 7
Starts streaming: July 13

One of TV’s longest-running modern legal dramas received an unexpected boost last year when one of the actresses on the show, Meghan Markle, got engaged to Prince Harry. The show’s seventh season serves as an extended farewell to Markle’s character, Rachel, a hard-working and principled paralegal who over the course of the series finished law school and passed the bar. These are also the last episodes starring Patrick J. Adams as Rachel’s husband, Mike, a brilliant law school dropout who gave “Suits” its initial premise: What if a high-powered New York law firm hired an impostor?
—

‘Final Space’ Season 1
Starts streaming: July 20

The spirit of “Futurama” and “Rick & Morty” are very much alive in “Final Space,” an animated science-fiction spoof that combines lowbrow humor, slapstick violence and some genuinely imaginative whiz-bang adventure. Olan Rogers created the series and also provides the voice of a dimwitted astronaut named Gary, whose long incarceration on a drifting, otherwise unmanned prison ship takes an unexpected turn when he meets an adorable-but-dangerous alien named Mooncake. Soon Gary finds himself caught in the crossfire of multiple intergalactic conflicts, in a story that gets more complex — and bloody — as it plays out.
—

Also of interest: “From Dusk Till Dawn” Season 3 (July 2) and “Good Girls” Season 1 (July 3).

New Netflix Original TV Series

‘Comedians in Cars Getting Coffee’ Season 10
Starts streaming: July 6

This amiably low-key chat show from Jerry Seinfeld moves from Crackle to Netflix, but the format remains the same: Seinfeld picks up a fellow comic or actor in a cool vintage car, they drive around and talk about showbiz for a bit, and then they settle in at a funky cafe to go a little more in-depth about the art and craft of entertainment. This season’s lineup is typically star-studded, as Seinfeld grabs cups of joe with Dave Chappelle, Ellen DeGeneres, Kate McKinnon and Jerry Lewis, among others.
—

‘Somebody Feed Phil: The Second Course’
Starts streaming: July 6

The writer and producer Philip Rosenthal returns for a second season of this surprisingly delightful spin on the food-and-travel show. Affable and curious, Rosenthal and his companions make friends around the world as they sample the cuisines of other cultures. The latest helping of “Somebody Feed Phil” will include trips to Buenos Aires, Capetown, Copenhagen, Dublin, Venice and New York — and plenty of shots of the host looking deliriously happy as he tries something new.
—

‘Last Chance U: INDY,’ Part 1
Starts streaming: July 20

After two seasons of following the triumphs and travails of the football players, coaches and tutors at East Mississippi Community College, the docu-series “Last Chance U” shifts its attention to a new school, in Independence, Kan. Once again, the show will follow a group of talented athletes trying to bounce back from the academic, discipline and injury problems that derailed their Division I dreams. But unlike the perennially powerful East Mississippi, Independence Community College is in the middle of a rebuilding process when this season begins.
—

‘Orange Is the New Black’ Season 6
Starts streaming: July 27

Critics and fans had a mixed reaction to this popular prison drama’s more experimental fifth season, which took place over the course of just a few days, during a prolonged riot-protest at Litchfield Penitentiary. This year’s run of episodes will track the aftermath as many of the incarcerated women are moved to a maximum security prison. “Orange Is the New Black” started out as the darkly comic story of what happens when a privileged young white woman is forced into a community dominated by the marginalized. But the wackier elements have been getting pared away as the show scrutinizes the systemic problems that hamstring America’s underclass.
—

Also of interest: “Anne With an E’ Season 2 (July 6), “Sacred Games” (July 6), “Samantha!” (July 6), “Sugar Rush” (July 13), “Bonusfamiljen” Season 2 (July 15), “The Joel McHale Show With Joel McHale” Part 2 (July 15), “Roman Empire: Reign of Blood” Season 2 (July 27) and “Terrace House: Opening New Doors” Part 3 (July 31).

New Netflix Original Comedy Specials

‘The Comedy Lineup’
Starts streaming: July 3

Netflix’s latest foray into stand-up comedy recalls the early days of Comedy Central, when young comics appeared on the channel throughout the day, performing in short segments on shows like “Stand-Up Stand-Up” and “Comedy Central Presents.” “The Comedy Lineup” brings more than a dozen emerging voices (including Michelle Buteau, Janelle James, Jak Knight, Matteo Lane and Phil Wang) to a small stage in Atlanta for 15-minute mini-specials, assembled into an anthology series.
—

Also of interest: “Jim Jefferies: This Is Me Now” (July 13) and “Iliza Shlesinger: Elder Millenial” (July 24).

Movies New to Netflix

‘Superbad’
Starts streaming: July 1

Some of this era’s funniest actors — including Jonah Hill, Michael Cera and Emma Stone — had their first real big-screen success with this 2007 comedy, about high-school nerds who devise a plan to elevate their status by buying booze for their more popular female classmates. Written by Seth Rogen and Evan Goldberg (who themselves went on to write hits like “Pineapple Express” and “Sausage Party”), “Superbad” is a loving throwback to ’70 and ’80s teen flicks about foul-mouthed youngsters having epic adventures while trying to satisfy their basest urges.
—

‘Sing Street’
Starts streaming: July 9

With “Sing Street” (2016), the writer-director John Carney completed the (unofficial) rock musical trilogy that began with the 2007 indie hit “Once” and continued with the underrated 2013 film “Begin Again” The most autobiographical of the three, “Sing Street” takes place during the early ’80s, in the dysfunctional household of a teenage boy who reacts to his parents’ economic and marital woes by forming a band with his new schoolmates. The kids’ music is infectiously upbeat, replicating the bounce and hooks of bands like the Cure and Duran Duran. But the story is less about how rock ’n’ roll can turn rags into riches than it is about how dedication to creative expression creates community and purpose.
—

‘The Shining’
Starts streaming: July 15

Stanley Kubrick’s adaptation of the Stephen King novel “The Shining” baffled a lot of critics and horror fans when it was first released in the summer of 1980. Kubrick devotees couldn’t figure out why he was applying his immense artistic talents to a tawdry pulp story about a psychic boy who watches his father (played by Jack Nicholson) go mad while tending an off-season resort hotel. King fans bristled at the deliberate pace and wooden acting — especially by Shelley Duvall, who played the caretaker’s mousy wife. But Kubrick’s carefully crafted nightmare-imagery and King’s fevered depiction of intense family discord have become staples of popular culture in the decades since, referenced regularly by other movies and TV series.
—

‘The Rocky Horror Picture Show’
Starts streaming: July 21

The 1975 movie “The Rocky Horror Picture Show” became a phenomenon thanks to midnight screenings, where audiences dressed up and shouted scripted insults at the screen. But honestly, this transgressive, tuneful musical might be even more fun to watch alone. A campy concoction of Old Hollywood horror movies, proto-punk fashion and drive-in smut, this film contains something wild in nearly every frame. And while the intentionally outsized style doesn’t showcase their subtlest acting skills, the stars Tim Curry (as a cross-dressing mad scientist) and Susan Sarandon (as a lost waif) are as charismatic here as they have been since.
—

Also of interest: “The Day After Tomorrow” (July 1), “The Fast and the Furious” (July 1), “Legends of the Fall” (July 1), “Once Upon a Time in Mexico” (July 1), “Pineapple Express” (July 1), “Practical Magic” (July 1), “This Is the End” (July 1), “Tammy” (July 5), “Geostorm” (July 22) and “Bad Moms” (July 23).

New Netflix Original Movies

‘The Legacy of the Whitetail Deer Hunter’
Starts streaming: July 6

The writer-director Jody Hill and the writer-actor Danny McBride have previously collaborated on the shaggy, hilarious film “The Foot Fist Way” and the raunchy HBO comedies “Eastbound and Down” and “Vice Principals.” Written with John Carcieri, their new movie, “The Legacy of a Whitetail Deer Hunter,” stars Josh Brolin as a celebrity outdoorsman named Buck Ferguson, who enlists his favorite cameraman (played by McBride) to film what Buck expects to be a moving weekend of bonding and shooting with his teenage son. Instead, the trip takes several disastrous turns, giving Hill and McBride plenty of chances to engage with their favorite theme: the ridiculousness of hyper-macho men.
—

‘White Fang’
Starts streaming: July 7

Jack London’s beloved 1906 novel “White Fang” has been adapted to the screen multiple times, but never quite as it has in this new animated version, directed by Alexandre Espigares (who won an Oscar for best animated short film a few years ago for helping direct “Mr. Hublot”), and featuring the voices of Nick Offerman, Rashida Jones and Paul Giamatti. Adopting a style that resembles a painting in motion, Espigares recreates the classically vintage feel of the book, while also covering much of its episodic story, about a strong Canadian pack-dog who gets passed from owner to owner.
—

‘How It Ends’
Starts streaming: July 13

Theo James stars in this apocalyptic thriller as a proud husband and father, who hustles to make it home when a sudden global cataclysm makes it seem that the end of the world is nigh. Forest Whitaker plays the hero’s disapproving father-in-law, who joins him on his journey and shares his struggle to navigate the chaos and danger around them. Judging by the trailer, “How It Ends” looks like an intense (and expensive) genre hybrid, combining elements of science-fiction, action-adventure and mismatched-buddy pictures.
—

‘Extinction’
Starts streaming: July 27

After Universal unexpectedly dropped this character-driven science-fiction drama from its 2018 release schedule, Netflix swooped in to save it, which ought to be good news for fans of low-key genre pieces populated by accomplished character actors. Michael Peña gets a rare leading role, playing a man who worries that he’s letting his family down — and then has to face his worst fears when invading aliens threaten his wife and kids. Lizzy Caplan also stars, in a gritty fantasy that promises to be more about emotions than action.
—

Also of interest: “Father of the Year” (July 20) and “The Bleeding Edge” (July 27).
