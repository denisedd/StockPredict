    Why You Should Never Skip the Opening Credits

    By JAMES PONIEWOZIK JULY 23, 2018

    An ongoing series devoted to the little things (a soundtrack, a sculpture, a scene, a bit, a title sequence, a music video, a line) we love.

    Why I Love The opening of “The Good Fight.”

    By James Poniewozik

    I’ve become an absolutist about watching TV title sequences. I think my radicalizing moment came when Netflix introduced the “Skip Intro” button in 2017.

    No. We do not skip the intro in this house. (Yes, even the “Homeland” credits. Sorry, that’s what you signed up for.)

    A title sequence is not a disposable wrapper. It is part of the program. It is, at best, the show’s homunculus, the creation in miniature. It sets a mood. It establishes themes. It brings you into the world of the series, atmospherically, like the ethereal trance of “Twin Peaks,”

    or geographically, like the god’s-eye flyover of “Game of Thrones.”

    One of my favorite shows of 2018 was “The Good Fight,” and it also has one of TV’s best title sequences, produced by Barnstorm VFX. It looks like this:

    “The Good Fight” airs (or “airs”) on CBS All Access, a streaming service you probably do not subscribe to. So you may have never seen “The Good Fight.” This is fine. Watching the credits is a good, quick way to get to know it.

    Quick nutshell: “The Good Fight” is a spinoff of “The Good Wife,” a legal drama that aired for seven seasons on CBS. Its protagonist is Diane Lockhart (Christine Baranski), an elite white Chicago lawyer who has become a partner in a largely African-American firm, whose clients have ranged from police-brutality victims to billionaires.

    So it’s a lawyer show. Here are some lawyer things:

    A tasteful vase.

    A desktop Newton’s Cradle.

    A set of bound volumes.

    A nice bottle of red wine. (“The Good Fight” is one of those shows, like “Scandal” or “Game of Thrones,” in which characters drink a lot of wine from fine stemware.)

    No! Not the wine!

    “The Good Fight,” like “The Good Wife” before it, is a show about power and privilege. But it’s also a show about disruption, collapse and chaos. In the premiere, Diane is getting ready to retire (to a vineyard, of course) when she discovers she’s lost her life savings to an investment scam. She has to find a new job.

    Everything …

    is …

    coming apart.

    I worry that I’m making the show sound melodramatic. It’s not. It’s a comeback story, about power, ethics and life’s absurd twists.

    “Absurd” is key here. “The Good Fight” is also dryly, surreally funny, which is another thing the credits tell you.

    Let’s rewind and listen for a second. Here’s the theme music, composed by David Buckley.

    It starts in stately mode. A little baroque harpsichord. Something you might listen to in a fancy waiting room or while nursing a fine Burgundy.

    As things start exploding, so does the music, with percussion and operatic vocals.

    By the end, the whole thing goes insane, apocalyptic. The vocals become war cries and shrieks. It’s hyperbolic, even — and I mean this as a compliment — ridiculous.

    Because it is all a little ridiculous! The cases on “The Good Fight” are serious, even of national interest (more on that in a second). But while the firm’s clients may be vulnerable, the firm that Diane joins is prosperous. The lawyers will be fine. (Well, except those taken out by a serial killer, but you’ll learn about that if you watch Season 2.)

    There’s an irony to this, which the show is deftly aware of. And so are the credits, which become a kind of mesmerizing, over-the-top video art of luxury destruction. A decanter of Scotch gets it …

    the vase gets it …

    even a row of designer handbags, detonating like a synchronized fireworks display.

    It’s kind of beautiful, really. The sequence announces the show as playful, willing to surprise you.

    (It does this, for instance, with the placement of the credits, which sometimes run at the beginning of an episode, sometimes 15 minutes or more in.)

    “The Good Fight,” it says, is a show that will change things up.

    Which brings us to this:

    The first season of “The Good Fight” went into production before the 2016 election and began just after President Trump’s inauguration. Diane, a liberal active in Democratic politics, became an inadvertent stand-in for a class of people arriving in a very different 2017 than they expected, and starting over.

    The second season addressed the Trump era directly. The episode titles counted the days of the administration (“Day 408,” “Day 415,” and so on).

    The characters took immigration cases, argued before a wet-behind-the-ears judge appointed by Trump and worked with the Democratic National Committee on a potential impeachment case.

    The credits changed with the themes, adding a TV image of Charlottesville to represent the serious, ugly, violent passions set loose:

    But here, too, there’s the ridiculous, the idea that history has arrived in the form of a late-night comedy sketch. All season, Diane’s high-rise window faced the apartment of a couple who liked to get it on wearing Trump masks.

    One storyline found the firm consulting with the D.N.C. about a certain much-rumored samizdat presidential video, which the show represented by having it play, unseen, on a laptop that cast a golden glow, like the briefcase in “Pulp Fiction”:

    It was a season about national crisis in the form of a killer joke, about living through a drama whose supervillains include a Russian leader who likes to be photographed fishing shirtless:

    The title sequence ends with everything going up operatically in smoke: furniture, finery, law books and the norms they represent:

    This is why you don’t skip the credits. A good sequence like the one in “The Good Fight” is a poem, a map, an invitation to join its storytellers as they blow up their world, real good.

    James Poniewozik is the chief television critic for The New York Times.
