    From Harbor to Peak, Hong Kong Bustles With Opportunity at Every Altitude

    Join us as we scale Hong Kong from sea level to its highest point. From business to lifestyle, discover a city throbbing with energy and pulsing with potential on all fronts. 

    VICTORIA HARBOR

    The significance of the harbor cannot be overstated in Hong Kong’s history. After all, Hong Kong means “fragrant harbor” in Cantonese and was the original title of Victoria Harbor before its renaming in 1841. “It’s a very deep natural harbor, well located, protected from extreme typhoons and a great place for shipping goods,” says Professor John Carroll, a historian at the University of Hong Kong. Picture a lively channel of junk boats ferrying everything from incense to tea — the lifeblood of the city’s economic development in the 19th century.

    Although Hong Kong still boasts one of the world’s busiest ports, it’s a much different scene today. The waterfront is in the throes of transformation: The promenades on both sides have been overhauled and beautified with such projects as Tamar Park, while the West Kowloon Cultural District and a new business district in Kowloon East promise to inject the area with renewed cultural and economic vitality. With great views, attractions and places to relax, the harbor has never been more accessible and enjoyable for locals and tourists alike.

    “Start-ups are helped by Hong Kong’s diversity and frenetic frontier spirit. They can leverage the city’s proximity to the giant, fast-growing Chinese market and Shenzhen, a hotbed of technology and manufacturing.”
    Antoine Blondeau, chairman of Zegal

    Cyberport Smart-Space, Telegraph Bay

    Among the communities of innovators found on the western flank of the island at the Cyberport Smart-Space is a legal tech start-up called Zegal, run by its co-founder Daniel Walker. The firm's chairman, Antoine Blondeau, also founded the AI start-up Sentient Technologies in San Francisco. “The start-up scene in Hong Kong displays a few of the characteristics of Silicon Valley, such as the appetite for risk, the cosmopolitan nature of teams and the availability of financing,” says Blondeau.

    Zegal is a digital platform that empowers companies to do routine legal work on their own, without the need to retain a law firm, while matching them up with professional lawyers for more complex needs. “We are offering legal solutions at scale to 25,000 businesses around the world,” explains Walker, himself a former corporate lawyer who first moved to Hong Kong in 2006. Setting up his business in Hong Kong was a no-brainer. “Hong Kong has one of the very few legal systems in the world that are very transparent and very trustworthy,” he notes. “It also has a very progressive outlook from law firms and regulators.”

    Jay Kim, a San Francisco-born investor who hosts a podcast series dedicated to start-up culture, adds that an ecosystem of accelerators, low taxes and a supportive network of venture capitalists set the stage for talented entrepreneurs to succeed. “It is a lot easier for a young person to make an impact in Hong Kong’s start-up scene than in Silicon Valley,” he says, “where it’s more of an old-boy network.”

    “We are able to make our own mark here in the city.”
    Jocelyn Liipfert-Lam, founder of Liipstiik

    Mid-Levels

    In Hong Kong, there’s no livelier and more vibrant area than the stretch from Central to the Mid-Levels to Sheung Wan, home to scores of galleries, cafes and independent businesses. Many in the creative scene, like the founder of boutique branding agency Liipstiik, Jocelyn Liipfert-Lam, can be found seeking inspiration here.

    TAI KWUN

    Formerly the Central Police Station compound, the sprawling colonial complex perched along the slopes of the Mid-Levels has found new life as an arts and heritage center. Since its May opening in 2018, this cultural landmark has been hosting live performances and art exhibitions. Along with the much-loved PMQ design center just down the street, this successful preservation of Hong Kong’s built heritage is one of the twin bastions of the city’s design and culture scenes, respectively.

    HONG KONG–ZHUHAI–MACAO BRIDGE

    If you’ve flown into Hong Kong International Airport recently, chances are you’ve glimpsed this 26-mile bridge cutting across the Pearl River Delta. The Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge comprises an eye-catching cable-stayed bridge with a tower height of 535 feet, a sub-sea tunnel and boundary crossing facilities. The entire project is 15 times the length of San Francisco’s Golden Gate Bridge and is designed to withstand earthquakes, typhoons and even a collision with a 10,000 ton vessel. The game-changing project cements Hong Kong’s position as a trade and logistics hub by slashing commute times between the city and Zhuhai on the Mainland, while creating new development and tourism opportunities in the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Bay Area.

    International Finance Centre

    In difficult times, Hong Kong is the kind of city that digs in its heels. When the IFC opened in 1998 in the Central district, it was during the wake of the Asian financial crisis, and the skyscraper designed by the Argentine architect Cesar Pelli became the physical embodiment of Hong Kong’s economic resilience and commitment to free trade. Today, it houses the Hong Kong Monetary Authority, along with the financial heavyweight UBS. The base of the IFC is also a shopping haven, with the four-story luxury mall where Apple opened its first flagship store in the city.

    Linked to each other and to the airport by high-speed rail, Hong Kong’s tallest and second-tallest skyscrapers symbolize the city’s lofty ambitions in finance and commerce.

    International Commerce Centre

    The ICC anchors the business and cultural district on the Kowloon side of the harbor. Just two minutes away from the IFC via the Hong Kong Airport Express, the city’s tallest building reaches nearly 1,600 feet into the sky — an expression of the city’s financial prospects. Deutsche Bank, Credit Suisse and Morgan Stanley have their offices here, while The Ritz-Carlton sets the benchmark for luxury hospitality from floors 102 to 118. Like the IFC, the first few floors feature the luxury retail complex, Elements.

    “The time is ripe in Hong Kong where anyone can make a big impact. It’s a supportive community with lots of co-working spaces, venture capitalists and accelerators coming in now.”
    Jay Kim, investor

    Tai Mo Shan

    “When most people think about Hong Kong they think it’s a concrete jungle, without realizing that there’s a lot of nature,” says Jay Kim, who has written a fitness book called “Hack Your Fitness.” From Hong Kong’s highest peak, Tai Mo Shan, it’s impossible to escape this fact. “There are plenty of hiking trails and beaches in Hong Kong to enjoy,” the investor continues. “I was living in the New Territories where there was a lot of greenery, which reminded me of home in San Francisco.”

    Besides local flora and fauna, the vantage point on Tai Mo Shan also reveals at a glance how well connected Hong Kong is and what advantages this connectivity confers. To the north, Shenzhen is within view, while the airport in the west is visible on a clear day. This is a globally oriented city with easy access to Mainland China, making it a natural base from which to start a business. As Kim sums up: “I’m always optimistic about Hong Kong.”
