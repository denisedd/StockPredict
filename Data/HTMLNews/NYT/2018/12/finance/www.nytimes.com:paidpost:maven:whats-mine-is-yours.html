    What’s Mine Is Yours
    Profiles From the Sharing Economy

    The sharing economy touches so many aspects of our lives today, it can be difficult to remember a time before its existence, back before it was normal to pay to share someone’s house in a different city, for instance, or ride communal bikes. With urban living on the rise over the past decade and smartphone use skyrocketing, group sharing is more popular than ever. According to eMarketer, 44.8 million U.S. adults used sharing-economy services in 2016, and that number is predicted to increase to 86.5 million by 2021. These services and apps — which connect owners, who have stuff to share, with customers willing to pay to experience those things — are altering the way we travel, work, shop, and view the world.

    Here are three people who embraced the sharing economy in their cities and discovered a whole new way of life with more freedom, less hassle and greater options than ever.

    Neville Mehra,
    an Entrepreneur With Wanderlust

    Neville Mehra, an Entrepreneur With Wanderlust

     In October 2012, Neville A. Mehra, who was his own boss at a technology consulting startup, realized he was already juggling clients in three different cities without a problem. “So I thought: ‘Wait a minute. Why don’t I just pursue my travel dreams and become fully nomadic?’” he recalls.

    He put his Maryland home up for rent and began traveling full-time. “I wasn’t fabulously wealthy or anything,” Mehra says with a laugh. “I just made it work by using sharing services.”

    In the last six years, Mehra has visited or lived in more than 120 cities, from Barcelona to Buenos Aires to Bangkok. He moves regularly, staying at each place anywhere from a couple of days to a few months. “The sharing economy has been transformative for me,” he says, “because it has enabled me to live my digital nomad lifestyle.”

    The entrepreneur takes advantage of every kind of group-share service imaginable: home sharing, bike sharing, car sharing and more. Co-working spaces with reliable Wi-Fi have been particularly vital to him and his business. “I’m only as good as I am connected,” Mehra says. “And it’s nice to interact with other people who are out there hustling and working remotely like me.”

    Mehra recently got married in Portugal, and now the couple is back in the Washington, D.C., area for a short stint. But they plan to start traveling again as soon as they can. “Sharing other people’s homes, cars and workspaces around the world has given me the ability to basically live like a local in so many of the places I’ve visited,” Mehra says. “That makes a big difference.”

    Jan Lower,
    a Dad With a Car
    to Share

    Jan Lower,
    a Dad With a Car
    to Share

     Finance executive Jan Lower  had a problem after his daughter Julia went away to college: The Chevrolet Equinox she used during high school was parked in the driveway of his Metro Detroit home, with 9,000 miles left on its lease. After reading an article about Maven, which enables owners in select cities to share their cars and trucks with others via short-term rentals, he decided to join. “I thought, ‘I’m just going to try this out,’” says Lower, 59. “It was a leap of faith.”

    He was happy at how smoothly it all worked. “I’ve been renting out this car almost every weekend,” Lower says. “It’s a wonderful program.” Maven takes care of everything from installing keyless entry technology to vetting drivers to a $1 million liability coverage during reservations. Lower only has to remember to keep the car clean, set the pricing and update the availability calendar. “It’s a truly turn-key system,” he says.

    Sharing the car helps him offset the costs of keeping it while Julia is away at college, Lower says. Another perk? He can tap into the Maven network and find an affordable rental for himself. “Every year I get mulch for my garden,” he says. “So now I can use Maven to get a pickup truck for a day.” And the best part is that whenever Julia comes home from college for holidays and internships, she still has a car to use.

    Now that he uses Maven, Lower has upgraded his plans for the family’s next vehicle. “One day I thought: ‘Aha! Maybe I'll get a sporty Camaro or a Cadillac ATS to share with everyone,’” he says. “Car-sharing makes you think about things differently.”

    Rebecca Maben,
    a Fashion Enthusiast With a Limited Budget

    Rebecca Maben,
    a Fashion Enthusiast
    With a Limited Budget

     Rebecca Maben was planning a big trip to Europe to attend a wedding, but she didn’t like what she saw in her New York City closet. “It was just a sea of black clothing,” says Maben, 28.

    A sports merchandise analyst for a department store chain, Maben typically would go to a fast-fashion store and buy an affordable outfit that she would wear only a few times. This time, however, she took a different approach. Inspired by a co-worker who had recently signed up for Rent the Runway, a sharing service that provides designer clothes for a fee, Maben decided to give it a try.

    “I was able to experiment with clothing that’s more fashion forward, made by designers I normally couldn’t afford,” Maben says. “It made me step outside of my comfort zone in a good way, and I had fabulous clothes for my trip.”

    ___

    ‘It opens your mind to new possibilities, new ideas and new experiences.’
    ___

    Maben didn’t cancel her membership when she returned home. She continues to use it to elevate her wardrobe, borrowing four pieces at a time and switching them out whenever she’s ready for a new look.

    Besides saving money, sharing clothes has paid off in other ways. “It sounds corny, but you know the quote, ‘Dress for the job you want, not the job you have?’” Maben says. “I am wearing things that are more executive level.”

    About four months after starting with Rent the Runway, Maben got a promotion. She won’t say that her new role is a result of her new wardrobe, but she does feel that the clothes boosted her self-confidence. “When I get dressed in the morning and I look at what I’m currently wearing, it makes me really feel like, ‘Oh, yeah. I’ve got this.’”

    This is not the only sharing service Maben uses: She taps into home-rental sites when she’s on vacation and ridesharing apps when she needs to get around New York. The sharing economy has given Maben access to a world of opportunity that previously seemed unattainable. “It opens your mind to new possibilities, new ideas and new experiences,” she says. “It’s changed my entire lifestyle.”
