    Cities at the Forefrontof Tackling Climate Change

    The world needs to act fast to change the trajectory of temperature increase. Cities committed to GCoM, the largest global alliance of cities and mayors tackling climate change, are already leading this action.

    At the 24th United Nations Convention on Climate Change in Katowice, Poland, in December, a consultation of scientists, city practitioners and NGOs responded to the IPCC 1.5 Special Report with a stark question: What does it mean for cities to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius in just over a decade? The 9,176 cities committed to the Global Covenant of Mayors for Climate & Energy are on the vanguard of efforts to mitigate and reverse the effects of climate change — and, through joint efforts like those of the three cities below, are already acting as key drivers of this vital mission.

    Using Data, Not Energy

    Seoul, South Korea

    For Seoul, one of the most technologically advanced and connected cities in the world, sustainability goals are interlinked with a “smart” strategy and open data. “We believe open data helps citizens have a better understanding about Seoul’s policies and motivates them to join the city government’s efforts,” says Park Won-soon, the city’s mayor.

    Under Park, the Seoul government launched the Open Government 2.0 policy in 2012. Under this initiative, all data, except for confidential or personal data, is shared freely and openly on one of the city’s digital platforms. By accessing two websites, Seoul Information Communication Plaza and Seoul Open Data Plaza, the city’s residents can get real-time air quality information and data. Seoul’s Eco-Mileage Program also lets them monitor their energy use and savings, and provides incentives based on the amount of energy saved during a certain period of time.

    Energy has been a core focus of Seoul’s sustainability efforts. Prompted by the idea that nuclear power’s demands on the environment ultimately make it unsustainable, the One Less Nuclear Power Plant initiative aims to save and produce renewable energy amounting to the energy produced by one nuclear power plant. “Over the past six years, through various programs, the amount of energy saving and renewable energy production reached 4.65 million tons of oil, which is almost the same as the energy produced by two nuclear power plants,” says Park.

    The Blue City Goes Green

    Chefchaouen, Morocco

    Chefchaouen, a picturesque town in northern Morocco, is a beloved tourist spot famous for its striking blue streets. But in recent years, this hillside community has been leaning green, taking major strides toward becoming a model for sustainable development in the region. “In Chefchaouen, civil society plays a central role in driving the city’s leadership and engagement in becoming greener, more sustainable and inclusive,” says Mayor Mohamed Sefiani.

    One such landmark project is its Energy Information Center, a part of the E.U.-funded Sustainable Urban Demonstration Project. Opened four years ago to raise awareness of saving energy among the public, it is the first initiative in Morocco that targets both businesses and individuals to provide information about energy savings. “The center aims to assist urban policy makers in the Southern Mediterranean region to address local sustainable energy challenges,” Sefiani says.

    Chefchaouen has also played an important role in Invest4Cities, GCoM’s initiative that invites investors and donors to fund the development of climate action plans. The aim is to raise up to $200 million for technical assistance for 400 cities, mainly in the Global South. This would also include $600 million for credit enhancement financing for projects related to public transit, e-mobility, bike sharing, energy-efficient buildings, clean energy, waste management and flood walls. “We’re proud of the progress we’ve made in my own city, Chefchaouen, to adapt to and mitigate the impacts of climate change, but there is always more to be done. Accelerating cities’ access, particularly for those in the Global South, to funding for sustainable infrastructure developments is critical as we work together toward the shared goals of the Paris Agreement” says Sefiani.

    Innovative Action on Climate

    Edmonton, Canada

    With its inclusive and innovative spirit, Edmonton, the capital of Alberta, is one of the country’s most determined advocates for tackling climate change. “Edmonton was one of the founders of the Partners for Climate Protection Initiative,” says Don Iveson, the city’s mayor. “We’ve been talking about climate change since long before it was taken seriously at a subfederal or federal level.”

    This year, the city convened a summit of international mayors, which issued the Edmonton Declaration, a bold call for governments at all levels to implement scientifically robust policies to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees C. An evidence-based approach is at the core of the city’s efforts to fight climate change. “Edmonton is a university town, with great fundamental research,” Iveson says. “The nation’s largest polytechnic is here. It’s a health-care and life-sciences innovation hub for western and northern Canada.”

    In line with the city-level efforts, Edmonton is home to a wealth of start-ups addressing sustainability through technology and innovation. One recent example is Landmark Homes, a builder that aims to use automation and building science to create “net zero” (i.e., zero carbon emissions generating) houses at the same price as conventional ones. “The owner, Reza Nasseri, is shifting net-zero from being a boutique, high-end thing to being ready for mass adoption, at no price penalty,” Iveson explains.

    He stresses that cities in particular need to play a strong leadership role in instilling urgency in the climate change issue. “Cities play a really critical role in that we’re able to put a climate lens on decision-making,” he says.

    What can people and cities do today?

    5

    things you can do as a city dweller to help reduce global warming:

    • Unplug your TV before you leave the house.
    • Install a smart thermostat.
    • Switch to more efficient light bulbs.
    • Waste less food and eat less meat.
    • Ask your mayor to join the Global Covenant of Mayors.
    5

    actions cities can take to limit the effects of climate change:

    • Measure your climate impact.
    • Set targets to reduce your greenhouse gas emissions and adapt to climate change effects.
    • Develop a plan to meet those targets.
    • Take action and engage your citizens.
    • Track and share your progress through the Global Covenant of Mayors.
