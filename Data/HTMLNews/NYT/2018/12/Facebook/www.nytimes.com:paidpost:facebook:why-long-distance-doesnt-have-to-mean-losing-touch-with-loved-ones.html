    Why Long Distance Doesn't Have to Mean Losing Touch With Loved Ones

    Staying Connected Just Got Easier

    Why Long Distance Doesn't Have to Mean Losing Touch With Loved Ones

    Staying Connected Just Got Easier
    ⌃

    Rowen Merrill has never known life without the sounds of a musical instrument nearby.

    “I don’t know how I got into music; it just happened,” says Merrill, 23, an aspiring professional musician from Altadena, Calif. “Growing up, music has always been around — my mom playing piano all the time. I was very much influenced by my parents.”

    Merrill practiced the violin throughout her childhood and began writing songs at 13 with the help of her father, Jonathan, a conductor, producer and composer. A decade later, they’re collaborating professionally. But getting there wasn’t easy.

    When Merrill moved to Nashville to study violin performance at college, she and her father were no longer able to just pick up instruments and play together whenever they wanted. Nor were little things like walking the dogs or discovering new coffee shops possible.

    “I had spent so much time with her as she grew up,” Jonathan Merrill says. “It was a big change.”

    What they missed from those moments are the building blocks that satisfy a hard-wired need within us all: connecting with those we care about. It’s part of what makes us human, and it is essential for our well-being, says Darby Saxbe, a psychologist at the University of Southern California.

    “Without human connection we would not survive,” Saxbe says. “The desire to feel connected is really built into our brains and bodies.”

    Connection does much more than just improve our social lives. It has tangible effects on a person’s overall well-being too, says Moira Burke, a research scientist at Facebook who studies how people communicate.

    “Interacting with people can be as good for your health as quitting smoking,” Burke says, referencing research popularized by political scientist Robert Putnam.

    But what happens when pursuits separate us physically from our relationships? How can Rowen Merrill stay connected with her family from 2,000 miles away while also pursuing her dream? The answer may lie with a new technology and its ability to expand the boundaries of human connection.

    Together, Even
    When Apart

    When battling distance and hectic schedules, many of us look to quality time — devoting a block of time to a shared activity — as the gold standard for connection. But experts view things differently. Connections form via “quality moments” or the everyday things we do together, according to Tamar Kremer-Sadlik, an anthropologist at the University of California, Los Angeles.

    For some, it might mean debating the latest episode of a TV show or sharing a meal. For Merrill and her father, it’s often talking about and listening to music.

    “She helps me stay current,” Jonathan Merrill says. “We listen to lots of different kinds of music together: pop, rock, country, jazz, world music.”

    The thread running through these interactions, and the force that helps us form bonds, is feeling present with someone, a phenomenon that may happen less often than we realize. In fact, Americans spend just 37 minutes a day, on average, with loved ones, a recent study by the research company 72Point found.

    Demanding schedules are partly to blame, Kremer-Sadlik says. When we feel strapped for time, we’re less likely to devote it to others. “People basically feel hurried all the time,” she says.

    While there’s no substitute for face-to-face interaction, research shows that video calling can be a powerful tool for fostering presence. The feeling of presence is the sense of being physically there with someone — seeing facial expressions and gestures, hearing a tone of voice. Morgan Ames, a postdoctoral scholar at the University of California, Berkeley, School of Information, points out that mediums like video calling are actually changing the way psychologists think about connection. Previously, experts held that frequent in-person interactions were essential to building relationships, but now it’s possible to connect with people even when they’re far away, she says.

    “Digital technologies can help us fulfill our desires for connecting with loved ones in many of the same ways face-to-face meetings can,” she says. “It is still not the same as being there in person, but it is a close second.”

    A New Path
    Toward Presence

    In Nashville, Merrill was loving her violin program and her new friends. But she still felt the need to call her parents almost daily.

    “I definitely missed my parents,” she says. They also texted and video chatted, but still “there were times that I really wanted to come home,” she adds.

    Noticing trends in communication, but recognizing the limitations of existing technologies, Facebook convened a team to create new ways for people to spend quality time together, even when they're physically apart. Years later, the result is Portal.

    Building Portal meant moving beyond existing platforms and creating something totally new.

    “There’s only so much you can do behind a screen if that screen is a laptop or a tablet or a cell phone,” says Jason Harrison, the engineering manager for the team.

    With Harrison and his colleagues working hard on their mission, elsewhere at Facebook AI researchers were making breakthroughs in camera motion detection. The Portal team leveraged these advances to make a big leap forward in how we communicate.

    AI powers some of the key features, like its Smart Camera and microphone, which help Portal make it feel like you’re right there with friends. But that solved only part of the problem. AI alone couldn’t recreate the smooth movements between an overall scene and focused attention that a camera operator masters only with years of practice. So the Portal team worked with cinematographers to refine the way Portal captures movement, by incorporating traditional camera techniques, such as panning left to right or zooming in and out.

    “We really took the approach of marrying the art and the science,” Harrison says.

    The Merrills have started using Portal, and it has redefined how they keep in touch. Its microphone and camera naturally pick up the voice and movement of the person speaking, which makes it feel more like you're having a conversation in person, Jonathan Merrill says. Just like those days of living in a house together filled with music, Portal allows the Merrills to feel like they’re in the same place. That means getting more of the everyday moments through which the family bonded in the first place.

    “You can see everyone is moving around and doing their thing,” he says. “It’s more relaxed and it’s more real. Right away, I felt more connected.”
