    Bridging the Digital Divide

    How one innovative program is closing the STEM gap and driving real improvements in academic performance.

    At the Verizon Innovative Learning lab, students can use virtual reality headsets to explore self-made animations.

    THE VERIZON
    INNOVATIVE
    LEARNING    lab at Franklin Delano Roosevelt Middle School in Bristol, Penn., is filled with students joyfully exploring technology: A child with a virtual reality headset navigates a digital world that also flashes on a flat-panel monitor behind him. Three separate 3-D printers buzz as they deposit layer after layer of filament to create colorful figurines. A group of girls discuss how to set up a laser cutter to engrave cursive letters in wood at the optimal depth. Small spherical robots programmed by mischievous students roll about, bumping into shoes and intermittently blasting musical notes.

    “Four years ago, we didn’t even have Wi-Fi in this building,” says Bernadette Hannah-Barone, a former FDR student turned instructional coach who has worked at this school district, just outside Philadelphia, for 23 years. She looks around the lab with pride. “This room was basically a storage locker.”

    Before 2014, the Bristol school district fell on the less fortunate side of the “digital divide,” a term reflecting the disparity between communities with and without adequate access to technology and the internet. As science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) resources became almost ubiquitous in other districts, FDR Middle School was left behind — like so many schools in low-income or rural neighborhoods around the country. “Our students couldn’t compete,” Hannah-Barone says.

    When children don’t have connected devices at home, it can create a “homework gap,” because they are at a disadvantage when it comes to researching topics they’re studying. In the U.S. today, nearly half of adults with household incomes below $30,000 a year don’t have broadband services or a traditional computer. And 24 percent of rural adults say access to high-speed internet is a major problem in their communities, a Pew Research Center survey conducted earlier this year found. This means many students nationwide are disconnected.

    “If young people don’t get an equal chance to experience sophisticated technologies early on, it impacts not only their career success but also civic engagement,” says Mark Warschauer, professor of education and informatics at the University of California, Irvine. “And the consequences can ripple out to the entire country.”

    The digital divide can damage the economy. “With more than four million unfilled jobs in science and technology, this isn’t merely a crisis of education, says Rose Stuckey Kirk, Verizon’s chief corporate responsibility officer. “It’s an economic crisis.”

    Verizon, by using its technology and resources to help with this important societal issue, has committed more than $400 million toward bridging the digital divide. Since 2012, the Verizon Innovative Learning Initiative has partnered with 100 under-resourced middle schools around the nation, equipping every student and teacher with free technology and 24/7 internet access. It also provides schools with extensive teacher training, support and the opportunity to engage in a unique, immersive curriculum to leverage technology in their classrooms.

    “Verizon Innovative Learning took the opportunity to intervene with some of the most under-resourced children in America’s middle schools,” Kirk says, “in order to fundamentally change the way teachers teach and children learn.”

    In 2014, FDR Middle School became part of the Verizon Innovative Learning initiative, receiving devices and free internet access, training and hands-on education. Two years later, the school debuted the first-of-its-kind Verizon Innovation Learning lab, which today includes virtual and augmented reality systems, coding equipment, 3-D printers and a multimedia production room. “With access to this technology and training, we have transformed our approach to teaching,” Hannah-Barone says. “It’s changed everything for this school.”

    Embracing New Tools for the Future

    Embracing New Tools for the Future

    A sign on the door of the Verizon Innovative Learning lab at FDR Middle School reads: “In this space, we change the world.” Inside, an elite group of students known as Tiger Techs work on personal STEM projects and also help classmates and teachers integrate technology into their classrooms. To apply to become a Tiger Tech, students have to undergo an interview and maintain good grades. It’s competitive. Last year, 45 FDR students applied, but only 19 were accepted.

    Near the laser printer, Tiger Techs Dema Harmon and Makenzie Farmer, both 13, huddle around a monitor, discussing how best to program the laser cutter. Dema first got interested in the lab because of the multimedia production room. "My friend and I made a rap," she says. “I’m a singer. I want to make songs and videos.”

    Makenzie aspires to be a fashion designer. “I designed a dress on my tablet,” she says. “Working at this lab makes me think I could do something that combines engineering and fashion.”

    Zandra Kawan, 13, is an aspiring astronomer. “This technology is fun, and I can use it for my future,” he says, after removing a headset. “When I’m outside at night, I look up and see planets, and then when I come in here, I can see them up close in virtual reality.”

    In the Verizon Innovative Learning program, the Tiger Techs also pick up valuable leadership and communication tactics, which can help boost their self-esteem. “I learned that I have the skills for teaching,” says Emily Voshchilo, 14. “Even if I don't go further into technology, this program made me realize I have skills I can use.”

    The program also addresses the gender disparity in STEM fields. “At FDR, you have a tech squad that’s composed of a lot of girls and a few boys,” Kirk says. “You are seeing girls in leadership positions. They’re confident, and they show others that they can master this technology.”

    With more than 4 million unfilled jobs in science and technology, the digital divide isn’t merely a crisis of education, it’s an economic crisis.

    When it comes to operating new devices or apps, students aren’t the only ones who need coaching. “Teachers can be uncomfortable using technology because that’s not how they learned to teach,” says Hannah-Barone. Part of her job at FDR is to make the tech transition easier for instructors, giving them in-depth training on how to incorporate technology into their classrooms.

    “In order for our students to compete with artificial intelligence one day, they actually have to be able to think on their own, to find problems and solve them,” Hannah-Barone says. “That requires the teachers to let go of the control.”

    The Impact? Schools Are Seeing Real Results

    The Impact? Schools Are Seeing Real Results

    Schools that partner with Verizon Innovative Learning, which encompasses more than a million students, are already reporting impressive outcomes: Of students who participate in the program, 53 percent are more engaged in school. According to a 2017 study conducted by research company Westat, after two years 6th grade students who participated in Verizon Innovative Learning schools improved on their math standardized tests at rates three times of those seen by their peers who did not participate.

    The program at FDR is especially effective at reaching students who have trouble learning in more traditional classroom settings. “We’re seeing advances with our special ed population,” says Kevin Boles, the school’s principal. “When we put technology in front of these students who often work better hands-on, we’re seeing noticeable improvements.”

    Attendance rates at FDR have also increased. “When you give the students a lot more freedom,” Boles says, “they feel better about being here.”

    In fact, students are dropping by every chance they get. “Students show up during their lunch and break times. We can’t keep them away,” Hannah-Barone says with a broad smile. She motions to the kids all around the lab, thoroughly engrossed in their work. “It’s amazing.”

    Verizon Innovative Learning aims to triple the number of partner schools over the next few years and enable 5G, the next generation of cellular network data transmission, in 150 of them. 5G will provide faster speeds, reduced delays and increased system capacity, which for schools will translate into more-immersive virtual reality, greater bandwidth for big-data analysis and faster load times for apps or educational videos. 5G will also allow for connected, “always on” laptops, even outdoors or in other remote areas where internet access is typically spotty.

    With the resources provided by programs such as Verizon Innovative Learning, the digital divide will get smaller, and more students will have the skills to improve their own lives and others’. “We are all dependent upon these children for the future success of our country,” Kirk says. “Let’s give them the opportunities they deserve to become the leaders they were meant to be.”
