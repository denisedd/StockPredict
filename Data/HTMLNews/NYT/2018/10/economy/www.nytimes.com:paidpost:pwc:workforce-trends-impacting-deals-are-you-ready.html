    Workforce Trends Impacting Deals: Are You Ready?

    It is more important than ever that dealmakers have a rigorous workforce plan focused on the value of people.

    By Carrie Duarte

    Over the past few years, changes in workforce trends and practices have accelerated significantly – more so than any time in recent memory. When Henry Ford installed the first assembly line more than 100 years ago and started mass producing automobiles, he inspired a generation of US companies and transformed industries.

    Today we’re in for another dramatic shift, driven in part by three underlying forces poised to change the future of workforces and consequently how M&A deals are evaluated in the years ahead.

    First, the growth of the gig economy has given workers more flexibility to choose when and where they work, and will therefore change the relationship between workers and employers. Second, artificial intelligence and other emerging technologies will likely automate many more workplace tasks, putting jobs in question and reshaping the kinds of work and skills that humans perform. Third, as the business case supporting a more diverse workforce grows, there are similarly meaningful deal risks and opportunities to consider.

    Human capital will be critical for decades to come, and the forces expected to disrupt virtually every workplace means it is more important than ever that dealmakers have a rigorous workforce plan focused on the value of people – specifically, the two unique qualities they bring to business: skills and diversity. This will involve considering the needs of companies’ future workforces and developing the appropriate strategies to respond, including asking what skills will be needed years from now and whether they will be developed in-house or bought or borrowed from third parties. Beyond workforce planning, understanding what plans and resources are in place to support upskilling efforts will also help companies remain competitive in markets that are continually changing.

    The gig economy: How workers could evolve

    Already, the growth of on-demand platforms have changed the way companies view labor, since they provide employers with the benefits of a relatively less expensive and more flexible set of skills available to them only when they need it. It has been estimated that in 2017 approximately 10 percent to 30 percent of adults in the US engaged in gig work as their primary job and/or to supplement their income. Otherwise known as freelance or contingent work, gig work ranges from drivers using online platforms to traditional freelance writers and software engineers. Contingent work has long existed, and technology has magnified the flexibility and opportunities for them.

    If the percentage of gig workers grow as some expect, dealmakers will likely face an increasingly complicated market given the cumbersome nature of these human assets, particularly in cases where they prove critical to the target’s business but can’t technically be considered “employees,” at least under current labor laws. It remains to be seen where regulations could go, and it will be important to proactively navigate the complexities of the gig workforce going forward.

    If an acquirer buys a gig platform or a firm with a significant share of contingent workers, it will need to address some unique challenges, such as retaining those workers since they are not technically categorized as employees with all the benefits and protections that come with that status. Among the questions for dealmakers: How are companies setting themselves up to attract and keep gig workers? What positions are most likely to be gig versus full-time employees? How should companies manage the distinct risks of gig workers, including cybersecurity and intellectual property risks?

    Skills gap: What humans can uniquely master, but robots can’t

    Indeed, human skills come at a premium. When it comes to adopting new technologies, it’s not just about cutting costs. It’s also aimed at boosting productivity – a goal that often gets lost when firms invest in digital labor without factoring how humans will play a role.

    For instance, US banks in recent years have invested in new technologies to automate and digitize work done in back offices, since digital labor is relatively less costly than US domestic staff. Robots can also operate 24/7 and don’t take sick days. However, companies risk seeing lower returns if they overlook the human skills needed to manage and maintain the machines.

    Companies that are most successful at adopting and adapting to new technologies prioritize workforce training and upskilling. At a recent PwC conference in New York City, futurist and author Tim O’Reilly underscored this notion. “What great companies teach us is that technology is a talent additive,” he said. “It actually improves the capabilities of your people.”

    From a deals perspective, organizations can start uncovering the risks and opportunities of digital labor by forming a deeper understanding of the skills of their workforce and by asking key questions: How prepared is the workforce in adopting new technologies? Has the organization assessed the workforce’s digital IQ? Is there a strategy around which employees will receive digital upskilling? Will the firm’s digital strategy come from top executives, or will employees working directly with new tools drive the strategy? Will the firm encourage employees to try new things and accept a healthy level of failure?

    Diversity: A spectrum of perspectives lead to better outcomes

    Skills are just one part of the equation. The diversity of a company’s workforce, including the spectrum of experiences, perspectives and backgrounds that employees bring, also influences a company’s value in the marketplace. More than that, diversity shapes an organization’s reputation and ability to retain and attract not only talent, but customers as well.

    Surveys suggest that having a diverse workforce enables organizations to better understand different segments of the customer population and anticipate their needs. Also, organizations that have greater racial and gender diversity in their leadership teams are more successful. These are all important considerations when investors review a deal. Not just in terms of determining a company’s valuation, but also when assessing whether an acquirer and target will work well together. For instance, if part of a firm’s mission is driving a diverse and inclusive culture and working environment, and it wants to acquire a company that hasn’t made this a priority, then the organizations could be at odds.

    Today most dealmakers are thinking about soaring valuations and whether the price of their next M&A will be worth it. No doubt these are very real concerns, but it’s worth taking a step back and assessing the underlying trends likely to influence deals. The complexities changing the dynamics of the workforce are bound to transpire in the deals investors consider. Will you be ready?

    Carrie Duarte is the Workforce of the Future and Human Resources M&A Leader at PwC.
