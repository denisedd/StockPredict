    The Indoor Generation

    How light and air quality affect our well-being

    You are probably spending more time indoors than you think. When surveyed, people in key countries across Europe and North America estimated that they spend 66 percent of their time inside. But further studies have shown that the average person actually spends a whopping 90 percent of their time between four walls.

    The truth is that most of us don’t even realize that all that time indoors has a detrimental effect on our health and well-being, similar to poor diet or lack of exercise. We are also unaware that pollution is often worse indoors than outdoors. The poor air quality in our homes, our offices and our schools is associated with costly health problems like asthma, respiratory disease, heart disease and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.

    And if you’re spending most of your time indoors, you’re probably not getting enough daylight, which leads to lower brain function, sleep quality, mood and productivity.

    Where pollution
    really counts

    PART ONE: FACTS

    The phrase “air pollution” brings to mind images of a factory belching smoke into the air or smog clouding a city skyline. But the air inside our homes and buildings can be up to five times more polluted than the air outdoors. According to a YouGov survey, nearly 80 percent of people don’t know this.

    But how does the air indoors get so bad? The air indoors is basically the same as outside air, with added pollution from all the materials around us. Anything from paint and cleaning products, to furniture and upholstery, to plastic toys and carpets, can trap dust and become a pollutant. Children’s bedrooms are often the most polluted rooms in the house.

    "We’ve gone from being an outdoor species to spending most of our time in dim, dark caves."

    Russell Foster, Nuffield Laboratory of Ophthalmology, Oxford

    Do you often cook, burn candles or dry clothes indoors? Each of these adds even more impurities. Moisture leads to mold and mildew, and their fungal spores contaminate the air.

    Spending so much time indoors also cuts us off from natural light. As a species, we evolved to need daylight. “From the year 1800 to 2000, we’ve moved from 90 percent of people working outside to less than 20 percent," says Russell Foster, the head of the Nuffield Laboratory of Ophthalmology and the Sleep and Circadian Neuroscience Institute at the University of Oxford. "We’ve gone from being an outdoor species to spending most of our time in dim, dark caves.”

    On average, an employee spends just 15 minutes outdoors during the workday, found the Munich-based chronobiologist Till Roenneberg in a survey of several thousand people.

    Lack of daylight disrupts our bodies’ circadian rhythms, the 24-hour cycles that tell our bodies when to be awake and when to be asleep. If these rhythms are frequently interrupted, our sleep quality, brain function and general health suffer.

    PART TWO: EFFECTS

    Your life, indoors

    Poor indoor air quality affects us physically and psychologically. It increases incidences of asthma, respiratory disease, heart disease and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. People in damp or moldy homes are 40 percent more likely to have asthma. Asthma alone costs the U.S. economy more than $80 billion per year. Indoor air pollution can also cause short-term problems like coughs, headaches, sore throats and eye irritation.

    The light inside your home or office may not seem dim to you, but that's because the amount of light we need to see is much less than the amount our circadian clocks need. Typical indoor light levels are only about 300 to 500 lux. Just sitting by a window gives you around 3,000 lux. Outside levels of light range from about 10,000 to 100,000 lux: enough to regulate your circadian clock in just 30 minutes of exposure. Simply put, the intensity of light we think we feel comfortable with is actually one-half — and sometimes one-third — the level our body actually needs to be healthy.

    "Light is an acute stimulant that directly alerts the brain."
    — Steven Lockley, Associate Professor of Medicine
    at Harvard Medical School

    The circadian clock is crucial in order for our bodies and minds to function at top condition, and it needs daylight.

    Lack of daylight disrupts our sleep, which in turn makes us less productive, less able to learn and more prone to mistakes. Our dim indoor light can also make us moody, and, in 5 percent of people, cause Seasonal Affective Disorder, with the percentage increasing in higher latitudes.

    Conversely, increased daylight in classrooms improves students’ math test results by as much as 15 percent. Office workers with a good view out their window perform 10 to 25 percent better on tests of mental function and memory than those with no view. “Light is an acute stimulant that directly alerts the brain,” says Steven Lockley, a neuroscientist at Harvard Medical School and Brigham and Women’s Hospital.

    Take action for
    a healthier indoor space

    PART THREE: SOLUTIONS

    Fortunately, there are some quick and relatively easy solutions to maximize your exposure to daylight and minimize the air pollutants at home and the office.

      What you can do to reduce the harmful effects of indoor living:

    • 01 Take opportunities to go outside every day.
    • 02 Get plenty of natural daylight during the day and sleep in complete darkness to respect the circadian rhythm.
    • 03 Let in fresh air at least three or four times a day.
    • 04 Limit sources of moisture — for example, keep the bathroom door closed while showering and turn on the fan or open a window.
    • 05 Have a carpet-free house or clean your carpets frequently.
    • 06 When cooking, turn on the hood fan and open the windows.
    • 07 Dry clothes outside as often as possible.
    • 08 Avoid burning candles.
    • 09 Clean regularly.

    "What the research reveals is that we cannot ignore the health of our indoor domestic and public spaces."
    — Peter Foldbjerg, Head of Daylight Energy and
    Indoor Climate at Velux

    More generally, we can also rethink building design and architecture, prioritizing daylight and fresh air. For example, using sustainable materials, designing wider windows and placing the most frequently used rooms in locations with south-facing windows will maximize daylight exposure.

    Research shows that there’s a big disconnect between how we think we’re living and reality. Awareness is the first step to addressing the effects of our indoor lifestyle.
