‘Cheers’ Said Goodbye 25 Years Ago: Raise a Toast With These 9 Essential Episodes

by Josh Wolk

May 18, 2018

Ted Danson and Shelley Long in Season 4, Episode 11 of “Cheers.”NBC, via Getty Images

Many classic sitcoms are spoken of in reverential tones because of their influence and historical importance, but they don’t actually hold up to modern viewing: The pace is too slow, the punch lines are too hoary, the laugh track is too obtrusive.

Not so with “Cheers.”

After 11 seasons, “Cheers” said goodbye 25 years ago (the 90-minute finale aired on May 20, 1993), but it remains a timeless delight — a feat of casting, consistency and sharp writing that some argue has never been matched. Very few comedies have made it so long without a dip in quality, let alone adjusted to the departure of two beloved cast members (Shelley Long, as Diane, and Nicholas Colasanto, as Coach) without losing a step. The show is regularly cited as a touchstone for newer sitcoms with will-they-won’t-they couples, or with surrogate families of pals, but “Cheers” remains as good as any of them, if not better — no matter the format.

Unless you need to follow every step of Sam and Diane’s courtship (seasons 1-5), or every misstep of Rebecca’s growing haplessness (6-11), skipping among seasons is reassuringly fun. (There’s a reason Norm and Cliff never leave.) Whether “Cheers” is an old favorite or you still need an introduction, these nine standout episodes provide a great time-lapse portrait of the show.

Nicholas Colasanto and Allyce Beasley in Season 1, Episode 5 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Coach’s Daughter’

Season 1, Episode 5. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

The “Cheers” pilot is rightly heralded as perfection, but you can skip its exposition (Sam, played by Ted Danson, gives Diane, a grad student, a job as a waitress after she is abandoned at Cheers by her professor fiancé) and jump to this Season 1 gem. Coach is nervous when his beloved, insecure daughter (Allyce Beasley of “Moonlighting”) visits to introduce her new fiancé, Roy — and he’s driven to madness when her intended turns out to be an insufferably sleazy door-to-door suit salesman. It’s amazing that all the characters are already fine-tuned by Episode 5, but this is Colasanto’s showcase as Coach, and it ends with a wrenchingly touching father-daughter moment that is played without a laugh. “Coach’s Daughter” should be piped into every home on Father’s Day.

Shelley Long and Ted Danson in Season 2, Episode 1 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Power Play’

Season 2, Episode 1. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

The Season 2 premiere opens where the Season 1 finale left off, with Sam and Diane on the brink of punching each other, only to passionately make out instead. Thus does their romance officially begin, but this episode quickly eliminates any worry that “Cheers” will get schmaltzy. For their first night together, Diane wants to go somewhere Sam has never been with a woman, so their only option in the whole city of Boston is her apartment. Her tiny pad quickly becomes a Thunderdome of crackling squabbles, barbs and a head game that leaves the Lothario Sam a hapless mess. It is instantly obvious why these two don’t make any sense as a couple, and yet it’s so much fun to watch them fight — and irresistible to watch them make up.

Shelley Long and Ted Danson in Season 4, Episode 11 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Don Juan Is Hell’

Season 4, Episode 11. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

Season 3 has Diane breaking up with Sam and getting engaged to her new therapist, Frasier Crane (Kelsey Grammer): In Season 4, having jilted Frasier at the altar, Diane is back to sparring with Sam as they try to repress their mutual attraction. In this standout episode, Diane makes Sam the subject of her Human Sexuality paper on Don Juan syndrome, a diagnosis he takes as a compliment before actually reading the study, which calls him an “eternal adolescent” and “sad and pathetic.” Sam finally realizes what the audience has long known: that his cheesy come-ons are a joke. Watching this epiphany floor him leads to an exemplar of one of the show’s hallmarks: a final scene in Sam’s office that grows surprisingly bittersweet before ending in a gigantic laugh.

From left, Ted Danson, George Wendt, Rhea Perlman and Woody Harrelson in Season 5, Episode 9 of “Cheers.”NBC, via Getty Images

‘Thanksgiving Orphans’

Season 5, Episode 9. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

When the bar regulars find themselves alone for Thanksgiving, they gather at Carla’s for a potluck dinner. Tempers fray when Norm’s enormous turkey (or “Birdzilla”) refuses to cook, and everything comes to a hangry head with a two-fisted, yam-hurling melee that edges out “Animal House” for Best Food Fight. It’s one of the most essential scenes in the “Cheers” canon, and watching the cast gleefully smear food in one another’s hair and faces is the most fun you can have without getting cranberry sauce in your ears.

Bebe Neuwirth and Ted Danson in Season 6, Episode 8 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Bidding on the Boys’

Season 6, Episode 8. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

Woody and Sam go on the block for Rebecca’s charity bachelor auction while, further down the bar, Lilith, Frasier’s new fiancé, is outraged by Frasier’s desire for a prenup, and the story lines outrageously dovetail. Bebe Neuwirth is at her prickly best here as Lilith, and she even gets a chance to let down her bun, loosen her posture and show off some of her Tony-winning dance skills. Meanwhile, it’s a marvel to observe just how seamlessly Kirstie Alley, as Rebecca, has stepped in for Shelley Long, giving the show a refreshingly new dynamic. Rebecca’s indifference to Sam’s slimy advances means freedom from a central plot-gobbling romance, and “Cheers” becomes a denser joke machine for all of its characters.

Kirstie Alley in Season 8, Episode 16 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Finally’ Part 2

Season 8, Episode 16. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

This may be Alley’s finest half-hour. In Part 1, Rebecca ended her four-year run of celibacy, with the millionaire tycoon Robin Colcord (Roger Rees), only for Sam to learn that Robin was seeing another woman. When Sam breaks the news to Rebecca in Part 2, she needs to choose between her self-esteem and her dream of a rich boyfriend. While her character began as brusque and (mostly) self-assured, the writers quickly discovered she was much more fun as a hapless, gold-digging mess. She is positively Lucille Ball-like in the final scene as she swings between faux-bravado and blubbering: Nobody cries funnier than Kirstie Alley.

Woody Harrelson in Season 9, Episode 9 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘Veggie Boyd’

Season 9, Episode 9. Stream it on Amazon Prime, Hulu or Netflix.

Woody Boyd (played by Woody Harrelson), the bar’s resident aspiring actor and former Indiana farm boy, gets a line in a TV commercial for a new health drink. (“I like it!”) But when he later tries and detests the revolting concoction, he is plunged into the depths of guilt because that technically makes him a liar. In a feat that defied decades of conventional sitcom wisdom, the ninth season of “Cheers” was its funniest, and this episode exemplifies why: The scripts have a more breathlessly farcical pace, and they are stuffed with side jokes that land well because of how well we know the characters. “Veggie Boyd” has a great B-story centered on Cliff (John Ratzenberger), whose feelings get hurt when everyone loves Sam’s new trivia napkins — a huge payoff after eight-plus seasons of watching Cliff bore everyone with his “little known facts.”

From left, Woody Harrelson, Kirstie Alley, Rhea Perlman, Kelsey Grammer and Ted Danson in Season 10, Episode 26 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘An Old-Fashioned Wedding’

Season 10, Episodes 25 and 26. Stream it on Hulu or Netflix.

Woody’s hourlong wedding episode (since split into two parts for syndication) is the perfect TV farce, the “Noises Off” of sitcom episodes. In the venue’s kitchen before the ceremony, the frantic gang has to deal with a dead minister, an A.W.O.L. chef, killer guard dogs, a jealous husband, a horny couple, and much more. With all the pieces set up in the first half, the second half is an ever-escalating, never-resting dance of mayhem; the longtime director James Burrows stages sequences that make you want to stand up and applaud, and the cast’s timing is [chef’s kiss]. The show’s creators have cited the classic Britcom “Fawlty Towers” as one of their original inspirations, and this giddy farce might well out-Fawlty the original.

From left, Shelley Long and Ted Danson in Season 11, Episode 28 of “Cheers.”NBC

‘One for the Road’ Part 3

Season 11, Episode 28. Stream it on Hulu or Netflix.

The writers brought back Diane to rekindle things with Sam for the supersized series finale, a high-concept callback that feels too busy and — after six seasons of distance from the Sam-Diane dance — unnecessary. But after Sam and Diane finally realize they aren’t meant for each other, Sam sheepishly returns to the bar at closing time for a moving and meta farewell, in which the characters talk about the meaning of life and the primary importance of good friends. It feels as if the characters and the actors are talking to one another, and to the audience as well — and as with the best closing “Cheers” scenes, the writers know when to relieve a heartfelt exchange with a big laugh. When Sam closes up for the last time, you’ll feel so close to these characters you’ll want to rewatch all 265 episodes not mentioned here, just to have more time with them.
