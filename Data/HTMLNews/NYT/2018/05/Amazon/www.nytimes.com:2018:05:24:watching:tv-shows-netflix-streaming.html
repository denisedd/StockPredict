Where Are Your Favorite Shows Streaming Now?

by Judy Berman

May 24, 2018

From left, “Atlanta,” “Crazy Ex-Girlfriend,” “Grey’s Anatomy” and “The Simpsons.”FX; CW; ABC; Fox

In the early days of legal streaming, Netflix was a one-stop shop for just about every TV series you could watch online. But in recent years, as that mega-platform has prioritized original productions over licensed content, Amazon Prime Video, Hulu and the proprietary platforms of various networks, like HBO and Showtime, have picked up the slack. Now, current and classic shows bounce between services, while series that never streamed before come online all the time.

It’s a lot to keep track of. So to help you navigate this confusing landscape, we’ve put together this regularly updated map for finding all your current and future favorites.

Kristen Bell in “The Good Place.”Justin Lubin/NBC

Netflix

‘Absolutely Fabulous’
Where to watch: Netflix and Hulu

One of Britain’s most enduring TV franchises is a sitcom about a debauched middle-aged public relations executive (Jennifer Saunders) with a strait-laced teenage daughter (Julia Sawalha) and a best friend who is 100 percent id (Joanna Lumley). The early seasons in particular have infinite rewatching potential.
—

‘American Crime Story’
Where to watch: Netflix (Season 1)

The most recent season of this true-crime anthology series, “The Assassination of Gianni Versace,” hasn’t hit streaming sites yet. But if you still haven’t seen the first season, an instant classic that probed the racial and gender politics of the O.J. Simpson trial, consider devoting a quiet weekend to bingeing through it.
—

‘Better Call Saul’
Where to watch: Netflix

This “Breaking Bad” prequel, slower and looser than the original, is a critical darling in its own right, telling the story of how a public defender named Jimmy McGill (Bob Odenkirk) became Albuquerque’s leading extralegal lawyer. The three seasons that have aired to date combine family drama, legal procedural, dark comedy and crime thriller into one fascinating, uncategorizable whole.
—

‘Breaking Bad’
Where to watch: Netflix

Whether it’s your first time (in which case, hold on tight) or you’re revisiting it for clues about “Better Call Saul,” watching this crime drama from Vince Gilligan is a uniquely gripping way to lose your faith in humanity.
—

‘Cheers’
Where to watch: Netflix, Amazon Prime (Seasons 1-9) or Hulu

Is there any comedy so essential that it demands to be included in the library of every major streaming service? Yes. Yes, there is.
—

‘Crazy Ex-Girlfriend’
Where to watch: Netflix

Comedy! Drama! Romance! Dirty-minded musical numbers! A nuanced depiction of mental illness! The CW’s “Crazy Ex-Girlfriend” has everything. The coming season, its fourth and final, is sure to up the ante on craziness, so there’s no time like present to catch up.
—

‘Frasier’
Where to watch: Netflix, Amazon Prime and Hulu

Having trouble sleeping? Try “Frasier.” This “Cheers” spinoff relocates the psychiatrist Frasier Crane (Kelsey Grammer) from Boston to Seattle, sets him up with a radio talk show, surrounds him with charmingly neurotic family and friends and offers viewers a relaxing virtual getaway to the world of rich-people problems.
—

‘Freaks and Geeks’
Where to watch: Netflix

Brilliant but canceled after just one season, this cult teen dramedy helped launch the careers of its creator, Paul Feig; its executive producer, Judd Apatow; and a cast including James Franco, Seth Rogen and Jason Segel. Made around the turn of the millennium and set in the ’70s, it’s a funny, sweet show about growing up weird that should resonate with viewers of any generation.
—

‘Friends’
Where to watch: Netflix

When “Friends” came to Netflix in 2015, it was a big deal. Stricken by phantom nostalgia for a time when pals passed idle hours on threadbare coffee-shop couches instead of in group chats, a new generation of teens and 20-somethings became superfans. But this era-defining hangout sitcom also retains its appeal for many who actually remember the ’90s, living on as an ideal comfort show more than as a realistic document of Gen-X young adulthood.
—

‘The Good Place’
Where to watch: Netflix (Season 1) and NBC (Season 2)

A show starring Kristen Bell and Ted Danson was always going to be lovable, but NBC’s afterlife comedy “The Good Place” has turned out to be something better: a fun and insightful exploration of moral philosophy. Be warned, though: While most network sitcoms are episodic, this one is highly serialized. Don’t even try to watch it out of order.
—

‘The Great British Baking Show’
Where to watch: PBS and Netflix

The reigning queen of comforting food television is this binge-worthy import, which pits talented home cooks from all over Britain against one another. It’s a refreshingly civil competition, in which all of the kind, self-deprecating contestants most definitely are there to make friends.
—

‘Grey’s Anatomy’
Where to watch: Netflix (Seasons 1-13) and Hulu (Season 14)

It’s never a bad time to start watching (or to catch up on) this long-running, hugely influential ABC medical drama from Shonda Rhimes. If 14 seasons sounds daunting, know that the show sags a bit in the middle, so you can skip around as needed between Seasons 5 and 9.
—

‘Mad Men’
Where to watch: Netflix

Aside from being one of the greatest dramas of its time, this story of the inscrutable ’60s ad man Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is so detailed that it practically demands to be rewatched.
—

‘The Office’ (Britain)
Where to watch: Netflix and Hulu

Ricky Gervais broke through to America as the star of this BBC workplace sitcom that he also created, wrote and directed with his frequent collaborator Stephen Merchant. Gervais’s narcissistic middle-manager character, David Brent, remains a cringe-comedy legend.
—

‘The Office’ (U.S.)
Where to watch: Netflix

This stateside adaptation of “The Office” is sweeter and kinder but still hilariously uncomfortable, casting Steve Carell as Michael Scott, a paper-company manager who is more awkward and clueless than malevolent. With its light serialization and low stakes, this recent NBC classic makes an ideal comfort show.
—

‘Parks and Recreation’
Where to watch: Netflix, Hulu and Amazon Prime

Originally envisioned as a spinoff of NBC’s “The Office,” “Parks and Rec” is a sitcom about a small-town bureaucrat (Amy Poehler) whose commitment to her job borders on obsessive. Another great comfort show, it balances story lines about fundamentally decent people with uproarious humor.
—

‘Star Trek’
Where to watch: Netflix

If you want to watch the latest “Star Trek” series, “Discovery,” you’ll have to shell out for CBS All Access. But you can find the original 1960s series on Netflix, and the earlier spinoffs — “The Next Generation,” “Deep Space Nine,” “Voyager” and “Enterprise” — are all available on Amazon Prime and Hulu as well as Netflix.
—

‘Twin Peaks’
Where to watch: Netflix, Hulu and Amazon Prime

Streaming introduced a new generation to this surreal murder mystery from David Lynch and Mark Frost, and it quickly got just as obsessed with it as the original fans were. That renewed interest led Showtime to bankroll last year’s genre-exploding work of television art “Twin Peaks: The Return” — which you can only stream on that network’s platform.
—

‘The West Wing’
Where to watch: Netflix

A liberal fantasy from a more innocent time, Aaron Sorkin’s “The West Wing” is all snappy banter and lofty ideals. Watch it to savor the sanity of pre-social-media political discourse.
—

From left, Sonja Sohn, Wendell Pierce and Dominic West in “The Wire.”Nicole Rivelli/HBO

Amazon Prime

‘The Americans’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video (Seasons 1-5) and FX (Season 6)

Tired of feeling confused when it’s prime time and your social media feed fills up with posts along the lines of, “!!! #TheAmericans”? If you’ve got Prime, a cable login and, frankly, a ton of free time, you might still be able to catch up before the sixth and final season ends on May 30.

‘Deadwood’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video or HBO Go

The pleasures of this cult HBO Western from David Milch include baroque cursing, moral complexity and a first-rate cast featuring Timothy Olyphant, Ian McShane, Molly Parker and John Hawkes. Watch it before the long-awaited “Deadwood” movie arrives — if it arrives.
—

‘Doctor Who’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video

After a 26-year original run, the British sci-fi saga “Doctor Who” called it quits in 1989. But the franchise returned to the BBC in 2005 and has been going strong ever since — available to watch in full on Prime. If you’re interested in the older episodes, you can find them on BritBox.
—

‘Friday Night Lights’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video

Get ready to cry about football, even if the sport means nothing to you. This NBC drama about Coach Taylor (Kyle Chandler), his family and the high-school students he and his beloved wife (Connie Britton) mentor is about as poignant as a show can be without getting maudlin.
—

‘Mr. Robot’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video (Seasons 1 and 2) and USA (Season 3)

If you like shows that question authority and blur the line between fantasy and reality, you’ll love this psychological cyber-thriller about a brilliant but paranoid hacker (Rami Malek) and his mysterious companion, Mr. Robot (Christian Slater).
—

‘Roseanne’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video

“Roseanne” may be back — and you can watch episodes of the revival on Hulu — but some former fans might prefer to revisit the original series. Seasons 1-6 are essential; Seasons 7-9 are not, although they are often zany and fun.
—

‘Sex and the City’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video and HBO Go

In this groundbreaking sex comedy, four female friends in their 30s stop being polite and start getting real. The show hasn’t aged perfectly since its 1998 premiere, but Carrie Bradshaw (Sarah Jessica Parker) and her pals’ bedroom foibles still make diverting light entertainment.
—

‘Six Feet Under’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video and HBO Go

Too often overlooked in conversations about influential early-2000s HBO shows, this emotional drama about a family that owns and resides in a funeral home isn’t exactly light viewing. But it does have plenty to say about life, death and how we choose to spend our limited time on Earth.
—

‘The Sopranos’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video and HBO Go

HBO licenses many of its shows to Amazon, but Vulture reported in 2017 that the companies’ deal was scheduled to expire in May 2018 and that HBO’s leadership said it had no plans to renew it. At this point, it’s unclear whether or when that will happen. But if you’re a Prime subscriber who still hasn’t watched the show that launched a thousand antihero dramas, you might want to prioritize it.
—

‘The Wire’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime Video and HBO Go

The journalist and TV writer David Simon ascended to auteur status when he created this dark, gritty social drama about Baltimore’s failing institutions. Its twists can feel like a punch in the stomach, but the insights it provides into crime, poverty and systemic oppression are vital.
—

Also on Amazon Prime Video: “Cheers,” “Frasier,” “Parks and Recreation,” “Twin Peaks.”
—

Hulu

‘30 Rock’
Where to watch: Hulu

When the news broke that Tina Fey’s quippy comedy about a feminist sketch-show producer named Liz Lemon (Fey) and her alpha-male boss, Jack Donaghy (Alec Baldwin), was leaving Netflix in the fall of 2017, fans panicked. Thankfully, all seven seasons wound up on Hulu.
—

‘Atlanta’
Where to watch: Hulu (Season 1) and FX (Season 2)

Two seasons in, it’s still impossible to predict where any given half-hour of Donald Glover’s surreal show is going — or even what genre its story will fall into. Season 2 is more serialized than Season 1, but you won’t want to skip any episodes of “Atlanta,” so you might as well watch them in order.
—

‘Battlestar Galactica’
Where to watch: Hulu

Ronald D. Moore’s grown-up reimagining of the wholesome 1970s space saga of the same name may be the defining sci-fi series of the 21st century. It’s also so ripe for binge-watching that “Portlandia” devoted much of an episode to spoofing its addictive properties.
—

‘Brooklyn Nine-Nine’
Where to watch: Hulu

Fox recently canceled this consistently strong police sitcom, earning the wrath of its small but enthusiastic fan base. (NBC almost immediately picked it up for renewal.) If you’re curious to know what the fuss is about, all five seasons are available to stream.
—

‘Buffy the Vampire Slayer’
Where to watch: Hulu

As the many adults who regularly return to it already know, you need not be a genre fan or a high schooler to enjoy this supernatural teen drama’s smart, witty and frequently empowering stories about a California blonde who kills vampires.
—

‘Fargo’
Where to watch: Hulu (Seasons 1 and 2)

Inspired by the Coen brothers film of the same name, this FX anthology series follows a different set of eccentric cops and crooks each season, in bloody crime capers that raise questions about the nature of good and evil. Start with Seasons 1 or 2 — the order doesn’t matter — but save Season 3 (which is still only available by rental) for after you’ve finished the first.
—

‘Golden Girls’
Where to watch: Hulu

In early 2017, our long national nightmare came to an end when “Golden Girls” finally started streaming. Now, whether you’re looking for camaraderie, camp, social commentary or just a stellar cast of older women behaving badly, you can find 180 episodes’ worth on Hulu.
—

‘Lost’
Where to watch: Hulu

If you’re embarking on this spiritual fantasy epic for the first time, know that the answers it finally provided to the many metaphysical questions it raised frustrated many viewers. And if you’re a longtime fan who feels the need to go back to the island, well, at least you know what to expect.
—

‘The Mary Tyler Moore Show’
Where to watch: Hulu

Before Carrie and Khadijah and Murphy and Ally and Hannah and Issa, there was Mary — a single career woman whose arrival on television in 1970 brought feminism to prime time. If you like the generations’ worth of TV characters and creators she influenced, you’re sure to love her.
—

‘NYPD Blue’
Where to watch: Hulu

Created with David Milch, this gritty, profane, often controversial ABC police procedural changed network TV when it debuted in 1993. “NYPD Blue” was an antihero drama that took on institutional corruption, years before “The Sopranos” and “The Wire” appeared on HBO.
—

‘Seinfeld’
Where to watch: Hulu

It turns out that a show about nothing never gets old. Although Netflix is home to Jerry Seinfeld’s current series, “Comedians in Cars Getting Coffee,” the full run of his game-changing ’90s sitcom can only be found on Hulu. If you aren’t a subscriber, you can stream a handful of episodes for free on Crackle.

‘The X-Files’
Where to watch: Hulu

Do you want to believe? Then point your inquiring mind at this paranormal drama about two F.B.I. agents who have a whole lot of chemistry. While the 2016 revival, which produced Seasons 10 and 11, is divisive, the original series remains iconic.
—

Also on Hulu: “Absolutely Fabulous,” “Cheers,” “Frasier,” “The Office (Britain,” “Grey’s Anatomy,” “Parks and Recreation,” “Twin Peaks.”
—

Other services

‘The Simpsons’
Where to watch: FXX’s Simpsonsworld.com

The bad news is that Matt Groening’s 29-season animated wonder still isn’t available on a major streaming service; the good news is that, if you have a cable login, you can stream every episode on FXX’s stand-alone “Simpsons” site. Among the special features on offer are audio commentary, themed playlists and interviews with the writers.
