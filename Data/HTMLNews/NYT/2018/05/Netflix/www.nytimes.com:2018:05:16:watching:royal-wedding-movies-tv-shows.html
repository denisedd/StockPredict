Forget Watching the Royal Wedding, Here’s the Royal Dirt

by Eleanor Stanford

May 16, 2018

From left: Jonathan Rhys Meyers in “The Tudors,” Cate Blanchett in “Elizabeth” and Vanessa Kirby in “The Crown.”Showtime; Grammercy Pictures; Netflix

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation website. Sign up for our thrice-weekly newsletter here.

As you may have heard, a couple of crazy kids in love are getting married at a castle in England on Saturday. If you’re wondering how to watch the Royal Wedding, have questions about its many idiosyncratic customs or want to watch a retelling of Prince Harry and Meghan Markle’s love story, we’ve got you covered.

But the royal family isn’t all fascinators and “happily ever after”: Hundreds of years of public life have given filmmakers and showrunners a lot of juicy royal material to work with, too. Not interested in the fairy tale? Sit back and enjoy watching Harry’s predecessors fight, scheme and rebel instead.

Jonathan Rhys Meyers and Natalie Dormer in “The Tudors.”Jonathan Hession/Showtime

TV Show

The Tudors

Back in the 16th century, the role of queen existed primarily to produce an heir. A daughter was considered fine, but only sons could really be trusted with the throne. King Henry VIII — glutton, narcissist and bully — was ruthless in his pursuit of a male heir, ultimately cycling through six wives over the course of his 36-year reign.

“The Tudors” takes a soapy look at this homicidal husband, bouncing merrily between the bedroom and the throne room with the help of an all-star cast and a lot of modern touches. Jonathan Rhys Meyers is Henry, Natalie Dormer is his temptress Anne Boleyn, and Sam Neill is the priest who helped Henry break with the Catholic church so that Henry and Boleyn could be married.

I want the current royal family’s dirty laundry

Claire Foy and Matt Smith in Season 2 of “The Crown.”Alex Bailey/Netflix

TV Show

The Crown

Queen Elizabeth II’s husband, Prince Philip, has long been a controversial figure. In public, he has a habit of making comments that strike many people as, if not overtly racist and sexist, then at least evidence of old-school British colonial values. Season 2 of Netflix’s “The Crown” is especially focused on the private life of Prince Philip, who is played chillingly by Matt Smith. Infidelity, Nazi family members, cruel parenting: The show doesn’t pull any punches in its portrayal of him. Meanwhile, the romantic affairs of the rebellious Princess Margaret add extra spice to this sumptuous telling of Elizabeth’s long reign.

I want to see Elizabeth II when she was young and sassy

A scene from “A Royal Night Out.”Ecosse Films

Movie

A Royal Night Out

Elizabeth was 19 and still a princess when the German forces surrendered on May 8, 1945. That evening, she and her younger sister, Margaret, secretly went out into London to join the spontaneous celebrations that went long into the night. This 2015 film is a merry comedy of errors, imagining what the princesses got up to on this “wizard all-nighter” as they escaped their chaperones to be ”ordinary” for one adventurous night. For one sister, it meant bonding with the common people, while for the other, it meant passing out in a wheelbarrow. We were all teenagers once.

I want a queen wielding some actual power

Cate Blanchett in “Elizabeth.”Grammercy Pictures

Movie

Elizabeth

Elizabeth I was known as the Virgin Queen because she never married, but unmarried didn’t have to mean chaste. Shekhar Kapur’s film “Elizabeth” has the princess (Cate Blanchett) canoodling with Robert Dudley (Joseph Fiennes) when she is accused of heresy and arrested by her sister, who was then on the throne. The film then follows her unexpected journey from terrified prisoner to powerful queen, not shying away from the violence and instability of England in the 16th century — a time when people there were burned alive for being the wrong kind of Christian. Concerns over whom she will marry and when she’ll provide an heir loom large throughout this poetically filmed biopic, pressing in on her until she makes a decision, declaring to herself, “I have become a virgin.”

I want to know about Elizabeth II’s greatest controversy

Helen Mirren and James Cromwell in “The Queen.”Laurie Sparham/Miramax Films

Movie

The Queen

A royal wedding can, of course, be followed by a royal divorce, and no divorce has drawn more public attention than when Harry’s parents split up. When Diana, Princess of Wales, died in 1997, she had been divorced from Prince Charles for a year and was no longer a member of the royal family. For the Queen, that meant deciding that there was no need for the royal family to add its voice to the public’s incredible outpouring of grief. She didn’t fly the flags over her palaces at half-mast, which many newspapers presented as deeply disrespectful.

Helen Mirren plays a chilly Elizabeth, who starts to doubt how well she knows her own people as she navigates her biggest controversy to date. Director Stephen Frears deftly splices archival footage with imagined discussions taking place between the Queen and those close to her, teasing out the tension between royal protocol and celebrity culture, as an old power system confronts a new one.

I want the, um, bedroom insight

A scene from “Tales From the Royal Bedchamber.”BBC

TV Show

Tales from the Royal Bedchamber

The gently bawdy BBC documentary “Tales from the Royal Bedchamber” does pretty much what it says on the tin. The historian Lucy Worsley is our lively guide to royal bedrooms through the ages, revealing how the ornate ceremonial beds were made, who slept in them and who was allowed to watch a king getting dressed (spoiler: more people than you might think). “Tales” mixes the typically staid elements of a BBC documentary — tracking shots of our presenter walking through a palace, detailed descriptions of objects used at the time — with clips of Worsley asking other historians with relish about who wiped the king’s bottom. The overall effect is a little bit disconcerting and a little bit fun, with the 45-minute documentary answering questions you never knew you had about royal bedroom habits. (“Bedchamber” is streaming on Amazon with a PBS Masterpiece subscription and is available on iTunes for rental or purchase.)

I want to see Britain grapple with its colonial legacy

A scene from “Empire.”BBC

TV Show

Empire

During Queen Victoria’s reign, the British Empire swelled to cover a quarter of the world. The British believed that Queen Victoria was the Empire in human form, which, in retrospect, wasn’t really something for her to be proud of. At the beginning of this five-part BBC documentary from 2012, the presenter, Jeremy Paxman, promises that the series will confront the horrors perpetrated in the British colonies. But perhaps inevitably, the series more often lapses into the usual celebrations of the empire’s former strength, although the fourth episode is unflinching in its recounting of the atrocities perpetrated by slave owners and British opium trade.

The series certainly has its flaws, but it stands as a fascinating example of contemporary Britain’s attempt to grapple with its colonial past. Paxman, a well-known British political journalist, visits former colonies, offering well-researched accounts of colonial events and their legacy. But he, too, has his blind spots, for example asking locals in his clipped upper-class accent whether they think British occupation was a good or bad thing. Surely it’s more complicated than that.

I want to know about the Nazi connections

Wallis Simpson and Edward VIII, the Duchess and Duke of Windsor, in Ashdown Forest, Sussex.Hulton Archive, via Getty Images

Movie

Edward VIII: The Nazi King

During World War I, the royal family changed its name from “Saxe-Coburg and Gotha” to “Windsor” in an attempt to hide its German roots. By the time World War II came around, Edward VIII had already abdicated. But during the war, he and his wife, Wallace Simpson, had dinner with Nazi ministers, and Adolf Hitler was reportedly very impressed by the couple.

This juicy tabloid-style documentary investigates those connections, building upon archival footage and interviews with historians and firsthand witnesses, complemented by some wonderfully cheesy reconstructions. We are told that Edward “winked awfully well” and that Simpson was a “gold digger.” We also learn that the couple was passing some sort of information to the Germans through a Nazi foreign minister named Joachim von Ribbentrop. Was Edward aware of Hitler’s plan to put him back on the throne as a puppet king once the Nazis conquered England? Who knows. But he certainly seems to have thought the Nazis had some good ideas.
