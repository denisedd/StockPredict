‘Arrested Development’ Is Back for Season 5. Here’s What You Need to Know.

by Charles Bramesco

May 25, 2018

From left, Will Arnett, David Cross and Portia de Rossi in “Arrested Development.”Sam Urdank/Netflix

The fourth season of Netflix’s “Arrested Development” required a staggering feat of multi-front planning: In the years following its cancellation from Fox, the main cast members had all grown considerably more famous and more difficult to coordinate, and the result was a season in which each episode was organized around a single character — a decision that, to many viewers, rendered it needlessly fragmented. As if to acknowledge the criticism, creator Mitchell Hurwitz unveiled a “remix” earlier this month, which re-edited the season into 22 shorter, chronological episodes. Reviews have been mixed. But any way you slice it, the reboot dished up enough plot in a single season to sustain a lesser series for several.

Season 5 was in some ways even more improbable (a recent Times interview with the cast has proved revealing in that respect), but its debut is finally nigh, set for May 29. Need a refresher? Here’s a primer to help you once again scale that gnarled and sickly Bluth family tree.

Michael

Jason Bateman in “Arrested Development.”Netflix

After threatening over and over to leave his family behind, Michael (Jason Bateman) finally followed through in Season 4, only to find that the work of holding the Bluths together had been holding him together, too. With a sizable loan from his sometime rival Lucille Austero (Liza Minnelli), Michael completed construction on his pet project, the Sudden Valley housing community. He did not realize that a lack of internet access, functional utilities and even a connecting road to the rest of Los Angeles made it fundamentally uninhabitable. (An enclave of registered sex offenders later found utopia there.)

Defeated, Michael slunk off to crash at the dorm room of his son, George Michael (Michael Cera), eventually getting voted out by the other roommates. His brief, less-than-illustrious stint as a movie producer began shortly thereafter, as Ron Howard (playing an affable, Hollywooded-up version of himself) approached Michael to adapt his life story to the big screen. A series of misunderstandings drew Michael into a love triangle with his own son and Howard’s daughter, Rebel Alley (Isla Fisher), cuing up a season-ending sucker punch.

Lucille

Jessica Walter in “Arrested Development.”Netflix

The meanest matriarch in Orange County spent the last season sorting through the fallout from her ill-advised flight for freedom that closed out the original run. Placed under house arrest, Lucille (Jessica Walter) and her husband, George Sr. (Jeffrey Tambor), plotted a fake divorce that was meant to free them up to build a lucrative wall on the Mexican border. But a combination of several increasingly personal betrayals — her family’s failure to show up for her trial at a seafood restaurant, a prison stint landing her in the cross hairs of a gang of middle-aged Chinese women, and a double-switcheroo in which her husband sent his twin brother to fulfill certain marital duties and the brother then cheated while cheating — compelled her to seek meaningful change. She concluded the season by resolving to enter therapy and pursue self-actualization.

George Sr.

Jeffrey Tambor in “Arrested Development.”Adam Taylor/Netflix

As a nod to Jeffrey Tambor’s Emmy-festooned side gig as the transitioning Maura Pfefferman on “Transparent,” George Sr. spent Season 4 grappling with a hormonal imbalance he couldn’t understand. As he and his twin brother went about scamming spiritually-minded executives with a bogus wellness retreat in the desert, George Sr. dealt with mood swings, impotence and new sensations in an increasingly unfamiliar body. After faking a video of a glorious, miles-long border wall and failing to convince Lucille Austero or her political rival, Herbert Love (Terry Crews), to build it in earnest, George Sr. made a bid at self-acceptance and began living openly as a woman.

George Michael

Michael Cera in “Arrested Development.”Sam Urdank/Netflix

College life transformed George Michael, his experiences while studying in Spain having awakened his latent manhood. Proudly flaunting his new “overt sexuality,” he put a move on his cousin (but not by blood!) and longtime crush, Maeby (Alia Shawkat). Circumstances conspired against him, however, and he soon landed in the middle of a “Social Network” parody revolving around a high-value privacy software that, in actuality, just made the noise of a wood block.

Still, George Michael found new stores of confidence as his alter ego, George Maharis, a Silicon Valley genius who attracted the attentions of Rebel Alley. After his deception fell apart, George Michael retained some of his newfound self-respect, which culminated in the final scene’s symbolic knuckle sandwich to his father.

G.O.B.

Will Arnett in “Arrested Development.”Mike Yarish/Netflix

The least self-aware Bluth continued to flounder in his own foolhardiness, starting with an inadvertent engagement to the extraordinarily boring Ann (Mae Whitman). Adopting her Christian principles into a dazzlingly sacrilegious magic act, G.O.B. (Will Arnett) was to be sealed in a tomb while dressed as Jesus Christ, but he unwittingly locked himself inside a prop boulder with no means of escape for two weeks. Laid low by a fortnight of living on candy vines, G.O.B. fell in with the entourage of a spoiled pop superstar and slid even further into a daze of liquor and Rohypnol. He found solace in the arms of Tony Wonder (Ben Stiller), his nemesis in prestidigitation, albeit through a deception that slowly melted into true-blue emotion.

Lindsay

Portia de Rossi in “Arrested Development.”Netflix

The pampered Lindsay (Portia de Rossi) struggled between her principles of selflessness and her truer nature of selfishness. A trip to India was supposed to bring enlightenment, but she soon began making the same old mistakes back home: After reconciling with her husband, Tobias (David Cross), and going in on a McMansion at the dawn of the housing crisis, she ran away with a face-blind environmental activist named Marky Bark (Chris Diamantopoulos).

Of course she grew weary of his threadbare lifestyle, and so she defected once more, for an affair with Herbert Love, which also left her unhappy. She seized some agency with an improvised political speech, riling up a crowd with anti-immigrant rhetoric, only semi-aware that she had become more like her mother than ever.

Tobias

Maria Bamford and David Cross in “Arrested Development.”Netflix

Foot planted permanently in his mouth, Tobias had perhaps the toughest go of Season 4. After mistaking a methadone clinic for a Method acting clinic and losing Lindsay to the free-living Marky, Tobias had a fling of his own with an addict named DeBrie Bardeaux (Maria Bamford). They busked for spare dollars as off-brand members of the Fantastic Four until lean times and repeated lawsuits drove them apart.

One regrettable appearance on a “To Catch a Predator”-type show later, Tobias reunited with DeBrie at an inpatient therapy program, where they staged an original musical based on the Fantastic Four. The constant humiliations, in conjunction with the use of blue body paint as part of his Invisible Girl costume, pushed Tobias to a psychological relapse all the way back to his Blue Man Group days. Never-nudism included.

Maeby

Alia Shawkat in “Arrested Development.”Adam Taylor/Netflix

The quick-witted but lazy Maeby scuttled around the periphery of other stories for most of the fourth season. She posed as the guru who instructed her mother, Lindsay, to return home from India and better herself. She went back to finish high school at age 23, drifting back into George Michael’s orbit for tutoring help and then taking the reins of his nonexistent privacy software. (She also arranged for her own mother to go on “dates” with Herbert Love, adding pimp to her jack-of-all-grifts résumé.)

When Maeby learned that she could be expelled for lying about her age, she rushed to rescue herself by sleeping with a boy believed to be the undercover cop at school. Turned out she had unwittingly bedded a minor, outed herself to the actual cop, and landed herself in the sex offender registry. Her next move: to the refuge of Sudden Valley!

Buster

Liza Minnelli and Anthony Hale in “Arrested Development.”Netflix

Freed of his domineering mother, a rudderless Buster (Tony Hale) looked for a new guiding force in life. He first tried Lucille, who got him sufficiently soused on fruit juice to miss his mother’s trial and ensure that she would remain in prison. He then turned to the army, accepting a new assignment piloting drones in the Middle East remotely, which he believed to be a particularly easy video game.

Despite serving from a swivel chair at a facility hidden in a strip mall, Buster still sustained an injury and received a replacement hand from military doctors who were eager to test out their new giant-size bionic prosthesis. After being used as a pawn by Herbert Love, a despondent Buster went on a juice bender and caught an eyeful of Lucille’s mangled body on the steps of the stair-car, becoming the primary suspect in a murder investigation.
