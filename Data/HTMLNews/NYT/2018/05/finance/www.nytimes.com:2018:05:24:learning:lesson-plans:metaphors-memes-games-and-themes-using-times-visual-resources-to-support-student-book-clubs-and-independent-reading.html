Metaphors, Memes, Games and Themes: Using Times Visual Resources to Support Student Book Clubs and Independent Reading

By Brett Vogelsinger

  • May 24, 2018

In English language arts classrooms around the country, more teachers and students are using words like “volume,” “stamina” and “independence” to talk about books. And teachers are structuring their classrooms around reading workshops, conferencing and student-led book clubs to turn these buzzwords into daily habits for their students.

These teaching strategies provide students with more choices in book genres and titles than have traditionally been offered in English class and, of course, promote a love of reading.

But, as with any differentiated-instruction technique, these models do not come without their challenges. Teachers must find ways to provide just the right amount of structure to make sure that students meet the objectives of the course curriculum while also giving them the time and space they need to read with greater stamina and explore the social aspects of reading.

One way I’ve learned to support independent reading and book clubs in my classroom is by using visual resources from The New York Times and The Learning Network. From matching games to making memes, these resources provide the structure needed to propel students to new understandings while respecting their independence as readers.

Are there Times pictures, articles or other methods that you especially like to use with independent or book club reading in your classroom? Please share your ideas in the comments.

_________

Match an image to your book.

You can use images to create engaging multiple-choice questions that promote deep thinking and prompt discussion and writing. Create your own matching game by writing questions and choosing, as answers, several images that can work for any book your students are reading. Then, invite students to select the image that best represents their book club or independent reading book.

For example, using these three illustrations from “The New York Times Book Review,” you might ask students: Which of these images best fits the book you are currently reading and why?

Or, since any novel will feature some sort of problem, ask your students: Which one of these illustrations best reflects the conflict(s) in the book you are reading and why?

At times, you may even be able to write a prompt using a single image. For instance, with this image you could ask: Which one of these figures best represents the protagonist in your book and why?

You might find that these image-driven prompts can spark discussions between book groups, as well — helping students find threads that connect their book choices to the choices of others, and providing them with plenty of ideas to add to their “Read Next” lists.

You can find more intriguingly ambiguous images that will work for your students and their books in The Learning Network’s “Picture Prompt” or “What’s Going On In This Picture?” series.

_________

Create memes, inspirational posters and more.

Books frequently explore issues, conflicts, life lessons and relationships, and The Times is chock-full of images that do the same thing.

You might have your students alter images from The Times to turn them into memes, inspirational posters and comics that relate to their books. This allows students to apply their learning and tap into their creativity without consuming too much time.

Here are few activities they might try:

1. Fill in the blank space in this image with a quote from your book. Explain to your group why you chose this quote and how it relates to the image.

2. Turn this image into a meme based on your book.

3. Add a line from your book to this image to transform it into an inspirational poster.

4. Add speech or thought bubbles to this picture to show what the protagonist in your book might daydream about while stuck in traffic, or what he or she would be listening to on the radio.

The “Well,” “Smarter Living,” and “Modern Love” columns offer more excellent sources for images that relate to theme, conflict, character and relationships.

And for even more ideas for creating with images, check out our lesson plan “From Superheroes to Syrian Refugees: Teaching Comics and Graphic Novels With Resources From The New York Times.”

Sketch, map, graph and chart key moments.

Practice “reading” pictures.

One of the challenges of integrating more choice into the classroom is figuring out how to provide whole-class instruction in foundational reading skills while allowing students the space and time they need to explore their own texts.

Practicing “reading” pictures can act as a shortcut to teaching and helping students apply strategies necessary for the close readings of texts — like noticing details, finding evidence, discovering patterns and making meaning from those patterns.

This is the premise of The Learning Network’s weekly feature “What’s Going On in This Picture?” where students view an image stripped of its caption and discuss what they think is happening in it.

For instance, take this image from a recent “What’s Going On in This Picture?”:

Let your students study the image and ask them:

• What is going on in this picture?
• What do you see that makes you say that?
• What more can you find?

To take it a step further, you might next share the image split into quadrants and assign students a specific quadrant to examine.

Ask them:

• What do you see that you did not notice before?
• What do you wonder now?
• What conclusions can you draw?
• When you look at your assigned quadrant and then at the other three, what patterns do you notice?
• What might those patterns mean?

Then, invite students to ask themselves these same questions about a particular scene or chapter in their book. What patterns emerge? What conclusions can they draw about a character, plot point or conflict? Students might discuss the meaning in those patterns in a book club meeting.

For more ideas about how to use this feature, see this lesson plan or this roundup of 40 of the most intriguing images.

_________

Think metaphorically.

Metaphorical thinking can be a powerful tool to interpret a text through a new lens, and The Times provides a plethora of images that — without captions included — invite divergent thinking as students interpret and apply them to their reading.

Here are a few possible prompts that can help students think through relationships and comparisons in their books:

1. If I told you that this picture symbolized something from the book you are reading, what would I be referring to and why?

2. What might this picture represent about a relationship in your book?

For example, Julianna, a ninth-grade student who was reading “One of Us Is Lying” by Karen M. McManus, interpreted this image this way:

A girl who is really smart is in a relationship with a boy who is a drug dealer and makes bad choices in general. He perceives himself as far lower than she is and this picture shows their dynamic in the book, like a physical representation of how they relate to each other.

3. Which character might this illustration represent in your book and why?

When looking at this image, Harrison, a ninth grader, observed that in Jeff Zentner’s “The Serpent King,” Dill feels “trapped in being forced to follow his upbringing, to be a godly person, when he really feels abandoned by God.”

4. The two images below illustrate separation — a subject that resonates with many young adult books and provokes curiosity from students. Is one person standing apart by choice? Is this a scene of someone being ostracized? If so, why might that be? What is not shown in this picture?

You might ask your students how these images relate to the characters in their books. Who is marginalized? Which characters are extroverted or introverted, and how does this affect their decisions? What challenges do they face in their life among peers?

The Learning Network’s daily Picture Prompt series often includes an arresting image that it invites students to interpret. You can also look to the Opinion section and columns there like “Voices” or “Disability” to find many more photos and Op-Art that invite metaphorical thinking.

_________

Visualize literary themes and universal ideas.

Times articles and multimedia resources offer a great jumping off point for students to explore familiar literary themes and even conduct further research into how they manifest in the real world.

You might find images that relate to common themes — like overcoming adversity, dealing with peer pressure or contending with nature — and invite students to explain how these visuals illustrate themes in their books. To go further, you can have them read related articles or watch videos that help them learn more about these topics.

For example, many books have something to say about humans’ response to fear.

Students could journal about a character or idea from their book that is represented by the picture below:

To go deeper, have students read the article that goes with the image, about overcoming our primitive response to fear, or watch this documentary video about risk-taking. They might discuss the effects of social pressure on a young character facing fear, how they themselves respond when they are afraid or other connections they can make to their novels. This could create a memorable book club discussion that loops between an image, film, nonfiction and fiction to examine multiple facets of a theme.

Another way students can explore themes in their books is by doing what Times illustrators and digital artists do every day for articles across the site — render artistic representations of the essential ideas of a text.

Invite your students to illustrate the main themes and universal ideas in their own books and share them with their book groups. Refer to “The Year in Illustration” for examples and inspiration.

_________

Tell your own stories.

At least once a week, the Picture Prompt series asks students to write the opening of a short story or poem inspired by an image.

Students might use these pictures to begin their own stories inspired by the genre, plot or conflict in the novel they are reading — or they could use them to explore a character in the book they are reading by imagining him or her in a new scenario.

_________

Show your #booklove.

Since both reading workshop and book clubs emphasize student choice, these practices are also meant to promote joy in reading and #booklove.

You might share this tweet from The Learning Network editor, Katherine Schulten:

Or this panel from Grant Snider for The New York Times Book Review, which The Learning Network has used to illustrate their 2018 Summer Reading Contest.

Students can reflect on how their book clubs have contributed to their own love of reading by finding an image in The Times that describes their thoughts and feelings about their book. They might look to “The New York Times Book Review” or anywhere on The Learning Network, where these kinds of images make a regular appearance.

Ask students to consider: What do you notice in this image? What details best reflect your experience with the book you read? Which best symbolize how this book has contributed to your love of reading?

This could be an informal journal entry or an exit slip. Prompts like these convey the expectation that students grow not just in comprehension but also in their love of reading and their identities as lifelong readers.
