    ‘When I See Racial Disparities, I See Racism.’ Discussing Race, Gender and Mobility

    MARCH 27, 2018

    We wrote last week about a sweeping new study of income inequality in the United States, and how it varies by race and gender. The data, including virtually all Americans now in their late 30s, showed how different the prospects of black boys are from those of white boys. We heard from hundreds of readers, who wanted to know more about a variety of topics: how black girls and women fared; how people of other racial backgrounds compared; details of how the study was done and how its conclusions were made. They asked what caused inequality, and what they could do to address it. Below are a selection of questions from 300 that readers sent us, and responses from two of the reporters of the original article, two of the economists who did the study, and two professors who study race in America. The questions have been condensed and edited for clarity. Related Article

    We wrote last week about a sweeping new study of income inequality in the United States, and how it varies by race and gender. The data, including virtually all Americans now in their late 30s, showed how different the prospects of black boys are from those of white boys. Even when black and white boys grow up near each other, in households with similar incomes, black boys fare worse than white boys in 99 percent of America.

    We heard from hundreds of readers, who wanted to know more about a variety of topics: how black girls and women fared; how people of other racial backgrounds compared; details of how the study was done and how its conclusions were drawn. Readers asked what caused inequality, and what they could do to address it in their own lives and in society.

    Below are some of the 300 questions readers sent us, with responses from two of the reporters of the original article, two of the economists who conducted the study, and two professors who study race in America. The questions have been condensed and edited for clarity. We also created additional data visualizations here that tell you more about women and people of different racial groups and income levels, and a tool that lets you make these animations for any combination of race, gender, income type and household income level you choose.

    Causes of Inequality

    +

    What are the common factors among black boys who stay rich or are upwardly mobile versus those who become poor or incarcerated or who stay poor?

    Priti Irani, Niskayuna, N.Y.

    The researchers found that the neighborhoods where poor black boys did well, and as well as their white peers, shared three factors: less discrimination, low poverty rates and, among low-income black families, a larger share of fathers present. (The fathers might serve as role models, or they could be a sign that these neighborhoods have other qualities that benefit families, like job opportunities and low incarceration rates.) There are few such neighborhoods in the United States, however. The research shows that neighborhoods with higher test scores and lower poverty rates make a difference for all children, whether rich or poor.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot

    There is a limit to what we know about the children in this study. The data comes from anonymous government records like tax returns and census questions, which means we know where these children were living, how much their parents earned and how they self-identified their race, among other things. But we don’t have answers to other questions readers have raised, like: Did these children have many books at home? Did they attend public or private school? Were they in mentoring programs? So we know that certain kinds of neighborhoods matter, and black boys earn slightly more in adulthood if they have two parents rather than one. But we can’t speak to some of the other differences that may separate children over the course of their lives.

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot
    +

    Why is racism the only explanation for this phenomenon? Perhaps something happens to black boys while they are growing up that makes them less capable of succeeding in the U.S. economy. For instance, maybe cultural forces cause black boys — but not black girls — to focus on activities that leave them with poorer time management skills than those that boys of other races are developing. So, why do the authors take the easy way out and blame amorphous racism instead of exploring more subtle explanations that do not make the situation seem hopeless?

    Michael Sundel, Falls Church, Va.

    Actually, the easy way out is to say there must be something wrong with these black boys. It is the easy way out that Americans have historically taken in trying to explain racial disparities in our society since the founding of the United States. Either there is something wrong with our policies, or there is something wrong with black boys (or black people). Either the United States is riddled with racist policies or inferior black boys. We have all sorts of evidence of racist policies. Where is the evidence that black boys as a group have “poorer time management skills” than white boys as a group? Personal observations of individual behavior is not evidence of group behavior. Racist ideas of black inferiority is the easy way out.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center

    It’s hard to conclude from this study that the problem here is “culture” for two reasons: Girls don’t appear to face the same racial disparities in income as boys, and boys face these disparities whether they’re raised poor or rich, by two parents or one. If culture were the primary driver here, you’d have to argue that boys and girls raised in the same family are exposed to fundamentally different cultures. You’d have to argue that rich black boys raised by married, college-educated parents in wealthy neighborhoods experience the same culture as poor black boys raised by single mothers in poor neighborhoods.

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot

    Pointing to racism as the problem is not the same as suggesting that this is a hopeless situation. If we start honestly teaching about our racist history and make meaningful efforts to eradicate racist policies and practices, then this problem could be resolved. It took us hundreds of years of very concerted effort to have racism so ingrained in our culture, and we will have to work equally hard to undo all of the damage we have done as a society.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia
    +

    Does the study take into account the inherited wealth of white boys? It seems that black parents raising boys in high-income brackets spend their salaries trying to stay in the good neighborhoods, so their boys have to basically start from the bottom again, despite previous opportunity, whereas white boys from affluent families have investments or money from their families that help keep them in the same economic circumstances.

    Cara, Houston

    We use information on parental home ownership, home value, home equity and vehicle ownership to evaluate whether differences in parental wealth — for children in families with similar parental income — explain the black-white income gap. We find that these proxies for wealth do not further close the gap, and in particular, large gaps remain for boys. This is not to say that wealth does not matter for children’s outcomes. Parents with higher incomes also have greater wealth, and children in those families generally grow up to have higher incomes. Our point is that controlling for the key sources of wealth that most low- and middle-income families have doesn’t explain the black-white mobility gap.

    — The Equality of Opportunity Project researchers

    White parents raising white boys in high-income brackets spend their salaries trying to stay in (and move up into) what they consider to be “good neighborhoods,” too. And the greater inherited wealth of white boys — which is due to discrimination — explains why white boys may financially supersede black boys of the same income level. But it does not explain why black boys raised rich “are more likely to become poor than to stay wealthy in their own adult households,” as The Times reported.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center

    Comparing Women and Men

    +

    It appears that the trajectories for black men, white women and black women are very similar, and only white men have an advantage. Is that accurate?

    Jessica Mandrick, New York, N.Y.

    Some readers have asked why we didn’t write a story about white male privilege — instead of the obstacles faced by black men — given that black men, white women and black women raised at the same income level have similar incomes. If we pull out and look across the whole data set, Asian-American men have higher adult incomes than white men. Hispanic men fare relatively similarly to white men. And incomes between Hispanics and whites will converge in about two generations if these mobility patterns stay the same. That is simply not true of the gap between African-Americans and whites.

    In other words, one of the real outliers in this data was black men, not white men. It is no doubt true that the gap between them is driven by both the advantages white men receive and the disadvantages black men face. But where you place the emphasis matters: You wind up with very different policy responses if you define this problem as one of white male privilege instead of one of structural obstacles surrounding black men.

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot

    It’s a great observation and an interesting way to look at the data. I thought that Noah Smith, a columnist at Bloomberg View, made interesting observations about this on Twitter -- he blamed it on the old boys’ club. “My hypothesis is that white men make more, even after adjusting for a bunch of other factors, because of a self-perpetuating good-ol’-boy network of white men in companies and professions, who award each other money due to affinity,” one of his tweets said.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot

    This is an excellent question. There are certainly gender differences in addition to differences across races (as is well known from prior work). And you are right that black men, white women and black women have similar individual incomes if they grow up in families that make the same amount of money; in contrast, incomes of white men are higher. Put differently, black males make as much as black females, while white males earn more than white females in individual incomes. The reason we focus on black men in particular is that they look distinct on many dimensions when we look beyond simple measures of income. Black men have much lower employment rates, lower high school completion rates and higher incarceration rates than black women and white men or women. This is not to minimize gender equity issues, of course — there is a large body of research studying factors that may lead to pay inequity and differences in career choices, work hours, etc., that we do not speak to in this study. But, in thinking about racial disparities, it does seem like there are a unique set of challenges for black men.

    — The Equality of Opportunity Project researchers
    +

    Could the lack of a significant income gap between black and white women from the same background be explained in part by the fact that black women are less likely to be married than white women and thus more likely to bear the majority (or all) of the financial burden for a household?

    James Melican, Melrose, Mass.

    This research compares the adult incomes of African-Americans and whites in two ways: at the individual level and the household level. Black and white women raised by families with comparable resources have similar incomes in adulthood at the individual level, if we compare those women just to each other. But white women are more likely to get married, and so they’re more likely to be part of a family with a second income, and thus have higher household incomes. It’s important to look at both of these measures, because they tell us different things. Individual income tells us that black and white women fare similarly as workers in the labor market. Household income tells us that white women fare better in the total resources they have at home.

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot

    Absolutely, great question. One could imagine that black women are obtaining higher incomes because they work longer hours or in different types of jobs. We explore this in the study, and surprisingly, we find that black and white women (who grow up in families that make the same amount of money) have similar wages, hours, employment rates, and work in similar occupations. But it is important to note that these similarities are for black and white children whose parents make the same amount of money. Since black children tend to grow up in families with lower parent incomes, the average incomes of black females is lower than the average incomes of white females.

    — The Equality of Opportunity Project researchers
    +

    Could we see more data on black girls, or clues as to why there is such a difference in their outcomes as compared to boys?

    Jamie Pfaff, Winnetka, Ill.

    We’ve created an expanded version of some of the graphics that ran with the original story. They let you compare data by gender, race, income and other factors.

    Previous research provides us with some clues as to why these gender disparities exist. A variety of studies have shown that boys are more affected than girls by disadvantage, such as growing up in poverty or facing discrimination. Even brothers and sisters in the same households fare differently in response to disadvantage — boys perform worse at school and get into more trouble. It’s unclear exactly why, though researchers have some ideas and continue to investigate it.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot

    It is worth noting that this study is focusing on just one outcome: income. Although this may be a dimension where black women are not as negatively affected as black men by discrimination, this doesn’t mean that discrimination is not undermining the well-being of black women in other very significant ways. One example is birth outcomes.

    Intersections between race and gender most likely lead to variation and nuance in the ways in which black men and women experience discrimination, and the negative consequences of it. Negative stereotypes about black men that link them to criminality and violence most likely explain the income findings for multiple reasons. Teachers tend to perceive black boys as more threatening and react more harshly and punitively toward them. Black boys have greater involvement with the criminal justice system and receive unfair trials and harsher sentences. That makes it legal for employers to discriminate against them based on their criminal records. The sad thing is that even black men who have not been involved with the criminal justice system are characterized as criminal by potential employers. Obviously, employment discrimination affects whether black men are hired and what they have the opportunity to earn.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia

    I would love to see more economic data and studies comparing black and white girls, too, particularly a longitudinal study focusing on wealth. Wealth is harder to assess than income, but we cannot let difficulty stop our research. I am hoping this study not only triggers more research into the discrimination affecting black boys — the causes of these disparities — but just as importantly triggers large studies comparatively studying the lives of black girls and white girls. I am hoping that this study does not trigger the false generalization that black boys have it worse than black girls, the false idea that black girls are not facing racism, too.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center

    Addressing Inequality and Racism

    +

    I am a mother of two black boys living in relative affluence in the suburbs of Philadelphia. What can I do to protect my children from this unrelenting toxic racism? My husband and I grew up in majority black countries and have no idea of the severity of our boys’ experiences with racism. Are there any evidence-based measures we can take? Thank you.

    Mimi Tessema, Dresher, Pa.

    Equip your children with facts about the racist history of our society and ongoing racism, so black boys don’t blame themselves for any unfair treatment they receive. Research has shown that youth who are aware of racial discrimination and whose parents have prepared them tend to be less negatively affected when it happens.

    When teaching children about racism, it also is incredibly important to highlight all of the ways in which blacks have resisted mistreatment and persisted in the face of adversity. This includes learning about black heroes and heroines. Racial pride has been associated with better academic and mental health outcomes, and can protect black boys from some of the harmful effects of discrimination.

    Parents can support their boys in identifying black male role models, especially in career domains that align with youths’ interests. Building mentoring relationships with positive black men in youths’ everyday lives can ensure that black boys are getting the advice and support they need to succeed.

    Parents also can get involved with their children’s schools and become members of local school boards, where they can identify discriminatory disciplinary practices and academic outcomes.

    This is my best parenting advice, but I want to stress that our attention should not be limited to helping black boys succeed against the odds. Rather, our focus should include changing the odds -- which means collectively working to eradicate racism from our society.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia

    This study suggests you can’t control everything that will affect your child’s prospects at home. Much of what matters lies outside your home — in your neighborhood, in your local schools, in the social networks and opportunity structures in your community. One family or parent can’t control all those things. But if you have the luxury of choosing where to live (and many people don’t), you can think about these factors in deciding where to raise children. Sheryll Cashin, a law professor at Georgetown, responded to this study with advice on Twitter along these lines, from her own life: “The key message here is that black boys are more likely to experience racism, and black parents with choices must choose neighborhoods carefully!”

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot

    It is nearly impossible to protect your boys against living and breathing racist policies. But those policies can be wounded or killed, especially at the local level. You can use your resources to support researchers and organizations that are uncovering racial disparities in your community, that are discovering the discriminatory policies behind those disparities and that are working to eliminate those policies and replace them with anti-racist policies of equal opportunity. A racist policy yields racial disparities. An anti-racist policy reduces or eliminates racial disparities. Anti-racist policies can protect your black boys.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center

    In my interviews for this story, one thing several researchers mentioned was the importance of black male mentors -- adults children could confide in and ask for advice, and who could talk with them about their experiences being black men in the United States. Another thing was the importance of being employed -- though as we discussed in the article, that can be more difficult because of racism. I thought Nikole Hannah-Jones, who writes about race for The New York Times Magazine, wrote powerfully about this in a Twitter thread about the original article. She wrote that one thing “black people of all incomes -- and particularly high income -- teach their children is that if they want to be successful it is not enough to be as good as white people. They must be better. They must expect less and work more. This is our culture.”

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot
    +

    What can be done by a single citizen to improve the lives of the disadvantaged black boys?

    Paul Bauer, Seattle

    Ultimately, the problem is a societal one and not an individual one, so solutions will have to be at the systemic level — like changing public policies and practices and policies within schools and law enforcement agencies. It is a mistake to think that there is any one thing a person can do at the individual level to sidestep the consequences of living in a racist society. But individuals can do some things. For example, they can pressure their employers to make sure hiring committees receive training in identifying and managing their own explicit and implicit biases. I urge you to read Michelle Alexander’s “The New Jim Crow” to learn about all of the ways in which the criminal justice system is a racist institution.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia
    +

    What does society do with that data to fix the dire problem stated? A whole group of individuals have little to no chance of “making it” even with the best of resources, attention, etc. The data does not suggest that there is a future date when this cycle will end. With no hope, there is no future. However, I do have hope that the systemic racism of white supremacy will come tumbling down, by any means necessary.

    Marvalee Leslie, NY

    I think there is some hope in this study. Some black boys -- albeit far fewer than would be expected on a level playing field -- do achieve and sustain financial success. The research does point to some specific communities where black boys do well, and as well as the white boys who grow up there. These neighborhoods are rare, and that part is discouraging. But the existence of such places gives researchers, policymakers and communities a concrete agenda: Can we better understand what’s happening in these places? What do these places have that other communities don’t? How can we replicate what’s going on there in more places?

    — Emily Badger, Reporter, The Upshot

    Researchers we interviewed for this story had some ideas for policy makers. To start, the study makes it clear that anti-poverty programs are not enough to address the disparities. Policies should take into account gender and race, across geography and income, and the fact that disadvantages affect people in different ways. Examples of anti-racist policies are the decriminalization of nonviolent crimes; job programs for black neighborhoods; money for schools and other institutions; and public health initiatives aimed at black people.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot

    I also have hope, but I know that dismantling white supremacy will take dedication and time. I see this study and others like it as helpful to the journey. Better understanding the nature of the problem will put us in a better position to address it.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia

    How the Research Was Done

    +

    I think among the public at large, we are skeptical when it comes to academic research such as this. One wonders: Did the authors start with a conclusion and then find the statistics to support their findings? I looked up the biographies of some of the authors who were part of the study and I think it’s fair to say they would call themselves progressives. Does that political affiliation impact the accuracy of this study’s findings? If the researchers identified as conservative, would we have the same results? If our political beliefs shape our opinions, can they also shape research? I would say probably.

    Bill Brown, Oakland, Calif.

    We pride ourselves on learning from the data and doing our best to let the data drive our questions, analyses and results using a scientific approach. Many of our results have previously been cited by people on both sides of the political spectrum.

    — The Equality of Opportunity Project researchers

    It seems to me that who you call the “public at large” is not skeptical about the actual racial disparities that the researchers found. From my reading, the critics of the study have a problem with the conclusions derived from the data on racial disparities. That is where one’s racial ideology, or what you call “political affiliation,” most comes into play. It is not about liberal or conservative. The political divide is anti-racist and racist. Anti-racists express racial equality. So when they see studies like this that showcase racial disparities between black boys and white boys, they look for racism, and what is wrong with our policies. Racists express racial hierarchy. So when they see studies finding racial disparities, they articulate racist ideas, and claim without evidence there must be something wrong or inferior about black boys or their black parents or their black culture and on and on.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center
    +

    In the research, were subpopulations broken out — children of sub-Saharan parents, black parents who had immigrated from elsewhere, biracial couples, U.S.-born African-Americans? These variables seem crucial in understanding the complexity of this situation and in understanding the intersectionality of whatever is going on. It is all too easy for reporting on such research to replicate the black/white binary in ways that can be destructive to popular and academic understandings of our social reality.

    Keith Walters, Portland, Ore.

    We did not break each race out into its respective subpopulations in this project, but this is certainly a valuable direction for future research.

    — The Equality of Opportunity Project researchers

    How the Article Was Made

    +

    As a black male (who is also a lawyer), these statistics demonstrate the obvious. However, many readers’ comments, to my horror, suggest that the findings of this article do not necessarily correlate with the existence of racism specifically geared toward black men. In fact, many readers’ comments suggest that the dilemma of black boys was their own fault or the fault of their communities. However, the title of The Times article states otherwise. Why did The Times decide to entitle the article “Extensive Data Shows Punishing Reach of Racism for Black Boys”? Was doing so an editorial license not supported by data, or does the data actually demonstrate racism? If so, exactly how? Were the journalists surprised by the number of comments that reflect the belief that racism is not a major factor in the lives of black men?

    Antar Jones, Brooklyn, N.Y.

    Our headline, and the story’s conclusion that racism plays a big role, was absolutely supported by the data. The researchers tested many other theories about the causes of inequality, and found that the gaps between black and white boys could not be fully explained by family income, family structure, education or accumulated wealth. However, they did find that discrimination — as measured by surveys and tests of racial bias — was correlated in low-poverty areas with how well black boys fared. As a reporter on this piece, I think this data, along with stories like yours, are important for making clear the role that racism plays in our society and in people’s lives.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot

    As an anti-racist, when I see racial disparities, I see racism. But I know for many racist Americans, when they see racial disparities they see black inferiority. So I was not surprised in the least by the number of comments claiming racism is not a major factor in the lives of black males. So many of our neighbors are unfortunately still living in their post-racial fantasy world. Let’s hope this study thrusts some of them into the racist real world.

    — Ibram X. Kendi, History and international relations professor, American University, and director, The Antiracist Research and Policy Center

    It seems there are also political motivations for the racism deniers. Some people are invested in denying racism and insinuating that these findings must be due to personal shortcomings of black men because this possibility means that we, as a society, are not responsible for doing anything to address our racist past or present. Denying racism gives everyone a pass to engage in business as usual and blame the horrible inequalities we see on individual shortcomings as opposed to racist policies and practices.

    — Noelle Hurd, Psychology assistant professor, University of Virginia
    +

    How were those beautiful animated data displays created?

    Thomas Chong, Los Angeles, Calif.

    Thank you for the kind words! The short answer to your question is that the visualizations were created with D3, a popular javascript library that makes some aspects of data visualization easier, and WebGL, a technology that, among other things, lets us draw thousands of points on your phone without overheating its little circuits. That said, it’s important to note that any specific technology – a new language, code library or plugin – is almost never the reason for any project’s success. We use whatever tools and technology best suit the story we want to tell. We made additional visualizations of this data, including one that allows you to enter variables for things like race, gender and income and see how differently life plays out for different groups of people.

    — Claire Cain Miller, Reporter, The Upshot
