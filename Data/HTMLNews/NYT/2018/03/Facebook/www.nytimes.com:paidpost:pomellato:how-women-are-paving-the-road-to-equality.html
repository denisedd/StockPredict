    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY POMELLATO

    Looking Back, Striding Forward

    How women are paving the road to equality

    As we mark International Women’s Day, the Milanese jeweler Pomellato is tracing down milestones in women’s rights through the years, from the explosion of the women’s movement in the late 1960s, when the brand was founded, until today. These touchpoints in the ongoing flowering of women’s rights are the foundation on which women around the world are building the road to true equality, opportunity and fulfillment.

    The women’s liberation movement has seen shining progress since the 1960s, when women couldn't get a credit card without a husband to cosign for them. From the heady origins of the women’s liberation movement half a century ago to today’s worldwide viral phenomena #MeToo and #TimesUp, we look back at women’s achievements over two centuries and the progress that has already been made, and draw inspiration for the hopes and dreams of the future.

    Scroll

    Early 20th century

    Punctuated by two world wars, the Great Depression and a subsequent economic boom, the first half of the 20th century sees the birth of new social orders and the rise of the feminist movement. Big progress is made in women’s suffrage, with the founding of the International Woman Suffrage Alliance in Berlin in 1904. Following British women over 30 years of age and Canadian women gaining voting rights in 1918, American women win suffrage at a national level in 1920. Key European countries grant suffrage for women during the 1940s, partly due to their crucial contribution in the work force during World War II. French women gained this right in 1945 while Italian women won suffrage a year later. Meanwhile, Eleanor Roosevelt is elected the head of the U.N. Human Rights Commission and becomes instrumental in drafting the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Women also start gaining recognition in science and sports, with Marie Curie being awarded the Nobel Prize for Chemistry in 1911 and women competing for the first time in Olympic events in 1900.

    1967

    The year 1967 remains a landmark moment as an international bill of rights for women is introduced for the first time in history. The U.N. Declaration on the Elimination of Discrimination Against Women is adopted, stating that discrimination against women is fundamentally unjust and constitutes an offense against human dignity. It set out equal rights for women in education, civil law, electoral politics and the workplace, among other articles.

    1971

    The journalist and political activist Gloria Steinem founds the National Women's Political Caucus, at an organizing conference attended by 320 women from 26 countries, including Bella Abzug, Betty Friedan, Shirley Chisholm and Myrlie Evers-Williams, supporting women seeking office at all levels of government. In the same year in Switzerland, women were just being given the right to vote. Said Steinem, founder of Ms. magazine, of the women’s movement: “This is no simple reform. It really is a revolution.”

    1973

    The monumental Roe v. Wade decision by the Supreme Court rules that a woman has a constitutional right to abortion, prompting a national debate that continues globally to this day. Meanwhile, the tennis champion Billie Jean King wins the “battle of the sexes” tennis match against the former No. 1 ranked player Bobby Riggs. This highly publicized event galavanizes the movement for equal rights and opportunities for female athletes.

    1975

    In France, the Veil Act, named after the health minister Simone Veil, is adopted in January, guaranteeing all pregnant women “whose condition puts her in a situation of distress” the right to terminate her pregnancy. Meanwhile, the U.N. proclaims 1975 as International Women’s Year, with the first global U.N. Women’s Conference held in Mexico City. Ever since, International Women’s Day has been celebrated on March 8.

    1988

    Benazir Bhutto becomes the first woman to lead a Muslim country in modern history and is elected prime minister of Pakistan. Said Bhutto of her career: “As a woman leader, I thought I brought a different kind of leadership. I was interested in women's issues, in bringing down the population growth rate...as a woman, I entered politics with an additional dimension — that of a mother.”

    1993

    For the first time in Britain, Trade Union Reform and Employment Rights Act guarantees every working woman the right to maternity leave. By the 1990s, 43 percent of women with children under five were working in Britain, compared with only 25 percent in 1973. On the other side of the pond, 58 percent of all American women over 16 were in the work force in 1993. And today, the global work force includes more women than ever before, with nearly three-quarters of mothers with children in the U.K. working full or part time.

    2000

    In the aim to improve the conditions of the world’s poorest countries by year 2015, the U.N. adopts the Millennium Development Goals. Among its key pillars are promoting gender equality and empowering women. Observed Johanna Sigurdardottir, former prime minister of Iceland, of the MDGs: “Women bear a heavier burden of the world’s poverty than men because of the discrimination they face in education, health care, employment and control of assets.”

    2010

    Kathryn Bigelow becomes the first woman to win an Oscar for Best Director for her feature film “The Hurt Locker,” which also earned a Best Film award. The actress Jessica Chastain, who appeared in Bigelow’s “Zero Dark Thirty,” said of the director: “You’ve said that filmmaking for you is not about breaking gender roles, but when you make a film that allows your character to disobey the conventions of Hollywood, you’ve done more for women in cinema than you take credit for.”

    2014

    At the tender age of 17, Malala Yousafzai, who as a young Pakistani teenager was shot in the head by the Taliban for her activism around girls’ education, is awarded the Nobel Peace Prize. She shared the prize with Kailash Satyarthi for their work against the suppression of children and young people, and for the right of all children to go to school. “I don't want to be thought of as the ‘girl who was shot by the Taliban,’” she has said, “but the ‘girl who fought for education.’ This is the cause to which I want to devote my life.”

    2018

    The #MeToo hashtag is created on social media as a way for women to share their experiences of sexual harassment. The moment rapidly swells to a movement, with millions of women posting their stories online. Within 24 hours, the hashtag is used on Facebook by more than 4.7 million people in 12 million posts. #MeToo has since inspired the #TimesUp movement, which aims to fight workplace harassment and sexual violence. It has already raised a $20 million legal defense fund, with more support coming from the entertainment world, global corporations and NGOs worldwide.
