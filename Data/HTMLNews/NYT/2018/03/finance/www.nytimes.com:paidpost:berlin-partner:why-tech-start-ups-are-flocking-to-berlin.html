    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY BERLIN PARTNER

    Why
    Tech Start-Ups     Are Flocking to Berlin

    Entrepreneurs from all over the world are moving to the German capital for its large talent pool, easy access to financing, spirit of innovation and inimitable way of life.

    Some of Berlin’s biggest modern redevelopment projects can be seen in and around Potsdamer Platz in central Berlin, a bustling public square and traffic intersection.

    Despite an impressive résumé, including two companies he had helped build from the ground up, Csaba Tamas sensed something was missing from his life. “I had the feeling I was losing my best years,” says the Hungarian entrepreneur, who successfully launched the Romanian subsidiary of the Austrian automation firm Keba Group. “There are areas in the world where things are happening at a faster pace. In the U.S., it’s Silicon Valley. In Europe, that would be Berlin.” In 2016, Tamas and his fiancée moved to Berlin.

    500

    new tech start-ups per year

    They weren’t the only ones. In 2016, Berlin added over 50,000 people to its population, which is now at 3.7 million. Nearly a fifth of this population is foreign, and the share is rising: of the 18,000 people who arrived in the first half of last year, 13,500 were from outside the country. Many, like Tamas, are attracted by the city’s cosmopolitan character, high standard of living and, of course, burgeoning start-up sector. Of the 1,837 tech companies counted in the 2017 German Startup Monitor, 350 were based in Berlin, and nearly 15 percent of all founders were foreign.

    Source: “Current Population,” Federal Statistical Office of Germany; “Population Register Statistics Berlin,” Office of Statistics Berlin-Brandenburg; “Deutscher Startup Monitor 2017,” German Startup Monitor

    Part One

    Speaking German, English
    — and Start-Up

    What’s bringing them to this city? For one, generous financing opportunities. Start-ups in Berlin absorbed over two-thirds of all financing in Germany last year, raising a total of €2.97 billion ($3.65 billion) — around $300 million more than London. This, coupled with the relatively low-cost but high standard of living and good public services, is attracting talent from all over the world. Human resource costs in Berlin are roughly 40 percent lower on average than in other German cities. Berlin’s location at the center of Europe, its affordable real estate (the average office rent in 2016 was €16.30 per square meter), proximity to political decision-makers and network of research institutions also adds to the attraction.

    “It’s a great place to be based for
    attracting talent, which is most start-ups’ biggest challenge.”

    — Claire Novorol, co-founder, Ada
    As one of the capital's largest transport hubs, Alexanderplatz, in the Mitte district in central Berlin, includes many historic buildings like the Rotes Rathaus, the city’s town hall.

    Berlin’s state government has helped, too, introducing a number of measures to attract entrepreneurs, especially in tech. The state-owned Berlin Investment Bank offers funding programs; municipal services that help business owners navigate bureaucracy are available in English; and the state’s business promotion agency, Berlin Partner, offers foreign entrepreneurs support in anything from finding the right office to sorting out visas for staff and jobs for spouses.

    $3.65

    billion in investment in Berlin start-ups in 2017

    Tamas was also eager to join the start-up fray. Once he arrived in Berlin, he quickly applied for a job as CTO-in-residence at FinLeap, a company builder for ventures in the fintech sector. “When I arrived, I was walking around the neighborhood and every second person was talking about the app,” he says. “It was a visible vibe on the street. Here, start-ups are everywhere!” In a matter of months, FinLeap connected Tamas with two would-be founders in search of a CTO, and together they set up FinReach, which helps banks digitize their services. Today, some of the largest banks in Germany, Austria and Spain use its services, including Deutsche Bank and Postbank. Of its 30-strong team, 25 come from an international background and experience.

    Step 5

    Notify the authorities

    U.S. citizens can enter Germany without a visa. If you then want to go into business for yourself in Berlin, apply for a residence permit within 90 days at the Foreigners' Registration Office.

    You can also apply for a visa at the German embassy in your home country. There, you can determine whether you will receive a corresponding work permit for going into business for yourself in Germany before entering the country.

    There are numerous organizations and services that help foreigners set up businesses in Berlin.

    • The “Welcome Berlin” app answers all-important questions about working and living in Berlin: app.welcome.berlin

    • Berlin’s Chamber of Commerce has a website in English, a founders’ manual and a “Business Welcome Service” with personal contacts for entrepreneurs.

    • Berlin’s Business Location Center offers detailed advice to foreign entrepreneurs and business owners as well as help in finding a location, investments, talent and trade networks.

    • The Reason-Why Berlin website features start-up success stories while also providing useful services and real-life tips on doing business and living in Berlin.

    The legal status of your business has far-reaching implications in terms of tax and regulations, so choose it carefully. Again, the above services and organizations can help you choose the right one. Some examples:

    • If you start up your business alone, it will automatically be a sole proprietorship.

    • A partnership constituted under civil law (GbR) is the simplest form of start-up where a team of people is involved. It forms automatically when individuals collaborate to pursue a common commercial goal, for instance, by preparing a business plan in order to go into business together.

    • A limited liability company (GmbH) is the most popular legal status in Germany after sole proprietorship. This is because liability is restricted to the level of company assets. The minimum share capital is €25,000.

    Berlin is the second-largest city in Europe and governed through 12 districts that vary widely in their strengths and style, so the location of your business matters.

    Do you want to be in the buzzing center of the international tech crowd? Get an office in Mitte or Kreuzberg. Do you need access to specialized high-tech companies and research institutions? Set up shop in the tech city Adlershof. Or do you prefer a mature environment with an aura of respectability? Then Charlottenburg might be for you.

    Scout the city, research businesses and speak to residents before you make your choice. Search your ideal location via the Real Estate Portal of the Business Location Center website.

    If your business is a corporation, register it with Berlin’s Commercial Register. Also, notify the regulatory agency (Ordnungsamt) in the district where it will be based. (If several people wish to run a business together, they must all attend together.) Afterward, the tax authorities will find you automatically.

    And that’s it — you’re good to go!

    Part Two

    Foreign Talent,
    Local Excellence

    The abundance of international talent also prompted the health start-up Ada to establish its headquarters in Berlin. Claire Novorol, its co-founder, splits her time between Germany and the United Kingdom, where she used to practice as an M.D. But together with her German co-founders, she picked Berlin as the company’s base. “It’s much more affordable to live in than London, San Francisco or New York,” she explains. “So we can attract talent more affordably than we could if we had our HQ in those cities.”

    Novorol expects the talent pool to become even bigger. “Berlin is very international,” she says. “It attracts a lot of enthusiastic, energetic people who are interested in making a difference, having an impact.” Ada also benefits from its close association with Berlin’s Charité, one of Europe’s largest university hospitals. “We have close ties with Charité and continually attract great, talented doctors from there,” she adds.

    The proximity to these top research institutions and talent is also what attracted the Russian laser optics specialist Viacheslav Artyushenko to Berlin. He set up the first collaboration between the Soviet Academy of Sciences and the West Berlin Laser-Medizin-Zentrum in 1989. In Berlin, Artyushenko was able to assemble a team of specialists from all over the world.

    In 1998, he founded Art Photonics, a company offering broad-spectrum fiber-optical solutions, in Adlershof, a technology park housing over 1,000 companies and 16 research institutions. “In Adlershof, there is a good chance to work with partners and customers directly,” Artyushenko says. “Our latest project was with the Technical University and Charité, in cancer diagnosis.”

    Q&A with Ijad Madisch, founder, ResearchGate

    • You founded ResearchGate in Boston, but decided to move it to Berlin. Why?

      In 2010, I spoke with Matt Cohler, who was an early investor in ResearchGate and one of our most important advisers, about where ResearchGate should have its headquarters. I wanted to go back home to Germany to work with my friends, and that’s why we agreed on Berlin. Back then, there were a handful of e-commerce start-ups, plus the incredible cultural scene that attracted people from around the world to the city.

    • What has been your experience with running an international start-up from Berlin?

      Berlin was the right decision for us. Sure, we’re far away from our investors and, especially in the beginning, things might have been easier if we had been closer to them. But what we needed most was a diverse and talented team to create a network that’s truly useful for researchers worldwide. We found these people here in Berlin — and I doubt you’d find them anywhere else. Our team is now made up of almost 300 people from 43 countries.

    • You continue to work mostly with U.S. investors as well as the U.S. market. Is American business culture compatible with German laws and Berlin’s way of life?

      We’re making the most of all three worlds. We’re a mission-driven business. We connect the world of science and open it up for everyone. Of course, we make money, but we do it by changing science for the better. That’s something German investors had a hard time wrapping their heads around. Our investors, who happen to be American, understand our vision. German laws haven’t held us back so far, and the Berlin way of life is an inspiration to our team. Almost everyone here is also involved in the city’s lively arts, culture and sports scene.

    The connection to institutions of higher learning is beneficial in other ways, too. “It’s a good source of students,” says Artyushenko. “We currently have one Ph.D. student working for our company who is writing his dissertation under the leadership of Professor Eichler, who is a kind of guru in laser technology in Germany.”

    “I see a good advantage for Europe in general and Berlin in particular. There are no borders for science and technology.”

    — Viacheslav Artyushenko, laser optics specialist, Art Photonics

    Part Three

    The Right Place
    to Succeed

    A year and a half into his life in Berlin’s start-up world, Tamas says the German capital is a great place to do business. Lower costs for talent are a clear advantage, he says, as is Germany’s brand. “‘Made in Germany’ must not be underestimated,” he says. “It gives you higher credibility. And this has a very, very good influence on sales.”

    Q&A with Osnat Michaeli, co-founder, Infarm

    • Why did you move from Israel to Berlin to set up your urban farming business?

      Berlin is a hub of innovation and home to an expanding start-up scene. The city draws incredible talent, many diverse skills, people from all over the world. So in terms of building a team, this was a great place for us to start.

      Germany also has a long tradition of quality manufacturing and processes that pushed us forward to develop a technology and a business model that can create real impact. And Berlin was the only place where we knew we could take the time and invest in R&D.

      I find Berlin is the most interesting, inspiring and exciting city in the world right now, with passionate and motivated people from all over the world who, together, make this a vibrant place. And you can feel this excitement, innovation and motivation anywhere you go.

    • Did you find it complicated to set up a company in Berlin?

      German bureaucracy is quite complex but, on the other hand, it’s very straightforward. We were very fortunate to meet people who helped us with everything we needed, and the government offers a lot of support to immigrants with productive ideas.

    • Looking back, has being in Berlin benefited your business in ways you didn’t expect?

      Definitely. First, I see the critical thinking of people here. People are not afraid to ask very challenging questions, and that helped us manifest our vision and define our strategy to create solutions.

      Also, the patience, the time that we could buy — I only now can appreciate how precious this time was. If we had established this business elsewhere, we may have had to make compromises due to economic pressures and dilute our vision or compromise on the quality of our product. But we didn’t need to do any of that. On the contrary — we had the chance to constantly refine and do better. And that’s something that really defines the identity of Infarm.

    But, Tamas adds, its cosmopolitan qualities are even more important. “German is not an easy language, but in Berlin you can survive quite well without it,” he says. “That really attracts people to Berlin: that there is an international community. Even Germans in Berlin are attracted by the international air, so they are more open, more liberal. And the spirit of innovation is strongly connected to liberal thinking.”

    This means that new ideas are often tried out first in the German capital. “Do you know the Malcolm Gladwell book ‘Outliers’?” says Tamas. “If you want to succeed, be in the right place — and that’s what Berlin is.”
