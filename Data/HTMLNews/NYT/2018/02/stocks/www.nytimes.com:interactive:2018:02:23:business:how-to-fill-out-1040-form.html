    Get to Know the New Tax Code While Filling Out This Year's 1040

    By JAN M. ROSEN FEB. 23, 2018

    Filling out tax forms may not be your idea of a great time. But if you want to learn how the new tax law will affect your personal finances, this year’s dreaded Form 1040 is a good place to start.

    Your current return, like this sample one prepared in consultation with Bob Meighan, a vice president of TurboTax, must follow the 2017 tax-year rules. But big changes will be in store for 2018 returns, and advance planning can help, whether you use software, as we did, fill out your form by hand or have a professional do it.

    Thanks to lower rates and a doubled standard deduction and other breaks, 2018 taxes will fall for many people. That appears to be true for this hypothetical couple in suburban New York, Samuel and Felicity Taxpayer.

    Our Hypothetical Family

    Dual Income

    Both Samuel and Felicity earn income, she as an employee of a design firm and he as a self-employed engineering consultant.

    5 Personal Exemptions

    They have three dependents: two children and an elderly parent who lives with them. Including the two of them, they have five personal exemptions.

    25% Bracket Now, 22% in 2018

    Their total income for 2017 was $183,911, but after deductions, their taxable income is $88,293. In 2018, it probably would be $116,097, minus a big deduction for consulting income and other breaks that reduce it to $98,206.

    Invested in Stocks, Bonds

    They have a small portfolio that generated less than $1,500 in interest and dividends so they don’t have to file Schedule B. But they sold some stock and used Schedule D. These “schedules” are supplements to the 1040.

    College Tuition

    Their son goes to college, qualifying them for a tax credit.

    Mortgage and Local Taxes

    They pay a mortgage because they own a house and their local and state taxes are high. That gives them big deductions in 2017. Under rules for 2018 returns, those deductions will be curtailed.

    2018’s Bottom Line: While TurboTax's "What-If Worksheet" indicates that the family would owe $3,896 more in taxes under the new law, I.R.S. guidance is still in flux and TurboTax did not include a sizable deduction for consulting income for which the couple is likely to qualify. That deduction would probably result in a small net decrease in their tax bill next year.

    While many people are eligible for the EZ filing method, the process can be more arduous for those with more complicated finances. Here is a guide to the current 1040 — with clues about the changes next year — using the Taxpayer family as an example.

    Getting Started

    First, gather your records and the official documents related to your income and deductions, because Form 1040 asks a taxpayer to list income. Then it factors in adjustments, deductions and personal exemptions to determine how much of the income is taxable.

    The Internal Revenue Service encourages taxpayers to file electronically, but people who prepare their own returns may mail in paper forms. Either way, the preparation is the same.

    Filling Out the Form

    Like most married couples, Samuel and Felicity are filing jointly LINE 2. Their five exemptions are on LINE 6.
    That tax break will be gone next year.
    Felicity’s salary is $105,000. From that, she made a $10,000 tax-deferred contribution to her 401(k) retirement plan. That meant her take-home salary was $95,000 (BOX 1 OF W-2), which she’ll enter on LINE 7 of the 1040. The 401(k) contribution is shown on her W-2, but not on their tax return.
    Samuel’s net income was $89,454 after office rent and other expenses. That is transferred to LINE 12 from SCHEDULE C.
    Taxable interest income of $236 is reported on LINE 8A.
    Their ordinary dividends of $864, which are reported on FORM 1099-DIV issued by financial institutions, are shown on LINE 9A.
    All are qualified, LINE 9B, as are most dividends from domestic stocks and stock-based mutual funds. They are taxed like long-term capital gains at 15 percent for most taxpayers. (For those in the 39.6 percent bracket, they are taxed at 20 percent; people in the 10 percent and 15 percent brackets do not pay taxes on qualified dividends or capital gains but must report them.) Samuel and Felicity did not have to file SCHEDULE B to list each interest and dividend payment received because they did not earn more than $1,500 in either interest or dividends.
    Late last year, they decided to sell three lagging stocks in their portfolio to reduce the taxes they would owe. A capital loss of $1,643 on LINE 13 comes from SCHEDULE D. That put their total income at $183,911 on
    LINE 22.
    TurboTax’s “What-If Worksheet” said that if their total income remains the same, their taxes would rise under the 2018 rules. But that conclusion did not account for a deduction for consulting income that would probably give them a net tax reduction.
    Next come two adjustments, often called above-the-line deductions. Samuel can deduct half his self-employment tax, which is for Social Security and Medicare: $6,320,
    LINE 27. Employers must pay one-half of those taxes for their employees and can deduct their share, so self-employed people, who pay the entire tax, can deduct half.
    On LINE 28 is $16,627 for Samuel’s tax-deferred self-employed pension plan. That is the maximum contribution he can make, as calculated by TurboTax. Employed people who do sideline consulting or freelance work can take similar deductions, or adjustments.
    The couple’s adjusted gross income of $160,964 is on LINE 37.
    It would remain the same under 2018 rules.
    Adjusted gross income is a crucial tax number because it affects many other numbers and may limit some deductions and credits. It is often the starting point for your state income tax return.
    Here is where the tax law changes will really kick in. After itemized deductions of $52,421 on LINE 40 (from SCHEDULE A) and five personal exemptions worth $20,250, LINE 42, the couple’s taxable income is $88,293, LINE 43.
    Under 2018 rules, personal exemptions are gone and the couple can deduct only $10,000 for state and local taxes, not $17,554. So itemized deductions would be only $44,867. And their taxable income would be $116,097. What a TurboTax worksheet does not state is that because Samuel Taxpayer is a consultant and his qualified business income of $89,454 is less than the couple's taxable income of $116,097, they would probably be able to deduct 20 percent of that business income, or $17,891. That would reduce their taxable income to $98,206, as Daniel Hemel, an assistant law professor at the University of Chicago, points out on Twitter.
    For 2017, the regular tax on that is $13,464, LINE 44.
    For 2018, even though they would be in a lower tax bracket, their tax so far would be higher: $17,360, TurboTax's "What-If Worksheet" shows. But that calculation doesn't include the $17,891 deduction for which they would probably qualify, based on current but still incomplete information, Mr. Meighan said.
    A complication in the tax code is that taxpayers are expected to calculate their regular tax, then the alternative minimum tax, which denies personal exemptions and certain other tax breaks, notably the deduction for state and local taxes, and pay whichever tax is higher. LINE 45 is blank on this return because TurboTax determined that the Taxpayers were not liable for the alternative minimum tax. That would still be true for them in 2018.
    The good news for many in 2018 is that the A.M.T., as it is known, will affect far fewer taxpayers.
    Next are their credits, which reduce taxes dollar for dollar. The Taxpayers claim credits of $1,428 on LINE 50 and $952 on LINE 68, both from SCHEDULE 8863, for Luke’s college costs.
    Self-employment tax of $12,639 on LINE 57 comes from SCHEDULE SE. Taxes of $20,000 were withheld from Felicity’s salary. LINE 64
    Because Samuel is self-employed, his earnings were not subject to tax withholding. Nor were taxes withheld on their investment income, so they paid quarterly estimated taxes of $2,500. LINE 65
    The bottom line is that they owe $1,223. LINE 78
    For 2018, if their adjusted gross income remains the same, after deductions and credits and with lower tax rates in effect, they would probably owe a little less than in 2017.
    Many Americans would be disappointed not to get a refund, but Samuel and Felicity are pleased with their 2017 results — they calculated their estimated tax payments carefully and did not give the Treasury an interest-free loan. Nor did they incur a penalty for failing to pay a sufficient amount of tax during the year.

    But with all of the changes underway, next year may be a lot trickier.

    Produced by Keith Collins

    Correction: March 2, 2018

    An earlier version of this article incorrectly described the probable effect of the new tax law on a hypothetical couple’s 2018 tax bill. The TurboTax “What-If Worksheet” that generated the projection for their 2018 taxes failed to indicate that the couple would probably be entitled to claim a sizable deduction for income earned from consulting. As a result of that deduction, the amount they would likely owe on taxes would decline by $43, not rise by $3,896.
