    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY KOREAN AIR

    Play Video | 1:51

    PinpointPrecision

    As in aviation, precision — down to the smallest detail — is paramount in the high-velocity world of speed skating.

    The blink of an eye separates first from fourth place in speed skating. In a sport where speeds reach nearly 37 m.p.h., only the most meticulous and tenacious of speed skaters manage to cross the finish line first. Lee Byoung-hoon — the 16-year-old South Korean speed skater who set the 500-meter record at the country’s national middle and high school championships in 2017 — explains the basic scientific principle behind achieving maximum velocity.

    “In order to speed up, you need to go against wind resistance by lowering your body,” the high school student, who is six feet tall, says. Lee began speed skating at seven, when his mother hoped the sport would help him lose weight and alleviate his asthma. The repetitive training regimen almost drove him to quit the sport initially, but his perseverance eventually paid off and Lee now says he can’t think of doing anything else.

    “Speed skating is science. Skaters have to do 12 strokes for the straight course, eight at the corners, and 12 for the other side. It’s like a mathematical formula.”

    Ko Suk-bongSpeed Skating Coach

    There are many parallels between aeronautics and speed skating. Like airplanes, speed skaters need to position their bodies to slice through the air and minimize air resistance in the most efficient way. In terms of precision, speed skaters also know how many strides it takes for them to complete a single lap. “From when I turn the first corner to where the coaches are standing, I have to take about 12 steps,” Lee says.

    Lee’s height is an advantage, no doubt. But his coach, Ko Suk-bong, says that an ideal physique is not enough. “He’s tall and is well built for a ninth grader. But more important, his reflexes are good and his cornering techniques are on par with older skaters’.” There are three basic techniques that professional speed skaters must master: the starting pose, the stroke to propel oneself in a straight line and cornering.

    The speed skater leans forward for the starting pose. The angle of his front ankle is 90 degrees and the heel of the skate is slightly lifted so that his weight shifts forward and the front knee is positioned above the toes. The back hip must be tight and ready to push forward when the race starts.

    The burst of the gun and the race begins. After pushing off, the skater crouches down to minimize air friction. He completely extends one leg to the side, pushing against the ice before swiftly retracting to propel himself. Both blades are parallel to each other and are pointed straight ahead. To ensure that his center of gravity lies over the supporting leg, the nose, knee, and toe must be completely aligned.

    When the speed skater turns the corner, he keeps an arm still and behind his waist to stabilize himself, swinging his other arm to create momentum. His body weight is transferred to the pushing leg to ride the momentum. He has to move quickly while keeping his center of gravity low; if he tilts too much or too little, he risks tumbling — a mistake that would cost him the race.

    The mechanics of a speed skater in action are complex, but when the race starts, he does not have any time to think through these steps. Techniques need to be second nature, and body and mind have to be perfectly in sync. Ko says, “If your mind responds before your body, you will be delayed by about 0.01 seconds.” The time difference could make all the difference between winning and losing. “So we have to practice to minimize mistakes.”

    To commit perfect executions to muscle memory, Lee’s whole life revolves around speed skating. The ninth grader trains for at least four hours a day when he’s in school and up to seven hours during the school holidays. “I’m a Sparta-style coach,” says Ko of his tough-love approach. “But it’s for the benefit of the skaters, so they can overcome hardship.” Half of the time is devoted to warm-ups and exercises outside the rink. There’s always running involved, as well as form-perfecting drills that Lee practices in front of the mirror, closely supervised by Ko, who refines and hones Lee’s techniques. On the ice, everything has to come together.

    All athletes have their own pre-competition ritual, and Lee is no exception. “I listen to music. Last year, they were all sad songs,” he says, adding that he has since changed his tunes, which now reflect his growing prominence in the speed-skating world. “This year, I’ve started listening to more upbeat music before competitions. I don’t want my mood to be dragged down.”

    Just like professional speed skaters, Korean Air has internalized the art of precision.

    Impeccable Service From the Ground Up

    Speed skaters spend a significant amount of time training outside the rink. For Korean Air, quality of service on the ground is just as important as the actual journey in the sky. From 2018, passengers flying with Korean Air from Seoul check in at Incheon Airport Terminal 2, which minimizes the amount of time needed for travelers to reach their gate. Upon request, first class travelers are greeted by a staff member who will accompany them from the First Class Check-in Lounge to the boarding gate.

    Flying Green

    For skaters and Korean Air alike, it’s all about slicing through air with maximum efficiency. Korean Air is constantly upgrading its fleet to adopt the latest fuel efficient technology and is also the first Asian carrier to purchase the cutting-edge Bombardier CS300, which is not only fuel efficient but also 75 percent quieter than similar models. Meanwhile, the wingtips and winglets are specially designed to reduce wind drag.

    Not Pinching Pennies

    While other airlines have been scaling back amenities and service offerings, Korean Air remains dedicated to providing for the comfort of its travelers. Passengers are given slippers, blankets and a toiletry kit, while the new 18-inch-wide economy seat reclines 118 degrees. Even the cabin lighting is calibrated to help passengers sleep. Korean Air is a restful way to fly and the carrier has been recognized by Airline Ratings as having the best economy class service for 2018.

    From Farm to Sky

    Professional athletes watch what they eat, and Korean Air goes the extra mile to ensure its passengers are well fed with healthy meals. Korean Air is the world’s only airline to own and operate a farm on Jeju island, cultivating its own vegetables and livestock that are used to create in-flight meals. Korean Air’s nutritious and tasty bibimbap dish — a traditional Korean meat-and-vegetable rice bowl — has enjoyed great acclaim.

    Global Reach

    Servicing 123 destinations in 43 countries across all six continents, Korean Air enjoys a world-renowned reputation. If you’re searching for the best ice-skating destinations, the airline can whisk you away to some of the world’s favorite options: Somerset House in London, Rockefeller Center in New York City, Yeouido Ice Park in Seoul, ICE*Amsterdam in Amsterdam and Shichahai Lake in Beijing.
