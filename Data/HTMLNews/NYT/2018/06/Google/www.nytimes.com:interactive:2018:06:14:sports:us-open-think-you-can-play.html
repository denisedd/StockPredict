    So, You Think You Can Play in the U.S. Open?

    By GRANT GOLD and JOE WARD JUNE 14, 2018

    Let’s say you made your way through 9,000 other qualifiers to play in this year’s U.S. Open. Here are a few obstacles that will test your nerves and your skill at Shinnecock Hills on Long Island.


    So, You Think You Can Play in the U.S. Open?

    By Grant Gold and Joe Ward June 14, 2018

    Let’s just say you somehow made it through 9,000 entrants to become part of the field of 156 golfers. Here are a few ways that Shinnecock Hills will test you.

    The U.S.G.A. relishes the chance to punish you if your shot strays too far. Miss the fairway by enough to get into the knee-high rough, and you will atone for your sins.

    You wouldn’t be alone. Even the pros lose their balls in this stuff.

    We thought you would have trouble in here, so Kyle Jorden, an assistant pro at Shinnecock, has offered to give you a tip.

    The tall, thick grass will grab your club, slowing it down and twisting its face.

    Set up so the ball is aligned near your back foot, take a lofted club and use a steep swing.

    This way, your club won’t have to travel through so much rough before hitting the ball.

    You’ll be tempted to hit this ball far to make up the distance you lost. Don’t. Hit it short and high and move on. 

    The ocean breezes that lure many to the Hamptons will have you cursing them before long. 

    While you’re spending your morning looking for your ball in the rough, the June sun is baking eastern Long Island.

    Before long the air above Shinnecock heats up and begins to rise. You find your ball yet?

    The rising hot air lowers the pressure over the land and the cooler air above the sea rushes in from three directions to take its place.

    The unpredictable wind direction is coupled with a course where the holes also seem to go in every direction. The combination is dizzying.

    The scorecard says it’s a par 3. It’s only 159 yards. You grab an iron. You’ve got this.

    Your caddy shakes his head. It’s an elevated green, so you’ll need more distance. He hands you that shiny, new hybrid you saw on TV.

    Depending on the wind, the pros will be hitting anything from a wedge to a 6-iron, trying to land it softly on a very small green. 

    The green is so small that our good friend Kyle, the assistant pro, says it’s like trying to hit into a baby pool at the end of a football field.

    Don’t hit short — or left — or right. You’ll land in a sand bunker so deep you will disappear from view.

    And don’t hit long either. The back portion of the green slopes so much your ball will roll down an embankment.

    This will be your shot from down there. Remember to breathe.

    And here is your shot from that front bunker. Do you have this shot in your bag?

    You’ll be lucky to get a double bogey 5. But don’t despair, Lee Trevino called this par 3 the “shortest par 5 in the world.”

    Don’t Forget to Enjoy Your Open
