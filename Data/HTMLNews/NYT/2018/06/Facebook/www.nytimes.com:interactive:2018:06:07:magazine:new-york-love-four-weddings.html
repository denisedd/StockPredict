    Getting Hitched

    Four, Out of Many, Ways to Get Married

    Photographs by Todd Heisler Text by Alex Carp JUNE 7, 2018

    Golden Terrace Banquet Hall, Richmond Hill, Queens

    When Sadhna Yunus, 32, was introduced to Yudesh Bissnauth, 36, at a dinner in Queens, “He was like, ‘Hey, I have you on Facebook!’ ” She was confused, but Yudesh was right: She’d added him by accident years ago, mistaking him for an old colleague with the same first name. “He said he looked at my profile, and he’s like, ‘Yeah, she’s out of my league.’ ” (She was, after all, a runner-up for Miss Universe Guyana in 2012.) But ever since Yudesh visited her in Toronto, where she lived — he plays for New York’s Galaxy Cricket Club and was there for a tournament — “we have not stopped talking.” Growing up in Guyana, he trained with the youth national team in Albion, the small town where Sadhna grew up. “We had to have passed each other,” she says. In the days before their wedding, a traditional Hindu ceremony, Sadhna sat for nearly six hours while an intricate mehndi pattern was inked on her hands and feet, and she and Yudesh each had their bodies covered in turmeric. “It’s a send-off,” she says. “Your parents are cleansing you to start your new life.”

    New Mulan, Flushing, Queens

    Susan Ha, 30, spent the night before her wedding at her parents’ house in Florham Park, New Jersey. When Richard Woo, 35, her fiance, arrived with his groomsmen in the morning, Susan’s friends and family had set up a series of tests and obstacles for him. “The wedding door games go back to ancient Chinese times, where the husband has to prove himself worthy of the bride before he is allowed to receive her,” Susan said. The first challenge required Rich to pick out Susan’s lips from nine lipstick outlines pressed to paper. “I had one of my bridesmaids FaceTime me,” she says, so she could spy on his progress — “I didn’t want to miss any of it.” The final challenge brought him upstairs, where she was waiting.

    Door games were followed by a traditional tea ceremony with Susan’s family, then another with Rich’s family in Sheepshead Bay, where his mother had made rice balls in ginger soup, symbolizing good luck. They held the wedding reception in Flushing, Queens, with a whole suckling pig for each of their 38 tables. “To have a whole pig,” Rich said, “it’s grand.”

    El Paraiso Tropical , Corona, Queens

    Everilda Bael, 37, and Candido Santos González Quiché, 38, met as children in Guatemala. “When we were in school, he was very romantic,” Everilda says. “He liked to tell me I was one of his loves.” When she was 17, she joined him in New York. (He now works for a contractor, and she found work as a cleaner.) They had a daughter, Marilyn, six years later, and then a son, Jared.

    At the reception, in Corona, Queens, Marilyn surprised her parents with the mariachi band from their church. She played violin and sang to her parents; her brother played guitar. “They’re good students,” Santos said. The couple arranged for a live broadcast of their wedding over Facebook and on a local television station in Guatemala.

    Brooklyn Church of God Holiness, East Flatbush, Brooklyn

    To celebrate their twenty-fifth anniversary, Jean-Pierre Lors, 51, and Marjorie Lors, 49, renewed their vows at the church founded by Jean-Pierre’s father in 1986, shortly after he came to the U.S. from Haiti. Jean-Pierre became a pastor there in the church’s first decade and, after his father’s death in 2014, the senior pastor; Marjorie is the church’s worship leader. They met, Jean-Pierre says, when “she became a member of the church and I was a Sunday school teacher. Very quietly, I had a crush on her, and she had a crush on me. And from there we started talking, little by little.”

    At their wedding, 25 years ago, “you would invite some people, but everybody would just come,” Marjorie says; the guests helped to cook and serve food. Their renewal was more intimate. William, 13, the youngest of Jean-Pierre and Marjorie’s four children, walked his mother down the aisle. The service included songs by the church’s twenty-person choir, scripture selected by the couple and a sermon by Mark Surbrook, a pastor who, on a church mission to Haiti, attended the birth of Jean-Pierre and his twin brother in 1967.

    “We’re still in love,” Marjorie says, “as if it was the first day.”♦

    Alex Carp is a research editor for the magazine. Todd Heisler is a Pulitzer Prize-winning staff photographer for The Times.
