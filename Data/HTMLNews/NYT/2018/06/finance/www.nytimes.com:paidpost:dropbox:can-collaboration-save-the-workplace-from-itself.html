    Can Collaboration
    Save the Workplace
    From Itself?

    How 2 companies redesigned their cultures to root out the grind.

    For seven listless years, Brian Vieira jumped from cubicle to cubicle chasing what he hoped would be bigger paydays. The work was solitary and the environments toxically competitive: Coworkers typically preferred bonuses to teamwork. Over time, the quality of his work declined, Vieira says, and he became stressed and antisocial.

    "I changed as a person. I didn’t want to go out with friends. I didn’t want to network with people," says Vieira, who recently changed careers and became an influencer marketing coordinator at the coconut water brand Vita Coco. "Finally, I’d had enough and said: ‘This is not right for me. I need to do something I’m passionate about in a team environment.’”

    At the same time, Chris Welwood worked in a rigid environment. Over four years, he grew restless with work that was never going to inspire him.

    “I didn’t connect with it,” he says. “It definitely wasn’t a career path.”

    Welwood pivoted, becoming a product manager in 2016 at Topps, the hallowed collectibles and candy company.

    In workplaces worldwide, a grind has taken hold. People are used to spending long hours on work that’s, well, about work.

    Experiences like Vieira and Welwood’s are all too common. In workplaces worldwide, a grind has taken hold. People are used to spending long hours on work that’s, well, about work: emails, instant messages, too much busywork.

    A McKinsey study estimates that highly skilled workers spend as much as half their workdays performing low-skill tasks. Another study published in Management Science found that workplace stress, caused by factors such as increasing demands and long hours, is the fifth leading cause of death in the United States.

    “Stress has huge consequences on people,” says Jeffrey Pfeffer, a Stanford professor and co-author of the study. “Research shows that people who are stressed at work are unproductive, more likely to quit and more likely to become sick,” says Pfeffer, who also wrote “Dying for a Paycheck.”

    With busywork and stress mounting, businesses are promoting collaboration to make jobs more creative and rewarding. But teamwork, they are learning, isn’t a panacea. It can add to an organization’s complexity and create even more work about work.

    “Collaboration makes work more effective and more interesting, but it's also harder,” says Charles Heckscher, a professor at Rutgers University and the co-director of its Center for the Study of Collaboration in Work and Society. “The work is much less structured, and it can be hard to know what you’re supposed to do.”

    Instead of forcing together ideas and employees, Vita Coco and Topps leveraged technology to stimulate synchronous collaboration and cultivate diversity of opinion. This approach helped both businesses ignite creative energy among employees and construct cultures that inspire workers to outhustle the competition.

    In 2012, Vita Coco, was eight years old and facing its own crisis: how to outhustle larger competitors without bursting at the seams.

    Brian Olney, now a senior director of marketing at Vita Coco, says the main challenge for his small team was to keep up with the torrid pace of the company’s product launches.

    “Every day was all hands on deck and just get stuff done,” Olney recalls. But he soon discovered “just getting by,” even if it led to great work, wasn’t a long-term recipe for success. Because the team lacked collaboration tools and synchronous processes, too many of its best ideas were sequestered in hard drives and cluttered inboxes, making it hard to match Vita Coco’s breakneck expansion.

    “Growth can become a problem if you don't have systems in place to keep everything together,” Olney says. “You keep going until you suddenly realize that it’s all falling apart.”

    To save his young team, Olney knew he needed to add structure and new ways of working. Much like Topps did, he found the ideal solution in Dropbox, a digital collaboration space that brings together innovative ideas and files to reduce busywork and unleash creative energy.

    Dropbox Unleashes Creative Energy and New Ideas

    When Topps began its digital modernization in 2012, Chris Vaccaro, the editor in chief of Topps Digital, made hiring diverse thinkers his priority. His digital SWAT team included those with backgrounds in product management, graphic design, communications and finance, all united by their digital product expertise and lifelong passion for trading cards.

    Shortly after forming, the new Topps digital team built and launched a revenue-generating mobile app fans could use to collect and trade digital baseball cards. Today, fans can collect and trade cards from other Topps sports and entertainment partners like the N.F.L. and “Star Wars.”

    “We found a way for collectors to have 10,000 cards sitting in their laps,” Vaccaro says.

    But throngs of new users brought another challenge: how to produce timely content for fans used to sports at warp speed on social media.

    ‘Dropbox has been part of the Topps modernization effort since Day 1. It’s been a creative engine for us.’

    —Chris Vaccaro, Editor in Chief of Topps Digital

    “We had to be more of a live-ops newsroom than a card company,” says Raul Salcedo, a production manager at Topps. For instance, if a star baseball player hit three home runs in one game, Topps needed to rapidly produce an eye-catching video card to commemorate the feat.

    To keep ideas flowing and creative assets centralized, Topps looked to Dropbox Paper, a collaborative online workspace where teams can generate new ideas and manage the entire creative process. In Dropbox Paper, Topps has cataloged each of the whopping 4.5 billion digital cards it has created since 2012. The Topps “catalog” also acts as a wellspring of new ideas.

    For instance, Chris Welwood encourages his colleagues to use Dropbox Paper each week to brainstorm fresh ideas for Topps’s digital “Star Wars” cards. His designers and strategists find inspiration from overlooked scenes and even sports card designs.

    “Dropbox has been part of the Topps modernization effort since Day 1,” Vaccaro says. “It’s been a creative engine for us.”

    At Vita Coco, Brian Olney knew the company’s expansion would benefit from more structure, but he had to be delicate: Strict protocols might sap his team’s creative energy. So Olney looked to Dropbox to help maintain a democratic flow of ideas.

    “We wanted a place to cross-pollinate,” Olney says, “to spark someone to look at a design and say: ‘That’s cool. Let me add to this.’”

    Dropbox Paper has helped Vita Coco generate new ideas at every level. Julia Guignard, the company’s social media designer, for example, was encouraged to pursue her passion for photography during downtime. She put together ideas for an e-commerce page in Dropbox Paper and then shot them in a back room at Vita Coco’s New York headquarters. Using Dropbox to collaborate with Patrick Baskin, Vita Coco’s senior global brand manager, Guignard refined the ideas and transformed the side project into an entire Vita Coco brand campaign.

    “We want every member of the team to go after ideas that the bigger companies won’t,” Baskin says.

    To survive, Vita Coco and Topps needed to outwit deep-pocketed competitors and revive timeworn products. The companies recognized that growth — personal and professional — came from meaningful collaboration and culturally driven creative energy. As Vita Coco and Topps applied Dropbox across their organizations, organic teamwork took form and transformative ideas sprang forward.

    “We try to create an environment where people are empowered to be creative,” Olney says. “I think our biggest fear is that one of our employees would have to show up in the office and go right to their cubicle to do reports all day while a manager manages them.

    “That's the day the collaborative culture we’ve built dies.”

    Studies about the intensifying grind and soaring levels of work stress have awoken us to the reality that the workplace has become harmful to our health and personal growth. Instead of treating symptoms of the grind, businesses need to examine root causes like mind-numbing busywork, pervasive groupthink and unstructured teamwork.

    As Topps and Vita Coco demonstrate, Dropbox can help businesses at various stages build cultures that are firmly rooted in meaningful teamwork, diversity of thought and boundless creative energy.
