    What We Learned From 3 Historic Diseases

    — and How It Just Might Save Humanity

    WHETHER IT’S HOW we get our morning news, the way we get to work, or even the treatments we receive when we’re sick — the fast pace of technological innovation affects our lives daily. But these developments have not always been so rapid. Throughout history, we’ve solved some of the biggest medical threats facing humanity through centuries of collective research and careful experimentation.

    Today, even with vastly greater knowledge, we face new challenges — aggressive forms of cancer, diminishing farmland and genetic diseases, to name a few. To solve them, it helps to look back at three historic diseases that science has conquered or quelled, to see how unbridled curiosity, tireless investigation and innovation have enabled some of our brightest minds to achieve scientific breakthroughs.

    Smallpox

    The world’s first vaccine and only eradicated disease

    Can a bovine virus suppress a devastating human disease?

    Smallpox

    The world’s first vaccine and only eradicated disease

    Smallpox

    Can a bovine virus suppress a devastating human disease?

    The earliest evidence of smallpox dates back to 1157 B.C., in the mummified corpse of Pharaoh Ramses V. Over time, trade routes and other factors spread the smallpox virus around the globe. By the 18th century, the disease killed 400,000 people in Europe each year.

    Early methods to fight smallpox were born out of hearsay and desperation. One remedy called for administering beer and “drawing the bedclothes no higher than the patient’s waist.”

    But there were also scientific approaches. As early as the 17th century, doctors had developed an inoculation method called variolation. They would transfer live smallpox matter from the skin of a smallpox patient to a healthy person, inducing a more mild and less lethal reaction than if the recipient had acquired the virus naturally. It was, however, an imperfect solution; variolated patients, while now protected against actual smallpox, could still transmit the virus.

    You could argue that modern immunization derives from Jenner’s use of cowpox to produce smallpox immunity.
    Dr. Arthur Boylston

    Then, in 1768, the English surgeon John Fewster made a curious epidemiological observation, says Dr. Arthur Boylston, the author of “Defying Providence: Smallpox and the forgotten 18th century Medical Revolution.”

    Many of the farmers Fewster variolated experienced no reaction to the virus. Fewster found that these patients previously had cowpox, a less fatal virus.

    Could a previous cowpox infection somehow prevent smallpox?

    Nearly three decades later, another doctor, Edward Jenner, tested Fewster’s observation by vaccinating a young boy with matter from a cowpox pustule. After developing mild symptoms and recovering, the boy was inoculated with smallpox — but did not develop the disease.

    “This was the first time someone was able to artificially induce immunity with something that wasn’t the disease itself,” says Dr. Boylston. “You could argue that modern immunization derives from Jenner’s use of cowpox to produce smallpox immunity.”

    Smallpox vaccination programs spread globally in the 19th and 20th centuries. In 1980, the World Health Assembly declared smallpox globally eradicated — the first and only eradicated disease to date.

    Late Blight

    The plant disease that helped spur germ theory

    How did a 19th-century famine revolutionize science?

    Late Blight

    How did a 19th-century famine revolutionize science?

    DEADLY DISEASES DON’T only infect humans. In the summer of 1845, Irish farmers noticed something terribly wrong with their potato crops: The plants’ green vines had blackened and their tubers had rotted, eventually becoming slimy mush.

    Many farmers attributed the devastation to bad weather or poor luck. What they did not know was that a fungal-like pathogen had infected their crops. In potato-dependent Ireland, this plant disease, called late blight, destroyed entire crops for nearly five seasons, resulting in a decade-long famine that killed more than a million people.

    This catastrophe led scientists to take a closer look at plant diseases and crop protection, giving rise to the field of plant pathology. In 1861, German botanist Heinrich Anton de Bary set up an experiment: He applied spores from one blighted potato to a number of healthy potatoes. Others he kept spore-free. Only the plants exposed to the fungus decayed.

    What they did not know was that fungal-like pathogen had infected their crops.

    Though people had observed pathogens since the 17th century, de Bary was one of the first scientists to show that they were not the result of diseases but, rather, the cause of them, says William Fry, Ph.D., a professor of plant pathology and plant-microbe biology at Cornell University.

    “De Bary spurred a real movement to think of plant disease being caused by pathogens,” says Dr. Fry. “His discovery stimulated scientists to accept the germ theory of disease, which Louis Pasteur demonstrated in the following years.”

    Late blight has not been fully eradicated, largely because the pathogen can quickly mutate and overcome plant resistances. But de Bary’s discovery nonetheless triggered major advances in the study of diseases in plants, as well as in humans and animals.

    Polio

    Better sanitation breeds a killer

    Can one vaccine prevent three types of a virus?

    Polio

    Better sanitation breeds a killer

    Polio

    Can one vaccine prevent three types of a virus?

    ALTHOUGH Evidence of the poliomyelitis virus dates back to ancient Egyptian carvings, polio was not clinically documented until the end of the 18th century.

    While polio could be fatal, major outbreaks were not generally recognized until the late 19th century, when modern sanitation systems were built and public health improved in many cities. Previously, infants were commonly exposed to the virus while they were still protected by maternal antibodies, says Dr. Walter Orenstein, the associate director of the Emory Vaccine Center and a former director of the U.S. Immunization Program. But as hygiene improved, people were exposed to polio later in life, when the antibodies they received from their mothers had disappeared.

    As polio epidemics killed and paralyzed tens of thousands each year, governments were desperate to find a way to prevent them. In 1953, Dr. Jonas Salk developed the first polio vaccine — an injectable, dead version of the virus. One of Dr. Salk’s major contributions, says Dr. Orenstein, was discovering that there were three disease-causing versions of the poliovirus, and an effective vaccine had to protect against all of them.

    “We saw this again with influenza — that you have to induce immunity against multiple different strains,” Dr. Orenstein says.

    As polio epidemics killed and paralyzed tens of thousands each year, governments were desperate to find a way to prevent them.

    After successful clinical trials, mass vaccination programs with Dr. Salk’s vaccine began in 1955, slashing U.S. polio cases nearly 90 percent.

    Scientists’ work wasn’t done, however. In the early 1960s, Dr. Albert Sabin, concerned that an inactivated vaccine would not be as effective as an active one, developed a live oral vaccine that prevented all three types of polio. Together, Dr. Salk’s and Dr. Sabin’s vaccines swiftly controlled the disease in industrialized countries.

    But polio remained a menace in the developing world: In 1988, there were an estimated 350,000 polio cases in more than 125 countries. That year, the global eradication effort began, and thousands of health care workers helped establish mass immunization programs in high-risk areas. As a result, polio cases have decreased 99 percent globally. In 2017, there were only 22 reported cases.

    The Next Evolution in Health

    Leaps by Bayer

    The Next Evolution in Health

    Leaps by Bayer

    Emory, nor Dr. Walter Orenstein, endorses or sponsors Bayer or any Bayer products or services.
    Illustration by Albert Tercero

    TODAY THE SAME SPIRIT of medical innovation that helped subdue these historic diseases is now inspiring scientists to alter the genetic code and harness the power of microbes to face new formidable threats, such as autoimmune diseases and diminishing food supplies.

    Bayer has crystallized this approach in a program called Leaps by Bayer, which gives young biotech companies more time and resources — both financial and technological — to develop breakthroughs that could someday save lives.

    “It’s so difficult scientifically and medically to cure a human or plant disease,” says Axel Bouchon, Ph.D., head of Leaps by Bayer. “We have to develop a new concept that allows enough time, financing, talent and boldness to address these huge challenges.”

    We have to develop a new concept that allows enough time, financing, talent and boldness to address these huge challenges.
    Axel Bouchon, Ph.D., head of Leaps by Bayer

    For Leaps by Bayer, this means addressing 10 key problems facing humanity and forming joint ventures that make significant investments in areas like DNA editing, microbiome and stem cells.

    Casebia Therapeutics, a joint venture between Bayer and CRISPR Therapeutics, combines Bayer’s protein-engineering capabilities with CRISPR Therapeutics’ breakthrough gene editing tool to research cures for inherited diseases, such as hemophilia and macular degeneration. In a separate collaboration with Versant Ventures, Bayer has formed BlueRock Therapeutics to develop stem cells that can repair heart muscles in heart attack patients and brain cells in Parkinson’s patients. And in agriculture, Bayer has joined forces with Ginkgo Bioworks to form Joyn Bio, which is researching how designer microbes can help plants fertilize themselves, reducing dependence on greenhouse-gas-heavy nitrogen fertilizers.

    “We can address these 10 challenges by building companies around great technologies and giving them enough runway and intellectual property access to immediately get started,” Dr. Bouchon says. “We want to drive a paradigm shift from treatment to cure in health care, and from industrial to sustainable in agriculture.”
