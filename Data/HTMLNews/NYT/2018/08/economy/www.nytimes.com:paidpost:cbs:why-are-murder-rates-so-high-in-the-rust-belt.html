    WHY ARE MURDER RATES SO HIGH IN THE RUST BELT?

    A

    llentown, Pa., set a grisly record in 2017, marking the highest number of gun-related deaths in the city’s history. In suburban Pittsburgh the previous year, two men opened fire on a dozen people in a backyard, killing an eight-months-pregnant woman and four others at a family barbecue. On Jan. 7, 2018, Detroit experienced one of its bloodiest Sundays on record: At least 14 people were shot or stabbed in various incidents, and two died, including a woman whose throat was slit.

    Violent crime has plagued the Rust Belt in recent years. Consistently, crime rates have been higher than the national norm in this region — which spans roughly from western New York to eastern Wisconsin, including Baltimore and sections of West Virginia. Out of the five metropolitan areas with the highest per capita murder rates, according to CBS News, four are deindustrialized cities: St. Louis, Baltimore, Detroit and Cleveland. What is it about former manufacturing hubs that lead to homicide and other brutal crimes? Here’s how vanishing jobs, alienated neighbors and shattered hopes swirled together to create a toxic storm that continues to burden the most economically depressed parts of this once thriving region.

    FROM DAZZLE TO DECAY

    In the mid-1900s, the area we now associate with rust was prestigious and wealthy. “During World War II, Detroit’s manufacturing plants were retooled for the war effort,” says Richey Piiparinen, the director of the Center for Population Dynamics at Cleveland State University. “The region really saved America.”

    With the end of the war came the golden age of capitalism: “It was a great time in America,” Piiparinen says. “Detroit had the highest per capita income in the nation in 1960.”

    Before long, however, people started migrating west, dreaming about California, and Silicon Valley burst into existence. Factories began moving south or offshore, and unemployment in former industrial towns started to rise, which had a negative effect on not just wallets but minds. “You had this death of a region that once flourished,” Piiparinen says. “And it provided a psychology of contrast that was hard to deal with — a very existential contrast.”

    A VIOLENT ECONOMY

    On Nov. 1, 2017, in a normally tranquil south Allentown neighborhood, two drivers collided, then stopped their vehicles and shouted at each other. After a few minutes, one man grabbed a shotgun from the back of his pickup truck and killed the other with a single blast.

    Crime declined throughout the United States from the early 1990s to 2013, then spiked in 2014 through 2016 for reasons experts are still trying to determine. But for certain areas of the Rust Belt, the murder rate has long been above average. “Industrial cities in the Midwest and Northeast, like St. Louis, Cleveland — and Pittsburgh, to a degree — have seen relatively high homicide rates over some time,” says Richard Rosenfeld, emeritus professor of criminology at the University of Missouri, St. Louis. “And that is clearly related to economic decline and the inability of some of those cities to climb back from deindustrialization.”

    ECONOMIC STRESS MAY GIVE YOU A SHORTER FUSE. THEN CERTAIN SITUATIONS MIGHT ESCALATE AND END IN MURDER.
    —
    Sociologist Emily Shrider, Research Associate at the University of Wisconsin, Madison

    What does the economy have to do with violence? “For decades, we’ve seen poverty, unemployment, segregation and lack of economic opportunities strongly correlate with higher violent crime and murder rates,” says sociologist Emily A. Shrider, a research associate at the University of Wisconsin at Madison. “Without economic options, for some people, desperate times call for desperate measures. You commit an armed robbery and maybe murder someone in the process.”

    The increased anxiety and financial pressures can exacerbate problems in other areas of someone’s life. “People say that being under a lot of economic stress can give you a shorter fuse,” Shrider says. “Then certain situations might escalate, and what could have been just an argument turns into domestic violence or ends in a murder.”

    A CYCLE OF DEADLY ADDICTIONS

    In Detroit, three men and one woman were arrested in August 2017 for running a drug and human trafficking operation. They allegedly preyed on people addicted to opioids and forced them into sexual slavery. In a separate 2017 case, the F.B.I. and a local task force exposed a Detroit-to-West-Virginia “pill pipeline” organized by violent gangs.

    The opioid crisis has hit the Rust Belt particularly hard. “We’re seeing very high opioid addiction rates in the counties outside Pittsburgh,” says Piiparinen, “as well as Youngstown, Ohio, and Appalachian coal-mining towns that don’t have anchor institutions — such as universities and hospitals — to help transition to a knowledge-based economy. That’s where the severe pain is.”

    Opioids may be on the rise in former factory towns because people who are out of work are more likely to be financially stressed, suffer from depression and have extra time on their hands, suggests Sean Safford, a professor of economic sociology at Sciences Po, in Paris. “They may turn to opioids as a way to deal with diminished circumstances.”

    THEY MAY TURN TO OPIOIDS AS A WAY TO DEAL WITH DIMINISHED CIRCUMSTANCES.
    —
    Sean Safford, Professor of Economic Sociology at Sciences Po, Paris

    A potential result? Increased gang activity. “When you have high demand for opioids, gang networks fight over who gets the opioid supply and disperses it,” says Piiparinen. “And violent crime goes up.”

    For some of those addicted, it can be a fatal cycle: Their bodies compel them to buy drugs, then their finances dwindle, which can lead to depression, making them seek more drugs to numb the pain. If they don’t have enough cash, they might steal what they need. Gangs move in. Income and quality of life plummet. Crime rises. Repeat.

    THE GENTRIFICATION EFFECT

    In 2014, two sisters in Pittsburgh’s gentrifying East Liberty neighborhood were murdered in their apartment, then doused with bleach and detergent. Their next-door neighbor later received double life sentences for the sisters’ homicide, as well as robbery and burglary.

    Another factor that can lead to increases in crime, at least in the short term, may seem counterintuitive: gentrification. “In the earlier years of gentrification, the crime rate actually gets worse before it settles down,” Shrider says. “New neighbors are more likely to call the police, which can cause an uptick in arrest rates. Also better goods are available to steal. And with an influx of newcomers, it’s harder for people to keep tabs on their neighbors.”

    Sociologists debate whether social media has broken real-world connections. But many agree that disconnection among neighbors correlates to higher crime. “Social ties, the connections we have with those around us, are weaker or less likely to exist when there’s a lot of turnover in a neighborhood,” Shrider says.

    She believes this may have been the situation with the Cleveland Strangler case, which took place in Ohio’s fractured Mount Pleasant neighborhood. In 2009, Cleveland police officers raided the house of a former factory worker and military man, and discovered 11 bodies, some buried in his yard. “It makes me wonder,” Shrider says. “Did poor neighborhood health have something to do with why this went on as long as it did?”

    TOPPLING THE HOMICIDE TREND

    What can be done to help turn around Rust Belt cities and neighborhoods experiencing increases in violent crime? One solution might be to attract businesses that will hire locals and pay good living wages. “We need more jobs — and to raise the minimum wage to $15,” says Anne Trubek, editor of the book “Voices From the Rust Belt.”

    Trubek, who was raised in Wisconsin, moved to Cleveland in 2006 and started her own company, Belt Publishing. “In Cleveland there are a lot of possibilities for launching your own business," she says. “It is a fascinating, complex place to live.”

    Piiparinen says that to bring jobs to the Rust Belt, “anchor organizations need to lead the way.” One example he says inspires hope is the Cleveland Clinic, which is working with the local community and looking into how artificial intelligence and other advanced technologies might be used to help correct the disparity in health outcomes in different neighborhoods.

    “We just need to get our A.I. chops up to figure out the local problem and grow our regional economy,” Piiparinen says. “Then we can decrease crime and become a global hub again in the next wave.”
