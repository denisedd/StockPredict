    Trump’s Endorsements: The Loyalists, Rising Stars and Safe Bets He’s Picked

    By AUDREY CARLSEN and ISABELLA GRULLÓN PAZ UPDATED SEPT. 10, 2018

    58 endorsed Republican candidates
    34 before their primaries
    2 who challenged G.O.P. incumbents

    President Trump has bucked the tradition set by previous presidents by endorsing many candidates during primary season, adding his influence to races while Republicans are still facing off against other Republicans. He has picked candidates he sees as the most electable, or the most loyal, or the ones preferred by Republican leaders in Congress.

    “That’s what makes what he’s doing unusual,” said Jennifer Duffy, a senior editor for the Cook Political Report. “Usually you don’t see a president heavily invested until after Labor Day,” she added.

    So far, most of Mr. Trump’s endorsed candidates have won. That’s in part because many of them did not face serious primary challenges or were running in heavily Republican districts, but it is also because he remains very popular among Republican voters. All but one of the few losses have come in general or special elections.

    Mr. Trump has held a handful of rallies in support of candidates, but it is his prolific use of Twitter to issue his endorsements — and the media attention that those tweets receive — that makes his stamp of approval unique. Here’s a look at the kinds of candidates that have earned his backing and what he’s said about them on Twitter.

    The president has also notably broken with tradition by endorsing candidates who are challenging Republican incumbents. That list includes Kris Kobach, a candidate for Kansas governor, whom Mr. Trump tapped to lead his voter fraud commission, now disbanded.

    Katie Arrington, a candidate in South Carolina’s First District, received Mr. Trump’s late endorsement against Mark Sanford, the Republican incumbent and a frequent Trump critic. Ms. Arrington made her opponent’s criticism central to her campaign, and it carried her to victory in one of the year’s largest electoral upsets.

    Mr. Trump has said that his endorsements make all the difference, tweeting:

    As long as I campaign and/or support Senate and House candidates (within reason), they will win! I LOVE the people, & they certainly seem to like the job I’m doing. If I find the time, in between China, Iran, the Economy and much more, which I must, we will have a giant Red Wave!

    — Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) August 8, 2018

    But the effects of his support are yet to be fully seen. While Republican voters remain tightly aligned with the president (they approve of the job he’s doing at a rate of nearly 90 percent), he remains highly unpopular with Democrats and some independent voters. Mr. Trump’s endorsement may actually prove harmful for some Republican candidates against Democratic opponents. So far, Republicans have lost support in every special election since Trump took office.

    “It’s one thing to win a primary, but you have to be able to win a general election,” Ms. Duffy said. “To use a basketball analogy, he’s picked the winner of the first half, but there’s another half to play.” And with the Democrats, she added, “there’s a different team on the court.”

    Note: The partisan score for Pennsylvania’s 18th District is based on an older congressional map, before it was redrawn, that was used for the special election there earlier this year.
