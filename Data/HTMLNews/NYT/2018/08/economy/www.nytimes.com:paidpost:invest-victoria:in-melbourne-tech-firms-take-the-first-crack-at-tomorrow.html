    Australia’s cultural and creative heart is a tech boomtown. Here’s why the world should take notice.

    On the map, Melbourne is tucked into Australia’s southeastern tip, a location that belies its outsize influence on the rest of the world. After all, Victoria’s coastal capital introduced us to flat whites, and it was a Melbourne-born chef, Bill Granger, who taught us the disarmingly simple secret to a tasty and healthy breakfast: avocado on toast.

    On the map, Melbourne is tucked into Australia’s southeastern tip, a location that belies its outsize influence on the rest of the world. After all, Victoria’s coastal capital introduced us to flat whites and it was a Melbourne-born chef, Bill Granger, who taught us the disarmingly simple secret to a tasty and healthy breakfast: avocado on toast.

    This prescience is extending into its tech sector of some 1,100 start-ups, valued at $1.6 billion in total. Encouraged by positive industry trends, tech giants like Salesforce, Square and, most recently, Google have come from Silicon Valley to the continent’s fastest-growing city.

    “We joke with our American friends that we can tell them the future and what tomorrow’s going to be like,” laughs Robert Wickham, Salesforce’s regional vice president for platform and ecosystem innovation, Asia-Pacific. Jesting aside, Wickham explains why Australia is an ideal gateway to Asia: “You can run a follow-the-sun model, which means we can service our customers with global operations 24 hours a day.”

    The cloud computing giant employs around 300 people in the city. “Access to talent is important,” Wickham adds. “And you have that in Melbourne.” More than 52 percent of residents have a tertiary education, and the city’s world-class institutes boast a competitive edge in software engineering, cybersecurity and data sciences. Melbourne produces 38 percent of the nation’s tech graduates, more than any other Australian state. “We’ve always been a state that supports our knowledge capital,” says Philip Dalidakis, Victoria’s Minister for Innovation and the Digital Economy.

    This July, after three years in the city, Google increased its Melbourne footprint, opening up a new office in the central business district. The brightly colored office will house more than a hundred employees when it’s fully staffed, up from an original team of four. James Merlino, Victoria’s Deputy Premier, describes the expansion as a “great shot in the arm, a great boost to our tech sector.”

    Melbourne’s skilled work force, coupled with the affordable cost of doing business, also makes it attractive for the regional operations of multinationals to perform more sophisticated functions. In addition to customer service, sales and marketing, Google’s Melbourne outfit is taking on software engineering work in cloud-based solutions and geo-mapping capabilities. “We have a cross-functional team represented by a dozen different parts of the business,” says Sean McDonell, the company’s site lead here. Meanwhile, the cloud-based customer services company Zendesk and mobile payment provider Square both picked Melbourne to open their first engineering labs outside the Valley.

    Even as Melbourne comes into its own as an international tech hub, it has had to create its own model. “We can’t replicate the Silicon Valley model, nor should we try,” says Dalidakis. Wickham, who also sits on the Innovation Expert Panel of the Victoria State Government, sees an opportunity for Melbourne to play to its strengths and deepen relationships between the government, universities and the private sector. “We need to bring in density and be intentional about getting the different parts of the ecosystem to work in unison,” he says, citing the example of the Melbourne Innovation District, a partnership among the city of Melbourne, Melbourne University and RMIT to activate the Carlton Connect precinct.

    Homegrown Success Stories

    In east Melbourne’s Richmond neighborhood, Patrick Llewellyn is pulling an espresso at the 99designs office, an airy, industrial loft in a former factory. “This area reminds me of SoMa in San Francisco,” he muses, referring to the trendy patchwork of warehouses that have been turned into coworking spaces, bars and studios.

    Spun off a decade ago from its parent tech company, SitePoint, 99designs is a global creative platform that makes it easy for designers and clients to work together on projects.

    As CEO, Llewellyn heads up a 120-strong team spread across Melbourne, Oakland, Calif., and Berlin. After joining the company in 2009, he moved to San Francisco to better service the company’s customer base in the U.S. and to be closer to the investment community in the Valley. The company received $35 million in financial backing from Accel, the venture capital firm that supported Dropbox, Etsy and Facebook. To date, the company has fulfilled almost one million projects and has 10,000 active designers on its network at any given time.

    A meeting with Victoria’s Premier, Daniel Andrews, two years ago persuaded Llewellyn to run the global business from his home city. “I could see how much he cared about the sector,” he says. “The government is very progressive, and the ecosystem has changed dramatically over the past 10 years. An influx of accelerators and incubators and the creation of high-quality local VC funds have transformed Melbourne into a city that can compete internationally. I see crowds building at start-up conferences, and there is a new wave of founders benefiting from this increased investment and supportive infrastructure.”

    Didier Elzinga, the founder of Culture Amp, agrees: “What’s impressive is to see how the tech scene is registering on the government’s radar, and we’re also seeing more interest on the venture capital side.” Culture Amp helps organizations improve their workplace culture by using its software platform to collect, analyze and act on employee feedback. Its client list includes more than 1,500 companies of all sizes and industries, including Adobe, Slack, Oracle, AirBnb, Yelp and McDonalds. Like 99designs, Culture Amp caught the eye of an American venture capital firm, Felicis Ventures, in 2015, after the managing partner noticed many of his portfolio companies had engaged Culture Amp’s services and decided to fund the start-up.

    Elzinga says that Melbourne is an excellent place from which to launch a global business. “I wanted to build a business that was global from day one,” he says, adding that he chose Melbourne over Sydney for the former’s superior quality of life. Besides offering excellent coffee, international cuisine and a host of accessible sporting amenities, the government has invested heavily in smart city infrastructure and has dedicated a $60 million fund to the sector. Many civic spaces have Wi-Fi, making it possible to work outdoors and still be connected to the internet. Everywhere you look, people are using their phones to pay, find their way around or hail a cab.

    Success stories like Culture Amp and 99designs are piquing the interest of more accelerators and venture capitalists, and blue skies are in the forecast for Melbourne’s tech scene. The Silicon Valley-based incubator Acceleprise has chosen Melbourne as its first global outpost and plans to announce its first cohort of start-ups at the start of 2019. “Melbourne’s ecosystem is very open,” says Rohit Bhargava, the local director. “The snapshot is that there are a lot of great founders and start-ups here.”

    To Bhargava, increased global attention is a natural outcome of his city’s tech maturation: “Melbourne is also the cultural and creative heart of Australia, which means that our entrepreneurs approach things very differently when it comes to finding solutions.”
