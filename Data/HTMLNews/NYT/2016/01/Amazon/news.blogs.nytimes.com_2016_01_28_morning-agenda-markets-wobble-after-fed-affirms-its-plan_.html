    MARKETS WOBBLE AFTER FED AFFIRMS ITS PLANS | The Federal Reserve did not raise its benchmark interest rate, but indicated that its plan to gradually increase it remained intact,
    Binyamin Appelbaum reports in The New York Times.

    The statement suggested that officials were weighing an increase as soon as the next meeting in March, but it made clear that weak global growth and volatile financial markets meant that it would raise rates gradually.

    The policy statement suggested that the Fedâs confidence in the economic outlook had deteriorated since December. âThe committee is closely monitoring global economic and financial developments and is assessing their implications for the labor market and inflation, and for the balance of risks to the outlook,â the statement said.

    Some sectors, including job growth, spending by businesses and consumers, and the revival of the housing market were doing well. But domestic economic growth apparently slowed in the final months of 2015 and the sharp fall in equity markets at the start of the year has weighed on confidence. The dollar also continues to strengthen, limiting demand for American exports.

    This weakness means that some analysts believe that the Fed is unlikely to raise rates again until the summer.

    Stocks fell broadly in response to the announcement, smothering a small rally earlier in the day, as The Associated Press reports.

    Investors sold tech stocks, already under pressure after a shaky outlook from Apple, as well as consumer stocks like travel booking sites and cruise lines. The dollar was steady after the announcement.

    Stocks swung on Thursday in Asia and oil led the retreat in commodities, as Bloomberg reports. Treasuries were also little changed.

    5 EX-BROKERS CLEARED IN LONDON LIBOR TRIAL | A jury acquitted five former brokers of charges that they helped a onetime trader at UBS and Citigroup manipulate an important benchmark interest rate known as Libor,

    Chad Bray reports in DealBook.

    The London jury is still deliberating over one count of conspiracy to defraud against a sixth broker, Darrell P. Read, who worked at the British financial firm ICAP. The jury had found Mr. Read not guilty on a separate conspiracy count on Wednesday.

    The acquittals deal a blow to the British authorities who have been criticized for not being as aggressive as the United States Justice Department in prosecuting financial crimes. The trials came after a half-decade investigation that led to billions of dollars in fines, cost one chief executive his job and damaged the reputations of some of the worldâs biggest banks.

    Lawyers for the six former brokers on trial in London argued that the men were being made scapegoats for a flawed system and that they had lied to Mr. Hayes about their ability to influence the rate.

    The former brokers worked at the British financial firms ICAP, RP Martin and the Tullett Prebon Group. Prosecutors had accused the men of helping Tom Hayes, a former trader at UBS and Citigroup, and others profit by rigging Libor, which helps determine the borrowing costs for trillions of dollars in loans.

    Mr. Hayes, the first person to go on trial in Britain over criminal charges related to Libor manipulation, was convicted in a separate trial in August of conspiracy to defraud.

    FRANK QUATTRONE TO STEP DOWN AS CHIEF OF QATALYST | Frank P. Quattrone is yielding day-to-day management of his firm, the Qatalyst Group, to one of his top lieutenants,
    Andrew Ross Sorkin and Michael J. de la Merced report in DealBook.

    George Boutros will take over as chief executive, overseeing overall management.

    The appointment is the first major leadership change since Qatalyst was established. Mr. Quattrone began the firm in March 2008, seven months after clearing his name in a bitter legal fight with the federal government.

    Mr. Quattrone had become known as a banker of choice during the dot-com boom, but was waylaid by charges of hindering a government investigation into initial public offerings at Credit Suisse. Those charges were dropped in 2007.

    Qatalyst has grown since 2008, from three employees to 53, and it says it has advised on more than 90 deals, including the sales of OpenTable, Motorola Mobility and Dropcam.

    The promotion of Mr. Boutros, who has worked with Mr. Quattrone since 1992, recognizes the role he has played at Qatalyst.

    Mr. Quattrone will become executive chairman and focus on strategy.
