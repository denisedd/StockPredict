    A TIDAL WAVE OF CORPORATE MIGRANTS SEEKING TAX SHELTERS | Until lawmakers in Washington reform the tax code, American companies will continue their exodus in search of lower tax rates,

    Andrew Ross Sorkin writes in the DealBook column.

    The announcement by Johnson Controls that it would renounce its United States corporate citizenship by selling itself to Tyco International, based in Ireland, is just the latest effort to flee the United States after a year that saw moves by Pfizer, Medtronic and Coca-Cola.

    Benefiting from the governmentâs $80 billion bailout plan for automakers and hundreds of millions of dollars in tax breaks over the last few decades did not seem to deter Johnson Controls.

    Johnson Controls and Tyco expect to save at least $150 million in annual tax payments, Leslie Picker reports in DealBook.

    It is the 13th inversion to be announced in 16 months, according to Dealogic, and has already attracted criticism. âThese efforts to shirk U.S. tax obligations leave American taxpayers holding the bag while corporations juice more revenues and profits,â said Hillary Clinton. Bernie Sanders called the companies âcorporate deserters.â

    And there are probably at least another dozen deals of meaningful size being negotiated, Mr. Sorkin writes.

    What will it take to pass legislation that will prevent such steady corporate migration? Mr. Sorkin asks.

    Shaming companies involved in inversions is not enough. Lawyers and accountants seem to find a way around every new tax law.

    Ultimately, inversions will only stop when the corporate tax code changes and makes it more attractive for American companies to be American companies.

    WHY SHKRELI WILL TALK ON SOCIAL MEDIA BUT NOT TO CONGRESS | Martin Shkreli can take to social media to communicate his views, but the Constitution lets him refuse to talk to those who desperately want to speak to him,

    Peter J. Henning writes in DealBook.

    Mr. Shkreli, the former chief of Turing Pharmaceuticals, plans to assert the Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination to refuse to answer questions from congressional committees and turn over documents related to a drug price increase.

    Testimony before Congress is under oath, unlike a tweet, and lawyers steer their clients clear of that if there is any risk that the statements could be used in a future prosecution. So we have the odd situation of someone who loves to talk picking and choosing when he does so, Mr. Henning points out.

    Mr. Shkreliâs lawyer has told the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform that his client planned to assert his Fifth Amendment privilege because Turing is being investigated by the Federal Trade Commission over possible antitrust violations. The agency cannot pursue criminal charges, but a violation could result in a prosecution.

    Mr. Shkreli can assert his Fifth Amendment privilege while proclaiming his innocence, but a letter from a member of the committee states that a failure to appear will result in Congress seeking to hold him in contempt.

    Congressional committees usually insist that witnesses who intend to assert the Fifth Amendment appear before them and refuse to answer questions in public, leading to some public haranguing.

    The law is less clear about whether a former corporate employee can assert the Fifth Amendment to refuse to turn over business records. Mr. Shkreli has received a subpoena for documents from Turing related to the price increase of a drug called Daraprim.

    It remains unclear whether Mr. Shkreli would be vindicated by asserting the Fifth, but he is likely to keep up his barrage of comments on social media. The right to silence can be frustrating to those who are demanding answers, Mr. Henning writes.
