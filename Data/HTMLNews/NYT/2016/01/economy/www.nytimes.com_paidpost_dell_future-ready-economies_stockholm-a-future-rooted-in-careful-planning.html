    Stockholm: A Future Rooted
    in Careful Planning

    Thinking ahead with smart investments in infrastructure and education pay off for an isolated city on Europe’s northern frontier.

    Watch the video below.

    The city of Stockholm, spread over 14 islands on the northern edge of Europe, realized long ago that its future depended on planning ahead not just to survive long winters but also to break through its isolation to forge connections to the rest of the world. This mindset of thoughtful planning continues to return rewards.

    Today, the Swedish capital is among the fastest-growing urban centers in Europe, with a population that’s expected to grow 11 percent to more than 1 million by 2020. It’s also a major exporter of technology, with 22,000 tech startups alone. The birthplace of Skype, Minecraft and Spotify, it has more “unicorns” per capita — startups valued over $1 billion — than any other region outside California’s Silicon Valley.

    The Stockholm metropolitan area ranked No. 9 among the 50 regions surveyed as part of the Dell Future Ready Economies Model, with higher-than-average scores in the three categories of human capital, commerce and infrastructure.

    Planning and investment in education, urban development and technology have created an environment that’s attracting new people, ideas and businesses. It’s a virtuous cycle that’s a common theme among the world’s future-ready economies.

    “Stockholm is the kind of city that we really like to point out,” said Jim Diffley, senior director at HIS, which partnered with Dell to develop the rankings. “You could compare them to Silicon Valley and that type of environment. They don't get the big splashy press because of where they're located, but they have been quietly making a lot of investment and being progressive.”

    One area of investment is education, where teachers and school officials are investing time and money to understand what tools are the most effective, said Suss Forssman Thullberg, former head of communications at the Swedish Agency for School Improvement.

    “We have a strong tradition in Sweden of innovative education,” she says. “It has taken a long time to really find out from the teacher’s perspective what are the tools that really make a difference.”

    At the Viktor Rydbergs School, for example, students are using the popular world-building game Minecraft to learn how to collaborate and build. ”They’re actually learning to understand each other and each other’s differences,” says Stina Afsenius, a teacher at the school. “It’s important to include technology.”

    Stockholm also invested early and aggressively in the Internet. In 1994, the Swedish government announced that it would create a fiber-optic network spanning its capital city. The network now includes enough fiber to wrap around the globe 30 times. All businesses and 90 percent of homes are connected, and about 94 percent of schoolchildren have access to the Internet at school.

    “They realized, very early on, what impact it could have on the city,” said Niklas Agevik, chief executive of Instabridge, the world’s largest crowdsourced Wi-Fi network. “Growing up in Stockholm had a huge impact on my view of technology, from having fun with it to what I’m doing today — building a company out of it.”
