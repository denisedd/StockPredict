    GOLDMAN TO PAY UP TO $5 BILLION TO SETTLE FAULTY MORTGAGE CLAIMS | Goldman is again paying the price for its role in the financial crisis, seven years after the worst of it,
    Matthew Goldstein reports in DealBook.

    The Wall Street firm said it had agreed to a civil settlement of up to $5 billion with federal prosecutors and regulators to resolve claims stemming from the marketing and selling of faulty mortgage securities to investors.

    Goldman, which reports its fourth-quarter earnings on Wednesday, said the settlement would reduce earnings in that period by about $1.5 billion on an after-tax basis.

    It had attracted criticism for making money by betting against the housing market in the run-up to the crisis. But its role in churning out faulty mortgages and securities backed by home loans to borrowers who could not afford them was smaller than that of other Wall Street firms, so its settlement is smaller than the sums paid by Bank of America and JPMorgan Chase.

    The agreement requires Goldman to pay $2.385 billion in civil penalties and $875 million in cash, and provide up to $1.8 billion in relief to consumers.

    The settlement comes after the $3 billion that Goldman paid to the Federal Housing Finance Agency in 2014 to settle claims over the sale of flawed mortgage securities. It also paid $550 million to the Securities and Exchange Commission for its role in putting together a collateralized debt obligation called Abacus.
