    Q.

    Was it overly harsh for federal prosecutors to seek (and win) long mandatory jail sentences for the father-and-son ranchers, Dwight and Steven Hammond, whose arson case supposedly inspired the refuge invasion?

    A.

    The federal law on arson on federal lands is just one of many statutes with inflexible mandatory sentencing. Inflexibility in such laws has backfired in a huge way, as has been clear for so long in tens of thousands of convictionsÂ for nonviolent crimes swelling American prison populations.

    DwightÂ Hammond, 73, and his family have, on the whole, been a creditable presence in their community, as Les Zaitz described in The Oregonian. But in the two arson fires in question, prosecutors argued there were circumstances putting people — and not just brush and wildlife — at risk. (Justin Wm. Moyer filed a deep, and murky, dive on the fires for the Washington Post.)

    With more flexible laws, the circumstances might have justified more leniency, but there was a case to be made for significant jail time, and the government pursued it.

    At the same time, it makes sense for Congress to take a hard look at the relevant federal law.

    Update, 1:34 p.m. | | After reading the piece, Bob Sallinger, the conservation director for the Audubon Society of Portland, sent this invaluable comment about a “gonzo birdathon” that is already planned for Malheur, and much more:

    Portland Audubon has always been very active at Malheur but we absolutely intend to amp up opportunities for the public to engage with the amazing place. And yes, our “gonzo birdathon” trip is going to Malheur this year (it was planned before the occupation). Maybe we will make it “Super Gonzo!”

    I would add one more thing to your list of solutions:

    In 2013, Malheur completed its Comprehensive Conservation Plan. This is a long-range vision and management plan that all refuges are required to complete. Malheur stood alone in the refuge system for deciding to have a very inclusive, transparent stakeholder process which included local ranchers, county commissioners, tribes, conservation groups, local, state and federal agencies, etc.

    We met many times over the course of three years and much to everybody’s surprise emerged with a consensus, collaborative approach that includes major initiatives on both the refuge and surrounding ranch lands. It is a plan that tries to respect both the ecology and the economics of the region. The groups involved have remained actively engaged in implementing the plan. It includes one of the largest wetland restoration efforts ever undertaken.

    On a landscape rife with tremendous challenges, where consensus and collaboration are being accomplished, we need to double down.

    This should be a call to action for the community, non-governmental stakeholders, and local, state, and especially federal agencies to invest deeply in the Malheur effort. It is a critically important opportunity, but it is fragile and, as current events amply demonstrate, it needs to succeed.

    The real tragedy would be if these out-of-state self-declared militias were able to leave the impression that Malheur is a flashpoint for conflict, when in fact it should be seen as an incredible opportunity for collaboration for both the ecology and the economy of the region.

    My hope is that the legacy of this illegal occupation will be a renewed commitment to that collaboration.

    Update, 1:58 p.m. | On Twitter, Shawn Regan of the free-market Property and Environment Research Center pointed to a 2014 analysis he wrote for The Wall Street Journal making the case for buyouts of grazing rights as a significant step toward a sustainable West.

    I’m all for such solutions and, just yesterday, had noticed a series of pieces Regan wrote about an earlier dispute over federally-owned resources in the West — around Tim DeChristopher‘s effort to disrupt auctions of oil and gas leases. DeChristopher landed in jail for nearly two years, when he and Regan (from different ideological vantage points) agree that it should be legal for anyone to acquire those rights, to use or keep in the ground.
