    A GROWING CONFLICT IN WALL ST. BUYOUTS | A new, insidious relationship has developed among private equity firms, the banks that lend them billions to fund their buyouts and the law firms that advise on these deals,
    Andrew Ross Sorkin writes in the DealBook column.

    Typically, a bank making a loan to a private equity firm planning a big acquisition would hire an external law firm to scrutinize the loan and the transaction.

    Instead of allowing a bank to hire its own lawyers to vet a loan, many large private equity firms, including Blackstone, Apollo Global Management, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts and Carlyle, now require the banks to use their chosen law firms. The private equity firms will also pay for the law firmâs services.

    This is referred to as âdesignated lender counselâ and is the latest conflict-ridden practice on Wall Street.

    Bankers and their lawyers complain that the law firms assigned have little allegiance to them and might not have their best interests at heart, but the pressure to secure these big deals mean few are willing to publicly criticize the practice.

    In the past, each bank lending money hired its own law firm so each transaction was scrutinized by multiple firms. The private equity firm still paid the legal fees.

    Private equity firms now defend having designated lender counsel, saying that it saves clients from having to pay double or triple the amount in legal fees.

    The Securities Industry and Financial Markets Association had already warned about the use of designated lender counsel in the context of municipal bond offerings. It said that municipalities should disclose the practice in their bond offering documents, noting that âAny undue influence by an issuer, however, that calls into question the qualifications or independence of underwriterâs counsel may create risk to the issuer.â

    G.M. INVESTS IN LYFT | The founders of Lyft, the ride-hailing service, imagined that the future of transportation would involve fewer cars on the road. Now a car maker is helping them reach that goal,
    Mike Isaac reports in The New York Times.

    General Motors invested $500 million in Lyft, making up half of its latest round of financing. The funding values the start-up at $4.5 billion, not including the new capital.

    In addition, G.M. will work with Lyft to develop an on-demand network of self-driving cars, moving into an area of research to which Google, Tesla and Uber have devoted enormous resources.

    They will set up short-term car rental hubs across the United States where people can pick up a vehicle and drive it for Lyft to earn money.

    Car makers have been rushing to adapt as consumer habits have changed and the use of ride-hailing companies has increased.

    There have been partnerships between carmakers and auto start-ups before. G.M. teamed with RelayRides, a car-sharing marketplace, Ford struck a deal with Getaround, another car-sharing start-up, and Daimler has been experimenting with a car rental service.

    Mr. Ammann said that the deal was an opportunity, rather than a threat, as G.M.âs core profit came from cars sold outside the urban environments where Lyft primarily operates.

    By branching out, G.M. has a better chance of navigating the changing trends in car ownership and use,
    Robert Cyran and Antony Currie write in Breakingviews. The hubs will give G.M. an extra market for its vehicles and the flexible approach aligns with part-time and multijob employment trends.
