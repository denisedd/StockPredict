    BANK RULE DISTORTING PERFORMANCE IS REPEALED | An âAlice in Wonderlandâ accounting rule that allowed banks to book huge gains because they were stressed has been overturned, Peter Eavis reports in DealBook.

    The rule was instated as the financial system was starting to crumble and the Financial Accounting Standards Board voted to get rid of it, along with several other changes.

    The financial industry had pushed for the rule, which had enabled struggling firms to print magical profits. But banks ended up despising the rule because of the confusion it added to their earnings statements.

    The rule took an accounting approach that says balance sheets need to closely reflect prices in the marketplace. If a bank holds bonds, its financial statements must reflect the value of the bonds in the market.

    This so-called fair value approach can prevent banks from hiding losses and provides information to investors assessing whether to lend to banks. During the financial crisis, it gave regulators and investors an approximation of the losses the banks were having on bad mortgage securities.

    But the enforcement of the rule that was scrapped on Tuesday allowed banks under stress to book profits on the possibility that they could buy back their own distressed bonds at a discount. The bank might not even have the cash to buy back its own debt even if it wanted to.
