    Mary Williams Walsh reports in DealBook.

    Puerto Rico passed a critical debt-payment deadline on Jan. 1 and made some of the payments due by defaulting on others. The defaults prompted two bond insurers to file suit.

    Prepa also owed debt payments at the time, but a group of its creditors helped, in effect, to finance the payments in an effort to keep the restructuring plan in play.

    Puerto Rico has roughly $72 billion of debt, most of it in municipal bonds. Prepa is one of the largest single issuers.

    Prepa had the authority to set its own rates for electricity. The law required it to set rates high enough to cover its costs, including borrowing costs, but it did not do that.

    Instead its base rate, which was fixed in 1989, had an adjustment added that rose and fell with the price of the fuel Prepa used. This meant that its profit increases were linked to fuel cost increases and becoming more efficient would have made it less profitable, said Representative Doug Lamborn, Republican of Colorado.

    Prepa also has a longstanding practice of bartering with municipalities on the island. It has not paid property taxes for its easements on municipal land for years, giving the municipalities free electricity in exchange.
