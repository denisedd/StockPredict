    Reducing Preventable Harm in Hospitals

    By David Bornstein
    January 26, 2016 3:21 am January 26, 2016 3:21 am

    Fixes looks at solutions to social problems and why they work.

    First of two articles.

    Each year, in the United States, millions of patients are harmed while receiving care in hospitals. They get infections, experience adverse reactions to drugs, develop dangerous bed sores, or come down with pneumonia from the very ventilators meant to help them breathe.

    The estimates of the number of people who die each year as a result of hospital errors have ranged from as many as 98,000 in a landmark Institute of Medicine report from 1999 to as many as 440,000 in a 2013 study.

    Itâs believed that most of these deaths could be prevented if health care providers always adhered to evidence-informed practices. In recent years, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, an arm of the Department of Health and Human Services, has sought to drive improvements by linking payments to hospital performance, including patient safety.

    But hospital care is astonishingly complex. In the United States each year, there are over 35 million hospital admissions and 136 million visits to emergency rooms. Doctors, nurses and technicians conduct so many procedures and dispense so much medication that even seemingly tiny error rates can translate to tens of thousands of patients harmed.

    Amid the complexity, the chaotic pace, and the increasing need for coordination, how can hospitals do better to improve patient safety? How can they ensure, for example, that every single time a patient receives a medication, precautions are taken to reduce the risks of an adverse drug reaction?

    Or that every time medical practitioners insert a central line catheter into a major artery or vein, they wash their hands, use sterile drapes, and remove it as soon as itâs no longer needed?

    Or that every patient confined to a bed is regularly watched to prevent pressure sores, which can cause life-threatening infections?

    The answer is systems with safeguards. One way to understand the problem is the âSwiss cheese model,â which was proposed by James Reason, an expert in the psychology of human error. In a good system, there are multiple layers of defense, each compensating for weaknesses in other layers. System failures occur when layers share the same vulnerabilities â and the holes go all the way through the cheese. Thatâs when mistakes harm patients.

    The big challenge isnât just designing systems with better defenses; itâs gaining acceptance for them, and properly implementing them. Over the past decade, for example, many hospitals have adopted a key feature of aviation safety â checklists â to improve safety in areas where standard protocols can save many lives, such as inserting central line catheters, using ventilators, and while performing surgery and assisting childbirth.

    When well implemented, the results have been stunning. In the groundbreaking Michigan Keystone project, a collaborative safety-driven endeavor by numerous hospitals that began in 2003 and was led by Peter Pronovost, a five-step checklist led to a 66 percent drop in central line infections. (Over the past 20 years, the rates of central line infections in the United States have dropped substantially.)

    However, as checklists have proliferated, the results have been inconsistent, often because front-line practitioners rejected them or adopted them halfheartedly. The devotion to protocol that pilots see as integral to their professionalism is derided by some doctors as âcookbook medicine.â Atul Gawande, a surgeon and contributor to The New Yorker, has said that the high value that physicians place on autonomy and independence makes them reluctant to submit to checklists. (Watch his TED Talk.)

    So the big question is: How can health systems be made safer when success means changing the attitudes and habits of health care professionals at a time when many are overwhelmed and deeply frustrated by all of the demands being made on them? What does it take to get them to embrace, with urgency, new ways of working?

    Many individual hospitals or health centers have made headway in improving safety. But the nationwide gains have been mixed, with wide variations across and within hospitals.

    Thatâs the essential problem that a major federal initiative called the Partnership for Patients, which was created in 2011 by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, is trying to solve. Its goals are ambitious: a 40 percent reduction in preventable harm for a range of conditions and a 20 percent reduction in hospital readmissions.

    In December, the Medicare and Medicaid services centers released an encouraging report. Based primarily on data from a randomized national sample of patient hospital charts (pdf), they estimated that between 2011 and 2014, patients experienced 2.1 million fewer incidents of harm compared with a baseline of 2010. Applying expected mortality rates and costs, they estimated that those gains translated to 87,000 deaths prevented and roughly $20 billion saved. (Some researchers have criticized the centersâ measurement approach, because it doesnât identify changes that can be tracked or compared in real time at the hospital level.)

    The Partnership is led by Dennis Wagner and Dr. Paul McGann, who have years of experience advancing change in messy, real-world systems. McGann led federal government programs to improve quality in nursing homes and home care. And from 2003 to 2006, Wagner ran a Health and Human Services initiative, the United States Organ Donation Breakthrough Collaborative, which was credited with quadrupling the growth rate of organ donation over four years.

    That experience is instructive. Each year in the United States, thousands of people die while waiting for an organ transplant. A donor can save as many as eight lives. But to make it happen, patientsâ families and caregivers in trauma centers need to prepare for donation even as they face the imminent death of a loved one or fight to save their patient. This is sensitive work, and itâs a big reason the national rate for organ donation from deceased patients was below 50 percent in 2012.

    Wagner enlisted the help of the Institute for Healthcare Improvement, which runs a program called the âBreakthrough Seriesâ to spread innovative ideas through a collaborative learning model. It works this way: Organizations agree to work together to solve a problem. They commit to similar goals. They engage in rapid-cycle testing of ideas and learning, and share their results with everyone. âItâs meant for situations where thereâs a clear gap between the way a current system works and the way it could work when thereâs knowledge to close the gap,â explained Marie Schall, who leads the instituteâs work in this area.

    Wagner found a small group of hospitals with organ donation rates over 70 or 80 percent. Clearly, it was possible to do better. Would they share their practices with others? He went to hospitals with low donation rates, and asked the leaders if they would join and commit to a 75 percent organ donation rate. Surprisingly, he saw that just making a simple request was often enough to bring people onboard.

    The collaborative organized learning sessions and showcased successes. Wagner saw that the best thing for the government to do was get out of the way and let hospital teams learn from one another. As more hospitals joined, the pace of learning accelerated. Wagnerâs team checked in regularly to track progress and challenge partners to do more. Within months, organ donation rates were rising.

    Two years after the project began in 2005, the collaborative held an awards ceremony in Pittsburgh, celebrating more than 1,500 people from 184 hospitals where organ donation rates had reached the 75 percent goal. Wagner recalled that everybody was asked to stand. âThen from the corners of the room,â he said, â50 people come forward looking for someone standing to hang a medal of honor.     Since 2006, the national rate of organ donation has remained at the significantly elevated level it was at when the campaign ended; however, there is still considerable room for improvement.
