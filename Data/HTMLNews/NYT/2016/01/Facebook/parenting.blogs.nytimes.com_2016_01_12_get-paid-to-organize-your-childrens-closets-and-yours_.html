    Get Paid to Organize Your Children’s Closets (and Yours)

    By Kj DellâAntonia
    January 12, 2016 12:37 pm January 12, 2016 12:37 pm

    Anyone can clean out a closet. We pull out all the clothes and apply some standard (for children, that’s little more than “does it fit?”; for adults, it ranges from “have I worn it recently?” to the organizational guru Marie Kondo’s now-famous question, “Does it bring joy?”) If an item passes, back it goes, while failures are headed for donation boxes. Typically, especially for ourselves, we hang too many things back up. When clothes are “as good as new,” or were expensive, sometimes it’s hard to persuade ourselves to give them away, even if on some level we know we don’t really want them any more.

    Last fall, I tried adding another question to the mix: Can I get cash for this? Online consignment stores and what essentially amount to used clothing buyers, particularly for designer goods, began popping up in my Facebook feed, promising to help me “reclaim the value” in my closet â and, I soon discovered, my children’s closets as well.

    Real world consignment shops are intimidating. It’s just too daunting a task; another errand for the list. Clothing resale sites promise to make the whole thing as easy as returning shoes to Zappos. Maybe, if I could negate the guilt of the “perfectly good” clothes by getting back even a little of the money I had spent on them, I would find it easier to let them go.

    After some exploring (try searching “online clothing consignment” or “online children’s clothing consignment”) I narrowed my choices. I would use a service only if it would send me a bag and a prepaid label. It had to take a wide variety of clothing brands, promise to donate to charity any clothing that didn’t meet its standards and, most important, pay me immediately and in cash or a cash equivalent. I didn’t want to wait until my clothing sold, or get stuck with a site credit I would never use.

    I had a few items for designer-goods-only MaterialWrld, the site I had first seen on Facebook, including several expensive mistakes, like a Diane von Furstenberg dress with the tags still on that I’ve had for years. For most of our clothing, I chose ThredUP.com and Dessie Kids. Both took most of the children’s brands I had, and ThredUP took women’s clothing as well. I emailed all three asking for what is generally called a “resale kit,” and received, within a little over a week, a branded bag, a prepaid shipping label and instructions from both ThredUP and MaterialWrld. Dessie Kids sent a bag and instructions, but asked that I email with the package’s weight to request a shipping label. They were happy with an approximate weight, so when the time came, I just stood on a bathroom scale with and without the package and did the math.

    I culled the children’s closets, pulling most from my younger children’s (the older children’s clothing tends to become hand-me-downs for younger siblings). I had stacks of pants, a few jackets, a number of collared button-down shirts (which my children only wore under duress.) To see how baby clothes fared in the online consignment world, I asked a friend for a few cast-offs, and she obliged with several sweaters and a pair of teeny overalls.

    In spite of warnings about backlog, all three sites responded within four weeks to my bags. Dessie Kids was the fastest, getting back to me within two weeks with a friendly email explaining what they did and didn’t want and making a single offer for the whole lot. MaterialWrld and ThredUP made individual offers on each item. (MaterialWrld explained why certain items hadn’t been accepted; ThredUP did not). None of the offers were high; my designer goods netted me just $175 (in the form of a gift card good at major retailers including Saks Fifth Avenue, Nordstrom and Barneys), while Dessie Kids paid $25 for a bag, which included mostly baby clothes and the total from ThredUP for both my clothing and the children’s clothing was just $61.09 (Dessie Kids offered payment through PayPal or a mailed check; ThredUP offers PayPal, a site credit or a donation to one of its selected charities). Still that’s $269.09 (less a dollar or two for the PayPal payments I chose) and five much cleaner closets.

    I made some mistakes in packing my bags. The most glaring was sending Dessie Kids pants that were above their limit of a size 8 (I hadn’t noticed that). I also sent two items that were apparently stained to MaterialWrld â it might have been worthwhile, for that higher-end site, to have things professionally cleaned before sending. (I will say I didn’t see the stains.) I also sent in clothing that was simply too worn.

    “The wash and wear thing is the thing that trips people up as much as anything,” said Myra Danehy, a founder of Dessie Kids. “How much wear is too much?” She suggested looking for fading, pilling or wear at the neck. If any of those things are visible, they’re likely to reject the item.

    “We’re picky,” she said. “We want our customers, when they receive an item, to say, ‘Wow. I paid so little for this and this is such a great deal.’ Because of that, we go over everything with a fine-tooth comb.” They’re always happy to respond to a customer’s questions about what to send, she said, or even about individual items.

    The best things to sell to online consignment-style stores are exactly the things that are the hardest to take out of the closet without a push â the things that make you say, “but this is as good as new.” They’re the things you thought your child would love or the formal clothes worn only a few times, or, for adults, the purchases that just didn’t work out, bought for someone you turned out not to be. The possibility of getting even a little money out of those items makes for an added incentive to let them go â even if, once it’s out of the closet, you realize something won’t meet resale standards. Now it’s out. Once you’ve recognized you’d be willing to part with something, it’s easier not to hang it back up.

    Older children can learn to do the same, said the professional organizer Laurie Palau, founder of Simply B Organized, who suggests involving children in closet clean-outs. “The earlier you can give your kids these organizing life skills, the better,” she said. But you have to work with them. “Too many people just say, ‘your room’s a disaster, go clean it up.’ Without a strategy, that becomes overwhelming.”

    Instead, help children navigate the process, Ms. Palau advised. Tell them to take one drawer, or the closet, and make three piles: keep, sell, donate. A fan of ThredUp (she has an unpaid partnership with the service and recommends it to her clients), she allows her two teenage daughters to use any money made from the sale of their clothing toward the purchase of new clothes, often on the site, where their dollars go further. “It’s an incentive to be mindful and say, ‘you know what, I don’t wear this, I’m not going to hang on to it just because,'” she said.

    Online consignment is unlikely to fund an entire fresh wardrobe. But it will help you to make the decision to give up things of your own that you’ve been hanging on to for the wrong reasons (especially if that reason is the all-too-common “but I spent too much on this”), and move along outgrown children’s clothes with less regret.

    It’s been a few months since my first foray into selling our clothes online. The seasons have changed; everyone has grown a little, school clothes and holiday clothes have refilled the closets. I think it’s time to send for another resale bag.
