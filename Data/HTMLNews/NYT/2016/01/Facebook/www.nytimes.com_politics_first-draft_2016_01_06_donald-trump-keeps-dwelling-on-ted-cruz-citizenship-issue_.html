    Donald Trump Keeps Pushing Ted Cruz Citizenship Issue

    8:55 am ET 8:55 am ET
    By
    Alan Rappeport

    Updated, 6:10 p.m. | Donald J. Trump continued to fan the flames of doubt over Senator Ted Cruz’s citizenship on Wednesday, suggesting that his Canadian roots might be a problem if he won the Republican presidential nomination.

    The decision to confront Mr. Cruz more directly comes as Mr. Trump, who has dominated most national and state polls for months, faces the prospect of losing to the Texas senator in next month’s Iowa caucuses. Popular among evangelical Christians and conservatives, Mr. Cruz has become a favorite to win the first contest of the nominating process.

    The clash represents a shift in the Republican race because Mr. Trump and Mr. Cruz have largely steered clear of each other thus far, professing mutual admiration and agreement on many issues. The billionaire businessman warned, however, that he would eventually have to go after Mr. Cruz if he continued to rise in the polls. This week he has started to make good on that threat.

    Mr. Trump also addressed the issue in interviews released on Tuesday, and his remarks follow similar lines of subtle attack that he has used against Mr. Cruz recently. Last month he sought to discredit the senator’s Christian credentials, saying “not a lot of evangelicals come out of Cuba.” Mr. Cruz’s father is Cuban.

    Mr. Cruz is also widely seen as eligible for the presidency because he was born in Canada and his mother was a United States citizen. Under the Constitution this qualifies him as a “natural born citizen,” but some continue to debate the definition of the phrase “natural born.” PolitiFact noted last year that while most experts have agreed that Mr. Cruz meets the constitutional criteria, the issue is not completely settled because the Supreme Court has never weighed in on such a citizenship challenge.

    Paul D. Clement, who served as solicitor general in the administration of President George W. Bush, and Neal K. Katyal, who served as acting solicitor general in the Obama administration, took up the subject in the Harvard Law Review last year.

    This is not the first time that Mr. Trump has stirred controversy over a politician’s citizenship. He previously spearheaded the so-called birther movement to pressure President Obama to prove that he was actually born in the United States. Although Mr. Trump was proved wrong on the issue, he succeeded in getting Mr. Obama to release his birth certificate, and many Americans continue to express doubts about his nation of origin and religion.

    Similar “birther” questions have dogged presidential candidates before. In 2008, doubts were raised about Senator John McCain’s eligibility to run for president because he was born in the Panama Canal Zone in 1936. The issue surfaced when George Romney, who was born in Mexico, ran in 1968. There was also some debate about the status of Barry Goldwater’s candidacy since he was born in the Arizona territory in 1909, before it was a state.

    The Obama administration appeared to be amused by the dust-up on Wednesday. Asked about the prospect of Republicans nominating someone who was born in Canada, Josh Earnest, a spokesman for Mr. Obama, said: “It would be quite ironic if after seven or eight years of drama around the president’s birth certificate, if Republican primary voters were to choose Senator Cruz as their nominee, somebody who actually wasn’t born in the United States and only 18 months ago renounced his Canadian citizenship.”

    Whether Mr. Trump’s strategy will work this time remains unclear, as Mr. Cruz, who has laughed off such suggestions, is immensely popular with the Republican Party’s conservative base.

    At an event in Iowa on Wednesday, Mr. Cruz said his eligibility to run was settled law.

    Mr. Trump remains skeptical. Discussing the subject on Fox News on Wednesday, he said that Mr. Cruz might want to consider taking the issue to court in a pre-emptive fashion to alleviate any questions about his citizenship.

    As for his motives this time, Mr. Trump said that he was hopeful that Mr. Cruz’s citizenship would not be a problem.

    “I want to beat him on his own merits,” Mr. Trump said. “I like Ted a lot. I’d love to see him get it straightened out.”

    The issue was already percolating down to the campaign trail in Iowa. The Barbara Shultz, 68, of Winterset, Iowa, who retired from a state job in disaster relief, is making up her mind between whether to support Mr. Trump, Mr. Cruz or Ben Carson. She had heard that questions were raised about Cruz’s birth this week.

    “If it does become a problem, I hope he will admit it,” she said. “I had not really thought about it until the past week. I just assumed he was a citizen.” She added that she hoped he hasn’t been hiding something.”

    And at Mr. Cruz’s events in Iowa on Wednesday, voters dismissed his birthplace as a non-issue, and few expected Mr. Trump’s concerns to resonate.

    “Minor issue. Peripheral,” Mike Miller, 59, said at a Godfather’s Pizza in Spirit Lake, where Mr. Cruz held one of five events. “I don’t think the Democrats will pursue it at all.”

    Asked if the comments might backfire and hurt Mr. Trump, Mr. Miller asked rhetorically, “Do you think anything can hurt Donald Trump?”
