    OIL MARKET TESTS BANKS | Markets and economies have already been rattled by low oil prices, and now the United States banking system is being tested too,

    Michael Corkery reports in The New York Times.

    Banks are marking down the value of loans and setting aside reserves to absorb losses as oil producers struggle to pay their debts.

    On Tuesday, Bank of America said provisions for credit losses increased by $364 million in the fourth quarter because of the downturn in the energy sector. Citigroup, Wells Fargo and JPMorgan Chase have also said that oil issues affected their earnings.

    Although profits are being squeezed, the big banksâ capital cushions are not threatened. Analysts say this is a testament to the regulations put in place after the collapse of the mortgage market in 2008.

    But the worst pain could lie ahead. Although many banks have reduced credit lines to oil producers, some lenders are loath to cut off financing completely for fear of forcing energy companies into bankruptcy, energy lawyers and consultants say.

    Banks and regulators are also trying to determine the value of loans and forecast future losses, in the face of conflicting opinions about whether prices could rebound.

    The hedges that energy companies made to protect against price drops are also set to expire early this year â some companies are still receiving as much as $80 a barrel.

    Bank executives have stressed that oil and gas exposure makes up a small percentage of overall loans, but analysts are still questioning whether banks have stored away enough reserves and bank stocks have been hammered.

    EXPECT SOME UNICORNS TO LOSE THEIR HORNS | With the market forcing highfliers among tech start-ups to seek money at lower valuations, you can expect a bloodbath as employers, founders and investors clash,
    Steven Davidoff Solomon writes in the Deal Professor.

    The biggest battle at unicorns, or start-ups with a valuation of $1 billion or more, will be between the common and preferred shareholders.
    Employees and founders often hold common shares that sit below the preferred shares held by venture capitalists, which have protections not given to common shareholders.

    Liquidation preferences mean preferred shareholders receive a minimum payment if the company is sold. Anti-dilution rights provide that when new money comes in at a lower valuation â a so-called down round â investors from prior rounds get compensated.

    A down round hits employees and founders hard.
    Directors on the board of a unicorn will have to be careful to avoid a lawsuit.

    The second battle will be the fight to retain employees. One of the thorniest issues in dealing with down rounds is how a former unicorn keeps its employees after destroying the value of their shares.

    If stock incentives are no longer an option, cash bonus plans or other incentive plans that pay cash in the event of a success may be necessary. But that just isnât as good as stock with a potential for a big payout, so expect some hard bargaining.

    These efforts are likely to fail, Mr. Solomon says. Some companies do not survive down rounds, not because of finances but because they cannot keep their employees.

    There will also be battles between new and old money as preferred-stock holders duke it out with new down-round investors. The old money may rely on their protections as preferred shareholders, but a desperate company may try to seize control.

    The new money could also insist on super-contractual protections, like more aggressive anti-dilution rights and higher liquidation preferences.

    There might also be fights between the founders and everyone else. The new money could be in a position to decide whether to keep the founders, who will have to work harder and own a smaller stake if they stay.

    LEGISLATION COULD ADD TO SCRUTINY OF REGULATORS | Congressional efforts to scrutinize the regulators that write the rules for Wall Street are gaining momentum, potentially creating more obstacles to closer financial oversight,

    Victoria Finkle reports in DealBook.

    A bipartisan group of senators is working on a package of regulatory reform bills that is likely to include a measure to subject the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, the Securities and Exchange Commission and other independent agencies to a heightened cost-benefit analysis and review process, people briefed on the plans said.

    Supporters say the legislation would provide more consistency and transparency in the rule-making process. Critics warn that it is intended to create additional burdens and delays, while making rules more vulnerable to legal challenges.

    The Senate group needs at least two more Democrats to support the legislation to avoid having the package blocked on the floor. The measures could be introduced in the coming weeks.

    Other proposals are being considered as part of the Senate effort, including measures to establish a nine-person commission to review duplicative and outdated rules already on the books, and a requirement that regulators publish advance notices for all major rule-makings.
