    OIL PRICES VOLATILE WHILE CHINESE MARKETS SINK | Investors started the year by steering away from risk. Oil prices rose after Saudi Arabia cut diplomatic ties with Iran on Sunday, Bloomberg reports. The Middle East accounts for about 30 percent of global oil output. West Texas intermediate crude gained as much as 3.5 percent before slipping again, after a 1.2 percent advance on the final trading day of 2015.

    Saudi Arabiaâs move came after the execution of an outspoken Shiite cleric, Sheikh Nimr al-Nimr, led to criticism from Iranian leaders and protesters storming the Saudi Embassy in Tehran. Iranian diplomats were given 48 hours to leave the kingdom, Ben Hubbard reports in The New York Times.

    Ric Spooner, a chief analyst at CMC Markets in Sydney, Australia, told Bloomberg that although there was no immediate threat to production, markets were pricing in increased risk as they anticipated the problems escalating. Iran plans to raise exports once international sanctions are lifted and Saudi Arabia produced 10.25 million barrels a day in December, according to Bloomberg data.

    Chinaâs CSI 300 index of blue-chip shares fell 7 percent, after disappointing manufacturing data and further depreciation of the renminbi, Paul Mozur reports in DealBook. The drop prompted a halt in trading on the first day that circuit breakers were put into place.
    Almost every market in Asia fell on Monday in response to the weakness in China and geopolitical instability.

    The yen and gold strengthened as investors sought haven assets, Bloomberg reports. The yen reached the strongest level since October, putting pressure on automakers like Toyota and Nissan that earn the bulk over their revenues in foreign currencies, while gold jumped 0.8 percent after an annual decline in 2015.

    JOHN ANGELO DIES | John Angelo, who helped found Angelo, Gordon & Coompany, has died at the age of 74, Bloomberg News reports. He died on Jan. 1 after a long fight with cancer.

    Mr. Angelo oversaw the New York investment firm for 27 years as it accumulated $26 billion in assets under management. He first worked the bond floor at the New York Stock Exchange in 1966 before moving to Bear Stearns.

    He also worked at L.F. Rothschild, where he became a partner, then vice chairman and a member of the board. It was there that he worked with Michael Gordon, with whom he would establish Angelo, Gordon & Company.

    Under his leadership, the investment firm built a business to meet the financing needs of North American energy companies and invested early in Chelsea Market, which helped transform the meatpacking district.
