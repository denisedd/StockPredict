    CHINESE STOCKS PLUNGE AGAIN | The volatility in Chinese markets has not let up. The main Shanghai share index fell 5.3 percent on Monday and the Shenzhen index ended the day down 6.6 percent, Neil Gough reports in DealBook.

    The selloff spilled over into Hong Kong, where the main Hang Seng index closed down 2.8 percent. Markets in the rest of Asia, however, experienced only modest declines.

    On Monday, the government set the renminbiâs official exchange rate stronger against the dollar.
    The strengthening prompted a scramble for offshore renminbi in Hong Kong, where the currency is more freely traded, albeit in smaller volumes.

    The overnight bank-to-bank borrowing rate for offshore renminbi in Hong Kong spiked on Monday to a record high of 13.4 percent. Such a sharp, short-term spike in demand can indicate a so-called short squeeze, in which investors betting that the currency will continue to weaken are forced to buy it to avoid losses, temporarily bolstering its value.

    Shares in Europe inched lower in early trading. The blue-chip FTSE 100 in London declined 0.4 percent while the DAX in Frankfurt fell 0.3 percent.

    Jeff Sommer writes in the Strategies column. The unpleasant truth is that there is no reliable way to predict the short-term path of the market.

    But you might not know that from the forecasts of major Wall Street investment houses. Many analysts have issued very specific predictions and the consensus on Wall Street is that the S.&P. 500 will rise 7 percent for all of 2016.

    Bespoke Investment Group, however, began their 2016 forecast with this disarming statement: âWe have no idea.â

    What is likely, if not absolutely certain, is that the Wall Street consensus will be wrong.

    The Bespoke report reviewed the recent history of Wall Street predictions and found them wanting. Predictions were inaccurate in every single year, sometimes by preposterous margins.

    That does not mean that market analysis is useless, but data from S&P Dow Jones Indices indicate that most people would fail if they tried to predict the direction of the overall market or of particular stocks and bonds.

    When it comes to where the market is heading this year, you might as well flip a coin, Mr. Sommer says.
