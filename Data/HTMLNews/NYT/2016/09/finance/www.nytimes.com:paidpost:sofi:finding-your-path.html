    When Lana Stojcic walks into Microsoft’s sprawling Seattle office in the morning, she often takes a moment to think about just how far her career, and her life, has come. Born in Bosnia, Stojcic, a product-program manager with the software giant, was forced to flee her home when the Bosnian War broke out in 1992 — she remembers bombs whizzing by her elementary school. Living in America seemed like an impossible dream back then, but after escaping to Germany, she ended up in the U.S. in 1999.

    Despite not knowing English when she arrived, Stojcic was determined about planting her roots in America. She wanted badly to get a university education and embark on a successful career. “I saw my parents lose everything,” she says. “I wanted to have a better life for myself and to help them.” However, those plans were nearly stymied when she had to pay for college. She ended up taking out a $25,000 loan for her undergraduate degree, which ballooned to nearly $70,000 when she went to get her MBA.

    It’s no secret that student debt is a problem in America — the average student is graduating with $35,000 in debt, up from $10,000 two decades ago — but what many people don’t realize is that those ballooning personal balance sheets are causing people to take less career risk, which then prevents them from ultimately earning more in the future.

    Earnings Equal Prosperity

    For Stojcic, who is still paying off her loans, the debt has been mentally taxing. She feels as though it has prevented her, in some ways, from taking jobs she may have wanted. But early on in her career, Stojcic realized that by focusing on earning more, rather than simply saving money, she’d be able to pay down her debt faster and be more prosperous in the future.

    I’ve always wanted to engrain myself into a technology organization. And Microsoft is the crème de la crème.”— LANA STOJCIC
    Training & Adoption Manager at Microsoft

    Her first job in digital marketing earned her about $30,000 a year, but she knew it would help her build skillsets that she would need to land higher paying jobs down the road. Jobs that followed did indeed come with larger salaries, but she wasn’t just trying to increase her pay in the short-term. She wanted to have a sustainable salary while working at a place where she could play a part in the ongoing digital revolution. “I’ve always wanted to engrain myself into a technology organization,” she says. “And Microsoft is the crème de la crème.”

    Getting there took some career planning — and some risks, too. In 2012, Stojcic took a pay cut to work at Intel in a role that taught her more about strategy, a skill that she needed in order to make a career switch from digital marketing to program management. “I knew (taking a pay cut) was a good decision, because it was a stepping stone to a job that was going to pay a lot more,” she says. “I knew it would get me into a more secure situation.”

    Her additional earnings power has helped her to improve her lifestyle, too. She recently bought her first home, a single-family home, in Seattle. “Between student loans and living in an expensive city, it wouldn't have been possible without working toward a better career,” she says.

    The Career Journey

    Increasing earnings power, becoming debt-free and finding a rewarding career takes — you guessed it — work. Unlike in generations past, getting to where you want to be doesn’t always involve a straight line. In fact, 65 percent of school-aged children will be in careers we've never heard of, says Robert Park, head of career strategy and professional development at SoFi, a student loan refinancing company. That makes planning a career path even more challenging.

    As a result of these constant changes, the average American will embark on five different careers throughout their lifetime, Park says, which could include a number of jobs. He says he’s already on his fourth career — he was in the military, then went into law, followed by consulting, and now he’s in the career talent space, where he says he’s found his rhythm.

    So what’s the key to having a successful work life? Being open minded, flexible and thinking strategically about opportunities that present themselves, says Park.

    Money certainly comes into play, though it’s not necessarily about a job with the highest paycheck. It’s about earning enough for your lifestyle, which can depend on the city you live in, your cost of living, the size of your family and much more. “Ask yourself, ‘What do you want to obtain that’s important to you?’” says Park. “From there, you have to target the occupations that satisfy your intrinsic motivators and leverage your strengths. Use your natural aptitude, but at the same time, find something that has earnings potential.”

    It Takes a Village

    It helps to talk to peers and career experts about that journey. In 2015, Stojcic went to SoFi to see if she could consolidate her student loans. The company, which also provides loan refinancing, offered her a much better rate, which is allowing her to pay off her debt in just five years compared to the original 10. But SoFi doesn’t just consolidate and refinance loans, it also has a number of career-related services that help customers succeed in the working world. For 12 months, Stojcic took advantage of its career advice offerings. She had regular meetings with Park, who was able to help her figure out what she wanted to do, how best to make connections and how to transition into a product manager role.

    Ian Shakil, co-founder of Augmedix, a startup that helps doctors take better notes by streaming appointments to a third-party, also tapped into the financial company's vast network of experts. Like Stojcic, he was mired in student loan debt from an MBA at Stanford and went to the company to refinance his loans before discovering its career counseling offerings.

    He enrolled in the company’s Entrepreneur Program, which allowed him to defer his loan payments for six months. Instead of the burden of his student loans, he was now able to focus on getting his company off the ground. That deferment also made it easier to not take a salary when he was starting out, similar to Stojic’s choice to earn less early in her career — each with the understanding they could make more in the long run.

    I made valuable connections and the mentors helped me make difficult business decisions and gave me guidance.” — IAN SHAKIL
    Co-founder and CEO of Augmedix

    SoFi’s peer and mentorship networks allowed him to meet other technology entrepreneurs, seasoned and early-stage, as well as investors. “It was cool to be with a similar cohort of recent graduates and early-stage entrepreneurs,” he says. “I made valuable connections, and the mentors helped me make difficult business decisions and gave me guidance.”

     Path to Entrepreneurship

    As the road to success becomes more complicated, many people are forging their own path to entrepreneurship, which is an increasingly accepted career choice. Around 530,000 new businesses were started each month in the U.S. in 2015, a 9.6 percent increase from the previous year, according to the Kauffman Foundation. But student debt can deter new graduates from this route — a 2015 study found that between 2000 and 2010, a one standard deviation increase in student debt reduced the number of small businesses with one to four employees by 14 percent. One of SoFi’s goals is to buck this trend, providing entrepreneurs alternatives by lessening the burden of student loan debt — all with the hope of empowering people to follow their dreams and start their own companies.

    Many of Shakil’s friends wanted to start their own businesses, but didn’t, in part because of their high student loan balances. But Shakil wasn’t stopped by his $140,000 in student debt. He wanted the autonomy, the opportunity to work hard and the chance to do something meaningful knowing that in the long run, he’ll likely make more money and be happier running his own company. “It’s exhilarating, like a roller coaster,” he says. “It doesn’t feel like work when you’re obsessed with it.”

    While it’s never fun to be in debt, Shakil and Stojcic know that they will pay it off, and perhaps more importantly, that it will pay off. They also know that every career milestone, be it a new job, a raise or, in Shakil’s case, more company growth, comes with a lifestyle improvement. Whether that’s a better place or the ability to live in a more costly city — earning more has helped them achieve their goals.

    It’s been an especially rewarding ride for Stojcic, who went from fearing for her life to building a successful career at one of the world’s most well known companies. “I’m doing something I love,” she says. “When I look back the last 15 years and the grit and grind it took, I can't help but be proud and feel accomplished.”
