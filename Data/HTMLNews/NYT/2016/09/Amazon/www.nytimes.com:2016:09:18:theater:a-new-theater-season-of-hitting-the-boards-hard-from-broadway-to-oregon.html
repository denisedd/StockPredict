A New Theater Season of Hitting the Boards Hard, From Broadway to Oregon

By Steven McElroy

  • Sept. 14, 2016

Dates are subject to change.

September

HOW TO KEEP AN ALIEN When the Irish writer and performer Sonya Kelly fell in love with an Australian woman whose work visa would soon expire, the long, paperwork-strewn bureaucratic process that followed became the fodder for this poignant, funny show. Coming to New York after well-received productions in London and Edinburgh, the play is part of the annual Origin’s 1st Irish Festival and a strong entry in the new season at the Irish Arts Center. Sept. 18-Oct. 1. Irish Arts Center, irishartscenter.org.

OCTOBER SKY A West Virginia teenager, inspired by the Soviet launch of Sputnik, ignores his father’s wishes to enter the coal mines. Instead, he pursues his interest in building rockets in this musical based on the 1999 film (in turn based on Homer Hickam’s autobiographical book “Rocket Boys”). The show features a book by Brian Hill and Aaron Thielen and a score by Michael Mahler (“Diary of a Wimpy Kid”). Rachel Rockwell is the director-choreographer. In previews. Sept. 22-Oct. 23. The Old Globe, San Diego, theoldglobe.org.

WHERE DID WE SIT ON THE BUS? The title question, which a teacher cannot answer, comes from a Latino boy during a third-grade lesson on civil rights and Rosa Parks. Brian Quijada’s autobiographical hip-hop solo show delves into issues of Latino identity. In previews. Sept. 19-Oct. 9. Ensemble Studio Theater, ensemblestudiotheatre.org.

VISITING EDNA A new play by David Rabe (“Sticks and Bones”) is certainly cause for excitement. That this one, about a woman fighting cancer and struggling with her relationship with her visiting son, stars Debra Monk and is directed by Anna D. Shapiro (“Mary Page Marlowe”) makes it even more enticing. The playwright’s son, Michael, is also in the cast, along with Ian Barford, K. Todd Freeman (so good in “Airline Highway”!) and Sally Murphy. In previews. Sept. 25-Nov. 6. Downstairs Theater, Steppenwolf Theater, Chicago, steppenwolf.org.

NAT TURNER IN JERUSALEM That’s Jerusalem, Va., where Nat Turner, who led a brutal slave rebellion in 1831, was later hanged. This new play by Nathan Alan Davis, a New York Theater Workshop 2050 Fellow, imagines Turner’s last night in jail, where he is visited by the lawyer Thomas R. Gray. Phillip James Brannon (“Bootycandy”) plays Turner, and Rowan Vickers is Gray, with Megan Sandberg-Zakian directing. In previews. Sept. 26-Oct. 16. New York Theater Workshop, nytw.org.

ALL THE WAYS TO SAY I LOVE YOU Judith Light stars in a solo show about a high school teacher and guidance counselor with secrets, in a new play by Neil LaBute, a frequent collaborator with MCC Theater; this will be his 10th premiere there. Leigh Silverman directs. In previews. Sept. 28-Oct. 16. Lucille Lortel Theater, mcctheater.org.

THE ROADS TO HOME Primary Stages starts its new season with a revival of Horton Foote’s 1982 trilogy of related one-acts set in the 1920s, about three Houston women struggling with the concept of “home.” Michael Wilson directs a cast to include Harriet Harris, Hallie Foote, Devon Abner, Rebecca Brooksher and Matt Sullivan. In previews. Oct. 5-Nov. 6. Cherry Lane Theater, primarystages.org.

CHASING RAINBOWS The life of Frances Gumm, the young woman who became the star we know as Judy Garland, provides the story for this new musical with a book by Marc Acito (“Allegiance”). Expect to hear Garland favorites including “You Made Me Love You” and “Over the Rainbow.” Tyne Rafaeli directs. In previews. Oct. 5-Nov. 27. The Goodspeed, East Haddam, Conn., goodspeed.org.

HOLIDAY INN: THE NEW IRVING BERLIN MUSICAL Hopefully we’ll be “Shaking the Blues Away” at this musical inspired by the 1942 film set in rural Connecticut, where a farmhouse becomes a celebratory inn with performances surrounding each holiday. The show features more than 20 songs from the Berlin catalog and, of course, a love triangle. Gordon Greenberg, who wrote the book — with Chad Hodge — directs, with choreography by Denis Jones. In previews. Oct. 6.- Jan. 1. Studio 54, roundabouttheatre.org.

THE CHERRY ORCHARD Geez, give a guy a Tony, and now he’s rewriting Chekhov! But seriously, it does seem like a perfect match: Stephen Karam (“The Humans”) adapts Chekhov’s play about the relentless march of progress despite our human tendency to stand in the way. The starry cast of this Roundabout Theater Company production, directed by Simon Godwin, includes Diane Lane, Chuck Cooper, Tavi Gevinson, John Glover, Celia Keenan-Bolger, Harold Perrineau and Joel Grey. In previews. Oct. 16-Dec. 4. American Airlines Theater, roundabouttheatre.org.

HIT THE BODY ALARM Winsome Brown (“This Is Mary Brown”) tells four stories about moments of crisis — Satan after his fall; Eve before she eats the apple; a suicidal actress; and the title piece, about failures in the American penal system — in her new solo show. Previews begin Sept. 20. Sept. 22-Oct. 2. The Performing Garage, theperforminggarage.org.

THE ENCOUNTER One of the hottest tickets at the Edinburgh International Festival last summer, Simon McBurney’s solo show, which went on to a sold-out run at the Barbican in London, uses innovative aural technology to tell the tale of a photographer lost in the Amazon rain forest. The story is based on Petru Popescu’s book “Amazon Beaming,” about the real experiences of the photographer Loren McIntyre (who eventually found his way out). Previews begin Sept. 20. Sept. 29-Jan. 8. Golden Theater, theencounterbroadway.com.

HEISENBERG A woman kisses a stranger on the neck in a strange, seemingly small moment that turns out to be the impetus for a complicated relationship in this two-hander by Simon Stephens (“The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time”). The Manhattan Theater Club production was well-received Off Broadway last year and heads to Broadway with the same cast — Denis Arndt and Mary-Louise Parker — and director, Mark Brokaw. Previews begin Sept. 20. Oct. 13-Dec. 4. Samuel J. Friedman Theater, manhattantheatreclub.com.

THE FRONT PAGE Bring your kids who have never seen a print newspaper to the welcome revival of this 1928 comedy by Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur about Chicago reporters chasing scoops. You can practically smell the ink! Jack O’Brien directs, and the cast is well worth your ticket dollars — Nathan Lane, John Slattery, Holland Taylor and Sherie Rene Scott among them. Previews begin Sept. 20. Oct. 20-Jan. 29. Broadhurst Theater, thefrontpagebroadway.com.

THE UNDERTAKING The Civilians delve into the concept of mortality in this new show from the company that brought us “Mr. Burns, a Post-Electric Play,” and “In the Footprint.” The new Brooklyn Academy of Music production will use verbatim and sometimes wine-soaked conversation between the Civilians’s artistic director, Steve Cosson, and his friend Jessica Mitrani, a Colombian-American artist, as the starting point, but in its investigative theater style will also rely on interviewees, in this case more than 200 people — hospice workers, funeral directors and shamans among them. Sept. 21-25. BAM Fisher, Fort Greene, Brooklyn, bam.org.

LOVE, LOVE, LOVE Can a marriage that grew partly out of a shared love of rock ’n’ roll and marijuana in the free-loving 1960s last through the ensuing decades of real-world challenges? Mike Bartlett raises the question in his latest play, which spans more than 40 years, as Michael Mayer directs a cast including Richard Armitage, Alex Hurt, Zoe Kazan, Ben Rosenfield and Amy Ryan. Previews begin Sept. 22. Oct. 19-Dec. 18. Laura Pels Theater, roundabouttheatre.org.

STUFFED The comedian Lisa Lampanelli puts four women together — one bulimic, one a compulsive eater, one overweight and fine with it and one super skinny — for an exploration of food and body image, in this production of WP Theater (formerly known as Women’s Project Theater). Ms. Lampanelli will also perform, along with Zainab Jah, Jessica Luck and Ann Harada, with Jackson Gay directing. Previews begin Sept. 23. Oct. 7-Nov. 6. McGinn/Cazale Theater, wptheater.org.

OH, HELLO ON BROADWAY God help us: The two peevish, old Upper West Siders Gil Faizon and George St. Geegland — alter egos of the comedians Nick Kroll (“Kroll Show”) and John Mulaney (“Saturday Night Live”) — are getting their own Broadway show. The pair, who have been performing onstage together for years, brought their act and their overstuffed tuna sandwiches to the Cherry Lane Theater for an Off Broadway run last year and head to the big time with Alex Timbers directing. Previews begin Sept. 23. Oct. 10-Jan. 8. Lyceum Theater, ohhellobroadway.com.

SEARED Theresa Rebeck digs into the celebrity-chef culture in her new comedy, commissioned by the San Francisco Playhouse, about a chef who becomes so popular he outgrows his small restaurant. Investors are hungry to cash in — should the chef sell out? Margarett Perry directs. Previews begin Sept. 27. Oct. 1-Nov. 12. San Francisco Playhouse, sfplayhouse.org.

SELL/BUY/DATE The chameleonic Sarah Jones (“Bridge & Tunnel”) plays multiple characters in this new play, directed by Carolyn Cantor, inspired by true stories of people affected by sex trafficking and the sex industry. Previews begin Sept. 27. Oct. 18-Nov. 6. Manhattan Theater Club at City Center, manhattantheatreclub.com.

MY NAME IS GIDEON: I’M PROBABLY GOING TO DIE, EVENTUALLY Gideon Irving, an idiosyncratic singer/songwriter who has made a career of performing in people’s living rooms all over the world (and then spending the night), makes his New York stage debut this fall. Coming directly from a well-received appearance at the Edinburgh Festival Fringe, Mr. Irving comes with stories, songs, multiple instruments and a quirky sense of humor. Previews begin Sept. 29. Oct. 6-30. Walkerspace, afo.nyc.

FALSETTOS Christian Borle, Stephanie J. Block and Andrew Rannells star in a revival of this musical about a gay man who leaves his wife for a man in the early days of the AIDS crisis. William Finn wrote the music and lyrics; the book was written by James Lapine, who will direct the production of Lincoln Center Theater, in association with Jujamcyn Theaters. Previews begin Sept. 29. Oct. 27-Jan. 8. Walter Kerr Theater, lct.org.

SCENES FROM COURT LIFE OR THE WHIPPING BOY AND HIS PRINCE Sarah Ruhl’s new political play looks at dynastic privilege through both the Stuart kings (Charles I and II) and the Bush brothers (Jeb and George W.). It’s one of several world premieres in Yale Repertory Theater’s vibrant 50th anniversary season; others include “Imogen Says Nothing,” by Aditi Brennan Kapil, next winter, and Amy Herzog’s “Mary Jane,” next spring. Mark Wing-Davey will direct Ms. Ruhl’s play, a Yale Rep commission. Sept. 30-Oct. 22. Yale Repertory Theater, New Haven, yalerep.org.

A LIFE David Hyde Pierce stars in this new play by Adam Bock (“The Drunken City”) about a man whose failed relationships have him consulting the stars, with unexpected results. Anne Kauffman (“Marjorie Prime”) directs. Previews begin Sept. 30. Oct. 24-Nov. 13. Playwrights Horizons Peter Jay Sharp Theater, phnyc.org.

October

MILES FOR MARY In this new work from the ensemble-driven company the Mad Ones (“The Essential Straight & Narrow”), devoted to explorations of American nostalgia, the fictitious Garrison High School Physical Education Department is organizing a 24-hour telethon in what the company calls “an analogue elegy to the Camcorder 1980s, Girls Track & Field, and the consecrated American High School.” Lila Neugebauer directs. Previews begin Oct. 2. Opens Oct. 8. The Bushwick Starr, thebushwickstarr.org.

TICK, TICK … BOOM! Nick Blaemire stars in this autobiographical musical about a struggling artist approaching his 30th birthday and grappling with his choices, written (before “Rent”) by Jonathan Larson. The Keen Company production will be directed by Jonathan Silverstein, Keen’s artistic director. Previews begin Oct. 4. Oct. 20-Nov. 19. Acorn Theater, keencompany.org.

VIETGONE From the vibrant imagination of Qui Nguyen (“She Kills Monsters”), a co-founder of Vampire Cowboys Theater Company, comes this play about a couple — possibly based on his parents — who meet in the United States after fleeing Vietnam in 1975. Fans of Mr. Nguyen’s work will be happy to know that the relative seriousness of the story doesn’t mean the show will be devoid of butt-kicking fight scenes. May Adrales directs, as she did for the premiere at South Coast Repertory last fall, and at the Oregon Shakespeare Festival, where the show continues through Oct. 29. In New York, previews begin Oct. 4. Oct. 25-Dec. 4. Manhattan Theater Club at City Center, manhattantheatreclub.com.

THE HARVEST Among a group of young, idealistic Idaho missionaries headed to the Middle East on a church mission, one man (Peter Mark Kendall) means to stay there for good, though he’ll have to overcome a significant obstacle — his sister (Leah Karpel) — in this new play by Samuel D. Hunter. The show, commissioned by Lincoln Center Theater’s LCT3 initiative, will be directed by Davis McCallum and also features Gideon Glick, Scott Jaeck, Madeleine Martin, Christopher Sears and Zoë Winters in the cast. Previews begin Oct. 8. Oct. 24-Nov. 20. Claire Tow Theater, lct.org.

LES LIAISONS DANGEREUSES Christopher Hampton’s adaptation of a steamy 1782 novel about sex, intrigue and cruelty among the aristocracy in pre-revolutionary France returns in a production starring Janet McTeer and Liev Schreiber, directed by Josie Rourke. The show is a Broadway transfer of Ms. Rourke’s 2015 Donmar Warehouse production (though most of the cast members are different, aside from Ms. McTeer). Previews begin Oct. 8. Oct. 30-Jan. 22. Booth Theater, liaisonsbroadway.com.

NOTES FROM THE FIELD Anna Deavere Smith, who applied her documentary theater prowess to the subject of the American medical system in “Let Me Down Easy” at Second Stage Theater several years ago, returns there with her latest solo show. This time, it is our education and prison systems, and the unfortunate habit we have of sending our young people from school to prison, that is under her microscope, in a show drawn from more than 250 interviews. This is a coproduction with American Repertory Theater, where “Notes” is just finishing a run. In New York: Previews begin Oct. 15. Nov. 2-Dec. 11. Second Stage Theater, 2st.com.

SWEAT A group of factory workers, friends who have shared much over the years, find their relationships threatened when layoffs and picket lines become a part of their lives in this play by Lynn Nottage (“Ruined”) that earned her the 2016 Susan Smith Blackburn Prize. Kate Whoriskey will direct. Previews begin Oct. 18. Nov. 3-Nov. 27. Public Theater, publictheater.org.

NATASHA, PIERRE AND THE GREAT COMET OF 1812 Rachel Chavkin is arguably the busiest director in town who hasn’t been on Broadway until now. Her recent work includes “Hadestown” at New York Theater Workshop, the remounted “Small Mouth Sounds” (through Oct. 9) and even a show at the Edinburgh International Festival this summer, where her Brooklyn company’s new show, “Anything That Gives Off Light,” had its premiere. Her production of this show, with book, lyrics and music by Dave Malloy, started at Ars Nova in 2012 and ran Off Broadway in a few incarnations, then had a recent run at the American Repertory Theater. Josh Groban is among the cast for this Broadway bow. Previews begin Oct. 18. Opens Nov. 14. Imperial Theater, greatcometbroadway.com.

THE PLOUGH AND THE STARS The renowned Abbey Theater, the National Theater of Ireland, visits New Jersey with a production of Sean O’Casey’s 1926 drama set during the 1916 Easter Rising, when a group of Irish nationalists rebelled against the British government and were brutally squashed. Sean Holmes directs this very short run, part of Peak Performances at Montclair State University. Oct. 20-23. Alexander Kasser Theater, peakperfs.org.

THE MAGIC PLAY A magician tries to maintain control over his show, even though he went through a breakup a few hours earlier, in what sounds like a singular new play by Andrew Hinderaker, at the Goodman Theater (a coproduction with the Roundabout Theater Company). The star is Brett Schneider, an actual magician, and Halena Kays directs. Oct. 21-Nov. 20. Owen Theater at the Goodman, Chicago, goodmantheatre.org.

VICUÑA A tailor to the stars works for a candidate on the rise — a real-estate tycoon and reality television star running for president, actually — in this new election season satire by Jon Robin Baitz (“Other Desert Cities”) that marks the playwright’s seventh Center Theater Group production. Robert Egan directs. Previews begin Oct. 23. Oct. 30-Nov. 20. Kirk Douglas Theater, centertheatergroup.org.

OTHELLO: THE REMIX Since its premiere in London in 2012, this fun and fresh hip-hop interpretation of Shakespeare’s “Othello” has been presented at the Edinburgh Festival Fringe, Chicago Shakespeare Theater and other places. But now, in this year of Shakespeare 400, it makes its first stop in New York, with John Leguizamo as one of the producers. The show is a creation of the Q Brothers (Gregory and Jeffrey Qaiyum), who have previously shaken up Shakespeare with “Funk It Up About Nothin” and “The Bomb-itty of Errors.” Previews begin Oct. 25. Opens Nov. 16. Westside Theater, othellotheremix.com.

MISS YOU LIKE HELL A mother-daughter road trip leads to unexpected places in this world premiere musical, at La Jolla Playhouse Commission, with a book by the Tony- and Pulitzer-winning playwright Quiara Alegría Hudes. The music is by Erin McKeown, and both collaborated on the lyrics. Lear deBessonet directs. Previews begin Oct. 25. Nov. 6-Dec. 4. La Jolla Playhouse, lajollaplayhouse.org.

DEAD POETS SOCIETY In his first season as artistic director of Classic Stage Company, John Doyle starts things off with this new play based on the 1989 film that starred Robin Williams as an inspiring teacher at an all-boys prep school. Jason Sudeikis will star, and the script is by the Academy Award winner Tom Schulman, adapted from his screenplay. Mr. Doyle will direct. Previews begin Oct. 27. Nov. 17-Dec. 11. Classic Stage Company, classicstage.org.

November

SWEET CHARITY Sutton Foster stars in a revival of this 1966 musical about a dancer-for-hire looking for real love, produced by the New Group in association with Kevin McCollum. Leigh Silverman (“Violet”) directs a fresh take on the classic show, with a book by Neil Simon, music by Cy Coleman and lyrics by Dorothy Fields. Joshua Bergasse (“On the Town”) is the choreographer. Previews begin Nov. 2. Nov. 20-Dec. 23. The New Group at the Pershing Square Signature Center, thenewgroup.org.

THIS DAY FORWARD The playwright Nicky Silver and the director Mark Brokaw, who worked together on “The Lyons,” are reunited for Mr. Silver’s latest comedy — about a honeymoon revelation that changes a couple’s future, and the impact on their kids decades later. Previews begin Nov. 3. Nov. 21-Dec. 18. Vineyard Theater, vineyardtheatre.org.

A BRONX TALE THE MUSICAL This show, a mafia story set in the 1960s, has been kicking around since Chazz Palminteri, who wrote it based partly on his childhood experiences, first performed it in 1989. It became a film, and after a developmental workshop in 2014 that added music by Alan Menken and lyrics by Glenn Sater, this new musical version had its premiere at the Paper Mill Playhouse earlier this year. Jerry Zaks and Robert De Niro co-directed and will do so again. Previews begin Nov. 3. Opens Dec. 1. Longacre Theater, abronxtalethemusical.com.

WOMEN OF A CERTAIN AGE The final installment in Richard Nelson’s three-play cycle “The Gabriels: Election Year in the Life of One Family,” finds the Rhinebeck, N.Y., family awaiting election results on Nov. 8, and contemplating the future. (The second play, “What Did You Expect?”, set in the middle of the general election, is already open at the Public Theater, and runs through Oct. 9.) Mr. Nelson once again directs, with a cast that includes Meg Gibson, Lynn Hawley, Roberta Maxwell, Maryann Plunkett, Jay O. Sanders and Amy Warren. Previews begin Nov. 4. Nov. 8-Dec. 4. Public Theater, publictheater.org.

MOUSE: THE PERSISTENCE OF AN UNLIKELY THOUGHT Daniel Kitson is a truly singular performer. A British storyteller and comedian, his shows at the Edinburgh Festival Fringe are routinely the hottest tickets in town. His newest, presented this summer at the Traverse Theater during the Fringe, is a story about loneliness, the choices we make in our lives and, naturally, a mouse. It’s hard to describe a Kitson show without giving away too much, but the main thing is that you should go (and book early). Nov. 8-27. St. Ann’s Warehouse, stannswarehouse.org.

THE BABYLON LINE Josh Radnor plays a bohemian writer in 1967 Greenwich Village who teaches a creative-writing class in Levittown, on Long Island, and forms a special bond with one of his students (Elizabeth Reaser) in this new play by Richard Greenberg (“Our Mother’s Brief Affair”). The Steppenwolf Theater Company regular Terry Kinney directs a cast that also includes Maddie Corman, Randy Graff, Julie Halston, Michael Oberholtzer and Frank Wood in this Lincoln Center Theater production. Previews begin Nov. 10. Dec. 5-Jan. 22. Mitzi E. Newhouse Theater, lct.org.

IN TRANSIT Life in the big city, told a cappella style. Written by Kristen Anderson-Lopez, James-Allen Ford, Russ Kaplan and Sara Wordsworth, the show, about 11 striving New Yorkers and their intersecting lives, is directed by Kathleen Marshall. Previews begin Nov. 10. Opens Dec. 11. Circle in the Square Theater, intransitbroadway.com.

MAN IN SNOW This new family drama by Israel Horovitz is one of more than 70 productions in an ambitious season at La MaMa, the downtown incubator and influencer that routinely draws varied works from around the world. Mr. Horovitz’s latest will have its world premiere at Gloucester Stage Company, where Mr. Horovitz was the founding artistic director before coming to New York. Nov. 10-27, lamama.org.

THE BAND’S VISIT A band made up of Egyptian policemen travels to Israel to perform a concert but ends up stuck in the desert, where they find the locals to be surprisingly accommodating, in this new musical based on the 2007 film. David Cromer will direct the show, which features a book by Itamar Moses (“Bach at Leipzig”) and a score by David Yazbek (“Dirty Rotten Scoundrels”). Previews begin Nov. 11. Dec. 8-Dec. 18. Atlantic Theater Company, atlantictheater.org.

DEAR EVAN HANSEN This musical about a high school student whose life is reinvented after another student’s life ends got a lot of well-deserved buzz after its premiere at Arena Stage (and a subsequent Off Broadway production at Second Stage Theater). Ben Platt, who led both productions in a moving and vocally impressive performance as the title character, will stay on for the Broadway production. The book is by Steven Levenson and the score by Benj Pasek and Justin Paul, with Michael Greif directing. Previews begin Nov. 14. Opens Dec. 4. Music Box Theater, dearevanhansen.com.

OTHELLO David Oyelowo and Daniel Craig, as the title character and his scheming sidekick Iago, respectively, ought to be quite the draw to the East Village this fall for Sam Gold’s production of Shakespeare’s tragedy about the lengths we might go when in the grasp of jealousy. Mr. Gold, the director who won a Tony Award for “Fun Home,” was supposed to also head up a production of Shakespeare’s “Hamlet” this season, but after it was pulled from the season at Theater for a New Audience, a possible subsequent production at the Public Theater has yet to materialize. For “Othello”: Previews begin Nov. 22. Dec. 12-Jan. 18. New York Theater Workshop, nytw.org.

ALLIGATOR Wrestling ’gators is the norm — and a livelihood — for twin teenage orphans in the Florida Everglades in this new play set to a rock score, by Hilary Bettis. The show, directed by Elena Araoz, is being presented by New Georges in collaboration with the Sol Project, a new initiative meant to increase the visibility of Latino and Latina playwrights. Nov. 22-Dec. 19. ART/New York Theaters, newgeorges.org.

THE BODYGUARD The reviews were a little wonky in London in 2013 (or downright rough, in some cases), but this musical based on the 1992 film that starred Whitney Houston was nominated for a few Olivier awards, toured around and resurfaced on the West End late last year. The show, about a pop diva, her bodyguard and a relationship that moves beyond the title character’s job description, has a book by Alexander Dinelaris (“Birdman”) and the well-known songs by Ms. Houston, including, of course, her cover of Dolly Parton’s “I Will Always Love You.” Deborah Cox will star in the first United States performance, directed by Thea Sharrock. Nov. 25-Jan. 1. Paper Mill Playhouse, Millburn, N.J., papermill.org.

THE PRESENT It’s not as well known as Anton Chekhov’s later plays, and it’s a sprawling mess in some ways, but “Platonov” hits classic Chekhovian motifs like unrequited love, the irresponsible aristocracy and a character people love to compare to Hamlet. Andrew Upton’s new play, based on “Platonov,” comes to Broadway in a Sydney Theater Company production starring Cate Blanchett and Richard Roxburgh, with John Crowley directing. Previews begin Dec. 17. Jan. 8-March 19. Barrymore Theater, thepresentbroadway.com.

December

RUDOLPH THE RED-NOSED REINDEER: THE MUSICAL We’ve seen a lot of holiday musicals over the years, but, at least in New York, we have yet to see this one, about the most famous reindeer of all. Dec. 1-18. Madison Square Garden, rudolphthemusical.com. (Fear not, the Rockettes’ “Christmas Spectacular” will still happen this year, too, running Nov. 11-Jan. 2 at Radio City Music Hall; rockettes.com/Christmas.)

ELEMENTS OF OZ You can leave your cellphone on at this performance — should, in fact — as The Builders Association takes a unique approach to telling the story of “The Wizard of Oz.” Audience members will be able to download an app that enhances the show on stage, though it sounds pretty interesting already. A mash-up of filmed and live performance that recreates and pays homage to the famous story, the show was co-produced by Peak Performances at Montclair State University where it was developed and had its premiere last year. Dec. 1-18. 3LD Art & Technology Center, thebuildersassociation.org.

AMELIE Phillipa Soo (“Hamilton”) is the shy Parisian waitress in this new musical based on the romantic 2001 film, which received five Oscar nominations and a slew of other awards. The book is by Craig Lucas, with music by Daniel Messé (who wrote the lyrics with Nathan Tysen), and Pam MacKinnon directs. The production is already planning an early 2017 opening on Broadway following this Center Theater Group production. (The world premiere was at Berkeley Repertory Theater last year.) Previews begin Dec. 4. Dec. 16-Jan. 15. Ahmanson Theater, centertheatergroup.org.

JITNEY Closing a loop, the Manhattan Theater Club will finally place the one play from August Wilson’s monumental American Century Cycle that has yet to hit Broadway, where it belongs. Ruben Santiago-Hudson will direct the drama about guys trying to make a living in Pittsburgh as unlicensed cab drivers in the 1970s. Previews begin Dec. 28. Jan. 19-March 5. Samuel J. Friedman Theater, manhattantheatreclub.com.

January

TRANS SCRIPTS, PART 1: THE WOMEN Paul Lucas’s play, drawn from many interviews with transgender women from around the world, presents a nuanced, layered look at the experiences, frustrations and challenges faced by the transgender community — and not just those in the spotlight. The show, which was well received at the Edinburgh Festival Fringe last summer, where it won a Fringe First award, will have its American premiere in Cambridge, Mass., with Jo Bonney (“Father Comes Home From the Wars (Parts 1, 2 & 3)”) directing. Jan. 19-Feb. 5. American Repertory Theater, americanrepertorytheater.org.

EVERYBODY One of our most interesting and prolific young playwrights, Branden Jacobs-Jenkins (“Gloria,” “War”), is back with another new work. In his latest, part of an exciting Signature Theater season that also includes revivals of Athol Fugard’s “Master Harold ... and the boys” and Suzan-Lori Parks’s “The Death of the Last Black Man in the Whole Entire World,” Mr. Jacobs-Jenkins riffs on “Everyman,” one of the oldest plays in the English language. Lila Neugebauer directs. Previews begin Jan. 31. Signature Theater, signaturetheatre.org.

February

THE GLASS MENAGERIE It’s not just you if it feels like déjà vu: This heartbreaking Tennessee Williams play was indeed revived on Broadway fairly recently, closing in 2014 (in a production starring Cherry Jones that ran again this summer at the Edinburgh International Festival). But classics are classics for a reason, and seeing Sally Field as the faded Southern belle Amanda is an exciting prospect. Sam Gold directs a cast that also features Joe Mantello, Finn Wittrock and Madison Ferris, in her Broadway debut as Laura Wingfield. Previews begin Feb. 7. Opens March 9. Golden Theater.

BIG RIVER The first in the popular, annual Encores! series of concert musicals for 2017 will be this musical adaptation of the popular Mark Twain story “The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn.” With a book by William Hauptman and music and lyrics by Roger Miller, “Big River” (Feb. 8-12) is the first in the three-show Encores! season, which also includes “The New Yorkers” (March 22-26), Cole Porter’s Prohibition Era romp, and the 1954 musical “The Golden Apple” (May 10-14), adapted from parts of the classic stories “The Iliad” and “The Odyssey,” featuring music by Jerome Moross and lyrics by John Latouche. New York City Center, nycitycenter.org.

SWEENEY TODD The demon barber of Fleet Street strikes again when this revival of the Stephen Sondheim/Hugh Wheeler musical, directed by Bill Buckhurst, arrives Off Broadway following a popular, intimate London run at Harrington’s Pie and Mash Shop. Previews begin Feb. 14. Opens March 1. Barrow Street Theater, SweeneyToddNYC.com.

JOAN OF ARC: INTO THE FIRE David Byrne and the director Alex Timbers, who collaborated with dizzying success on “Here Lies Love,” return to the Public Theater with Mr. Byrne’s new rock musical-concert about the rise of Joan of Arc. Feb. 14-March 19. Public Theater, publictheater.org.

SIGNIFICANT OTHER A single, young gay man hungers for love as his friends seem to all be getting married in Joshua Harmon’s (“Bad Jews”) play that was well-received last year in an Off Broadway production at Roundabout Theater Company. Mr. Harmon, and the director, Trip Cullman, will both be making their Broadway debuts. No word on the cast yet, but with luck, Gideon Glick will reprise the main character. Previews begin Feb. 14. Opens March 2. Booth Theater, significantotherbroadway.com.

THE PRICE Terry Kinney directs a revival of Arthur Miller’s sibling rivalry drama about the bargains made in a lifetime, and their eventual costs. Previews begin Feb. 16. March 16-May 7. American Airlines Theater, roundabouttheatre.org.

COME FROM AWAY While terrorist attacks took place in the United States on Sept. 11, 2001, 38 planes were forced to land in Gander, Newfoundland, instead of their intended destinations. This musical by Irene Sankoff and David Hein, a Toronto couple, looks at the Gander community’s generous response to the extraordinary situation. The show, directed by Christopher Ashley, has been presented at the La Jolla (Calif.) Playhouse and Seattle Repertory Theater, is currently onstage at Ford’s Theater in Washington and heads to the Royal Alexandra Theater in Toronto in November. Phew. It is expected to then arrive on Broadway, where performances are to begin Feb. 18 for a March 12 opening at theGerald Schoenfeld Theater; comefromaway.com.

BURN THIS Lanford Wilson’s 1987 drama about a group of downtown New Yorkers in the aftermath of a friend’s death will be the first production staged in the newly renovated Hudson Theater, which opened in 1903 with a production starring Ethel Barrymore, and hasn’t been used as a theater in years (though it was given landmark status decades ago). Jake Gyllenhaal stars with Michael Mayer directing. Previews begin in February for a March 6 opening. Hudson Theater, thehudsonbroadway.com.

March

MISS SAIGON Set against the fall of Saigon, the musical tale of a Vietnamese girl and her relationship with an American soldier first opened on Broadway in 1991, following a London premiere in 1989. The show, with music by Claude-Michel Schönberg and lyrics by Richard Maltby Jr. and Alain Boublil (with additions by Michael Mahler), was revived in a successful London production in 2014. That production, including its stars, Jon Jon Briones and Eva Noblezada, and its director, Laurence Connor, makes the move to Broadway. Previews begin March 1. Opens March 23. Broadway Theater, saigonbroadway.com.

HELLO, DOLLY! Bette Midler is the mischievous matchmaker in this revival of the 1964 musical with music and lyrics by Jerry Herman and a book by Michael Stewart. David Hyde Pierce will co-star as Horace Vandergelder and Jerry Zaks directs. Previews begin March 15. Opens April 20. Shubert Theater, hellodollyonbroadway.com.

OSLO J.T. Rogers’s play about a lesser-known bit of history — when a Norwegian couple helped initiate the talks between the Israeli prime minister, Yitzhak Rabin, and the P.L.O. leader, Yasir Arafat, to hammer out the Oslo accords — will move from a successful Off Broadway production to Broadway. The cast and the director, Bartlett Sher, remain in place. Previews begin March 23. Opens April 13. Vivian Beaumont Theater, lct.org.

CHARLIE AND THE CHOCOLATE FACTORY Christian Borle stars as the impresario of magical bonbons in this new musical, based on Roald Dahl’s popular novel about a boy’s life-changing discovery of a golden ticket, which had its premiere in London in 2013. The book is by David Greig (“The Strange Undoing of Prudencia Hart”), with music by Marc Shaiman, lyrics by Scott Wittman and Mr. Shaiman, choreography by Joshua Bergasse and Jack O’Brien directing. Previews begin March 28. Opens April 23. Lunt-Fontanne Theater, charlieonbroadway.com.

CAESAR/X The Acting Company will take up residence briefly at the New Victory with its annual two-play season of shows that will tour before visiting New York. For 2017, the company looks at assassination and the ideological landscape that leads to such acts, by pairing Shakespeare’s “Julius Caesar” in repertory with “X,” a new play by Marcus Gardley (“The House That Will Not Stand”) about the assassination of Malcolm X. March 17-26. New Victory Theater, newvictory.org.

THE LITTLE FOXES Both Laura Linney and Cynthia Nixon will play the scheming and ruthless Regina Hubbard Gibbons in a revival of Lillian Hellman’s 1939 drama about cutthroat Southern aristocrats in 1900 Alabama. The actresses will take turns playing Regina and her unhappy, alcohol-soaked sister-in-law, Birdie. Daniel Sullivan directs. Previews begin March 29. April 19-June 4. Samuel J. Friedman Theater, manhattantheatreclub.com.

LINDA VISTA Can a misanthrope with a failed marriage — and in his 40s — find a new beginning? Tracy Letts, the Pulitzer-winning author of “August: Osage County” and the more recent “Mary Page Marlowe,” poses the question in this new play at Steppenwolf Theater Company. Dexter Bullard directs. March 30-May 21, Steppenwolf Theater, steppenwolf.org.

April

SAMARA Soho Rep describes this latest work from the idiosyncratic experimental writer-director Richard Maxwell (“Isolde”) as “a spiritual and visceral celebration about whatever it is we call home.” In uncharacteristic fashion, Mr. Maxwell will relinquish the directing duties to Soho Rep’s artistic director, Sarah Benson. Previews begin April 4. April 16-May 7. Soho Rep on Walker Street, sohorep.org.

ANASTASIA A young Russian princess is separated from her family in the lead-up to the Russian Revolution, and her quest to rediscover her past after losing her memory is the story of this new musical inspired by both the 1997 animated film and the 1956 film. The show recently had its premiere at Hartford Stage Company. The book is by Terrence McNally, with music by Stephen Flaherty and lyrics by Lynn Ahrens. Darko Tresnjak directs a cast including Christy Altomare, Derek Klena, John Bolton and Caroline O’Connor. Previews begin March 23. Opens April 24. Broadhurst Theater, anastasiabroadway.com.

ARCHDUKE This world premiere by the Pulitzer finalist Rajiv Joseph (“Bengal Tiger at the Baghdad Zoo”) is about three strangers in 1914 Belgrade who learn they have tuberculosis, and the unlikely story that leads to them inciting World War I. Giovanna Sardelli will direct the Center Theatre Group commission. Previews begin April 18. April 30-May 28. Mark Taper Forum, centertheatergroup.org.

May

ESCAPE TO MARGARITAVILLE A knockabout singer-bartender named Tully falls in love with a more pragmatic type — a career-minded tourist — in this new musical that features songs by Jimmy Buffett, both old favorites and some new ones. The book is by Greg Garcia and Mike O’Malley, and Christopher Ashley will direct this La Jolla Playhouse world premiere. Previews begin May 16. May 28-June 25. La Jolla Playhouse, lajollaplayhouse.org.

June

MEASURE FOR MEASURE Simon Godwin directs Shakespeare’s problematic play about repression, lust and hypocrisy — a few human characteristics that never go out of style. June 17-July 16. Theater for a New Audience, tfana.org.

These listings were compiled with the assistance of Suzanne O’Connor.

Correction: Sept. 25, 2016

Several entries in the theater listings last Sunday contained errors. The family in “Women of a Certain Age” is awaiting election results on Nov. 8, not Nov. 4. In “Anastasia,” a young Russian princess is separated from her family, not her mother, in the lead-up to the Russian Revolution. The title of David Byrne’s new rock concert is “Joan of Arc: Into the Fire,” not “Saint Joan: Into the Fire.” Josh Groban is only now joining the cast of “Natasha, Pierre & the Great Comet of 1812”; he did not join when the production had a brief run at the American Repertory Theater. And the new production of “A Bronx Tale” is based on the one-man show by Chazz Palminteri that inspired the film; it is not a solo show.
