    Episode 3: The future of health care

    Healthy Connections

    How technology, data and open communication could help foster better health care

    Scroll

    Introduction

    When an elderly woman with heart failure due to her deteriorating artificial aortic valve was admitted to the Sønderborg Hospital in Southern Denmark, Dr. Niels Kragh-Thomsen had to react quickly. As the head of cardiology, he has seen similar cases routinely referred from his satellite hospital to the “heart center” in Odense. Just a few years ago, before the advent of digital reporting, the longer time needed to collaborate on a treatment decision could have affected the patient’s chances.

    Now, however, Dr. Kragh-Thomsen was able to instantly share and discuss the patient’s echocardiograms with his colleagues at Odense University Hospital. A decision was promptly made, and the patient was transferred immediately for a valve replacement.

    Denmark’s interconnected health care system serving a population of 5.5 million is a good example of how data access and collaboration could simplify clinician workflow — saving time and money while improving outcomes — especially in a world with an increasingly aging population.

    Today, as people live longer, they are more likely to develop chronic conditions, placing tremendous and unsustainable strains on health care systems around the world. The challenge of providing effective health care at an affordable price will require not only new technology, but also a new way of thinking about care by providers, governments and patients, according to an international perception study sponsored by Philips.

    The Future Health Index (FHI) research, which involved surveying 2,659 health care professionals and 25,355 patients in 13 countries, found that most countries greatly value integrated health care systems and reveals opportunities to encourage broader adoption around the world.

    What Are Integrated Health Solutions?

    The proportion of the world’s population over 60 is expected to nearly double, to 22 percent, by 2050, according to the World Health Organization. Much of this growth will be concentrated in developing countries, where many already lack access to health services and infrastructure. At the same time, longer life means an increase in chronic conditions such as heart disease, dementia, cancer and diabetes — all of which require long-term commitment by patients and health care systems — and, subsequently, significant resources.

    “Health care is just not sustainable the way it is run today,” says Helen Routh, senior vice president of Strategy & Innovation at Philips. “We have to get to better outcomes at lower costs.”

    The solution is making connections via a new, integrated approach to health care. Leveraging technology and connectivity to address communication gaps could also help ensure constant feedback throughout a patient’s journey along the health continuum — from healthy living and disease prevention to diagnosis, treatment and the return home.

    This “connected care” approach facilitates integration of all parts of the health system, from patients and their families, friends and employers to doctors and hospitals to insurers and governments. It relies on real-time communication, made possible through emerging technologies like secure networks, devices that monitor key health indicators and software algorithms that analyze and interpret the data. If implemented successfully, integrated solutions can enhance efficiency and outcomes in the long term.

    Key Definitions

    Access

    The perceived level of access that patients have to a range of health care solutions and services across all health needs

    Integration

    The perceived state of functional integration and interoperability between health care systems

    Adoption

    The perceived proliferation, take-up and use of, and familiarity with, connected-care technology

    Patient

    People aged 18 or older who have visited a health care professional within the past three months

    Health care professional

    Those who work in health care as a doctor, surgeon, nurse practitioner, registered nurse, licensed practical nurse or nurse across a variety of specializations

    Connected-care technology

    Technology that enables sharing of information throughout all parts of the health system. This can range from computer software that allows secure communication between doctors and hospitals to a watch that tracks a person’s heartbeat

    Global Differences:
    Developed vs. Emerging Economies

    The readiness to address future health challenges with connected-care technology and integration varies from country to country, according to the Philips Future Health Index. Developed countries are slowed down by bureaucracy and regulation, while emerging economies struggle with providing access to health care and good infrastructure.

    For example, the United Arab Emirates leads the ranking, partly thanks to patient and health care professional readiness to adopt connected-care technology; 43 percent of UAE patients surveyed say the health system in their country is very or completely integrated. Japan, on the other hand, ranks lowest on the index, with a perceived lack of access to health services and knowledge regarding connected care — about a quarter of Japanese patients surveyed agree they have access to the information and resources they need to live healthily.

    Developed countries score better in terms of access to health care resources, while emerging countries such as South Africa and the UAE are blazing a trail for technology adoption, with more health care professionals expecting connected technology to be used to manage health in the future.

    While three-quarters of health care professionals in developed countries agree that their patients have access to the treatments needed for current and future medical conditions, these economies tend to approach integration and connected care with heightened vigilance around data privacy and security. This presents a significant potential obstacle to connected care in some countries. In Germany, for example, half of the health care professionals surveyed see privacy and security concerns as a top barrier to the adoption of connected-care technology, versus only 16 percent in South Africa and 18 percent in the UAE.

    On the other hand, there is the possibility of emerging countries “leapfrogging” more developed countries through targeted partnerships and the quick adoption of new mobile technologies. The explosion of mobile devices in some parts of the world, such as Africa and Southeast Asia, is a prime example.

    A case in point is Indonesia, where the number of smartphone users is expected to reach 69 million in 2016. Tapping into the trend, the Padang city government teamed up with PT Philips Indonesia for the implementation of a new telehealth platform that helps address the high maternal mortality rate via Mobile Obstetrical Monitoring. The solution uses a mobile phone application where midwives can create health profiles of pregnant women by collecting data from physical examinations and tests done at local nursing clinics or at the patients’ homes. This integrated approach aims to drastically reduce Indonesia’s maternal and fetal mortality rates, which is also one of the government’s Millennial Development Goals.

    Patients

    Over half of health care professionals and 43% of patients name bureaucracy as a major barrier to the coordination of health care.

    Connected care requires patients and health care professionals to be comfortable with technology. The Future Health Index study found a clear generation gap, not only in the area of technology, but also in accountability. Beyond the disparities between developed and emerging countries, the research points to other divides in views on health system integration, medical technology, and the promotion and maintenance of health overall — between young and old, and health care professionals and their patients — that will need to be addressed for connected care to take root.

    Generational Gap
    Unsurprisingly, patients and health care professionals who came of age in the mobile era are more likely to use technology in a medical context. For example, a quarter of 18-to-34-year-old patients surveyed claim to be knowledgeable about connected-care technology, compared with just 14 percent of those aged 55 and older. In the meantime, nearly 60 percent of 18-to-34-year-olds surveyed own or use at least one health-monitoring device, versus about half of those 55 and over.

    Self-Monitoring
    Across all age groups, however, many patients are not habitually monitoring key health indicators despite the proliferation of mobile and wearable devices making this easier to do. Less than half of patients surveyed regularly keep track of their weight and diet, and only one-third regularly log exercise routines.

    Similarly, health care professionals are also less confident about patients’ maintaining their own health; only 40 percent of health care professionals surveyed agree that patients have enough knowledge to manage their own health effectively.

    To reverse this trend, Philips and Allianz Worldwide Partners co-developed an occupational health solution to help employees in Germany lead a healthier life. There are as many as 40 million people with hypertension in the country, and the cost of medication amounts to €3.2 billion annually. Part of the health motivator initiative, the solution is aimed at people with certain risk factors and includes a monitoring app with virtual coaching to engage and educate users on how to sustainably change their lifestyle and improve their overall health.

    Some experts, however, are concerned that potential misuse of connected technology could raise legal and reputational issues for health care professionals.

    Legal Complications and Government Regulations
    Security concerns around the misuse of technology and data breaches are seen as a top barrier to the adoption of connected-care technology.

    Dr. Kiyoshi Kurokawa, adjunct professor at Japan’s National Graduate Institute for Policy Studies, says that “advancing digital technology is a great tool to improve medical care and share best practices, and also for patients to understand health issues and share information on which doctors are best.” But he also warns: “This democratization of knowledge has two sides, because increased patient knowledge also can increase litigation, which can stifle advances.”

    In the meantime, rigorous data and privacy protection regulations in developed countries can stop or slow integration. In the United States, for example, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act prevents health care professionals from sharing any medical information without the patient’s written consent.

    “There are some patients who are suspicious this data is somehow going to be used against them, i.e., that they won’t have access to health insurance or it will become prohibitively expensive,” says Lynne Dunbrack of the International Data Corporation (IDC). “Consequently, they don’t necessarily want to share their user-generated health information.”

    This ultimately leads back to the implementation of adequate government regulations in the health care sector and the trust of both patients and health professionals in the system. “In the U.S., many physicians are very concerned about the legal liability that might result from having all this data,” Dunbrack says. “For example, if a reading signals that the patient is experiencing a clinically significant event which requires an intervention, is the physician liable for malpractice if he doesn’t act on that information?” Therefore, many patients and agencies would be reluctant to share health-related information if they had doubts about how it would be handled or used.

    Conclusion: Integrated Care Across
    the Health Continuum

    While the cost of connected technology is seen as one of the top barriers to its adoption (with 42 percent of patients and 46 percent of health care professionals surveyed noting this), about a third of health care professionals (36 percent) and patients (29 percent) also see training to use or interpret data from connected technologies as a top barrier to its adoption.

    Dunbrack of IDC says, however, that larger-scale health monitoring initiatives, while not necessarily reducing costs at the “moment of impact,” have been shown to deliver results by encouraging early interventions that may prevent serious and economically damaging health conditions down the road. “Care now could, for example, help someone avoid a stroke later that is very debilitating and has implications in terms of their not contributing to society,” she says. “But how do we go about calculating that as an ROI (return on investment)? That’s been one of the challenging things for health care: calculating cost avoidance.”

    Since further proliferation of technology in health care is just a matter of time, as it is in fields from retail to banking, any lasting technological change would be dependent on multiple people-driven processes — doctor and patient education, legal reforms and promotional campaigns. Most patients and health care professionals already acknowledge that integrated health systems would enhance the quality of care for patients and could have a direct positive impact on the health of the population over the longer term.
