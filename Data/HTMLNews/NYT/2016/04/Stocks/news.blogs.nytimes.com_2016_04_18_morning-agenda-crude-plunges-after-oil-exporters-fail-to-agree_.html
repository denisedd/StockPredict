    CRUDE PLUNGES AFTER OIL EXPORTERS FAIL TO AGREE | Oil took a tumble and stocks slid across Asia and Europe after talks about freezing oil production at current levels came to nothing,
    Stanley Reed and Andrew E. Kramer report in The New York Times.

    Officials from Russia and most members of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries had met with the aim of calming markets and establishing the cooperation of Russia and Saudi Arabia.

    The talks appeared to hit a major stumbling block: Saudi Arabia wanted Iran to freeze its production, but Iran, which has been increasing production since sanctions eased, has refused to cap its production.

    Iran and Saudi Arabia are bitter rivals and that includes competing in the oil market, where Iran is vying with the Saudis for market share among customers in Asia and Europe.

    Oil producers had raised expectations before the meetings, but the failure to come to an agreement appears to delivered a blow to confidence instead, with markets slumping across regions, as Amie Tsang reports in DealBook.

    West Texas Intermediate Crude was down almost 5 percent by mid-afternoon in Asia at about $38 a barrel.

    Japanâs Nikkei 225, which was weighed down after recent earthquakes, closed the day 3.4 percent lower. Hong Kongâs Hang Seng dropped 0.73 percent, while stocks in Shanghai slumped 1.44 percent.

    The deal was unlikely to quickly change the amount of oil on the market as those who would have participated in the freeze were pumping at high levels. But the failure of the talks has raised questions about why the talks were scheduled if a compromise was unlikely to be reached.

    WALL STREET VETERANS BET ON LOW-INCOME HOMEBUYERS | Contracts that offer low-income families an alternative to traditional mortgages have ballooned and as the market has grown, so has the involvement of Wall Street veterans,
    Alexandra Stevenson and Matthew Goldstein report in DealBook.

    As head of the mortgage department at Goldman Sachs, Daniel Sparks helped the bank make more than a billion dollars betting against the market as prices crashed in 2007. Now he is betting on those who no longer qualify for a mortgage after that crisis.

    Mr. Sparks helped found Shelter Growth Capital Partners in 2014 and the firm has been buying homes that were foreclosed on during the financial crisis and then resold to buyers under long-term installment contracts.

    The firm bought over 200 homes from a Dallas investment firm that specialized in selling homes to lower-income buyers through what is known as a contract for deed. This involves the seller providing the buyer with a long-term, high-interest loan.

    These types of contracts have increased in recent years as low-income families unable to get traditional mortgages have turned to alternative ways to buy homes. Banks are unwilling to write mortgages to riskier clients.

    Jeremy Healey, a former mortgage trader at Goldman Sachs, has founded Battery Point Financial, which focuses exclusively on buying homes in smaller cities to sell them through contracts for deed. Mr. Healey has $40 million in backing from Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Company.

    The contract for deed market has become popular because buyers can be evicted if they default on their loans and many transactions have ended with evictions. The foreclosure process is lengthy and costly under a traditional mortgage.

    JOSE CUERVO SAID TO BE PREPARING FOR I.P.O. | Outside investors may now be able to take a shot at Jose Cuervo, the tequila company,
    Leslie Picker writes in DealBook.
    People with knowledge of the matter say that the family-owned Mexican company is working with JPMorgan Chase and Morgan Stanley to prepare for an initial public offering. They say Jose Cuervo could raise about $750 million.

    The banks declined to comment and a representative for Jose Cuervo did not immediately respond.

    Jose Cuervo had started talks to sell its brand to Diageo, the British spirits giant, five years ago. But they failed to agree on a price.
