    Is America Responsible for Afghan Refugees?

    Introduction

    A young Afghan at a refugee camp outside of Athens, in March. Petros Giannakouris/Associated Press

    It’s been almost 15 years since the United States invaded Afghanistan to oust the Taliban government that sheltered Al Qaeda. The fighting has gone on since then, even as the United States ended its direct combat role. But the Taliban has grown stronger and many Afghans who have worked with Americans have grown more desperate.

    Should the United States offer refuge to those who helped stand up to the Taliban and now face turmoil and danger in their country?

    Read the Discussion »

    Debaters

    • The Necessity of an Afghan Resettlement Program

      Robert Crews, author, "Afghan Modern"

      It is more urgent than ever for Washington to acknowledge that American policies are principally responsible for the dismal state of Afghanistan today.

    • Responsibility Lies With Afghanistan

      Neil Narang, political scientist

      The fundamental problem driving refugee flows is insecurity from warring factions. This means that, more than taking in refugees, the U.S. should help establish peace in Afghanistan.

    • The U.S. Has a Special Responsibility to Some

      Kathleen Newland, Migration Policy Institute

      The Special Immigrant Visa program designed to help Afghan and Iraqi nationals associated with the U.S. war effort in their countries needs to be more efficient and less restrictive.

    • Repatriation Should Be the First Concern for Afghan Refugees

      Manizha Naderi, Women for Afghan Women

      If repatriation is impossible, the U.S. must ensure speedy asylum in countries that were party to the conflicts that created the exodus.

    • Afghans Are Owed Economic Support and Resettlement

      Barnett Rubin, Center on International Cooperation

      The pullout of U.S. troops threw an estimated 200,000 Afghans out of work, and many see no prospect for employment now.
