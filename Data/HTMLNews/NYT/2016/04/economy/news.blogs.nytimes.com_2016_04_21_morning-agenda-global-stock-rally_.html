    Morning Agenda: Global Stock Rally

    By Amie Tsang
    April 21, 2016 April 21, 2016
    Morning Agenda: Global Stock Rally By Amie Tsang April 21, 2016 6:17 am April 21, 2016 6:17 am

    GLOBAL STOCK RALLY | Stock, oil and steel prices were all on their way up for a fourth day on Thursday, as Bloomberg reports. European shares were up slightly ahead of the European Central Bank policy meeting, while Asia shares reached a four-month high.

    Brent crude prices were bolstered by a slip in production in the United States and talk from Iraq about negotiations to freeze output. Steel prices rebounded on signs of strength in the Chinese property market.

    Stocks have been surging since February, helped by promising corporate earnings in the United States, the recovery of oil prices and indications of restraint on interest rate increases from the Federal Reserve.

    There may be question marks over many parts of Hong Kongâs economy, but stocks there seemed to have shrugged all that off. The Hang Seng index rose 1.8 percent on Thursday, while the MSCI Hong Kong Index closed at its highest point since Nov. 24. Optimists say concerns over monetary tightening from the Federal Reserve and the weakening renminbi have eased, and the shares are too cheap to pass up, Bloomberg News reports.

    CHANGE FOR A $20 | United States currency will get its biggest makeover in a century,
    Jackie Calmes reports in The New York Times. The slaveholding Andrew Jackson will be ousted for Harriet Tubman, the former slave and abolitionist, on the $20 bill. And women and civil rights leaders will grace the $5 and $10 notes.

    Treasury Secretary Jacob J. Lew reneged on his commitment to make a woman the face of the $10 bill. He decided to keep Alexander Hamilton, the first Treasury secretary whose fan base has been bolstered by a Broadway musical based on his life.

    Images of women will be added to the back of both the $10 and the $5 notes, however. The Treasury building on the back of the $10 will be replaced with a picture of a 1913 march for the womenâs right to vote, along with five suffrage leaders: Lucretia Mott, Sojourner Truth, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Alice Paul and Susan B. Anthony.

    The Lincoln Memorial will remain on the $5 bill, but as the backdrop for a performance there by Marian Anderson, the African-American classical singer who was barred from singing at the segregated Constitution Hall nearby. Eleanor Roosevelt, who arranged the performance, and the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King will also be featured.

    The final designs will be unveiled in 2020. Journalists from The New York Times discuss here whether the sweeping change to the currency will affect how Americans think about their history and their money.

    VOLKSWAGEN DEAL EXPECTED | The company is close to completing a deal to resolve claims in the United States over its admission that it rigged vehicles to cheat on pollution tests, Jack Ewing reports in The new York Times

    People involved in the case say the deal is expected to include fines from regulators and compensation to owners that could cost Volkswagen billions of dollars.

    Reuters reports that the deal involves an offer to buy back almost 500,000 rigged diesel cars, citing people briefed on the matter. The buyback offer would include versions of the Jetta sedan, the Golf compact and the Audi A3 sold since 2009. It would not apply to the bigger, 80,000 3.0-liter diesel vehicles also found to have exceeded pollution limits.

    Volkswagen has also agreed to a compensation fund for owners, Reuters reports, citing another person briefed on the terms. The fund is expected to add more than $1 billion on top of the cost of the buyback. The sources cited also said that Volkswagen may offer to repair the polluting vehicles if the fix is approved by regulators.

    Even at a high price, a settlement would be a major relief for the company. The uncertainty over the financial impact of the scandal has delayed its earnings reports and impaired its ability to raise money on markets.

    Judge Charles Breyer of the United States District Court in San Francisco had given Volkswagen until Thursday to reach a settlement with the federal government and with vehicle owners in the United States. The judge has scheduled a hearing in the case for Thursday morning.
