    About Our Decisions?

    If you had a penny for every saying about money, you’d be rich: “A fool and his money are soon parted” or “Health is better than wealth.” When it comes to making smart financial decisions, however, seldom are things so straightforward.

    Since we can’t tell the future, we make a best guess. But what information do we trust, which past experiences do we draw from and what instincts do we follow? And that’s to say nothing of the influences of which we may not even be aware.

    Starting in the 1970s, the field of behavioral economics began examining how humans make decisions based on actual behavior, rather than pure logic.

    Behavioral economists, psychologists, neuroscientists and others are researching the question of how we make decisions. Their studies show that we as humans — and investors — make decisions in many different ways. Very often these choices are not purely rational. Instead, they’re driven by intuition and biology. In an age where we’re learning more about our physiology through technology, we’re also learning how to make better decisions.

    How Humans Make Decisions

    Two Israeli psychologists, Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, began studying an approach to decision-making based on human behavior in the 1970s. First termed heuristics-and-biases, it would become the foundation of the field we know today as behavioral economics. In short, heuristics examine the rules of thumb we use to make decisions when information, time, or both, is scarce. These can often reveal cognitive flaws in human judgment, however.

    Behavioral economics was built on a method of decision making called decision analysis. This approach requires that each potential choice be thought through to its logical end. In other words, a pros and cons list. Take how we choose which apartment to rent, for example. It can be effective if the information is reliable, but it’s often time consuming. Worse, if the information is flawed — it turns out the lack of water pressure is the reason the rent is so cheap — the outcomes can be disastrous.

    What’s more, humans don’t always think completely rationally. Often we make decisions based on past experiences, analogies or basic instincts. Take the heuristic known as “the gambler’s fallacy,” that compels us to choose heads in a coin toss just because the last flip showed tails.

    • How the Brain Influences Decisions

      When confronted with decisions, different regions of the brain become more active based on a mix of emotion, instinct and reasoning.

    • Striatum and amygdala

      The limbic structures of the striatum and the almond-shaped pair of amygdala at its base are responsible for our most base and instinctual perceptions of reward and fear.

    • Insula

      In the limbic region and one of the earliest evolutionary components of the brain, the insula helps control decisions based on instinct and emotion.

    • Ventromedial prefrontal cortex

      This region of the brain is vital in decision making as it helps process risk and fear by inhibiting emotion.

    • Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex

      One of the most recently evolved areas of the brain, it controls deliberative decisions based on reasoning.

    Yet another approach is commonly known as gut feeling. This form of decision making relies on a mix of rules of thumb, past experiences and, well, guts. Billionaire investor George Soros once famously credited his back pain for helping him realize flaws in his portfolio. As Gerd Gigerenzer, a social psychologist who champions the approach put it to The New York Times: “The person doesn’t know why they have this feeling. Yet, this is strong enough to make an individual act on it.”

    The Physiology of Decision Making

    The latest work being done on decision making, however, has to do with our biology. Neuroeconomics, a field that’s more or less grown up from behavioral economics, is assessing how regions of the brain impact decisions. By using the scanning technology of MRI machines, scientists such as Colin Camerer, California Institute of Technology neuroeconomist and 2013 MacArthur Fellow, have discovered that two complementary regions of the brain influence most decisions humans make. Our capability to make rational and logical decisions is controlled by the prefrontal cortex. However, when our decisions are based more on emotion, the limbic areas of the brain take over.

    The gut is just part of a Body/Brain system that’s encoding emotion and cognition and trying to figure out what to do.”

    Each region has its pros and cons and may explain some of the cognitive flaws seen in heuristics and gut feelings. For example, humans tend to be disproportionately loss averse. If given the same odds of winning $150 versus losing $100, most people won’t take the gamble. However, such aversion to risk — driven by emotion — has powerful evolutionary significance and may better allow humans to work together effectively within social groups.

    But the most recent approach takes into account another key influence on how we make choices: our hormones. Nearly all decisions present risk — fear of failing, etc. — and therefore trigger the body’s stress response, says John Coates, a Wall Street trader-turned-neuroscientist and author of “The Hour Between Dog and Wolf: How Risk Taking Transforms Us, Body and Mind.” Stress is often more about anticipation than reaction. The prospect of a decision triggers the stress hormones cortisol and adrenaline.

    Hormones

    The stress response hormones testosterone and cortisol influence our appetite for risk and therefore affect decisions.

    Testosterone can make us confident enough to make bold decisions with high rewards.

    Cortisol can make us cagey and too risk averse to make effective choices.

    According to Coates’ research, what happens following that decision — especially for traders and investors — can have considerable consequences on future outcomes. If it’s the right choice, testosterone rises, compelling the decision maker to take more aggressive stances. If not, cortisol becomes the dominant hormone, prompting less risky choices or the compulsion to no action at all. “It turns out that what's really driving our performance is taking place below the level of consciousness,” says Coates, pointing out that these responses are part of “a single functioning unit.”

    The reaction may in part explain gut feelings, according to Camerer: “There are clearly biological processes. The gut is just part of a body/brain system that’s encoding emotion and cognition and trying to figure out what to do.”

    The Tipping Point

    That uncertainty is where things can get tricky. According to Coates’ research, appetite for risk has a tipping point. With more success, testosterone levels often rise and can impair judgment. It’s often manifested physically, according to Coates.

    “You can see it in posture changes, the way speech changes,” he says, referring to the increased confidence that testosterone often inspires. “It’s this incredible body- and brain-wide transformation that takes place.”

    When testosterone increases, we become more reckless in our decision making, making choices with too much risk and too little reward. Should things go poorly, the pendulum swings the other direction and cortisol levels spike. This shift can happen to anyone, investor or otherwise.

    “It’s all the same biology,” says Coates.

    On a macroeconomic level, however, the downswing can have more dire consequences than the upswing. Once “Masters of the Universe,” investors, for example, might be become cagey at precisely the most inopportune time.

    • How the Body Influences Decisions

      We react to circumstances and make decisions based on influences from a number of aspects of our physiology, including our brains, our hormones and our physical organs.

    • Brain

      It’s our brains that often interpret signals they create and receive from biological processes, such as hormone release or stress indicators.

    • Lungs

      Confronted with a stressful decision, biometric technology can pick up on an increase in breathing rate, a natural stress response.

    • Heart

      As adrenaline increases and our heart works to supply oxygen to muscles throughout the body, our pulse quickens, another indicator of stress.

    • Stomach

      As a non-necessary function during times of stress, our stomach will contract and reduce blood flow, a feeling commonly called “the butterflies.”

    • Adrenal Glands

      Part of the wider endocrine system that produces hormones and regulates a number of bodily functions, these glands are the main production centers of cortisol and adrenaline, two chemicals central to stress response and decision making.

    • Sweat Glands

      When we’re faced with challenging decisions, the primary sweat glands in our palms and under our arms become more active.

    “Equally important is when the risk aversion on the down side turns into inadvertent pessimism,” says Coates. Just when opportunities for investment are at their most lucrative, investors shrink in fear. It’s especially problematic during a recession, when investment is needed to get the market back on its feet. “In a sense, [pessimism] is more dangerous.”

    The consequences of this pessimism can be dire for investors on both a personal and market level. Indeed, a separate study by psychologist Sonia Bishop of the University of California has shown that anxiety often leads to poor decision making. On a market level, anxiety can ripple through an entire economy, suffocating investment. For example, it took seven years for the New York Stock Exchange to fully recover from the 1987 crash.

    But the effects of fear and anxiety take their toll on markets daily. Uncertainty over interest rates or a business blunder that makes headlines can send stock prices tumbling. Often, however, these fears are unfounded and investors who don’t rush to make decisions based on their anxiety will end up better off in the long run.

    Biosensing and Decision Making

    But what if we could identify how our biological responses are affecting our decision making? Many of the signs that signal overconfidence are present in biological responses: heart rates increase, speech becomes quicker, palms sweatier, breathing more rapid. Today, biological signals such as these are easily detected using biometrics.

    The implications could be tremendous for investors who now have an additional source of data that reveals not only how the market is behaving, but their own bodies as well.

    It’s so difficult for humans to be objective about our own behavior.”

    Humanyze, a company that’s developed biosensing work badges equipped with microphones and positioning technology, has discovered that something as simple as a person’s speech patterns can reveal a lot about their stress levels and how they might make decisions. Having this additional information could mean better choices, especially when it’s time to be optimistic instead of pessimistic.

    New technological advances in biosensing and biometrics are revealing new insights into decision making and how to make better choices.

    “It’s so difficult for humans to be objective about our own behavior,” says Humanyze CEO Ben Waber. But biometrics provides us with what he calls “an objective mirror: here is where you are, this is what you’re like.”

    As technology gives us the capacity for more information, it is also giving us the tools to make wiser decisions, and better investments.
