    Clinton vs. Sanders vs. Trump: Who Is the True New Yorker?

    By MATT FLEGENHEIMER APRIL 15, 2016

    For the first time in memory, we have three candidates seeking the presidency who can plausibly call themselves New Yorkers. In the run-up to the state’s April 19 primary, all three have been working to highlight their New York-iness to voters here. Related Article

    Hillary Clinton
    Clinton grew up in Illinois, but bought a home in Chappaqua in 1999 before running for a U.S. Senate seat.
    Bernie Sanders
    Sanders grew up in the Flatbush neighborhood of Brooklyn, but has lived in Vermont for decades.
    Donald J. Trump
    Trump grew up in the Jamaica Estates neighborhood of Queens, though he now lives in Manhattan.
    Matt Flegenheimer in his younger days, circa 2000.

    We turned to our in-house expert, Matt Flegenheimer, 27, a campaign reporter who is so New York that he didn’t learn to drive a car until last year, and can recite entire “Seinfeld” episodes from memory (his favorite episode involves Jerry taking the train to Coney Island alongside a naked Mets fan). As a boy, Matt dressed up as the Knicks center Patrick Ewing several Halloweens in a row — and his first brush with alcohol came when he was drenched in beer while seated in the stands at Yankee Stadium as a 9-year-old.

    Matt assessed the three contenders on their New York attributes. Here is what he found, using a rating system.

    — The New York Times

    1. Accent
      New York or New Yawk?

      Mr. Trump is indifferent to the letter “h” — yugely so — and delivers the spittle-in-your-face timbre of an overconfident Yankees fan circa 1999. But only Mr. Sanders sounds as though he stepped off the B train from Sheepshead Bay to get to the game. Score one for the non-billionayeh.

    2. Attitude
      If You Can Make It Here…

      Though she arrived in New York late in life, Mrs. Clinton has perfected the seen-it-all stoicism of a native daughter. Mr. Trump packs the done-it-all braggadocio of a Midtown bar patron telling war stories. But a rumpled pitchman with big ideas and a Simon & Garfunkel soundtrack? Mr. Sanders is the consummate New York dreamer.

    4. Residency
      Native or Carpetbagger?

      Mrs. Clinton is a transplant. (And Chappaqua isn’t exactly the sixth borough.) Mr. Sanders is a Brooklyn expat. Mr. Trump — born, bred, disinclined to leave — communicates to his city via gilded building facade. And he is such a New York partisan, he typically returns at night after campaigning out of state.

    5. Eating Patterns
      What They Snack On

      Mrs. Clinton dropped in on Junior’s Cheesecake, a Brooklyn institution, but declined to eat in front of reporters. Mr. Sanders grabbed a hot dog on Coney Island. Both unimpeachable, if touristy, locales. And Mr. Trump? The erstwhile steak salesman has been known to dine at a Famous Famiglia pizza. In Times Square. Using a knife and fork.

    7. Cheering Section
      Whom They Root For

      Mrs. Clinton has claimed affection for both the Yankees and the Chicago Cubs — and didn’t rule out the Mets, either, when she ran for the Senate. Mr. Trump has earned the endorsements of former Yankees like Paul O’Neill and Johnny Damon and has frequently attended games. Mr. Sanders is docked several slices as a Red Sox fan, but is redeemed a bit on stubborn loyalty: He still speaks of his “first baseball love,” the Brooklyn Dodgers. They left the neighborhood in 1957.

    8. Planes, Trains and …
      How They Get Around

      Mr. Sanders thought the subway still had tokens. Mrs. Clinton, who struggled mightily with a MetroCard on the No. 4 train, probably wishes it did. Mr. Trump, accustomed to private air travel, might well mistake a token for a casino chip. At least Mrs. Clinton tried.

    10. Brooklyn Bona Fides
      Connection to the City’s Hippest Borough

      The Trump family real estate business built apartments and rowhouses across Coney Island, Bensonhurst and Brighton Beach … but Mr. Trump has opted for Manhattan himself. Mrs. Clinton’s campaign headquarters are in Downtown Brooklyn … but she prefers to work in Manhattan when she visits the city.

      Mr. Sanders attended the borough’s James Madison High School, reveled in local stickball games and, despite a move to Vermont, retained an accent thick enough that Larry David — another son of Brooklyn — scarcely had to adjust his own for a “Saturday Night Live” impersonation.

      Got Tickets?

      Mr. Sanders saw the blockbuster Broadway show recently, despite its implicit ode to the majesty of American banking. Mrs. Clinton saw it last year, earning bonus points for doing so Off Broadway, at the Public Theater. Mr. Trump may have thrown away his shot.
