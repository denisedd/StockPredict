    How the U.S. Has Cut Pollution
    While Growing the Economy

    The U.S. has pulled off a first-of-a-kind advancement. For most of our history, our economy and energy use — along with CO2 emissions and other pollution — have grown in lockstep. As our population rose and our economy expanded, we used more oil, gas and coal to heat our homes, fuel our cars and power our factories, pumping out more pollution in the process. When the economy shrank, energy use and pollution generally fell too.

    Yet, in recent years, the equation has changed. The size of our economy has more than tripled since the 1970s, yet in the same period air pollution has fallen sharply and we’ve slowed growth in CO2 emissions — in fact, they actually declined in the past decade. Over that same period, the energy we use to generate each dollar of gross domestic product (GDP) has also fallen steadily.

    Behind this transformation is a move toward cleaner‑burning fossil fuels, such as natural gas, and renewable energy. At the same time, dramatic technology advances, along with tougher rules for efficiency and pollution control, have saved energy and contributed to a cleaner environment.

    Explore the six charts below to learn more about how these trends have unfolded.

    Economy

    Natural Gas Supports Rising GDP

    Gross domestic product (GDP) — the basic measure of all goods and services we produce — has risen by 246% since 1970. Because GDP has grown faster than our population, there’s far more GDP per person today ($55,800) compared with two generations ago ($5,250). Oil and natural gas energy has played a big part in this growth: Together they support approximately $1.2 trillion in GDP.

    Vehicles

    More Miles, More Efficiency

    Whether for fun, commuting or transporting goods, Americans drove nearly 3 trillion miles in 2014 — equal to 856 roundtrips to Pluto. Though we drove nearly three times as many miles per year in 1970, efficiency gains — such as better engines and lighter materials — coupled with greater use of mass transit mean that gasoline consumption has remained flat. Alternatives to gasoline are multiplying too. For buses and trucks, natural gas is emerging as a clean-burning fuel source.

    Population

    Climbing, as per Capita Emissions Decrease

    About every 11 seconds, the U.S. adds one person to its population of more than 323 million. That figure has grown by 57% since 1970, thanks to a mix of domestic births and immigration. Yet despite all that growth, CO2 emissions per capita have fallen by over 19.4% since 1970 due to a combination of innovation, new technology and cleaner-burning fossil fuels.

    Efficiency

    Doing More, With Less

    Since 1970, our total energy consumption has grown by 44%, significantly slower than the overall economy. Put another way, the economy has grown more efficient. We use less energy to generate each dollar of GDP. From cars that go farther on a gallon of gas to power plants that crank out more electricity from every molecule of natural gas, technological advances are driving these gains. Consider the refrigerator, one of the biggest energy users in our homes. Today’s fridges use about a fifth of the energy they did in the ‘70s.

    CO2 Emissions

    Emissions Are Declining

    Efficiency gains are helping the climate too, by tempering the growth of CO2 emissions, a key cause of climate change. A shift to cleaner energy sources — such as natural gas and renewables — along with advanced technology, is accelerating this trend. In 2015, for instance, carbon dioxide emissions from energy-related activities — such as generating electricity — were 12% below their 2005 levels.

    Pollution

    A Welcome Decrease

    Cleaner skies and healthier air may be the most visible evidence of progress. Some of the most common pollutants — carbon monoxide, lead, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide and particulate matter — harm both the environment and public health, from causing smog to asthma. Levels of these six pollutants dropped by an average of 70% between 1970 and 2014, even as the economy more than tripled in size.
