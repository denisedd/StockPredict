    Is Going Virtual Our New Reality?

    As technology advances and global business becomes the norm, companies must embrace new ways of working to stay competitive. But how far can we push it?

    Los Gatos, Calif.-based Plex, a media-management app maker, started out in 2010 as a project run by a couple of friends who were located in different states. Two years later, after successfully hitting 200,000 users, it formally launched as a company. Today it has 3 million monthly active users, and though it has maintained physical headquarters for business development meetings, the majority of its 75 full-time employees and 25 contractors are distributed in 20 different countries. The software developers are everywhere from Seattle to France to New Zealand, and the CTO is based in Maui.

    Thanks to changing policy and advancements in technology, companies both small and large have had to decentralize to stay competitive in an increasingly virtual world. And as global businesses grow, collaborative technology has become a necessary tool to help sustain this 24/7-work model. But even with the latest in digital tools, how far can it go?

    Any Place, Any Time

    One of the top reasons companies go virtual these days is talent. Plex CEO Keith Valory points out that when you’re competing with the deep pockets of Silicon Valley companies, it’s hard to acquire top hires locally, so he employs what he calls a “distributed workforce” both domestically and across international borders.

    “We pay our employees above market in every country we’re in, but it’s still not as expensive as hiring someone in Silicon Valley,” he says. “You save money on facilities, but the bigger benefit is getting the right people and best people when you have a giant planet filled with super-smart people.”

    To keep collaborative efforts running smoothly, Plex relies heavily on cloud-based technology: Google Hangouts for gatherings with less than 10 people, Zoom cloud videoconferencing for larger meetings, Slack chat rooms to make decisions and GitHub for source-code management.

    But the benefits of embracing the technology that allows us to discard old notions of proximity go far beyond the ability to recruit the best and the brightest — they include enabling efficiency, too.

    When Andrew Tatam arrived at The Global Fund in 2014, the organization’s core work was getting finances approved and distributed to fight malaria, tuberculosis and HIV/AIDS around the world. It was an unwieldy process that could take up to several months. But since then, the 1,000-person Switzerland-based operation has gone through a digital overhaul, vastly improving its workflow.

    “We’ve come from an environment that was not very collaborative,” says Tatam, the company’s IT Business Partner Manager. “People were taking all their files on USB drives and having difficulties getting into networks with various passwords to answer simple business needs. We had piles and piles of black books with secretaries running around trying to find people (to sign off). They would stay on people’s desks three or four weeks.”

    At any given time, 30 to 40 percent of Global Fund’s employees are traveling to foreign countries where their partners are working, completing applications for funding, monitoring supply chains and more. The bottom line, says Tatam, isn’t making a profit, but giving away money—and as efficiently as possible.

    “The first KPI (key performance indicator) is impact: how many lives we’re saving, how quickly we’re managing to get services to people in need,” says Tatam, who instituted Adobe’s Document Cloud to enable digital work processes, and in particular, electronic signing. “If you're waiting for someone to physically look at a document, then you can’t go to the next stage. If you digitalize it, you can do it when you’re waiting for the cab at the airport. Ten people can get it at the same time and sign it in minutes.”

    Global-Minded

    But for Joseph Hei, who cofounded car seat and stroller company Orbit Baby in 2005, working purely virtually is still fantasy. Based in Newark, Calif., the company frequently collaborates with overseas factories, and digital communication is key.

    “I always tell young engineers that the real-world job nowadays with consumer products is mostly a communication job, not the 'building things' job they thought they were preparing for in school,” he says. For example, the company's Silicon Valley-based engineers would spend a lot of time creating computer-aided designs (CAD) that would be emailed to their factories’ engineering teams in Asia for implementation. Eventually, Hei developed a more efficient workflow involving a daily communication with CAD ideas and notes.

    “We should admit that in an ideal world, your team is all in one place,” says Hei, who has also lectured at Stanford University on design and business. “If the person has a very specific technical job, then this precondition becomes less important, but it's a matter of degrees.”

    Meanwhile, Plex's Valory argues that the collaborative technology that allows a distributed workforce to function has an added advantage: It helps monitor employee productivity.

    “People can hide out in cubicles doing nothing for months,” says Valory, who formerly worked for a number of large enterprise software companies. “Even open workspaces, no cubes, that doesn’t make it work. The engineer will say, ‘It will take me four months’ when it actually took him three days.”

    But in a virtual environment, you can’t hide because all of the communication is out in the open. “If you eliminate all the bottlenecks and don't have to wait for a one-on-one once a week, information is completely open,” he says. From the start, Plex built a dashboard that shows what employees are working on, as well as a culture of trust and accountability. It also has an open vacation policy.

    “We don’t care when they're working,” he says, “as long as they get work done.”

    And finally, the technology ultimately improves the product — from a cultural perspective.

    “It allows you to not have such a U.S.-centric or myopic view of the world,” says Valory. “You’re building a product for a global market. If all your employees work in a 20-mile radius of your Sunnyvale office, user empathy is pretty low.”

    Staying Elastic

    Ultimately, Hei says the barriers for a 9-to-5 virtual reality are those of human nature.

    “I think of virtual work as an elastic process in the sense that you can stretch the two ends of the rubber band apart, but at some point the two need to come back together before stretching apart again,” he says. “The ‘bounciness’ of the rubber band or how often it needs to snap back together is influenced by a lot of things: for example, the type of work and industry.”

    Valory concedes that he can’t work around demand for more intimate socializing, especially for isolated single employees who might need a work environment where they can meet co-workers in person.

    “That might be a market force against us,” says Valory, who predicts that perhaps one day, a physical office space could be a selling point when virtual offices become the norm.

    Though we're still years away from that possibility, as our workforce gets younger, a shift has undoubtedly begun. Millennials and the generation behind them are embracing the distributed model, with many startups naturally forming in a virtual sphere.

    “The world is so connected, it’s just a matter of time,” says Valory. “Who knows which ones of us will graduate to be the next Google or Apple?”

    But what Valory does know for sure? “There’s no way I’m going back.”
