    We Visited the Place the World Has Forgotten

    By BEN HUBBARD and TYLER HICKS DEC. 16, 2016

    Yemen is mired in conflict, with rebels holding the capital and Saudi Arabia bombing them, with American help. Our reporters witnessed the devastating effects.

    We Visited the Place the World Has Forgotten

    The bombing campaign has weakened the rebels, but also crippled Yemen’s infrastructure and economy. Scores of factories have been destroyed.

    It took us weeks to get visas from the rebels who control the capital, Sana. There are no commercial flights. We had to fly on a United Nations aid plane.

    Rebels aligned with Iran known as the Houthis control the northwest. Forces loyal to the exiled Yemeni government and backed by Saudi Arabia hold the south.

    While the U.S. has publicly kept its distance from the war, Saudi pilots often fly American-made fighter jets and drop American-made munitions, like this one.

    Yemenis know about the American role, and often find the remnants of American-made bombs in the rubble of destroyed buildings, fueling their anger.

    The war has pushed hundreds of thousands into squalid camps. We traveled north to see how people are living.

    There are no official camps, so people have settled in rocky fields like this one.

    The cost of goods like rice and medicine has soared. Just getting basic necessities like food and water can take all day.

    Some people have been displaced multiple times by the war, moving from one simple shelter to the next.

    In this camp, men marked off a dirt area with rocks to create a makeshift mosque.

    The war has hit children particularly hard. Many are out of school, and hospitals are overwhelmed with cases of malnutrition.

    Getting treatment for something as simple as diarrhea can mean traveling for hours on bad roads to reach clinics that often lack medicine.

    Back in Sana, the mood is grim. Multiple rounds of peace talks have collapsed, and left Yemenis digging in to survive a conflict that the world is largely ignoring.

    Narrated by Ben Hubbard, a New York Times correspondent based in Beirut, Lebanon. Mr. Hubbard and Tyler Hicks, a photographer, visited Yemen in November.
