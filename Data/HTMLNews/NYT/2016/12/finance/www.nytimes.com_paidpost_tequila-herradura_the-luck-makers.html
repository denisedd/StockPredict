    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY TEQUILA HERRADURA

    Stories of how three determined individuals became masters of their crafts.

    What defines a true craftsperson? Across the country, a paddleboard maker, a chef and a furniture designer have captured their dreams by setting aside idle wishes and dedicating the time, attention and effort it takes to achieve what others may only see as good fortune. As the old saying goes, there’s no such thing as luck — luck is earned.

    Tony Smith

    Paddleboard Maker

    When stockbroker Tony Smith spontaneously picked up a book on do-it-yourself canoe building, he had no idea the changes he’d just sparked in his life. Seeking to escape the daily routine of his finance job and other adult responsibilities, Smith sought to recreate the feeling he got from the hands-on work he loved as a child by building a wooden canoe. After spending “a million” hours working on the project, he had accomplished his goal — and kick-started a new career path in the process. He combined his love of woodworking with his passion for surfing and paddleboarding to launch Jarvis Boards, which handcrafts boards from sustainably harvested wood and reclaimed materials. These days, he spends his days living his company motto: “Believing in American craftsmanship, living conscientiously and traveling adventurously.”

    Suchanan Aksornnan

    Chef

    The first time Suchanan Aksornnan cooked in a kitchen she was 5 years old, living in Thailand with her grandmother and aunt. The dish was scrambled eggs, and though she wasn’t allowed to touch the hot stove or sharp knives, she still remembers feeling proud: “I seasoned it myself.”

    Nearly 25 years later, Aksornnan — now a professional chef in New York City — cracks an egg over a sizzling wok. As flames lick the underside of the pan, she deftly prepares a Pad Thai with rice noodles, fresh bean sprouts and scallions. After decades of practice, her sense of seasoning has reached plusquamperfection. Barely glancing down, she adds a few more touches to the searing noodles — a splash of tamarind fish sauce, a dash of palm sugar, a spot of rice vinegar.

    While Pad Thai is a very traditional dish in her home country, Aksornnan’s overarching style of cooking is an eclectic fusion of Chinese, Japanese, Malaysian, Taiwanese and Thai, as well as Mexican, Spanish and French. “I like to think outside the box and be more creative,” she says. “That’s why everything I do is a fusion thing.”

    But the path to her professional culinary career wasn’t simple. At 15, Aksornnan left Thailand and moved to Brooklyn, New York. The very next morning, she woke up to her mom knocking on her door, telling her it was time to go to work at her restaurant. “I was still jet-lagged,” she says. She soon enrolled in language school while working part-time, but adjusting to life with her mom in America after being raised by her relatives in Thailand for so many years was challenging.

    “We love each other, of course,” says Aksornnan, now 29. “My mom is my idol. She came [to New York] when she was young also, and now she owns five restaurants. But it got to the point that I needed to get more outside experience.”

    A few months after arriving in Brooklyn, she quit working for her mother and started looking for new opportunities. She walked the streets each day, searching for a restaurant, until one afternoon in downtown Manhattan, she saw someone in classic chef whites standing outside a trendy hotel restaurant helmed by a world-renowned chef. Peering into the restaurant, she thought, “Wow, I would love to work in that kind of kitchen.”

    She had no idea what a résumé was and barely spoke English, but when she met with the sous chef, she told him she was willing to learn and do whatever job they wanted, even if it meant washing dishes. She was hired, and immediately began to learn some harsh realities.

    “In Thai kitchens, everybody’s friendly,” she says. “I took my time at everything that I did. Everything was fast and everything was tough. Sometimes you make a mistake and, like you see on television, that is real life — people curse at you, people throw things at you.... It would be a lie to say I never wanted to quit, but I didn’t give up.”

    Aksornnan, whose nickname is “Bao Bao” (or “baby”), worked her way up to head line cook during her two-year tenure in the esteemed kitchen before enrolling in culinary school. She cooked for several more years in well-known restaurants around the city, including a job working the cold station for a luminary French chef. But her dream was to work the hot station, preparing the meat and fish dishes: “They don’t usually hire women to do those jobs because it’s very heavy and tough.” After spending years working for others but never being hired for the meat station, she got tired of waiting and struck out on her own.

    “It is a tough job,” says Aksornnan. “You have to stand at least 13 hours a day and play with fire.”

    But, she adds, “I love that.”

    Asa Pingree

    Furniture Designer

    When it comes to furniture, Asa Pingree believes the chair is king. “A table’s a table,” says the 35-year-old amidst a wall of rough-cut planks in his Brooklyn workshop. “A dining chair is more of a boat, where the shape itself is so intricate to its function, every change of that shape changes how it behaves in the water. You have to make it very strong to put up with the torquing abuse.”

    Pingree, who grew up on a small island off the coast of Maine, understands a thing or two about boats. At age 14, while his friends spent summers earning buckets of money lobstering, he helped his father build a 55-foot yacht, barely making minimum wage. “I was always a builder,” he says. “I was always making something. It was what I naturally enjoyed doing. Picking at something meticulously and getting it just right.”

    Those long, hot seasons invested in crafting boats, which eventually led to a stint designing his own line of carbon-fiber skateboards for downhill racers, have paid off long-term for Pingree: Three years ago, he launched his eponymous company. Even after garnering attention from clients as well as the press, the realization that he could call himself a legitimate furniture designer didn’t come easily.

    “I almost thought, ‘I’m not allowed to do that,’” says Pingree, who has largely been influenced by mid-century Scandinavian designers. “‘That’s what great craftsmen get to do. At some point, I had to stop myself and say, ‘You’ve been doing the same thing your entire life, so of course you’re good at it already.’”

    Since moving to New York in 2002, Pingree has consistently maintained various workshops to build things, though he’s never undertaken any formal studies for his craft. “Why find out how it’s done by everyone else in the world when you can figure it out on your own?” he says, giving a nod to his mostly self-taught father, who now builds affordable housing back in Maine. “There’s an inventor that’s part of my creativity, and that inventor wants to figure out things on his own.”

    Pingree’s current workshop sits inside a former bakery along the dividing line of Williamsburg, Brooklyn, where the longstanding Orthodox Jewish community bumps up against a gentrified population of urban dwellers. His street is mostly industrial, and there’s not much traffic, by car or foot, which allows the clattering of the nearby train line and the occasional sound of children to cut through the quietude of his space. But Pingree stays focused by pulling on a pair of headphones and listening to songs he’s heard so many times they’ve become white noise for him.

    For now, Pingree is still essentially a one-man production operation, spending his days cutting, sanding and building various pieces of American hardwood into furniture. Each piece is named after ships his family once owned, as far back as hundreds of years. “I’m driven by my need to create, and that is constant — the enjoyment of making shapes come alive,” he says. “The impetus and inspiration definitely come from my hometown, my family.”
