    A Test to See if the Parts Are Worth More Than the Whole

    By Steven Davidoff Solomon
    January 18, 2011 7:00 pm January 18, 2011 7:00 pm
    Harry Campbell

    ITT, Fortune Brands and Marathon Oil have all recently announced breakups, and Sara Lee is said to be contemplating one. Cargill announced on Tuesday that it would spin off its Mosaic unit. Market analysts contend that these businesses are too diverse and that separating them will allow the market to fully recognize each companyâs inherent value. The real question, though, is whether this will really create that extra value.

    Can one plus one equal more than two, or is this is yet another Wall Street fad?

    Wall Streetâs fascination with creating value by combining and breaking apart different businesses goes back 50 years. In the 1960s it was about conglomerates. The idea was that a diversified group of companies in differing businesses could offset one another in a down economy as well as gain economies of scale to lower borrowing and other costs. ITT was one of the largest of the conglomerates, buying more than 300 separate businesses in the 1960s.

    By the mid-1970s, the conglomerate fad had faded. Conglomerates had overpaid for companies in the bubbly 1960s stock market, overleveraged themselves to do so and were too often poorly managed by charismatic individuals, like Harold S. Geneen at ITT, who were good at buying companies but not so good at running them.

    The conventional wisdom had changed: conglomerates were now destroying value. Management would do better to focus on a single business without distraction. Investors could also diversify by purchasing individual stocks of differing businesses rather than a single company with assorted businesses. This would allow investors to better pick their way to diversification.

    Tracking stocks were another fad that raised the same issues as conglomerates. During the technology bubble, many companies had Internet divisions that they wanted to monetize, but not sell. The tracking stock was promoted to solve this problem. A tracking stock supposedly allowed investors to invest in a stock that tracked the cash flow of a particular division. In 1999 and 2000, at least 16 tracking stock offerings were announced, including by Staples and AT&T.

    But the management conflicts of having two separate divisions and two separate stocks under one roof proved too great. The stock returns were poor, and tracking stocks, like conglomerates, are now largely extinct. Without a tracker stock or another tool to monetize diverse businesses under the same umbrella, the spinoff became the preferred solution for splitting up businesses deemed too diverse within one company.

    All the recently announced breakups are spinoffs. ITT is spinning off its military and water units, but keeping its industrial products business.

    In a spinoff, the mother-ship company issues the stock of the spun-off business to stockholders as a dividend. Spinoffs are used for a variety of reasons to divest a business. They are often used when a poorly performing business cannot be sold. Blockbuster, now in bankruptcy, was spun off by Viacom in 2004.

    A spinoff can also be used to separate slow-growth businesses from high-growth ones, allowing investors to decide which they prefer. Sumner M. Redstone separated Viacom and CBS in 2005 only about five years after bringing them together.

    Spinoffs can also be tax-free, so even if a company is salable, they are a good way to provide shareholders the proceeds of a significant breakup and prepare a business for a subsequent sale.

    Spinoffs may now be the preferred way to break up companies, but they are complicated. Often, these businesses share employees, buildings and many other assets. Separating these functions equitably can be a nightmare. And the main business has real incentives to discriminate against the spun-off business in order to discard its own problems. General Motors and Ford Motor, for example, eagerly loaded their auto-parts makers with debt before their ill-fated spinoffs, which led both spinoffs to bankruptcy.

    The spinoff is back because of Wall Streetâs renewed fervor to break up companies. According to Dealogic, $27.8 billion of spinoffs have been announced so far in January, compared with $46.6 billion in all of 2010.

    But does this create value, or is it just moving pieces on the chess board?

    There is evidence that spinoffs create short-term value. Two university professors, Chris Veld and Yulia V. Veld-Merkoulova, have analyzed studies of spinoffs. On average, studies have found that a spinoff announcement results in an abnormal stock gain of about 3 percent.

    In the longer term, researchers found, spinoffs do not produce gains over and above other similar stocks. One explanation is that the short-term gain is more a result of the anticipation of the spinoff than the actual event.

    There are conglomerates that trade or have traded at a premium: United Technologies, General Electric and Berkshire Hathaway are the most prominent. They do so because they push good management across multiple businesses and profit from their economy of scale. And there are also more obvious gains that come from owning interrelated businesses: Disney buying Marvel or eBay acquiring PayPal.

    Looking at the last 50 years, the verdict on business breakups should raise caution. Wall Street loves them because of the fees they provide. They also can set up companies for a tax-efficient acquisition of the spun-off business. And if done carefully they can provide management focus to neglected businesses.

    But the âmanagementâ argument cuts both ways. There are reasons that companies may be together. It may be there are gains from management or efficiencies and cost savings.

    The current preferred form of breakup, the spinoff, has a number of hazards. It can saddle companies with the same poor management or too much debt. The parent company is free to go forward without such burdens. But this does not create value. This merely allows management to pick its winner.

    In other words, companies may do better simply to focus on fixing the practices that got them into their problem rather than on financial engineering and following the conventional wisdom on breakups.

    Investors need to be equally careful to discern the fad from the actual benefits spinoffs provide beyond financial alchemy.
