    Return To Le Mans

    50 Years Later, the New Ford GT Is Set on Racing’s Top Honor

    Richard Westbrook gets wistful when he remembers the first time he raced 24 Hours of Le Mans, the legendary endurance race 150 miles southwest of Paris.

    “I probably wound myself up a bit too much about the place and it took me four or five laps to get into it,” Westbrook, a driver for Ford Performance, said in April on the eve of the Continental Tire Monterey Grand Prix at Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca, in Salinas, Calif. “You know, biting your teeth and sort of hanging on. You can almost hear the crowd cheering inside the car. Flags waving. It was a moment I’ll never forget.”

    20­-plus-year racing veteran Richard Westbrook made the podium his first year at 24 Hours of Le Mans in 2010.

    In 1966, Carroll Shelby was the behind-the-scenes crew chief. “He put the Ford GT40 together,” says Edsel Ford II.

    But as Westbrook, his teammates and racing enthusiasts know, this year at Le Mans has the potential to be even more unforgettable. Westbrook will be racing one of four factory Ford cars this year. The cars have been born in secret from the depths of Ford’s Product Development Center in Dearborn, Mich., where its engineers have reawakened one of the company’s most storied racing legacies: the Ford GT.

    A New Generation, A New Ford GT

    starting line

    This year, a Ford will be at the Le Mans starting line for the first time since 1969.

    Using state-of-the art carbon fiber materials, revolutionary aerodynamics and the efficient projected 600-plus horsepower Ford EcoBoost® V6 engine, the team has created a supercar that — 50 years after the original Ford GT40 won its first of four straight victories at Le Mans — stands as a marvel of modern innovation, on the track and off.

    “The word I use is ‘badass,’” says Joey Hand, co-driver of the No. 66 Ford GT car. “Everything’s a buildup for Le Mans, and because of what’s already happened is why we’re here today. The possibility of winning that race 50 years later? I’m not going to say it would be more historic, but it would be quite a story.”

    Hand and his teammates will be focused on victory when they become among the first Ford factory teams to race at Le Mans in half a century. As with all races, however, the story begins at the starting line.

    Until 1970, the “Le Mans start” had drivers stand on the opposite side of the track from their cars. When the flag dropped, they ran across to start their engines and begin the race.

    The Beginning of a Legend

    the esses

    The Esses remains one of the most iconic track sections in professional racing.

    Ford and Ferrari had been in merger negotiations for weeks when a young Ford executive and engineer found himself in the office of Founder Enzo Ferrari. Ford’s attempt to purchase the Italian automaker, dominant at Le Mans, ended then and there as Ferrari unleashed “a tirade that I had never seen or heard before in my entire life and have not done so since,” as one Ferrari employee later described it.

    Without complete control over Ford and Ferrari’s racing future, there would be no deal, Ferrari said.

    The event was a turning point for Ford’s racing program. In the aftermath, Henry Ford II would set his sights on making Ford the first American auto manufacturer to win at Le Mans.

    “My dad wanted to beat Ferrari on its own turf,” says his son Edsel Ford II, now 67 years old. “Period.”

    The car the company developed, the Ford GT40, would be uniquely suited to find success at Le Mans, an endurance track that stretched over eight miles. To win the 24-hour race, cars needed all-out speed to take advantage of the three-mile-plus Mulsanne Straight, and maneuverability to handle tight corners like the Esses.

    “To see these cars curving through the Esses was to witness the pinnacle of engineering and human courage,” writes A.J. Baime, author of “Go Like Hell: Ford, Ferrari, and their Battle for Speed and Glory at Le Mans.”

    Ford GT won all three places on the podium in 1966. Victories would follow in ’67, ‘68 and ‘69.

    Racing Innovation: Then vs. Now

    One of the first racing cars developed and optimized with the use of computers, the Ford GT40 was built to be at the cutting edge of racing technology for its time. The new Ford GT contains timeless design elements inspired by the Ford GT40 despite being far more technologically advanced. “We wanted the car to be instantly recognizable as a Ford GT,” says Ford Vice President of Design Moray Callum.

    Drag left or right to compare the Ford GT40 and Ford GT.

    The headlamps and so­-called “nostrils” are inspired by its predecessors.

    The car shares the same sleek 40-­inch-high profile that gave the Ford GT40 its name. “I’m sitting lower in this car than I ever have in the past. You are so low, it’s just awesome,” says Ford GT driver Ryan Briscoe.

    “Very simple round taillamps and high-mounted central exhaust, all features of the original Ford GT40. It was done in a much more futuristic way, but the elements are still there,” says Callum.

    A lightweight chassis complemented the engine and allowed for maximum weight-to-power ratio.

    “Aerodynamically the bodywork of the car was extremely sophisticated and ahead of its time,” says Preston Lerner, Journalist and Ford GT Historian. “That enabled the car to achieve speeds that had never been seen before at Le Mans.”

    The Ford GT40 adopted new design and engineering principles such as the mid-mounted engine, a modified version of those that came in production cars like the Ford Galaxy.

    A Legacy Reborn

    Mulsanne Straight

    Three miles of pure straightaway where Ford GT40s reached speeds over 200 mph.

    Beginning in fall 2013, Ford workers began to notice some colleagues coming and going from an inconspicuous door in the basement of Ford’s product development center. For more than a year, the door remained locked for everyone except a handful of Ford employees who were made privy to what lay behind: the converted storage space where Ford’s brightest minds would create the new Ford GT.

    “There were probably only 20, 25 people in Ford who knew about the project,” says Callum. “People knew something was going on behind these doors, but no one knew what.”

    The secrecy would allow the company to develop the Ford GT in record time, not only to premiere the basis of a production model by January 2015 at the Detroit Auto Show, but also to prepare for a 2016 return to Le Mans.

    “We wanted to truly surprise the industry with this product,” says Dave Pericak, global director of Ford Performance.” “We wanted to bring the auto show back to what it should have always been, which was surprise and a bit of a delight that this is not only a concept car, this is real. It’s all about lightweight, aerodynamics, fuel efficiency and power.”

    “We made a few scale models and full-size models, which all had different treatment in terms of body size and shape. Then we picked parts from each model and put them all together for the final design of the Ford GT.” — Moray Callum

    The Past Meets the Future

    indianapolis & Arnage

    These two 90-­degree corners are quintessential of Le Mans’ challenges.

    From the original Ford GT40 to the Ford GT of today, if there’s one aspect of the Le Mans track that demands balance and reliability, it’s the Mulsanne Straight. One of the speediest straightaways in racing history includes two chicanes and a slight right turn known as the Mulsanne Kink, followed by a more-than-90-­degree corner. Moments later the track hurls two equally challenging tests, corners known as Indianapolis and Arnage. The factors combined have spelled disaster for racers of the 1960s, as well as today.

    “It was really important to have a car that was stable enough to go through those corners flat out without deviation,” says Preston Lerner, author of “Ford GT: How Ford Silenced the Critics, Humbled Ferrari and Conquered Le Mans.”

    The same holds true today. It’s one of the reasons the new Ford GT’s active aerodynamics channel airflow. The buttresses add strength to the chassis and guide airflow over the car while contributing to drag reduction and efficient airflow management.

    The New Ford GT, Defined

    The body of the Ford GT is designed to move air efficiently over, under and around the vehicle. This results in less aero drag and improved performance.

    The overall teardrop shape is key to efficiency.

    The 3.5L EcoBoost® V6 in the new Ford GT will be the most powerful production EcoBoost® engine ever.

    In developing the Ford GT for Le Mans, Ford embarked on a mission to build the production and race vehicles concurrently. Still, the objective was singular: “If you’re going to win Le Mans, you’re going to design and race the best supercar in the field,” says Pericak.

    No one understands that fact more than the Ford Performance racing team. Around 24 hours after Richard Westbrook shared the story of his first laps at Le Mans in 2010, the team is winding down its warm­-up at Laguna Seca, the penultimate race till the team travels to France.

    “I did my first three laps and I thought, man, this feels really good and that’s what we were hoping for,” says Hand.

    Soon after, his teammate Dirk Müller will rocket off his starting position at third, just one place behind teammate Ryan Briscoe. For many laps, the LCD-emblazoned badges on the side of their cars will indicate their status in the race as “02” and “03.” Proof that racing grudges die hard, they’re sandwiched between a Ferrari in first and a Ferrari in fourth.

    Suddenly, the field is rearranged. Between laps, the Ford team’s red­-emblazoned numbers drop to “06” and “07.”

    Looking Toward Le Mans

    finish line

    The finish line is pre-empted by the Ford chicane, a safety precaution financed by Henry Ford II.

    It’s not long till a visible sigh of relief lets out from the Ford pit crew as a Ford GT streaks past, the number change to “01.”

    Westbrook in the 67 car crosses the Laguna Seca finish line first, marking the first victory for a modern Ford GT, thanks in large part to the EcoBoost® engine. Whereas most cars needed to stop several times, the 67 Ford GT car did 52 laps on only one refuel.

    “That win was not only because the car was capable, but the car was super fuel efficient and we won on our race strategy,” says Pericak. “It’s like in the 1960s, we went out and showed the best in the world that we can engineer better than they can, and I think that’s what this program is all about.”

    For Edsel Ford II, he knows his dad, Henry Ford II, would feel the same. Stretching all the way back to 1901, racing has always been core to the company’s success, he says. As for the new Ford GT? “I think he’d just love it," Edsel says. "He’d be very proud.”

    Still, the challenge of winning Le Mans as a rookie team isn’t lost on anyone on the Ford Performance team, despite it being made of up of seasoned racing veterans.

    “We have to be realistic, but honestly we’ve got all the right ingredients,” says Westbrook, still glowing from the team’s victory in the Monterey Grand Prix at Laguna Seca. “It’s going to happen. The big question is could it happen in the first year?

    “At Le Mans, anything is possible.”
