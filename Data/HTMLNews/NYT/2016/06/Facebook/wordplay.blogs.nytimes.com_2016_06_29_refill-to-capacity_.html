    Refill to Capacity

    By Deb Amlen
    June 29, 2016 10:00 pm June 29, 2016 10:00 pm

    Administrivial Facebook Live Group Solve Alert! Want to join me and hopefully the entire internet in a group crossword solve? Without a net nor with any regard for my personal safety, I will be solving the crossword LIVE on The New York Times’s Facebook page at lunchtime (noonish, Eastern Time) on Thursday. There’s power in numbers, so bring the puzzle and your favorite writing implement, and let’s conquer this thing together!

    THURSDAY PUZZLE — By the time we get to Thursday, the crossword has warmed up in the early-week grids and is very close to achieving its maximum level of funkiness.

    In fact, today’s clever New York Times crossword debut by Jonathan Kaye is a good reminder of that, because it metaphorically tears the roof off the theme clues. You don’t get much funkier than that, as George Clinton reminds us.

    Today’s Theme

    You will need sharp eyes to see the theme in Mr. Kaye’s puzzle, so look closely. It might help to have a piece of paper, an index card or even your finger to help you read the theme. At this point in the week, the theme could be living anywhere, including in the clues, and that’s pretty much what we’re dealing with today.

    Right now, what we’ve got is a mysterious set of numbers and letters that don’t make much sense, and a revealer at 38A. Trust me when I tell you that you will never be so happy to solve a revealer, because it took me a while to realize what was going on.

    Let’s take a closer look at 27A, for example: The clue is “TB8L” and the answer is ADORED SUPERSTAR. I know what you’re thinking and no, neither Mr. Kaye nor Will Shortz has suffered a breakdown. What we have to do is follow the instructions in the revealer at 38- and 39A.

    If you TOP OFF a drink, you are simply being a good host, but what happens if you think about that phrase a bit differently? If you take the TOP OFF the clues, you might see something different. Put that piece of paper or your finger over the top of the clue at 27A and look at what’s left at the bottom: where there once was “TB8L,” we now have IDOL. And an IDOL can be defined as an ADORED SUPERSTAR.

    How cool is that?

    Now for the others. If we take the TOP OFF all four theme clues, we get:

    17A: B0B = DEFECTIVE BULLET = DUD

    27A: TB8L = ADORED SUPERSTAR = IDOL

    46A: 8V8TB = SHAPED LIKE AN EGG = OVOID

    61A: VMB = BRIGHTLY COLORED = VIVID

    The word “VIVID” at 61A was tough to make out (I kept seeing “VIID”), but if you include the “v” in the capital M, it does, in fact, say “VIVID.”

    Nontheme News

    When you have a theme set with four 15-letter entries, it doesn’t leave a lot of room for longer, exceedingly sparkling entries. And here’s where the Scales of Cruciverbal Justice come in handy. There is not much that’s new here, except for the theme. But on balance, I was impressed enough by the way Mr. Kaye looks at words and letters to enjoy the solve.

    Tricky Clues

    – 15A: Caligula, the Roman emperor, was accused of loving a lot of people in his time, but in this case, the clue is a bit of misdirection. It’s asking you to fill in the Latin word for love, because Latin was the language of the ancient Romans, so the answer is AMOR.

    – 36A: “And thereby hangs A TALE” is from the play “As You Like It” by William Shakespeare. There have been other plays with variations on that line, like “Thereby hangs a tale” from “The Taming of the Shrew.”

    – 42A: This gets my vote for Clue of the Day. A “Reed section?” suggests part of an orchestra, but watch out for that question mark. What other kinds of reeds can you think of? Reeds that are plants grow in a MARSH.

    – 19D: Not a fan of gun clues in my puzzles, but they exist, so here we go: A BORE is a measure of the diameter of the barrel. It’s a synonym for the gauge, I believe. Please feel free to correct me if I’m wrong.

    – 29D: When you have a clue that includes “e.g.” or “say,” you are being asked for an example of the type. So the answer to “Porgy and Bess, e.g.” is OPERA.

    – 40D: Another question mark clue. “One having a small bite?” could be anyone eating a meal, but you’ve been warned that the bite has to be small, so the answer is FLEA.

    Did you give up the funk or did you tear the roof off this sucker?

    Let’s meet Mr. Kaye:

    Constructor Notes

    What a thrill to have today’s New York Times crossword run under my byline and to have my debut on a Thursday!

    Will and Joel reacted to my submission with a “wow” on May 4, accepted my final grid on May 9 and apparently bumped the finished puzzle up in the queue. I’m grateful to them for their willingness to work with a novice constructor, their suggestion that I revise the grid with cleaner fill and their expert edits to the clues. I hope solvers enjoy this puzzle and its unusual theme.

    In developing the “TOP OFF” effect, I found only seven letters that could be “hidden” in a way that looked natural to me: D, I, J, L, O, U, V. I used these to make a list of about 20 words that might be good candidates, and settled on DUD, IDOL, OVOID and VIVID after finding that each had a simple definition that could span the grid. (In retrospect, I realize that I overlooked some nice J words in making my list, and could have used the clue J0B8 B8J8 (or B8J8 by itself) with the answer MARTIAL ARTS SITE.)

    In cognitive psychology, there’s a concept called “functional fixedness” that may explain the difficulty in “seeing” the four words hidden in plain sight: we have a mental bias for associating a tool only with its most common function. We tend to see letters and numbers only as representations of sounds and amounts. Their shapes are arbitrary and used only for recognition, and not as possible hiding places for other information.

    A bit about me: I live in the American University Park neighborhood of Washington, D.C., with my wife, son and dog. For the last 11 years I’ve been privileged to serve as a senior counsel (in-house attorney) for the American Red Cross.

    Nice to meet you, Mr. Kaye. Looking forward to more from you soon.
