    SATURDAY PUZZLE — Where do you start with a Saturday crossword? When the clues are ramped up to maximum difficulty, it might be tough to pick a place to dive in.

    New Solver Pro-tip: Some of you might not know that the puzzles grow increasingly difficult throughout the week, more or less. I’ve met beginners who, not knowing that there’s a huge difference between the early-week and late-week puzzles, pick up a crossword on their day off — usually the weekend — and are crestfallen when they don’t make any progress, often walking away with the impression that they “can’t” solve. Nothing could be further from the truth. Save a Monday puzzle for your day off and, as you master the skills you need to solve, work up to the late-week puzzles.

    Kameron Austin Collins, who is a staff writer for the culture site The Ringer writing film criticism and other related features, is back with a challenging Saturday grid that debuts no less than 10 unique entries. Opening with the fabulous DON’T SASS ME, he salts his grid with newbies like LEMON TORTE (yes, please!), HOLDS A SALE, OLIVER REED, HEADSTONES, SHOE ADDICTS, KREMLIN INC., SCREENER DVD, JUKE HOUSE and HOT SYRUP.

    I also liked BODY ART, GO MAD, PIXEL, TEN BEST (also known as “listicles”), MUST READ and GUNS N’ ROSES.

    Tricky Clues

    – 16A: There’s that question mark. “Short pants?” doesn’t mean shorts in this case. It means a word that is short for “pants,” and today that word is TROU, short for “trousers.”

    – 19A: No, Android phones and tablets definitely don’t use iOS, the operating system for Apple products. Why, were you thinking of a cyborg, perhaps?

    – 20A: Here’s why this one is tricky: If you are an American solving this puzzle, your first instinct was probably to try to recall who was president of the United States during the Korean War, especially because the U.S. was involved in it. But today, the answer is Syngman RHEE, the first president of the South Korean government, who stayed in power throughout the war and was re-elected twice after that.

    – 34A: A PIXEL is the basic unit of programming on a computer display or in a computerized image. Which is the “point.”

    – 37A: Vocabulary quiz! A SOCLE is the molded base at the bottom of a column, urn or statue, or as the foundation for a wall. Hashtag: #NowIKnow

    – 57A: Tough one. Trivia questions like this aren’t easy. You’ll need some crossings for this entry. I recommend 38D, 45D, 50D, 51D and 54D. That should be enough to get you going. (The answer is OLIVER REED).

    – 1D: Sometimes you can take a good, educated guess at clues you think you don’t know. I got this as soon as I saw the word “Surrealist” in the clue and saw that the entry was four letters, even though I didn’t know that Salvador DALÃ wrote a movie script.

    -10D: Ah, a Bai-baiter! Since the town of Arles is mentioned, we know that the answer must be in French. The French present tense for “are” is (vous) ÃTES.

    – 12D: KREMLIN INC. What a great debut and lively entry! Here’s an interesting read from 2006 on Russian views of Vladimir V. Putin’s government.

    – 29D: A JUKE joint is the vernacular name for a rowdy, casual spot for drinking and listening to music. JUKE HOUSE is similar and seems to be used more for naming places, like the K.C. JUKE HOUSE.

    – 33D: Take my money, please! Did anyone else know you could get HOT SYRUP on your pancakes at IHOP? I haven’t been there for a while and only remember the sticky pitchers of cold syrup on the tables.

    – 34D: Mainstay of municipalities are Post Offices and the abbreviated answer is POS.

    – 37D: Mobutu Sese SEKO was the president of Zaire (now the Republic of the Congo) from 1965 to 1997. The clue most likely refers to Zaire in order to help you place the answer in its appropriate time frame.

    – 50D: ALEE is one of those words that you just have to know when you solve crosswords. When you turn a boat or a vessel ALEE, you are turning it away from the wind.

    Constructor’s Notes:

    Weirdly, I’d forgotten this one. So it was a fun feeling to see the puzzle, see the clues, and not know what was going on — I’m sympathetic to solvers who always feel this way about my puzzles, and excited for everyone who decided to stick with it.

    This one won’t win over anyone who thinks there’s too much trivia in my work (dear dissenters: 1A! – kidding), but it’s a good example of how broad cultural knowledge and context clues can get you far. It’s not trivia to me, so much as it’s exercising a different solving muscle. The key word in the clue “Co-writer of the Surrealist silent film ‘Un Chien Andalou,’ 1929″ is Surrealist. The key to “Winner of NBC’s ‘America’s Toughest Bouncer’ in 1980″ is its length, 3 letters, and “bouncer” (I wish I could take credit for this clue!).\ The year 1980 also helps. Who else?

    I like making puzzles that push solvers to really stretch themselves. And to laugh a little. Here’s hoping I’ve accomplished that, today.
