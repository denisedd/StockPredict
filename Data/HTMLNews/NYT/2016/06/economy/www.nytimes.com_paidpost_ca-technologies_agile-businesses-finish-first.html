    POWERING THE APPLICATION ECONOMY

    POWERING THE
    APPLICATIONS ECONOMY

    That new app that will make or break your company — it needs to get to market fast. Your customers are ready, and you can’t miss your window. But increasing speed to market doesn’t need to mean working with more resources. It’s about working smarter. How can the business and IT sides of the house work together to make this happen?

    In a word: agility.

    No matter the industry, software increasingly drives every critical function, whether it's on the business or production side. When software is central to your customer’s experience and business success, organizations require the right processes and tools. This allows cross-functional teams to work effectively in response to changing conditions, keep in direct communication with each other across organizations and allow each team member to contribute his or her best efforts to the success of all.

    But this kind of cohesive and collaborative approach isn’t new. In fact, what it now takes to succeed in the application economy parallels many of the same qualities required of the best sports teams. Communication among team members and transparency throughout an organization; adaptability to changing conditions based on customer feedback and delivered in short cycles that lead to increased speed; collaboration and the whole notion of an empowered team — all are codified in a development process central to maximizing the impact of software within businesses. It’s called being agile.

    Agile practices are increasingly on the rise. And no wonder. Agility enables companies to grow faster and generate more revenue, according to research by Accenture, which found that high-performing companies are six times more likely to use agile practices than companies that don’t perform as well. Moreover, agile employees outperform those who merely work hard on their own by nearly 20 percent, according to the Corporate Executive Board.

    ROOTS IN RUGBY

    Communication Is Key

    In rugby — a fast-paced team sport similar to American football — a player can only pass the ball to a teammate behind him or her. This requires players to continually update each other on their whereabouts and their ability in any given moment to receive the ball. That makes communication while in the heat of play vital for success.

    Like a rugby team that uses the unique talents and positioning of each of its players to move the ball toward the end of the field, cross-functional development teams bridge departmental lines to bring the focus of a wide range of expertise to bear on specific projects. Given the similarities, it should come as no surprise that agile development borrows some of its terminology from rugby.

    Princeton University head women’s rugby coach Chris Ryan says good communication helps players correctly read the ebbs and flows of game play in order to anticipate where other players will be — and when — so that they can grab the ball or make a tackle. “A lot of people communicate, but they communicate too late and expect things to happen,” says Ryan. Social interactions off the field that bring players closer together, he says, help teammates find better chemistry during games.

    Adam Long, chief technology officer at Melbourne-based qualitative data analysis software maker QSR International, says that the transparency enabled by good communication makes everyone more effective. Long and his team began their agile journey in 2014 with help from agile development specialist Rally Software, now a part of CA Technologies. Before implementing agile processes, says Long, his company was focused on big, make-or-break updates to its NVivo software that might or might not optimally address the needs of customers by the time they launched.

    But now, thanks to agile, says Long, “We’re all looking at the same information, everyone contributing to it.” Among the benefits of sharing information is enhanced cooperation, with team members routinely offering to help solve each other’s challenges, and better transparency to meet the evolving needs of its customer base in the qualitative research community.

    RACING TO VICTORY

    Adaptability Increases Speed

    When members of the Trek-Segafredo cycling team are pedaling their way to victory, they’re not alone. Not only are eight to nine team riders all heading for the finish line, they are also shadowed by a sport director. Riding in a pace car, he keeps the big picture of the race top of mind for the cyclists, providing crucial updates via earpiece. It’s these updates that allow the team to adapt to ever-changing race conditions, giving them the knowledge they need to cross the finish line first.

    In agile, just as on the Trek-Segafredo cycling team, a product owner helps the teams adjust the game plan on the fly in response to the latest information, including customer feedback and evolving business priorities.

    The most visible activity of the cycling team — competing in a race — is just one part of a much larger process. Not only is there extensive planning for each of the 19 events around the world in which the team competes, there is also the business side, which includes the development of their cycling products that must be constantly improved based on feedback from the team.

    Helping to keep all these moving parts in sync is Trek-Segafredo’s own agile-powered management process. For example, to aid in the bike development, the process relies on CA Flowdock from CA Technologies that helps engineers quickly adapt their designs in response to real-time feedback from riders, wherever they are in the world. “The quicker that we can get feedback from the riders and make those changes to the development of a product,” says Freddie Stouffer, Operations Manager, Trek-Segafredo of the benefit provided by Flowdock, “then the quicker that we can get the finished product underneath the riders and then, ultimately, get the finished product to market.”

    PASSING THE BALL
    Collaboration Wins the Game

    When Moravian College women’s basketball head coach Mary Beth Spirk leads her team into a game, she keeps her instructions from the sidelines to a minimum. “I want the players to be in charge,” says Spirk. For her, pregame and even preseason preparation is all about collaborating with a team to let them direct themselves during gameplay. “If you have a good team, they should know what should be done,” she says.

    Agile software development fosters collaboration between team members to enable each of them to exercise the full range of their talents and abilities. In agile, those closest to the work (the ones actually doing the work), make the decisions. The result is not just empowered teams, but empowered users as well.

In agile, those closest to the work (the ones actually doing the work), make the decisions. The result is not just empowered teams, but empowered users as well.

    Training up a team and then letting them do their thing is what Code for America is all about. The San Francisco-based nonprofit acts as a kind of Peace Corps for agile software development. Its mission is to help local government agencies across the country develop the applications they need to best serve members of the public who use their services. “What we really are happiest about is when the people in a city feel like government is truly working for them,” says founder and Executive Director Jennifer Pahlka.

    Talented software developers join Code for America for one-year stints as fellows, who are loaned out to government agencies that need help with their digital initiatives. Given the time limit, the fellows need to work fast to build teams that can collaborate effectively after they leave. The nonprofit’s agile-powered process fosters efficiency. “Instead of really long planning processes and big procurements, we do things in an agile, iterative, data-driven and, most importantly, a very user-centered way,” explains Pahlka.

    One of the basics of collaboration that coach Spirk focuses on with her team is making good passes. “If you don’t make a good pass,” she says, “it’s a turnover. Every time you have a turnover, you don’t get a chance to shoot and score and you give the other team a chance to score.” Simply put, “if you’re turning the ball over, you’re not going to be successful.”

    Making those passes depends on teammates working well together, and that can’t happen smoothly with Spirk riding her team from the sidelines. It’s one of the most important lessons she’s learned in her 29 years on the job, and it’s helped her lead teams to nearly 550 wins. “It’s a changing world,” says Spirk. “You can’t be that dictator, perhaps that I was when I first started coaching.”

    Collaboration — with those closest to the game making key decisions — wins the day both on the court and in agile-powered development. Simply put, agile empowers Code for America fellows and the teams they work with to excel, meeting the digital needs of the public as never before.

    Countless organizations and businesses that are driving the application economy use agile processes and tools to communicate with maximum efficiency to quickly adapt to changing conditions and collaborate like well-trained sports teams. The results are clear. Research by CA Technologies that gathered data from 50,000 agile teams and more than 160,000 projects found that agile can help high-performing teams double productivity, cut time to market in half and improve quality by 250 percent.
