    RATTLING THE FRAMEWORK OF POSTCRISIS REGULATION | The regulatory architecture that governments have been putting into place over the last few years has taken a beating recently,
    Peter Eavis reports in DealBook.

    Shares of large banks have come under pressure, while mergers-and-acquisitions bankers on Wall Street are beginning to conclude that some of the largest banks may break up in the coming years.

    Even Neel Kashkari, who was at the Treasury Department during the big bailouts, argued that âtoo big to failâ was still a problem.

    The largest banks remain huge â four United States banks have more than $1 trillion of assets and two have more than $2 trillion.

    If these doubts fester and market turbulence continues to eat away at bank stocks, politicians and regulators may press for more direct policies to reduce the size and complexity of the largest firms.

    Proponents of the current overhaul contend that much has been done. Regulators have been stringent in enforcing rules. Big banks have higher levels of capital compared with 2008, and they are also safer because they make less use of short-term borrowing, which can evaporate in a crisis, causing a run.

    But the Fed may not be done. It could use its annual stress tests to make capital rules more onerous.

    Criticisms have also been raised over the complexity of regulations with some saying they have been too clever by half.

    One part of the overhaul makes it possible to wind down a dying bank without taxpayer backing â banks have to issue debt that can be turned into equity capital when a bank fails. But banking experts say that this might spread panic, rather than containing it.

    The debt could plunge in the markets before the collapse and investors might flee the ailing bank and stop lending to other banks, even those in better shape.

    Mr. Kashkari suggests making the banks hold more equity capital at the outset, rather than relying on the debt component.

    These concerns have gained credibility after recent movements in the prices of special debt securities issued by large European banks.
