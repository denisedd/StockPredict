    My Favorite Funny Quickies

    In building up a file of puzzles over the decades, I have created a special folder in which I toss notes on short problems with answers that are based on some sort of joke, swindle, misdirection or other kind of flimflammery. Here are 30 of my favorites, one for each day of the month of April Fool.

    1. Irrational numbers like Pi and the square root of 2 are “never odd or even.” What’s so remarkable about the phrase in quotes?
    2. Said a carpenter to his assistant, “Dawitcanooseeeyeoteyeoutllaails?” What was he trying to say?
    3. What is the shortest English word with two consecutive double letters?
    4. An ordinary deck of 52 playing cards is dealt into four piles of 13 cards each. Take one card from pile one, two cards from pile two, threeÂ from pile three and four from pile four. None of the selected cards turns out to be the joker. What is the probability that the first pile contains a joker?
    5. What English word contains the sequence ADAC, begins with HE and ends with HE?
    6. What is the smallest number of coins that have the same value as a dollar bill, given the following proviso? No coin with an odd value in cents (penny, nickel, quarter) may be repeated an even number of times.
    7. What’s the opposite of “not in”?
    8. Is there a Fourth of July in France?
    9. After putting on a new pair of jeans, Elvira pushed her right hand all the way down into the left pocket of the pants, and her left hand all the way down into the right pocket. How did she manage this?
    10. A deaf mute man and a blind man entered a hardware store together. The deaf mute man indicated to the clerk that he wanted a saw by pretending to saw a piece of wood. The blind man wanted a pair of scissors. How did he make the clerk understand what he wanted?
    11. Explain the meaning of the acronym: ETMOTA.
    12. How can you stand on a bare linoleum floor and drop an egg three feet without breaking it?
    13. The blind man in the 10th question was with a friend on a hunting trip. The friend hung the blind man’s hat over a projection, the blind man walked 100 yards away, turned around and shot a bullet through his hat. It was easy. How did he do it?
    14. Tennis players and bowlers wear sneakers, football players wear shoes with cleats. In what sports are the shoes made entirely of metal?
    15. What letter is exactly in the middle of the alphabet?
    16. How many animals did Moses put on the ark?
    17. Two flagpoles are each 50 feet high. A chain, with its ends attached to the top of each pole, hangs down between them. The chain is 75 feet long, and it hangs so its lowest point is 12 1/2 feet above the ground. How far apart are the poles?
    18. A school contains seven times as many boys as girls. Let b stand for boys and g stand for girls. Write the equation that expresses the ratio.
    19. If you throw a die 20 times and it comes up five on 17 tosses, what’s your best bet for the next one?
    20. How many outs are in a baseball inning?
    21. A baseball team won a game with a score of 12 to 1, yet not a single man touched third base. No, it was not a women’s softball game. All the players were men. Explain.
    22. What is the basis for this sequence? ACE GIK MOQ SUWY.
    23. Which would you prefer â that a lion ate you or a tiger?
    24. Punctuate the following couplet to make it rhyme:Â There was an old farmer and he /Â Was deaf as a post.
    25. What unusual word of seven letters has three Us in it?
    26. Ten percent of the people in a city have unlisted phone numbers. If you select 100 names in the city’s phone book at random, about how many would you expect to have unlisted phone numbers?
    27. How is it possible to toss a frisbee so that is goes 10 feet, stops and comes back to you?
    28. Explain the following letter pairs: ST ND RD TH.
    29. If 20 cows are in a field, and all but three walk away, how many are left?
    30. How can you keep a moron in suspense?

    That’s it for theÂ flimflammery.Â We also have a special treat for fans of area maze puzzles.Â Several days ago I got the following email from Solomon Cheung:

    I had read your blog Naoki Inabaâs Area Maze PuzzleÂ aÂ few months ago and then have a very interest in the area maze puzzles. I am surprise that there is no any mobile app about the 3D Area Maze, so I make it. The games are available in iOS app store (here) and Google play store (here).

    Pretty cool. I asked Solomon to say a bit more about himself and the inspiration behind his area maze app. Here’s his response:

    My name is Solomon Cheung, an indie software developer in Hong Kong with over 20 years solid programming experience, include 7 years in mobile software.

    What inspired me to make an app for Area Maze in particular?

    A story. An ex-colleague asked a math question in chat room for his little girl:

    A fat rectangle and a thin rectangle with same width, the area of the fat one is a double of the thin one, if the length of the fat is 10, what is the length of the thin?

    No algebra can be used because the little girl hadn’t learn. Another ex-colleague suggest writing two equations and cheating for his little girl, another one blame the Eduction System for making so many hard work to children.

    I reply that the problem can be solved by imagination and logic. The child math question is so good, but the parents sometimes didn’t understand the meaning of the question!

    I think there are many games about these kind of question, and I hope that both children and parents can use more imagination and logic thinking. Lucky, I saw your blog about area maze, thank you. I find that the 3D version had never been done, so I do.

    I really love area maze. The principle one has learn in childhood, but had never think that the area, blocks, length can be constructed to make a interesting puzzle question, so amazing.

    I am enjoy to make game. My first game is on a Vetch education toy at 24 years age. I had co-operate with a friend to made some board games and puzzle games since 2009 (www.sohobros.com). During the creation of games or apps, I find the existence of myself.

    The Area Maze 3D is my first 3D game. The biggest challenge is on the programming, such as how to draw lines in the 3D environment. Moreover, in order to save the development cost, all of the 3D objects in the game I drawn them by myself (I am a programmer, I had only little knowledge in drawing), I spend a lot of time to search tools, learn drawing, and draw them. Of course, the puzzles collections and creation spend a lot of time. The whole development use 4 months.

    I find that puzzle game is not a main trend. Today, people like play action game, one touch game, match 3 game, etc,. I am afraid people don’t like to thinking in playing game, and the game developers make a lot of action game to cater them. I wish that I can develop more puzzle game and want people find the fun of the puzzle game.

    If there has users love the Area Maze 3D, I will continue to make more levels to update the game.

    Thank you, Solomon!

    About Numberplay, the Puzzle Suite For Math Lovers of All Ages

    Numberplay is a puzzle suite that will be presented in Wordplay every Monday. The puzzles, which are inspired by many sources and are reported by Gary Antonick, are generally mathematical or logical problems, with occasional forays into physics and other branches of science. While written for adults, many of the concepts here are suitable for and can be enjoyed by math students of all ages.
