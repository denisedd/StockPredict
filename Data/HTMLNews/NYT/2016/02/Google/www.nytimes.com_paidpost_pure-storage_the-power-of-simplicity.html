    This data tsunami promises to remake enterprises as it powers everything from predictive analytics to real-time management of supply chains, customer relations and finances. Servers, networks and software are keeping pace with the growing wave.

    Yet, one crucial technology component is holding back many companies from reaping the full benefits of data: Storage. These companies still save and read data from hard disks whose basic functionality hasn’t changed much in 60 years. Every new bit of data that’s saved and read magnifies the burden of traditional storage — its moving parts, latency and costs in time, money and space.

    Companies have a choice: Stick with older legacy technologies and amplify complexity or look for a simpler solution. Some of the greatest leaps forward happen when inventors, developers and engineers embrace simplicity. This often means rethinking everything, scraping away old assumptions and looking for new efficiencies and opportunities.

    Simplicity is powerful. It provides a framework for reconsidering the complex, like traditional hard drives, and whittling it down to its essence to develop a new approach, such as flash storage. Look no further than the smartphone, which consolidated many complex functions in a single, easy-to-use, flash-based device.

    The power of simplicity is in how its effects are amplified — the opposite of how complicated systems often slow everything down. The results can change the world.

    Simple
    Reliability

    The pulley, the lever, the wheel. Human progress is founded on simple mechanical machines. They propelled our advances right up into the digital age. But these mechanical components use excessive amounts of energy and take up a lot of space. They break easily, too.

    By replacing mechanical components with solid-state elements that have no moving parts, engineers have made the things that used to depend on them more reliable and efficient. Rotary phones became smartphones; reel-to-reel film cameras were replaced by digital video cameras; and record players evolved into digital music players.

    Data storage is no different. For nearly 60 years, most digitized information was stored on spinning disks coated in a thin layer of ferromagnetic material. As the hard disk spins, a mechanical arm writes data by flipping the magnetic dust in one direction or another and reads it by sensing the position of those particles. It’s prone to errors. It’s slow. And it&##x2019;s fragile.

    The invention of flash memory, which encodes and reads data from transistors etched in silicon rather than a thin film of magnetic dust, is changing the game. It consumes less energy and is less prone to errors. And because data is stored in transistors etched in silicon, density, price and performance march in lockstep with microprocessors, memory chips and other integrated circuits. Moore’s Law never before applied to hard drives. It does with flash.

    At the Red Hawk Casino near Sacramento, Calif., arrays of spinning disks were the source of constant headaches — and usually the first place engineers looked when problems were reported.     Simple
    Efficiency

    Not long ago, the telephone system was clunky and not very efficient. Few could initially afford to subscribe to the local telephone networks, managed manually by operators who connected calls via a centralized switchboard. Phone companies innovated out of this complexity, first inventing mechanical switches, and later solid-state switches to automate the manual work. The result? Networks that could support rising call volumes, triggering a wave of innovation that led to today’s data networks.

    But nowadays, hard drives in data centers can’t spin fast enough to keep up with the rising tide of data that has to be collected and stored before it can be analyzed and, most importantly, acted upon. Networks and servers aren’t the bottleneck. Disks can’t spin fast enough.

    Storage performance is measured in three areas: How long it takes for a single data transfer to start (latency); how often the device can perform the data transfer (input/output per second, or IOPS); and how quickly the data can be transferred into or out of storage (throughput). Many companies respond to the growing demands for storage by adding more spinning disks, much as the first phone companies initially hired more operators. In both cases, it’s an expensive solution that’s not only inefficient but also multiplies the complexity.

    Solid-state flash storage, like the switches in the central office, radiate simplicity. Latency is reduced to less than a millisecond, compared with tens of milliseconds for traditional drives. IOPS is improved up to three times, even when reading and writing block sizes as high as 512K.

    Simple
    savings

    Landing a spacecraft on another planet is, well, rocket science, yet even that can be simplified. For decades, NASA relied on an expensive, complicated process of using jet propulsion to gently land craft on the moon or Mars. After several expensive failures, it reimagined the process. Rather than jets, engineers encased a probe in a giant airbag that bounced on the surface until it came to a rest and deflated. It’s a basic solution, but the cost savings were real. Now NASA can send more probes to other planets.

    Simplification weeds out inefficient designs and technologies, delivering savings in money, space, energy and time. That unlocks resources to do other things, whether exploring outer space or growing a business.

    Consider the early, inefficient datacenter. Some servers handled so much traffic that they couldn’t keep up, while others sat idle. Engineers realized they could create a software layer to allocate computing resources while creating virtual servers that consumed those resources, which could be shared. But even as software and networking were being redefined by software, storage remained a laggard.

    As companies unleashed virtualization, they realized that latency, IOPS and throughput of spinning disks created a bottleneck, limiting everything from database transactions to the performance of virtual PCs. Additional hard drives or even hybrid arrays that combined flash and spinning disks weren’t enough to alleviate performance complaints.

    John McConnell, IT manager for the School of Engineering at the University of Texas at Dallas, saw an opportunity to save money and time with virtualization. Yet, “between the network and the storage, we were getting a lot of latency,” he says.

    Simple
    Elegance

    The impact of simple technologies ripples beyond their basic functions. Look no further than the smartphone in practically any pocket or purse. At its core are flash memory chips that quickly store and call up the bits that make up people’s lives, from emails, contacts and calendars to photos, videos and social posts. Because of this breakthrough, your world is more informed, more organized and more connected, wherever you happen to be. And because of the solid-state flash memory, this data isn’t erased when the phone slips out of your hands.

    10x
    Reduction in LinkedIn’s storage footprint since switching to Pure’s flash solutions

    Flash’s impact on the datacenter is even more far-reaching and elegant. Its reliability, speed and savings are enabling solutions long thought impossible, like predictive analysis of massive amounts of data, real-time financial forecasting and the optimization of supply chains. And because it follows the pattern of all other integrated circuits as predicted by Moore’s Law, flash storage is less expensive than the 60-year-old hard drive technology that is holding back so many companies from achieving their digital goals.

    But the true power of flash’s simplicity is in how it makes enterprise technology more like consumer technology while, at the same time, it helps companies meet consumer expectations. Gone is the need for forklift upgrades of storage every five years with risky data migrations. Instead, storage can be upgraded instantly and paid for as simply as renewing a magazine subscription. In addition, the systems can be monitored and maintained from a web browser.

    Storage is more than just a data archive – it’s a strategic imperative. We needed a solution that could deliver consistent and reliable performance.
    — David Clarke, vice president, technology development, Workday

    Today, speed is king. Google’s research finds that people will visit a website less often if it’s slower than a rival’s by just 250 milliseconds — literally the blink of an eye. For LinkedIn, flash unclogged a major IO bottleneck: Queries that used to take more than 30 milliseconds now take fewer than two.

    “I don’t need to worry about that anymore,” says Neil Pinto, vice president of production engineering operations at the social networking site. “It definitely helped us solve the problem of scale and that shows up in our numbers – 300 million (subscribers) and growing.”

    LinkedIn was also able to reduce its storage footprint as the same workload could be handled by fewer devices, resulting in an 8x reduction in operational costs for power, cooling and rack space. Database refreshes that used to take more than 24 hours now are performed in minutes with Purity Operating Environment. And because there was more throughput overall with faster storage, existing servers worked more efficiently.

    “We had about a 20 percent improvement on the server side so we didn’t have to go to beefier servers and we didn’t have to buy more,” Pinto says. “It was a win-win on all sides.”
