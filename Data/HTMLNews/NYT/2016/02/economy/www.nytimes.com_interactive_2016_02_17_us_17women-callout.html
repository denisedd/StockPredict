    We Asked, You Answered: Is It Important to Elect a Female President?

    FEB. 16, 2016

    Related Article

    The Times asked readers whether electing a woman as president was important, and nearly 10,000 readers shared their views. Here is a sampling of responses, which have been condensed and edited.

    1. ​West Hartford, Conn.
      ‘It shows the world and our women that your gender matters’
      Elizabeth Rousseau, 15 | Student

      To me, a female president marks a success in a world still plagued by gender discrimination. If we elect Hillary or any woman to the highest national office we have, it shows the world and our women that your gender matters, but we respect each other as equally capable human beings. I think a woman in the Oval Office is incredibly important right now as we need Supreme Court justices who will stand by a woman’s right to choose. While a minority in United States politics, women are a very powerful in terms of raw voters, and have the knowledge and understanding to help and protect ethnic and racial minorities.

      Another white man will not have the personal experience of societal prejudice against him that can drive a woman to fight for what is right, not just because she knows right and wrong, but because she’s lived with it.


    2. Stone Mountain, Ga.
      ‘Every other major country in the world has had female leadership’
      Yvonne Isaac, 67 | Consultant

      It is of the utmost importance. With more than 50 percent of women in the work force, the continuing efforts to restrict women’s control over their own bodies and the attempts to roll back the rights of all folks perceived as marginal, these are the times to elect a woman. All the other issues, including national defense and the economy, are handled by women all over the world. Every other major country in the world has had female leadership. It is our time.

    3. ​Collingswood, N.J.
      ‘Being a mother has totally changed the way I think’
      Kari Hexem, 34

      When I was in my teens and 20s, I didn’t see as much difference between women and men. It is only now, in my 30s, as a new mother and juggling an intense career, that I have really begun to appreciate the different challenges women face succeeding in the workplace. Being a mother has totally changed the way I think about being a professional woman. And it makes me want to see a woman become president.


    4. ​Exeter, N.H.
      ‘Yes, it’s time’
      David Archambault, 56 | Small-business owner

      When I was an elementary school principal, I used to cringe every time I walked into a classroom and saw the classic poster of the American presidents. What thoughts go through a little girl’s mind when she realizes for the first time there’s not a single woman depicted? Yes, it’s time.


    5. Toronto
      ‘I hope to see a woman president in my lifetime’
      Shima Shakory, 22 | Medical student

      Like some in my generation, I believe that the best person should be elected president, regardless of sex or race. That being said, unlike some in my generation, I understand the importance of a woman president. The worst part about the fight for equality is that people think the fight is over. Little girls all over the world need to see strong women leaders to picture themselves in similar roles. I hope to see a woman president in my lifetime, because to think that there are no strong women presidential candidates out there is to be a product of our sexist society. Exhibit A: Hillary Clinton – president-ready since 2008, if not before.

    6. Winooski, Vt.
      ‘Lincoln wasn’t great because he was male’
      William K. Hart, 34 | Physician

      It’s not important that a woman become president, because that puts too much emphasis on gender, which should be an insignificant aspect of politics. It is also not important that a man be president. Emphasizing gender, race and religion as pertinent factors affecting electability, leadership and vision creates an odd double standard and silly expectations that these qualities will reshape the office of the president. Lincoln wasn’t great because he was male any more than Kennedy was great because he was Catholic; greatness is a characteristic that transcends demographic categories. The president’s responsibility is too large to assume that gender will influence her governance any more than any other factor that shaped the course of her life.

      We’ll have a female president soon enough, and whether or not she achieves greatness will have nothing to do with her gender.


    7. ​Austin, Tex.
      ‘This shouldn’t even be a conversation’
      Carla Hinojosa, 32 | First-grade teacher

      This shouldn’t even be a conversation. I teach first grade at an all-girls school and they look at me like I’m crazy when I tell them we’ve never had a woman president. Trying to explain this imbalance of power, trying to break down what sexism is to a 6-year-old, it really makes you realize how absurd our country is. I believe these girls will grow up believing in themselves in a different way than I did.


    8. Jersey City, N.J.
      ‘Electing a woman as president simply because of gender is sexist’
      Ana M. Acosta, 20 | Student

      Although a woman elected as president would represent a benchmark in the movement for gender equality, electing a woman for president simply because of gender is sexist. There would be no progress achieved if the movement for gender equality utilizes the same methods that have kept women out of positions of power for so long. This would be sexist and backwards. If any progress is to be gained at all, then as voters we need to look past gender and, with a critical lens, focus on the policies that the candidates are presenting.

    9. ​West Hartford, Conn.
      ‘There are still many men and boys who view women as less’
      Leigh Duvall, 44

      I do not feel that most women – not my peers nor my daughters’ peers – believe they are less capable or qualified than men in any arena. That in itself is a triumph. But there are still many men and boys who view women as less. They are the ones who need to see a woman in our nation’s highest office.

      The men who see a uniformed woman in a hospital and think “nurse,” the ones who assume the supervisor is the man in a tie instead of the woman standing beside him, the ones who call women “emotional” or “soft,” or better suited to caring for children and home. This pervasive mindset amounts to a box that women live in, even if they don’t subscribe to sexist views themselves.

      It would be nice to believe that women stand as equals beside men, and it’s wonderful that today’s women know they can and should, but we are paid less, and we are underrepresented in positions of power, and too often, a well-meaning man winks and calls us “honey.”

    10. ​Philadelphia
      ‘I will not cast my vote based off of gender’
      Anna Michelson, 19 | Student

      While I understand and support Secretary Clinton’s campaign for the presidency, I do not believe that I should be choosing a nominee based on gender. Having feminists rallying for her and essentially insulting women who are not voting for Clinton is most definitely not a way to gain momentum or support. Promoting a vote for Clinton based off of her being a woman is promoting sexism. While I support Secretary Clinton in her campaign to become the first woman president, I will not cast my vote based off of gender. I will be casting my vote based on who I believe will be the most successful and progressive in office.

    11. Pittsburgh
      ‘We should be asking more men this question’
      Alice Thompson, 27 | Medical assistant

      It is extremely important for me to see a woman president. I believe firmly that women have been left out of the political process for so long that we don’t realize how strange it is to be 50 percent of the population and have zero percent representation at the presidential level. This country has been controlled by men from the beginning. It is time for a woman to have a chance to represent the people and to begin to develop the role of president as woman. What’s crazy is that we even have to ask that question. It should be important to every man who has ever had a mother or sister or daughter. We should be asking more men this question.



    12. ​Cleveland Heights, Ohio
      ‘I am consistently disappointed by the lack of gender equality’
      Erin Sieke, 24 | Medical student

      As a female medical student, I am consistently disappointed by the lack of gender equality in an age where women are supposed to have the same opportunities as men.

      Women are paid less for equal work, have fewer opportunities to pursue leadership positions, are passed over for promotions in favor of male colleagues of equal or lesser qualifications, and are buttonholed from pursuing competitive specialties due to a male-dominated system that does not allow reasonable accommodations for pregnancies, maternity leave and child-rearing. Who is better qualified to represent the interests of women than a woman herself?

      I hope that by the time I have children, I won’t have to explain to my daughters why teachers, adults and supposedly educated individuals tell them that they can’t or shouldn’t pursue their goals based on their gender. The best way to achieve this goal is to continue to fight for women in positions of leadership in all areas: from politics to medicine to business to universities to grocery stores.

    13. Seaside, Calif.
      ‘Elect the very best possible candidate’
      Paul J. O’Leary, 49 | Police officer and writer

      It’s important that we elect the very best possible candidate to do the job and move America forward. If that person happens to be a woman, she should get our absolute support and respect.

    14. ​Lake Forest, Ill.
      ‘Today’s generations are O.K. taking the back seat’
      Cherise Kennerley, 49 | Marketing firm owner

      It is very important. Hard to get behind Hillary, as she stood by a cheating husband. That demeaned women and redefined ‘our place.’ She doesn’t represent who I am, as I would have made much different choices. I came up in the late ’80s, experienced the corporate ‘man,’ but met many that stood by me, respected me. Was even hired for a director-level job while I was pregnant 17 years ago. As I have taught men and owners of small businesses, there’s no place for emotions in business. Taught my daughter the same. Today she is in college in astrophysics and plays well with boys. It’s the girls she can’t respect. Today’s generations are O.K. taking the backseat. O.K. with being treated poorly. That irritates her and me. All I can do is tell her to use it to her advantage.


    15. Houston
      ‘A woman leader is more likely to be empathetic yet pragmatic’
      Ash Kulkarni, 45 | Technology channel marketing executive

      Women empowerment first. I am a father of two young girls. I want them to see yet another glass ceiling shatter. Other developed and developing nations — Sweden, India, Israel, the U.K., Germany to name a few — have elected women leaders that have proven far more capable than their male counterparts. A woman leader is more likely to be empathetic yet pragmatic, less likely to take rash unilateral disastrous foreign policy and military actions.

    17. San Jose, Calif.
      Women represent the ‘aspirations and expectations’ of voters
      María Gabriela Huertas Díaz, 24 | Urban planner

      I believe younger generations better understand that many current societal divides cross gender barriers. Especially for young women, that outlook might be the product of a privileged life compared with older women, where the fight for many of the rights we enjoy now are not part of our personal memory. However, issues like income inequality and the concentration of wealth may disproportionately affect women, but the issue remains the same: Some people have too much and contribute too little.

      A woman president must be elected because she best represents the aspirations and expectations of most voting Americans. The fact that she is a woman should not be part of the conversation. If we want equality, we must accept it in all forms, and that includes getting elected based on experience, vision and initiatives, not gender.

    18. Renton, Wash.
      ‘It’s important that we have the opportunity to run’
      Kelley Voss, 25 | Project engineer in the energy industry

      In first grade, my teacher told anyone who would listen that I would be the first woman president of the United States. He was kidding, of course, but it filled me with pride for my gender and our place in society. It inspired me, and I dreamed about voting for the first female president. Now I’m an engineer in a male-dominated field, and I realize that the best thing we can do as a society is ignore the gender of a person when deciding whether they are the best candidate for the job. Voting for a woman simply because she is a woman is no better than voting against her because she is a woman. It means stripping away her opinions, policies and experiences, and diminishing her entire existence to one attribute, her gender. As a woman — as a human — that’s the last thing I want to see. So to me, it’s not important to see a woman elected president. It’s important that we have the opportunity to run, and that our voices are heard, but we don’t need to win the White House for that to happen.

    19. ​Los Altos, Calif.
      ‘I don’t want to see just any woman in the White House’
      Enid Davis, 69 | Children’s librarian

      I am a feminist and founded the Palo Alto, Calif., chapter of N.O.W. in 1972 when I was 26. A woman doctor recommended that I join the women’s movement in order to meet other women who wanted a career in addition to marriage and children. I was married at 20 and had two children by age 28. I am still married to my husband and am now a grandmother.

      As a children’s librarian, I studied the books in my public library. Very few were about confident women and girls. I wrote the first book that examined racism and sexism in children’s literature. I don’t want to see just any woman in the White House. I truly believe that Hillary is the most qualified candidate in both parties to carry on a tradition of social consciousness begun by such historic figures as William Lloyd Garrison, Lucretia Mott, Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton.
