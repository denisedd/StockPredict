    U.S. AND EUROPE REACH AGREEMENT ON DERIVATIVES | The Commodity Futures Trading Commission and the European Commission agreed on a common set of requirements in regulating derivatives clearinghouses,
    Liz Moyer reports in DealBook. They say the move will ease capital constraints for banks that clear derivatives trades through the CME Group in Chicago, the Intercontinental Exchange in Atlanta and LCH Clearnet in London, among others.

    The agreement still needs to be approved in the United States and Europe, but it would end three years of heated debate on how to establish common standards that ensure greater stability in financial markets.

    Clearinghouses stand between banks and other buyers and sellers of futures, interest-rate swaps and other derivatives, which companies use to hedge risk. They try to reduce risk by requiring traders on both sides to post collateral. Regulators have pushed for more clearance through clearinghouses, rather than through private arrangements, since the 2008 financial crisis.

    However, the lack of an agreement between European and United States regulators over equivalent rules meant that European banks faced higher capital requirements to clear trades through American clearinghouses. And they are already seeing their profits being crimped.
