    LOW OIL PRICES AND A RECKONING ON DEBT | Energy executives and their bankers are preparing for a prolonged downturn that could change the energy industry in a way not seen since the turmoil of the late 1990s gave rise to mega-mergers like Exxon Mobil,
    Clifford Krauss and Michael Corkery report in DealBook.

    Crude prices have plunged more than 70 percent over the last 20 months, but until recently, companies were able to ride out the slump using hedges to sell their oil for more than the market price.

    These hedges have expired in recent months, leaving oil companies low on cash and unable to pay their debts. They are also realizing that a recovery in oil prices is at least a year away â too long for many companies to hold out.

    If prices hold at such low levels â oil traded near $28 a barrel on Tuesday â as many as 150 oil and gas companies could file for bankruptcy, according to IHS, an energy research firm.

    That is a relatively small slice of the industry, but hundreds of other companies that piled on debt to grow into significant players in the shale oil boom are now likely to be acquired or sell their assets. As much as a third of the oil industry could be consolidated as a result of the downturn.

    As losses have mounted, investors and lenders have laid the groundwork for the rebound â buying assets in fire sales and making new loans. But even opportunists are still uneasy. Many investors fear they could be too early if they jump in now â they had already been burned by forecasts that predicted oil would recover last year.

    THE REAL SECRET TO AVOIDING CORPORATE TAXES | If you thought there was a problem with inversions, wait until you hear about earnings stripping,
    Steven Davidoff Solomon writes in the Deal Professor column. Thatâs where the real gains can be made from an inversion.

    Once an inversion is complete, the companyâs headquarters is overseas, but its American operations are still taxed in the United States. So the company arranges for its United States operations to borrow from the foreign parent company large amounts of money, on which it will pay interest. Under the United States tax code, the interest payment can be used to offset the American earnings, reducing the tax bill there.

    A 2004 study of 12 corporate inversions found that âmost of the tax savingsâ found in corporate inversions was attributable to earnings stripping. Four companies engaged in almost 100 percent earnings stripping, costing the Treasury about $700 million over two years.

    So not only are companies lowering their taxes through inversions, they are also eliminating large chunks of the United States taxes that they previously owed.

    If you are an American taxpayer, it means the burden of making up lost revenue falls more heavily on you.

    It does have some benefits. The American subsidiary must do something with the money it borrows and it may be that it invests the money in the United States in research or plants.

    Rules limiting this kind of behavior are being considered, but they are still only a pipe dream. Instead, the corporate runaways are winning and taking easy money out of the pockets of the United States taxpayer, Mr. Solomon writes.

    US FOODS PLANS TO GO PUBLIC | US Foods intends to go public, less than a year after a planned merger with its rival Sysco collapsed because of opposition from government regulators,
    Michael J. de la Merced reports in DealBook. A filing listed a $100 million preliminary target to determine the registration fee, but otherwise did not disclose fund-raising ambitions.

    The pace of companies entering the public markets has slowed, but US Foods would be the biggest to file for an offering so far this year.

    An initial public offering presumably was not the first choice for the owners, Clayton, Dubilier & Rice and Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Company. Private equity firms tend to prefer a quick and decisive sale to an initial offering, which can draw out the process of selling their stake.

    The private equity companies had bought US Foods from the Dutch supermarket operator Royal Ahold for $7.1 billion in 2007.

    TRUMP AND SANDERS WIN NEW HAMPSHIRE PRIMARIES | Donald J. Trump and Senator Bernie Sanders harnessed working-class fury to surge to victories in a New Hampshire primary that drew a huge turnout,

    Patrick Healy and Jonathan Martin report in The New York Times.

    The success by the two outsider candidates dealt a rebuke to the political establishment, all but guaranteeing that each party would have a bruising race for the nomination.

    With more than 80 percent of precincts reporting, Mr. Trump had received 35 percent of the vote and Mr. Sanders approached 60 percent.

    Exit polls show that Mr. Trump ran strongest among voters who were worried about illegal immigrants, economic turmoil and the threat of a terrorist attack on the United States.

    The win by Mr. Sanders was a painful rejection of Hillary Clinton, who has a deep history with New Hampshire voters. He drew support from a wide cross-section of voters, even edging her out among women.
