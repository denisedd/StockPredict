Who Killed the Sheridans?

The police say a prominent New Jersey lawyer killed himself and his wife of 47 years. Their sons refuse to believe it.

The Sheridan brothers, from left, Mark, Dan, Matt and Tim.CreditCreditStefan Ruiz for The New York Times

By Michael Sokolove

  • Feb. 5, 2016

It was still dark, a half-hour until sunrise, when the fire trucks and police vehicles came wailing down country roads in central New Jersey and stopped in front of 49 Meadow Run Drive in Skillman, north of Princeton. The two-story, four-bedroom house sat on a one-acre lot in a 1970s-era subdivision that defines a kind of rustic suburbia. The back of the property was wooded, and there were still several farms nearby, some with fairly large herds of cattle.

Smoke billowed from a window on the second floor. Firefighters did not have to bust down the front door to get inside, because it was unlocked, as were several other entrances to the house. The dense smoke slowed them as they climbed the stairs to the second floor, but once inside the burning master bedroom, they extinguished the flames quickly. The fire had been confined to this one room, even though it was fed by an accelerant — gasoline, poured on the floor from a container that had been carried up from the garage. Two bodies lay on the floor, face up.

At roughly 6:45 that morning, Mark Sheridan was awakened by a call from his twin brother, Matt, telling him that their parents’ house was on fire. Their father, John Sheridan Jr., was a confidant to governors, a former state transportation commissioner and currently the chief executive of a hospital in Camden. Mark, one of four brothers, had followed his father’s path: He was counsel to the New Jersey Republican Party. That weekend, however, he was trying to leave his work behind. He and his wife, Jennifer, were staying at a boutique hotel on the Upper East Side, celebrating their 12th wedding anniversary.

The couple immediately threw their clothes and belongings back in their bags; when their car did not come quickly enough after Mark called for it, he went down to the garage to get it himself. They weren’t out of Manhattan yet when Matt, who was getting updates from a neighbor, called again and told Mark that his parents were dead.

Mark began making his own calls as he drove. ‘‘I don’t know how fast I was going, but I was flying,’’ he says. ‘‘I think my first call was to Chris Porrino’’ — who was Gov. Chris Christie’s chief counsel — ‘‘and I say: ‘Chris, my mom and dad died, and nobody’s answering my questions. I understand the Somerset County prosecutor is at our house. I don’t know the Somerset County prosecutor, but can you have him call me?’ And Chris is a great guy, and he says, ‘Sure,’ and I’m literally pulling into my parents’ neighborhood when the prosecutor calls me.’’

The prosecutor, Geoffrey Soriano, told him that the deaths were no accident. ‘‘He says to me: ‘It’s not just a fire. It looks like it was set intentionally, and it looks like your parents were stabbed.’ And I say, ‘All right,’ and I thank him.’’

Mark went into ‘‘lawyer mode.’’ He was determined to keep his emotions at bay, to ask questions, sift through information and begin to make some sense of what had just happened to his parents. He had picked up one of his younger brothers, Tim, along the way. Another brother, Dan, was already in front of the house amid what was now a crush of people: neighbors, curious onlookers, the police and news media. Matt was the one brother still absent; he lived at home with his parents but had left the day before to go fishing on Fishers Island in Long Island Sound.

A detective approached Mark Sheridan as he was getting out of his car and told him that family members were not allowed in the house. It was an active crime scene. ‘‘They’re asking us a lot of questions, but we’re not getting a lot of information,’’ Mark says. ‘‘They say, ‘Where’s Matt?’ And I tell them he’s on 287, just crossing from New York into New Jersey, and it will be about an hour before he’s here. And they say, ‘O.K., when he gets here, we would like to talk to you all.’ ’’

Standing with other family members in a neighbor’s driveway, Mark did not have much to do but peer up at the bedroom where his parents died. Smoke was no longer visible, but he could still smell it.

Nine days later, Governor Christie and two of his predecessors, Thomas Kean and Christine Todd Whitman, gave eulogies for John and Joyce Sheridan at a memorial service in Trenton, New Jersey’s capital. Another former governor, Jim Florio, was among the thousand or so mourners inside the vast theater at the Trenton War Memorial. When it was his turn to speak, Mark Sheridan addressed a portion of his remarks to his two young children. Consider all their grandparents had done, he instructed them — the family they built, their service to the public — and look at the large crowd, including the many dignitaries, gathered in their honor. ‘‘This is what it means to earn your last name,’’ he said.

Later reports described the scene that emergency responders encountered in the master bedroom. John Sheridan, 72, had been found on the floor at the foot of the bed, with a partly burned armoire on top of him. Joyce, 69, was found to the left side of the bed. Matches, knives and a gas can lay nearby. Both husband and wife were dead at the scene. Joyce’s autopsy, performed by an assistant state medical examiner, Eddy Lilavois, showed that she suffered first- and second-degree burns and 12 stab wounds, mostly to her scalp, forehead and hands. The one that killed her was a wound to the chest that perforated her aorta. Her death was ruled a homicide. John, according to Lilavois’s autopsy, had five knife wounds to his neck and torso and a blunt-force injury that caused five broken ribs. The presumption was that the fractures came from the armoire falling on top of him. Unlike Joyce, he had soot in his mouth, nose and lungs, which indicated that he was alive when the fire started.

John’s cause of death was listed as ‘‘pending further studies,’’ and the manner of death ‘‘pending investigation.’’ Yet the Somerset County prosecutor’s office was already giving strong hints that detectives were no longer searching for suspects — if, in fact, they ever had been. ‘‘We are quite confident that there exists no threat to either the immediate neighborhood or to the local community,’’ Soriano’s spokesman told reporters four days after the fire. The medical examiner had concluded that the cuts on John Sheridan were ‘‘hesitation wounds,’’ a term of art that Mark Sheridan first heard three days after his parents died, during a tense meeting at Soriano’s office in Somerville.

‘‘I say: ‘What’s that? What are hesitation wounds?’ And one of the prosecutors says that’s what typically happens when someone tries to kill themselves and can’t accomplish it. And so that’s a powder keg. I’m thinking to myself: What are you saying? What are you talking about? Speak up if you’ve got something to say. Don’t lawyer me.’’

The detectives laid out their theory: John Sheridan had killed his wife, set fire to their bedroom and then killed himself. ‘‘They told us, ‘If this is what we think it is, we’ll find the reason,’ ’’ Mark Sheridan says. ‘‘ ‘We’ll go through the emails and phones and other records, and there’s always something. Dad’s got a girlfriend, Mom’s got a boyfriend, there’s financial problems, a record of violence, there’s always something.’ ’’

After the initial shock wore off, Mark and his brothers were furious. A horrific crime had occurred. Two people dead with stab wounds. A fire deliberately set. Where was the urgency? Was a manhunt underway? Why had the detectives settled so quickly on such a preposterous theory of the case? Nothing could seem more unbelievable to the family than the idea that John Sheridan killed his wife and himself. The Sheridan brothers felt themselves being thrust from the role of grieving sons to that of amateur detectives.

The family hired its own pathologist, Michael Baden, a consultant on numerous high-profile cases, formerly the chief medical examiner in New York City and the host of the HBO program ‘‘Autopsy.’’ With Lilavois, the state’s medical examiner, in the lab with him, Baden re-examined the bodies. The two doctors looked at the shape and depth of the wounds and considered whether they could have been caused by the two knives recovered at the scene, of which at least one, a kitchen carving knife, was used on Joyce Sheridan. The other was a serrated knife for slicing bread, with a rounded end. It would not have been an ideal weapon, and it wasn’t clear if it caused any of Joyce’s wounds. Most significant, Baden and Lilavois agreed that a third knife caused John Sheridan’s wounds, but investigators had not recovered it. How, in a murder-suicide, could a weapon be missing?

When the Sheridan brothers were allowed to enter the house midweek, after detectives were done taking evidence from the location, they came upon a piece of metal on the bedroom floor. It had melted, then resolidified. It could have been a piece of the armoire or some other metal item in the bedroom. Or it could have been a knife. The recovered knives, however, were kitchen knives, made of metal alloys that burn at only very high temperatures — the kind of intense heat that quickly engulfs an entire room, if not a house. Only part of the bedroom was burned.

John Sheridan’s body was found near the melted metal. He still wore his cotton undershirt; it was charred but not burned. A month later, an insurance adjuster discovered a fire poker in the adjoining bathroom, even though there was no fireplace upstairs. Could that, rather than the tumbling armoire, have been the cause of John Sheridan’s broken ribs?

The family was angry. Why hadn’t these items been recovered by detectives? Mark Sheridan described his reaction to the melted metal: ‘‘I pick it up and I fling it across the room. Danny comes in and throws it across the room the other way.’’ A week after the deaths, he wrote the prosecutor an email, which was not composed with his usual lawyerly detachment. In the midst of enumerating what he perceived as a string of investigative shortcomings, he wrote, ‘‘I am on the verge of losing my mind.’’

John Sheridan knew how to get complicated things done. He knew the order to go in. If it was a negotiation, he knew what the other side needed. His face was square and solid, like that of a Chamber of Commerce president or small-town banker. It was impassive. You couldn’t read anything in it but competence. He served as New Jersey’s transportation commissioner in the early 1980s under Gov. Thomas Kean, when NJ Transit, then only a bus service, took over all the passenger railroad routes from Conrail. He was co-chairman for the transition committee for the state’s next Republican governor, Christine Todd Whitman. He spent years in Trenton, running the lobbying practice of Riker Danzig, a law firm based in Morristown, N.J. His world was one of relationships, connections, persuasion. He was a fixture at Pete Lorenzo’s Cafe, a clubby steakhouse that for decades served as a shadow State House, a well-lubricated junction of deal-making, political gossip and intrigue. But he was more often found there at lunch than in the boozy after-hours. ‘‘John wasn’t someone who had to schmooze people,’’ says Jim Weinstein, who worked under Sheridan and later became transportation commissioner himself. ‘‘He made patient, lawyerly arguments based on public policy and shared interests.’’

Sheridan took naturally to the role of wise man and mentor. ‘‘To the day he died, if I had a problem, I’d go see John and talk about it,’’ his brother Peter says. ‘‘How do we deal with it? What should I do?’’ John advised him on how to get into law school; years later, he steered the political process that led to his brother’s appointment to the federal bench by President George W. Bush.

In 2005, John Sheridan moved into management at Cooper Hospital in Camden. The position was a departure from previous jobs, but his dexterity in the overlapping spaces of government and commerce proved to be no less useful. Cooper had ambitious plans but needed regulatory approvals and public financing. In partnership with the hospital’s chairman, George Norcross III, who is often described as the Democratic boss of South Jersey, Sheridan helped build Cooper into a health care powerhouse. During Sheridan’s tenure, the hospital opened a medical school and teamed up with the MD Anderson Cancer Center to build a $100 million treatment center. ‘‘In my next lifetime,’’ Norcross says, extolling Sheridan’s uncommon serenity amid the hurly-burly of high-stakes business dealing, ‘‘I would want John Sheridan’s personality.’’

Joyce Sheridan was feistier than her husband, whom she met when he was tending bar in West Orange to pay his law-school tuition at Rutgers. Once, when Governor Whitman called the house looking for John, Joyce held the phone out just a few inches from her face and said to her husband, ‘‘I thought I told you to tell her to stop calling at dinnertime.’’ Joyce had been a high-school social-studies teacher. She volunteered for the classes with the most-challenged students, those who struggled academically or had trouble in their backgrounds.

On Sept. 25, 2014, a Thursday, three days before the fire, John Sheridan spent a rare workday afternoon at home. A neighbor, Marsha Stencel, walked by with her granddaughter and saw both Sheridans outside, decorating the home for Halloween. Joyce made the kind of fuss over Halloween that some people do for Christmas. She invited Stencel and her granddaughter inside and showed them displays set out on tables: lit-up houses, ghoulish figurines. A set of antique papier-mâché pumpkins sat atop the mantelpiece.

The Sheridans were neighborly, but they weren’t an especially social couple. They spent almost all their free time together, either with their sons and grandchildren or buying antiques for their New Jersey home and for the Federal-period house near Cooperstown, N.Y., that they had painstakingly restored. Some years back, John delighted his wife with a gift that he fetched for the Cooperstown property on a drive up to New Hampshire: a double-seated outhouse from the 1800s. He had a hole dug and set it up in the backyard, because he didn’t consider it authentic unless it could be used.

‘‘Joyce ran the place,’’ says Stencel, who was their neighbor and friend for almost four decades. ‘‘She was in charge. They had someone cut the grass, do repairs. I don’t know if John would have known the right end of a screwdriver to use. That wasn’t him. He worked, and he worked a lot. But he did what good husbands do. When Joyce needed him, he was there.’’

Stencel had no sense that day that anything was amiss. ‘‘They were joking around with each other,’’ she says. ‘‘They were normal, just John and Joyce.’’

The next day, a nephew of the Sheridans who had a lawn-care business came by to work on the yard and gently chastised Joyce when he saw her carrying grocery bags in from the car. Near the end of her teaching days, she took a serious fall at school that precipitated a cascade of orthopedic problems and over the last 15 years underwent more operations than her family could count — on her back, shoulders and neck. She rarely complained, but she took a mix of painkillers. John was back at work at Cooper Hospital. He met with Gary Lesneski, the hospital’s general counsel, about a pending negative report on Cooper’s cardiac unit, a matter that was said to be troubling him. But, Lesneski says, ‘‘It was business as usual when I left his office that Friday.’’

John Sheridan bought a couple of new dress shirts earlier in the week. He picked up his dry cleaning. He had a meeting scheduled at Cooper on Sunday afternoon. On Saturday evening at 6:36, less than 12 hours before the fire, he sent an email from his iPhone in response to a question from Norcross about whether Cooper was eligible for some post-Hurricane Sandy disaster-preparation grants. His answer went on for a couple of hundred words. It still reads like a cogent and clearheaded piece of writing.

On March 27, 2015, after six months of nearly total public silence about the deaths, Soriano’s office issued its report. John Sheridan’s manner of death was changed from ‘‘undetermined’’ to ‘‘suicide.’’ The report also found that no one had been in the bedroom but the couple. That meant John Sheridan was his wife’s killer. It was like a criminal indictment, except there was no one to put on trial.

The report did not speculate about the couple’s last hours. About whether, for instance, they had gone to sleep that night, and John at some point crept out of the bedroom to gather the knives and other materials, or if he instead hid the knives in advance. Or if, perhaps, there had been some kind of all-night row that culminated in blood and fire. In writing about the knife wounds on each of the Sheridans, Soriano emphasized the contrasts in their location and severity. Joyce had cuts on her hands, which were considered defensive in nature; John had no hand wounds. Referring to John, he wrote: ‘‘All five wounds have been characterized by the assistant medical examiner as superficial in nature, consistent with self-infliction. However, one wound to the neck caused a small perforation to the right jugular vein and would be fatal without medical treatment.’’ Two causes of death were given for John Sheridan: ‘‘sharp force’’ injury — the cut to the jugular — and smoke inhalation.

The toxicology reports revealed no surprises. Joyce had a mix of prescription painkillers in her system — ‘‘Mom was on enough meds to kill a small farm animal,’’ Mark Sheridan says — and John tested positive for what were described as cardiac drugs. DNA evidence was not conclusive. The report said samples collected from the knives were ‘‘consistent with that of John Sheridan’’ or could not ‘‘exclude’’ him as a suspect. But the actual lab reports indicated the swabs did not yield a usable sample and revealed not much more than that they probably came from a white male.

No motives were put forward. Detectives looked at the couple’s cellphones and computers and conducted what they said were more than 180 interviews with family members, neighbors, friends and co-workers. They did not uncover any of the usual explanations, the ones that they told the sons would probably turn up: girlfriend, boyfriend, terminal disease, mental illness, financial woes. The best they came up with was that John was unusually agitated at work, over the pending state report regarding fatality rates after cardiac operations at Cooper. ‘‘With regard to work-related issues, family members and colleagues characterized John Sheridan as ‘disproportionately concerned,’ ‘genuinely worried’ and ‘overly worried,’ ’’ Soriano wrote.

The report did not name the people who provided this information, nor did it say what John Sheridan had said or done specifically to leave an impression that he was not himself. Soriano concluded his report with several bullet points, which amounted to a sort of connect-the-dots finding of murder-suicide. Several related to the absences of things: no evidence of forcible entry to the home; no indication anything was stolen (six folds of cash totaling $950 were found on a night stand); no one having noticed any suspects departing the house or neighborhood.

By the time the report was issued, the Sheridans knew what would be in it. This did not lessen the sting. ‘‘It’s the goodness in people’s lives that you hang on to,’’ Peter Sheridan, the federal judge, told me. ‘‘The prosecutor’s conclusion destroyed their legacy and the good that John and Joyce had done in their lives.’’

Even in death, John Sheridan was politically connected. County prosecutors in New Jersey are appointed by the governor and confirmed by the State Senate, and candidates for the job typically have a political sponsor. Geoffrey Soriano had been a lawyer in private practice in Somerville with a job as the municipal prosecutor in Branchburg and North Plainfield. His political patron was the state senator Kip Bateman, whose father, Raymond Bateman, a former president of the State Senate, was a longtime friend and political ally of John Sheridan.

Not that it helped the Sheridan family. After Soriano’s report came out — and Mark Sheridan attacked it in local newspapers — Raymond Bateman seemed to support his son’s protégé by writing a column in The Home News Tribune, a Middlesex County daily, that made the assumption that John Sheridan was mentally ill. It went on to compare him with the German airline pilot who deliberately crashed a commercial airliner into the Alps, killing everyone onboard. ‘‘As I write with tears about my friend, John, and, yes, as I, in horror, think about the innocent lives lost in the airplane crash,’’ Bateman wrote, ‘‘I recognize that mental depression must just completely overwhelm a person’s normal judgment.’’

Mark Sheridan didn’t see anything that had impinged on his father’s state of mind or warped his judgment. The way it looked to him, an unimaginable family tragedy was now being compounded by official incompetence and rank stupidity. His interactions with the detectives left him exasperated. He would lay out his lawyerly arguments and try to convince them that they had put their facts together illogically or had not even gathered all the pertinent facts, but they were unmoved. He considered himself to be dealing with ‘‘misfits.’’

Joyce Sheridan’s brother, Peter Mitchko, had urged Mark to publicly take on the prosecutor sooner, go after him in the newspapers rather than continue to lobby him privately while waiting for the final report. ‘‘The boys were fighting for their dad’s reputation,’’ he says. ‘‘My issue is: Who killed my sister?’’

Could it have been his brother-in-law?

‘‘No, it’s not conceivable. Anyone who knew John and Joyce doesn’t think it’s conceivable. And the complicated way they said he committed suicide? You stab yourself? Burn a building? The only thing he didn’t do is eat poison. He doesn’t leave a note. Nothing makes sense.’’

Last spring, less than three weeks after Soriano released his report, Mark Sheridan filed a lawsuit on behalf of himself and his brothers. It sought to have their father’s manner of death changed from suicide back to undetermined, which would have the effect of reopening the question of who killed their parents. A three-judge panel instructed the family to instead pursue the case with the state medical examiner’s office, and in December, the Sheridans sent Baden’s written report to the medical examiner and formally requested a change in the manner of death. The matter could yet end up back in court.

The state’s answer to the initial lawsuit did not address the merits of the investigation; it just argued that the family had no standing to mount a challenge. ‘‘The court should dismiss this appeal for lack of jurisdiction because no legal basis exists to compel a medical examiner to change his opinion about manner of death,’’ states the response, which was filed by the attorney general’s office.

Soriano would not be interviewed for this article, despite repeated requests, and New Jersey government lawyers successfully defended a suit filed by The Philadelphia Inquirer seeking access to investigative files on the case. It’s not clear what in the law would prevent Soriano from releasing more information. He did give one interview, after the March final report, to Tom Moran, a columnist for The Star-Ledger. When asked about the reconstituted puddle of metal on the bedroom floor, Soriano said: ‘‘I don’t know what it is. It could have been anything.’’ About John Sheridan’s motive for committing a murder-suicide, he said: ‘‘What we tried to do was gather all the relevant evidence. I don’t know what else was going on in his life.’’

One of the younger Sheridan brothers, Dan, is president of a human-resources firm. The other, Tim, is in the home-restoration business. Matt, Mark’s twin, was working as a stockbroker but living in his childhood bedroom. I had heard Matt described as the ne’er-do-well twin, but Mark told me that was not true. ‘‘We’ve been polar opposites since Day 1,’’ he said. ‘‘Matt is fun, carefree. Takes everything as it comes. Me, not so much. Every family’s got differences, but we’re all close. We’ve hung together pretty well through this.’’

Matt left for his fishing trip the day before the fire. He favored an extreme form of the sport in which participants wear wet suits, swim several hundred yards from shore and cast for striped bass from rock formations, sometimes at night when the bass are thought to be most interested in feeding. He said goodbye to his mother on the way out and gave her a kiss. The last thing he remembers her saying to him was, ‘‘Don’t do anything stupid.’’ When he got back to Meadow Run Drive, after the deaths, the police searched his car. According to court documents, they found a small amount of cocaine, a digital scale and plastic bags coated with white powder. In one twist the Sheridan case would take, he was arrested that day. He has not been charged, and no trial date has been set.

On one of the occasions I met with Mark at his Newark office, Matt joined us, wearing a sport jacket and tie. ‘‘He’d get mad if I wasn’t dressed correctly,’’ he said, referring to his brother. The twins have a similar manner: direct but wisecracking, with an inclination toward dark humor. They still talk about their parents in the joking way that adult children often do. Matt said he was usually in close touch with both his parents during the day and often responded to what his mother would call ‘‘emergencies.’’ I asked what kind of emergencies, and both brothers laughed. ‘‘Everything was an emergency with Mom,’’ Matt said. ‘‘She’d call me up and say: ‘I’ve got an emergency. Can you get some Reese’s Peanut Butter Cups on the way home?’ ’’

Matt said his father had indeed been troubled by the negative report about Cooper’s cardiac unit, but he did not consider those work issues to be even remotely plausible as an explanation. (Nor did anyone else I talked to who knew John Sheridan.) Had they considered the possibility that their mother initiated some kind of episode that ended in the two deaths, because by all accounts, she was the more hot-tempered of the two? ‘‘We did,’’ Matt said. ‘‘We’ve had to have some miserable conversations among ourselves about the different possibilities, but we’ve had them.’’ They concluded it wasn’t possible. Because of her injured shoulders, Joyce, at 5-foot-2, could not have raised the knife high enough to cause the wounds on her husband’s chest and neck. And they didn’t think she could have created much force even if she had.

Soriano’s report does not speculate on whether the murder-suicide was planned or a result of some sudden rage. The presence of the gas can, which came from the garage and was used to fuel a generator that powers a basement sump pump, would suggest planning, as would the knives carried up from the kitchen. But nothing else — neither forensic evidence nor interviews — suggests that John Sheridan was planning anything other than continuing to work.

‘‘So he went into a rage and got a gas can?’’ Mark said. ‘‘And then he did a few wounds and stopped being in a rage? Rage killings don’t work that way. It’s not one and done.’’

Matt added: ‘‘He switched knives? Why did he do that?’’

They went back and forth like this for a few minutes. I felt as if I were listening in on a conversation they had been having for months. Where was the other knife? If the fire was hot enough to melt a knife, why didn’t their father’s shirt burn? Why didn’t the whole bedroom or even the whole house go up in flames? Matt sighed and said, ‘‘We’re not detectives, but we’re not idiots.’’

In their telling, the whole Sheridan universe holds together. Their parents were civic pillars. Collectors of antiques. Their marriage made it through the inevitable bumps and endured for nearly a half-century. The prosecutor turned up not even a shred of a motive for John Sheridan. All four brothers were where they said they were — their cellphones had been examined; they were nowhere near Meadow Run Drive — and none were under suspicion.

What about Matt, with his supposed possession of cocaine, and the scale and plastic bags? In the absence of alternative theories as to who killed the Sheridans, his arrest and whatever involvement he did or did not have with drugs has been a source of speculation and rumor. On the advice of his criminal lawyer, Matt would not talk about the drug arrest or anything related to it, so it was up to his brother to address it. I asked Mark Sheridan if he was upset when his brother was arrested on drug charges a few hours after his parents’ deaths. ‘‘Yeah, I was pissed,’’ he said. His tone of voice indicated he might still be.

Had they considered that their parents were murdered by someone looking for Matt? ‘‘Yeah, absolutely we’ve talked about it,’’ Mark said. ‘‘We don’t believe it. First of all, Matt’s car wasn’t there. Matt wasn’t there on weekends, or hardly ever. So I don’t believe it, and nobody’s going to convince me otherwise.’’

The family, however, strongly believes that the police never seriously considered any other suspects — that they zeroed in on John Sheridan and then spent months trying to prove their initial hunch. In light of that, how could the family eliminate any theory? ‘‘I can’t,’’ Mark Sheridan said. ‘‘Anything’s possible.’’

‘‘My parents had a relationship like none I’ve ever seen,’’ Mark said one morning when I was talking with him alone. ‘‘They were always together.’’ His father was once an avid golfer, but he stopped playing, except occasionally for business, because he preferred prowling for antiques with his wife on weekends. ‘‘Even if you could justify Dad killing himself, you will never justify him killing his wife of 47 years. If something had gone on at work or he had money problems, O.K., but why would he kill her?’’

Sheridan had just arrived at his office after an hour-plus commute from his home in Tewksbury. We sat in a conference room across from a big window overlooking Newark’s Penn Station. He has his father’s facial features but not his conciliatory, bring-everyone-onboard personality. ‘‘My dad was the cool hand,’’ he said. ‘‘I’m the drop-the-gloves guy.’’ Sheridan is a litigator who defends insurance companies against large claims. He has been referred to as an ‘‘attack dog’’ in his role as lawyer for the state Republicans. He does not object to the portrait. He characterized the numerous lawsuits he filed against New Jersey governors when his party was out of power as ‘‘torturing one Democratic administration after another.’’ His side gig was rich in entertainment value, a front-row seat to the theater of New Jersey politics. ‘‘You’ve got two choices,’’ he said. ‘‘You either take it all seriously, or you laugh at it.’’

He put a paper coffee cup down in front of him when we started talking but never touched it. We happened to be meeting on the same day that David Wildstein, a former Port Authority executive and Christie ally, was in court to plead guilty on charges connected to the politically motivated lane closures on the George Washington Bridge. Two others were criminally charged at the same time. After his parents’ deaths, Sheridan stepped down from his role as counsel to the state Republicans, but he continued to represent the party’s interests in the scandal and is defending Christie’s 2013 gubernatorial campaign committee in a civil suit brought by people who were stuck in traffic on the bridge. He checked his cellphone just twice. He returned one call, to his wife, over the course of four hours. He is consumed with restoring his family’s reputation. ‘‘I’ll be damned if I’m going to allow them to tarnish my father and my mother and their legacy based on this half-assed investigation,’’ he said.

The state’s theory makes no sense to Mark. His father lived an orderly life, and yet his supposed crime was inefficient and sloppy. He set a fire, a common tactic only when a perpetrator intends to flee and not be tied to a crime. ‘‘I’ve said to the prosecutor, ‘It’s bad enough you’re calling my father a murderer, but you’re also calling him a moron, as though he weren’t smart enough to work this out,’ ’’ Mark said. ‘‘ ‘You’re telling me this was planned, but he’s better than that.’ This would be the worst plan he ever came up with.’’

Various experts have looked at the evidence in the case and found it unconvincing. Lawrence Kobilinksy, chairman of the department of sciences at John Jay College of Criminal Justice, noted, in particular, the missing weapon — ‘‘that’s huge,’’ he said — as well as the lack of a motive, the unpersuasive DNA findings and an arson that ‘‘makes no sense’’ in the context of a murder-suicide. The case, he told me, should be reopened.

Mark acknowledges that at times he may sound detached from the horror of what happened to his parents. It is a coping mechanism, a way to avoid being emotionally wrecked. He spent his entire childhood inside the house where his parents died. He told me, almost offhandedly, that he and his brothers had been planning to have the structure ‘‘ ’dozed’’ — bulldozed — so they could sell the vacant lot. But they have delayed doing so. There are still bloodstains everywhere: a bloody fingerprint outside the door of the master bedroom, stains on the walls downstairs. Was it all really tested by investigators? Might it reveal the identity of the killer? These are the kinds of questions he contemplates. Knock down the house and have the rubble trucked to a landfill, or preserve it for the forensic value of the blood on its walls?

The family is now waiting to see if the medical examiner’s office will change John Sheridan’s cause of death. ‘‘This is my shot at the prosecutor,’’ Mark said at one point in our conversation, referring to one of his recent court filings. ‘‘My shot at the medical examiner is going to come next.’’ I asked him about Christie, who oversees the state’s medical-examiner system and who appointed the acting state attorney general, John Jay Hoffman, whose office was opposing the appeal of the manner-of-death finding. Isn’t there some way for Christie to help him? ‘‘I am trying my best not to take shots at the governor,’’ he said.

Sheridan understands why people are intrigued by the mystery of what happened to his parents: ‘‘I’ve said to my brothers, if we weren’t living this nightmare, it would be an interesting thing we’d be following.’’ He knows some will find it odd that his privileged and politically connected family feels badly served by law enforcement. ‘‘I get it,’’ he said. ‘‘I totally get it. I know it sounds offensive, but how do you deal with it if you’re not this family? If you don’t have the education, the knowledge, the connections, the financial wherewithal to fight the fight? I’ve got access. I’m sitting at the police station on the day my parents died, and the governor is calling to offer condolences. Not many people get those calls, right? And at the same time, you’re just getting screwed over, and there’s nothing you can do about it.’’

Sometimes, at night, he dwells on an alternative explanation for his parents’ deaths. The backyards in the Sheridans’ neighborhood in Skillman are heavily wooded, and few of them are fenced. Anybody could have crept through the night, walked into the house and killed John and Joyce Sheridan. An almost prosaic explanation. Some crazed stranger did it, a person so unhinged he didn’t even bother taking the wads of cash in full view. Awful, but comprehensible.
