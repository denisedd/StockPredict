    Morning Agenda: Push Continues on Insider Trading Cases Despite Limits on Prosecutors

    By Amie Tsang
    May 20, 2016 May 20, 2016
    Morning Agenda: Push Continues on Insider Trading Cases Despite Limits on Prosecutors By Amie Tsang May 20, 2016 6:34 am May 20, 2016 6:34 am

    PUSH CONTINUES ON INSIDER TRADING CASES DESPITE LIMITS ON PROSECUTORS Federal prosecutors want to send a message that insider trading cases can still be made, Matthew Goldstein, Ben Protess and Alexandra Stevenson report in DealBook.

    When the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit overturned the convictions of two hedge fund managers, Todd Newman and Anthony Chiasson, it imposed the greatest limits on prosecutors in a generation. And the government predicted a chilling effect on future insider trading investigations.

    But the office of Preet Bharara, the United States attorney in Manhattan, and the Securities and Exchange Commission have pushed on, charging William T. Walters, a high-rolling Las Vegas kingmaker, and Thomas C. Davis, a former investment banker, with insider trading.

    “Brazen insider trading continues to be a blot in our securities markets, and so the integrity of our markets continues to be a priority for this office,” Mr. Bharara said at a news conference to announce the charges on Thursday. But he did add that “there is conduct that we think is nefarious and undermines faith in the market and undermines the strength of the market that will not be able to be prosecuted because of the Newman decision.”

    Mr. Walters, often considered the most successful sports bettor in the United States, was owed money by Phil Mickelson, the golfer, and Mr. Davis, a former chairman of Dean Foods. Prosecutors say that the debts were at the center of a long-running insider trading scheme. They accused Mr. Walters of using stock tips from Mr. Davis to help him generate about $40 million in profits and avoid losses. Mr. Walters had lent Mr. Davis, whose finances were in trouble, money in return for feeding Mr. Walters boardroom secrets as far back as 2008, the authorities said. Mr. Davis has agreed to plead guilty and is cooperating against Mr. Walters.

    Mr. Mickelson was not accused of wrongdoing, but the S.E.C. listed him in a civil complaint as a relief defendant, arguing that he was “unjustly enriched” and must disgorge “ill-gotten gains” he made from trades Mr. Walters recommended. Mr. Mickelson owed money to Mr. Walters at the time. He has agreed to repay nearly $1 million and his lawyer said he took “full responsibility for the decisions and associations that led him to becoming part of this investigation.”

    Mr. Walters’s lawyer said his client had done nothing wrong. “Bill Walters is a true American success story, whose extraordinary accomplishments as a lawful sports gambler have been widely recognized and lauded,” Barry Berke, the lawyer, said in a statement. “Mr. Walters and his counsel look forward to his day in court.”

    The case against Mr. Walters comes as prosecutors reassess their ability to bring insider trading cases. The appeals court ruling required “proof of a meaningfully close personal relationship that generates an exchange that is objective, consequential and represents at least a potential gain of a pecuniary or similarly valuable nature.”

    The announcement of charges indicated that “the Southern District is still in the insider trading prosecution business,” said Daniel Richman, a former federal prosecutor, who is now a professor at Columbia Law School. He referred to the federal prosecutors in the Southern District of New York, based in Manhattan.

    PUERTO RICO’S DEBT CRISIS ADDRESSED IN BIPARTISAN BILL Republicans in the House of Representatives have made a plan to lead Puerto Rico out of its financial quagmire, Mary Williams Walsh reports in DealBook.

    The bill would put Puerto Rico’s fiscal affairs under direct federal control and establish a legal framework for reducing its $72 billion of debt. It would work like a Chapter 9 municipal bankruptcy in the United States, but with differences intended to reassure those creditors who believe Chapter 9 works against them.

    The bill was drafted by the House Committee on Natural Resources under direction from Speaker Paul D. Ryan, who told his fellow Republicans to work with Democrats. There are provisions that different stakeholders have said they did not want but now seem willing to tolerate.

    Josh Earnest, the White House press secretary, said the bill showed signs of “mean-spirited policy-making” by Republicans. It called for young minimum-wage earners on the island to take a pay cut. But Mr. Earnest added that the White House could accept that to get a bill passed.

    Puerto Rico’s financial affairs would come under a federal control board, in spite of elected officials warning that heavy-handed oversight from Washington would have unacceptable colonial connotations. The board’s seven members would be selected by President Obama from candidates proposed by Republicans and Democrats in Congress. At least one of them would be a Puerto Rico resident.

    The board would have the power to subpoena records and hold hearings, something of potential concern to Puerto Rican officials past and present as well as the financial community. Conceivably, the investigations could show that some of Puerto Rico’s debt was created unlawfully, and the island could seek to have it voided, leaving bondholders empty-handed.

    The board would also guide Puerto Rico through a court-supervised debt reduction. It would not be able to declare “mission accomplished” and leave until Puerto Rico regained normal access to the capital markets.

    ENERGY TRANSFER DELAYS COULD SCUTTLE MERGER WITH WILLIAMS Energy Transfer Equity has no options except to run down the clock, Leslie Picker reports in DealBook. If the pipeline company can delay filing the documents needed to complete its merger with the Williams Companies, it might not have to close the deal and pay a chunk of cash to shareholders. A new lawsuit in the six-month-old dispute has highlighted how prestigious Wall Street law firms are battling over the deal.

    This lawsuit, the third by the Williams Companies, was filed just six weeks before the June 28 deadline after which Energy Transfer, based in Dallas, can walk away. It asked the court to stop Energy Transfer from trying to break up the deal.

    It focused on the role of the lawyers. Cravath, Swaine & Moore served as counsel for Williams and Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz were Energy Transfer’s primary lawyers.

    Williams’s lawyers lodged complaints about delay tactics, saying that Energy Transfer’s lawyers remained “conspicuously quiet” and “noticeably silent” over issues that have threatened to scuttle the deal.

    The lawsuit also heaped blame on Latham & Watkins, another firm representing Energy Transfer, for failing to come up with a tax opinion needed for the deal to close.

    Kelcy Warren, the chief executive of Energy Transfer, responded that by filing three lawsuits in six weeks, Williams had “contributed materially to the very delay in completing the Securities and Exchange Commission’s review.”

    The contentiousness between typically buttoned-up lawyers reflects the hostility that has festered between their clients practically from the beginning.
