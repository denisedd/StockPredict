    Morning Agenda: Bayer Offers to Buy Monsanto

    By Amie Tsang
    May 19, 2016 May 19, 2016
    Morning Agenda: Bayer Offers to Buy Monsanto By Amie Tsang May 19, 2016 7:23 am May 19, 2016 7:23 am

    BAYER OFFERS TO BUY MONSANTO Monsanto said on Wednesday that it had received a takeover bid from Bayer of Germany, Michael J. de la Merced reported in DealBook.

    Monsanto did not disclose details of the offer, but said its board was reviewing the proposal. A merger would create a giant in antibiotics, genetically modified crop seeds and pesticides with an annual revenue of more than $67 billion.

    There had been speculation about Bayer’s interest in Monsanto with crop prices falling. Dow Chemical and DuPont agreed to merge last year.

    Monsanto, as the world’s biggest seller of seeds, was seen as an attractive target. As Bloomberg reports, its problems also made it vulnerable. It cut its earnings forecast, clashed with some of the world’s largest commodity trading companies and became locked in disputes with the governments of Argentina and India. Its shares have slipped 1.4 percent so far this year.

    It had tried to bid for Syngenta in a deal worth $47 billion, but Syngenta rejected it and was taken over by the China National Chemical Corporation this year. After the bid failed, Monsanto said it was no longer interested in huge mergers. As recently as Wednesday, Brett D. Begemann, Monsanto’s president and chief operating officer, told Reuters that talk of a takeover was “wild speculation.”

    Bayer confirmed to The Wall Street Journal and Bloomberg that it had met with Monsanto executives to discuss buying the company.

    SANDY WEILL’S WINE COUNTRY RETIREMENT Sanford I. Weill built Citigroup into a too-big-to-fail colossus, and dominated the New York charity and social circuit for more than a decade. Now he can be found attending wine country social events or riding his John Deere farm utility vehicle across his property to visit the neighbours, Randall Smith reports in DealBook.

    In spite of being a quintessential Brooklyn native, he and his wife, Joan, have decamped for Sonoma, Calif., and spend more than half their time there far from Wall Street’s high-octane environment and its critical onlookers.

    The fate of Citigroup and the role that giant banks played in the financial crisis of 2008 do not get discussed much there.

    In his heyday in the 1990s, Mr. Weill was renowned for his deal making. He made a series of ever-larger mergers that culminated with Citicorp and Travelers Group forming the behemoth Citigroup in 1998. This last deal helped knock down the Depression-era Glass-Steagall Act, which separated banking from the riskier securities business.

    But Citigroup suffered setbacks and he stepped down as chief executive in 2003 after a bruising regulatory investigation of biased Wall Street research uncovered his personal efforts to get a star telecommunications analyst to reconsider a tepid neutral rating on AT&T stock at a time when AT&T’s chief executive was a Citigroup director. Mr. Weill was not charged.

    He and Citigroup were also cited as contributors to the 2008 financial crisis. When he retired as chairman in 2006, Mr. Weill held 16.6 million Citigroup shares, then worth $768 million. Forbes listed his net worth at $1.8 billion in 2007. But the stock lost more than 90 percent of its value after his handpicked successor allowed Citigroup to build up huge exposure to toxic subprime assets.

    Mr. Weill disputed suggestions by two friends that the move west was motivated partly by a wish to get away from “all the noise around Citi.” Mike Conway, Mr. Weill’s chief of staff, said, “The Weills are not running away from anything. They are just trying to expand their horizons.”

    Mr. Weill does not appear to have lost his thirst for success. His property includes a vineyard that produces syrah under the “Weill a Way” label. The 2012 Weill a Way Exposition 3 Syrah, which retails for about $79, got a 99 score from the wine critic Robert Parker in 2015.

    Morgan Twain-Peterson, the owner of Bedrock Wine, which produces Weill a Way, said Mr. Weill has suggested increasing profit margins by raising the price. “He often says, ‘I don’t understand your business model at all.’ He always wants the wine to be priced higher.”

    EXODUS CONTINUES AT CREDIT SUISSE Five technology bankers at Credit Suisse defected to the Jefferies investment firm on Tuesday, Leslie Picker reported in DealBook. Most had spent decades at Credit Suisse, according to six people briefed on the moves. Another consumer banker also quit last week to work at Royal Bank of Canada, three of these people said.

    Departures having been accumulating. Bankers covering health care resigned this month to join Centerview Partners, while another banker who handles private equity relationships went to Goldman Sachs.

    People with direct knowledge of the moves said there was concern that investment banking had become less of a priority since Tidjane Thiam became chief executive of Credit Suisse. Mr. Thiam had announced a plan to realign the investment bank, and its traders and deal makers, to serve the needs of private banking and wealth management. Bankers were worried about a shift away from trading, mergers and acquisitions and initial public offerings to low-risk activities.

    European banks in general have been struggling to retain vital employees. Other firms, like Deutsche Bank, have lost several prominent bankers recently. They are more restricted in how much they can divide bonuses in cash versus stock, making it easier for an American competitor to poach bankers. Shares at Credit Suisse and Deutsche Bank have been cut in half over the last year, shrinking the potential stock value of their compensation.

    Jefferies and smaller advisory firms have been able to capitalize on this because they are not required by regulators to defer compensation and have the firepower to offer big pay packages and build up investment banking groups.
