    Will Regulating E-Cigarettes Mean Fewer Will Quit Smoking?

    Introduction

    John Stillwell/PA Wire, via Associated Press

    The Food and Drug Administration issued new regulations on tobacco products, including electronic cigarettes. While the rules are aimed at curbing the use of e-cigarettes by young people, some worry that the rules will make it more difficult to obtain e-cigarettes, which many believe — including the Royal College of Physicians, a major British medical organization — are a safer alternative to traditional cigarettes and helpful to those trying to quit smoking.

    How will the F.D.A.’s decision affect anti-smoking efforts?

    Debaters

    • The Rules Ignore Clear Benefits for Adult Smokers

      Kenneth E. Warner, professor of public health

      Even if e-cigarettes prove a lasting risk on kids, those kids will not experience the consequences for 40 years, during which time 10 million adults will die prematurely from smoking.

    • The E-Cigarette Industry Needed This Oversight

      Harold P. Wimmer, American Lung Association

      Claims that e-cigarettes can help smokers quit are troubling. Quitting tobacco is difficult, and Americans deserve proven and effective methods to help them successfully quit tobacco.

    • This Is a Challenge to Abstinence-Only Scare Tactics

      Amy L. Fairchild, professor of sociomedical sciences

      Don't lose sight of what the new F.D.A. rule states: E-cigarettes are less harmful than smoking combustible tobacco products.

    • Now Ban the Sale of Mentholated Tobacco Products

      Delmonte Jefferson, National African American Tobacco Prevention Network

      Research shows that a menthol ban could save millions of lives -- particularly black lives -- and reduce youth initiation to tobacco.
