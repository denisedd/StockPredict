    David Gelles reports in DealBook.

    FED MAKES ANOTHER PUSH TO LIMIT FALLOUT FROM BANK FAILURES | The Federal Reserve is proposing new measures to prevent a chaotic bank collapse,
    The Financial Times reports.

    It unveiled a proposal for lenders to stop counterparties from immediately exercising certain contractual rights when a bank goes into bankruptcy â essentially pausing before asking for their money back. The Federal Reserve is trying to find ways to handle a bank collapse without jeopardizing financial stability or requiring a taxpayer-funded bailout.

    The proposal would require contracts to be rewritten and fund managers would waive their right to terminate contracts and claim payments for a period of up to 48 hours when a bank enters bankruptcy. This would give time for certain contracts to be transferred to a solvent institution.

    The move would affect asset managers, hedge funds and insurance companies that do business regularly with banks through contracts known as qualified financial contracts.

    The proposal has already been criticized as ineffective. Donald Hawthorne, a partner at the law firm Axinn, Veltrop & Harkrider, told The Financial Times that the proposal would be counterproductive as institutional investors may start to terminate their positions and move their business elsewhere, possibly causing the very problems regulators are trying to remedy.
