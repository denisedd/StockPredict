    Lenders on online marketplaces are not average Joes, but hedge funds and other sophisticated parties aiming to extend credit to borrowers who might be turned away by a big bank or find the interest rates there prohibitive. So what is called peer-to-peer lending is really business-to-people.

    A study by professors at Columbia University and Fordham has examined the effect of an arcane opinion issued last year by the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, which called into question whether loans originated by Lending Club violated usury laws.

    Most states have usury laws, which cap the rate of interest that can be charged. South Dakota and Utah, among others, do not have this cap, which is why credit cards are issued from those states â the state where the loan originates is where the applicable usury law holds sway. Then the Second Circuit ruled that when a loan was issued in one state but sold in another state to someone other than a bank, the second stateâs usury law holds sway. This meant that high interest rate loans might be illegal.

    Under this ruling, it was easy to predict that the worst borrowers would no longer get loan offers on the peer-to-peer sites, because lenders would be concerned that they could not resell the loans.

    And the authors of the study did find that lending to these vulnerable borrowers simply stopped. The case is on appeal to the Supreme Court.

    The central question is, if you cap interest rates, are you shutting off credit to people who need it most? The study found that peer-to-peer lenders provided a valuable service. Instead of getting a credit card loan that charged 20 percent interest, low-income borrowers were paying lower rates thanks to the availability of this credit.

    Yes, Lending Club deserves more scrutiny, Mr. Davidoff Solomon writes. But marketplace lenders are not banks, they do not have the same central importance and they are not too big to fail. So far, they are doing more good for consumers than bad.
