    How Would You Fix the Subway?

    By Vikas Bajaj
    May 24, 2016 11:21 am May 24, 2016 11:21 am

    New Yorkers have very strong feelings about their subway, so it comes as no surprise that an editorial on how to fix the system publishedÂ on SaturdayÂ got more than 600 comments. Many readers offered suggestions for improving the system and others questioned whether it could ever be fixed given the mendacity of New York politicians. In the spirit of keeping the conversation going, I offer responses to a sampling of the comments by our readers (some comments have been excerpted for brevity).

    Toll skepticism

    Res Ipsa, NYC: This sounds great in theory, but in practice, the tolls will likely become just another revenue grab by the State. The people of NY need some assurances that the money will actually be used for the stated purposes and not just become another pot from which elected officials can make withdrawals at will.

    Vikas Bajaj: There is no question that residents and government watchdog groups will need to monitor how government officials implement the program and use the money generated by tolls. The M.T.A.âs budget is subject to audits by the state comptroller, who would no doubt pay close attention to how the agency was using a significant source of new revenue. It is healthy to be skeptical of lawmakers making big promises but the fear that toll revenue will be abused should not be a reason not to act.

    XY, NYC: I ride the subway all the time into the city. I occasionally drive in. Yet I am completely opposed to putting tolls (which are a regressive tax) on the East River crossings. I do not want Manhattan to be even more of a gated community.

    Minmin, New York: No fares on the East River until locations in Brooklyn and Queens that are badly served by public transportation have better transit options. Many Bridge and Tunnel folks â to use the term many Manhattaners use to demean residents of the boroughs–drive not because they want to, but because there is almost literally “no way to get there from here” at least without spending an hour and a half on busses and subways. One way. And to echo the sentiments of another poster: do we really want Manhattan to be even more of a gated community?

    VB: We should, of course, try to make sure that the proposed fees are not regressive. But keep in mind that less than half of the cityâs households own cars. And the poor are much less likely to own vehicles. Car ownership is higher in Queens and Brooklyn but even in those boroughs the poor are probably not driving into Manhattan regularly.

    As a Queens resident I fully agree that the M.T.A. and the city ought to improve transit in that borough and in Brooklyn. But transit service is actually quite good along the four bridges that will get new tolls under the plan. Transit service is weakest the further you get from Midtown. That is one reason the creators of Move NY propose to lower tolls on bridges like the Robert F. Kennedy (formerly Triborough) and the Verrazano-Narrows. In the areas served by these bridges many people have to drive because there is only limited bus or subway service. The good thing about the plan is that these are areas that will see an expansion in transit service paid for by the new tolls.

    Lisa Overton, Manhattan: Iâd love to see the Times do some analysis of the MTA budget over the last 50 years. How much advertising revenue is there? How much are the unimaginative COs paid? I suspect there are some solutions there.

    VB: Good question. We will look into it.

    Mr. Toronto, New York: The NYC subway system has an enormous ridership, generating yuge revenues. Has anyone audited the trail of these revenues? Before imposing any new tax that itself could be misappropriated, why not investigate the books of the NY city subway authority? They surely are not spending a dime on upkeep or maintenance, as is every other subway system in major cities around the world.

    VB: The state comptroller does audit the M.T.A.âs budget and the authority is overseen by legislators in Albany. It is clear that one of the biggest problems at the authority is that it didnât spend enough on maintenance and upkeep over the years. This is not necessarily or entirely the authorityâs fault. The state has often given it too little money to maintain the system it operates now. Consider this from a 2014 report by the state comptroller, Thomas DiNapoli:

    Despite this progress, achieving a state of good repair throughout the system has been elusive. The MTA has sought funding levels for its capital programs that have not materialized. The current five-year capital program, for example, invests 43 percent less ($13.8 billion) than the amount identified by the MTAâs own needs assessments.

    Strollers, backpacks and bikes

    George S, New York, NY: As for crowding, bikes should be banned from the subway, at least during rush hours, and the wearing of backpacks should be prohibited as well (a person wearing one takes up the space of at least two people). Large, side by side strollers should likewise be banned (the size of the modern stroller is ridiculous, some of them the size of a Smart car!). Eventually the system will also have to accommodate the glass barriers at track side used in many other systems worldwide.

    At some point the NYC system is going to have to upgrade away from the cheap swipe cards and move to more of a distance based smart card (like the London Oyster card).

    VB: Certainly, some of these suggestions could help create more space but they will only help a little and it won’t be easy to police them or practical to do so. Backpacks might be annoying but commuters need to carry things to school, college and work.

    I completely agree with you about the addition of glass barriers and sliding doors on platforms. As stations become more crowded, these barriers and doors will become an important safety device to protect commuters from falling onto the tracks. If combined with air conditioning, they will also make platforms much more comfortable during the summer.

    The M.T.A. is planning to replace its current fare card, which is truly outdated. I have used Oyster and I like it but I am not sure switching to the system will do anything to reduce congestion or create more capacity on trains.

    Fare changes

    jill: Congestion Pricing!

    1. Use it to move some subway usage to off peak hours, especially early morning usage. (For example, right now LIRR senior fares are only good for trains arriving after 10 AM at Penn Station). Expand that concept to the subway (wasn’t that a promise of Metrocard?)

    VB: This would require a much more complicated fare-payment system. Also, officials ought to think hard about raising prices at a time when many city residents struggle to pay the base fare of $2.75 per ride (or $116.50 for a monthly pass). A monthly pass amounts to more than 10 percent of the income of New Yorkers living in poverty, according to aÂ recent reportÂ by the Community Service Society and the Riders Alliance. Higher fares during rush hour would disproportionately hurt those who can least afford to pay more.

    Anne McIntosh, Manhattan: Someone from the MTA should visit the city of Bordeaux, France. Their unintrusive, electric train runs above ground and adds a touch of the whimsical as it moves the inhabitants through the city!

    Making developers pay

    Tom Gilroy, Brooklyn: Hereâs a radical idea â not; when every single real estate developer lies on the âEnvironmental Impact Studyâ of their tax-reduced addition of yet another sky scraper of overpriced residential units, claiming magically there will be âzero impactâ to parking, traffic, schools, sanitation, public space, fire department, or police department â why not make the developer pay for a new subway station as part of his building costs? or a few new trains?

    VB: Variations of this idea have been used in New York and elsewhere. Urban planners have a term for what you describe: transit-oriented development. The idea is that transit attracts people and real estate development so the development should help pay some or all of the cost of new transit service. The last subway station that the M.T.A. opened, 34 Street-Hudson Yards on the 7 line, wasÂ financed by bondsÂ floated by the city that will be paid off with tax revenue from the new real estate development in that area. The city and state should certainly learn from this station and use this approach again in the future when it makes sense.

    Encouraging cyclists

    Krista, Texas: Better, safer bike lanes might allow a good million or so people to get over their urban cycling jitters.

    VB: As a cyclist, I fully endorse this idea! The Move NY plan will send money to each community board that it can use for things like bike lanes. Hopefully, they will put the funds to good use.
