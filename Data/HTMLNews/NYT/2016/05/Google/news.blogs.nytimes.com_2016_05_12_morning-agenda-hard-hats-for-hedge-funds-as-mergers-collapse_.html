    HARD HATS FOR HEDGE FUNDS AS MERGERS COLLAPSE | More than $400 billion worth of corporate mergers have been withdrawn in the United States since the beginning of January â almost three times the previous record for the same period, Leslie Picker reports in DealBook

    The broken deals have whipsawed hedge funds that focus on merger arbitrage, placing bets on the likelihood that deals will be completed. Merger arbitrage was the worst-performing investment strategy in April other than macro funds, according to Hedge Fund Research.

    It was historically a calm hinterland of the investing world. Traders would buy up shares in a company being acquired that were trading slightly below the offer price, often by just pennies. It was considered low-risk trading for small returns.

    Now, investment funds are working with lawyers to pore over agreements to make sure they are placing bets on deals that are unlikely to come apart. But the riskier environment does offer more opportunities for greater returns as the market becomes more skeptical about pending deals.

    The collapse of these enormous deals has chilled the market, but for those still determined to go ahead, it means there is more focus on the deal terms. Executives and boards want to learn from the mistakes of deals gone awry.

    A ruling that blocked the proposed merger between Office Depot and Staples has prompted scrutiny of the decision by the two companies to forgo presenting their case in court, The Wall Street Journal reports.

    Judge Emmet G. Sullivan of Federal District Court for the District of Columbia has a reputation for thinking out loud in court and his early comments did not bode well for the F.T.C. This momentum might have been hard for Staples and Office Depot to sustain. And presenting their case would allow the F.T.C. to pick apart their evidence when they had thus far been allowed to freely take shots at the government case.

    So they decided to deprive the F.T.C. of a chance to confront their chief executives, also passing up the chance to make their best case to the judge.
