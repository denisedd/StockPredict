  1. Comptroller

    The comptroller is the city’s chief financial officer, in charge of a staff of 700 and responsible for, among other things, overseeing the city's pension funds, auditing city agencies, issuing reports on the economy and reviewing potential city contracts. Scott M. Stringer, the current Manhattan borough president, defeated former Gov. Eliot Spitzer in a hotly contested and often bitter Democratic primary. He is expected to coast to victory in the general election.

      • Public Advocate

        The public advocate serves as an ombudsman for the city government and is first in the line of succession if the mayor is unable to serve. But the office has limited power and scant resources, and it is not well known to voters. After a five-way primary, Councilwoman Letitia James beat State Senator Daniel L. Squadron in a run-off election that attracted fewer than 200,000 voters. She is all but certain to win on Tuesday.

          • Statewide Ballot Proposals

            Voters across the state will weigh in on six ballot proposals this year. The referendum that has attracted the most attention, Proposal 1, would permit casino gambling statewide and allow development of up to seven full-scale casinos. The proposal's offical ballot language has drawn criticism for presenting the measure in a positive, rather than neutral, light.

            1. Proposal 1: Authorizing casino gaming

              Authorizes up to seven casinos in the state as allowed by the Legislature, with the goals of creating jobs and generating tax revenue.

            2. Proposal 2: Additional civil service credit for veterans with disabilities certified post-appointment

              Allows disability credit to be retroactively applied to Civil Service exams if a veteran took the exam before being certified disabled.

            3. Proposal 3: Exclusion of indebtedness contracted for sewage facilities

              Continues allowance for cities, towns, counties and villages to exceed their debt limits in order to pay for sewage facility construction.

            4. Proposal 4: Settling disputed title in the Forest Preserve

              Resolves a century-old dispute between the state and private parties over land in the Adirondack Forest Preserve.

            5. Proposal 5: In relation to a land exchange in the state Forest Preserve with NYCO Minerals, Inc.

              Allows a mining company to expand its operations into 200 acres in the Adirondack Forest Preserve. In exchange, the company will give New York State land worth at least $1 million and agree to restore the 200 acres to its earlier condition once mining operations are complete.

            6. Proposal 6: Increasing age until which certain state judges can serve

              Raises the mandatory retirement age to 80 for Supreme Court justices, up from age 76, and Court of Appeals judges, up from 70.

          • Borough President

            Voters will have a choice, in most areas, to select a party candidate for the office of borough president. The office, while limited in power, plays an important role in zoning and community board issues. And though it has traditionally been viewed as an engine for political patronage jobs, the office has also been a stepping stone to higher office (David N. Dinkins, Manhattan, later was elected mayor) and an attention-getting platform (Marty Markowitz, Brooklyn).

            In Manhattan, Councilwoman Gale A. Brewer from the Upper West Side defeated three experienced candidates to win a crowded Democratic primary in September. She will face Republican David Casavis in the general election.

            Melinda R. Katz, a former assemblywoman and former councilwoman who lost in the 2009 Democratic primary for comptroller, won a bruising Democratic primary in Queens. Ms. Katz faces Tony Arcabascio, a Republican and political newcomer, on Tuesday.

            In Brooklyn, State Senator Eric Adams, a Democrat, is a virtual lock to succeed Marty Markowitz, who will leave office after three terms because of term limits.

            City Councilman James S. Oddo, a Republican, will face Democrat Louis Liedy in the general election.

            In the Bronx, Ruben Diaz Jr., the Democratic incumbent, will face Republican challenger Elizabeth Perri in the general election.

            • District Attorney

              There has been no shortage of drama in the race for Brooklyn district attorney. After losing in the Democratic primary to Kenneth P. Thompson, the incumbent, Charles J. Hynes, said he would abandon his bid for a seventh term, despite remaining on the Republican and Conservative Party ballot lines. He soon reversed course, choosing instead to run as a Republican in Tuesday's general election, and effectively giving him a second chance to win re-election to the office he has held since 1990. Hynes' reversal has upset some Democrats, including the mayoral front-runner, Bill de Blasio, who has openly backed Mr. Thompson, who won the primary by 10 points.

              In Manhattan, incumbent District Attorney Cyrus R. Vance Jr. will face Republican Peter Gleason his bid for a second term. Incumbency provides a powerful advantage in district attorney races -- it is exceedingly rare for a sitting district attorney not to be re-elected.

                • City Council

                  City Council members are elected every four years, and all 51 seats are up for grabs this year. Council members are responsible for passing local laws, making decisions about land use, investigating and overseeing city agencies and approving the city's budget.
