New Yorkers’ Questions for the Mayoral Candidates

This year, for the first time, we invited readers to participate in the editorial board’s mayoral endorsement interviews by sending their own questions for the candidates. We got many submissions, from every borough, on subjects from housing to hurricanes, schools to stop-and-frisk. After narrowing the list down, we posed one question to all the candidates, then had them choose a second question at random.
Published:
July 14, 2013

  1. One Simple Idea

    Question From Christopher McHale of Brooklyn

    The last two mayors sought to make practical changes in the city’s quality of life by introducing straightforward ideas like smoking bans and bike lanes. Name one simple idea that could significantly improve daily life in New York City.

    Sal Albanese
    Former City Council member

    My fair tolling plan, which would reduce congestion, which I believe is choking many neighborhoods. By imposing tolls in bridges that have a lot of mass transit options, you would keep a lot of trucks off the street. Because what’s happening now is many trucks are looking to use bridges that don’t have any tolls on them. My plan would add tolls to certain bridges and reduce tolls to other bridges, and that would reduce traffic congestion, especially truck traffic, which is choking many neighborhoods.

    Bill de Blasio
    Public advocate

    I have trouble separating that from my larger program because I think if we’re doing things like creating affordable housing or addressing policing issues, for the people involved, that is the highest impact there could be on their quality of life. So I think you’re talking about something that reaches everyone, at least some. Well, I would pick up then in the same place. I think the expansion of bike lanes more deeply into the outer boroughs is an example. I think there’s a lot of areas that still need them and would benefit. The same with bike share. Obviously bike share is nascent, but it’s started closer in Manhattan and the areas closer in. I think if you’re talking about a more holistic impact it would be to really make it five borough in both cases. Make bike lane expansion and bike share expansion truly holistic in the city.

    Adolfo Carrión Jr.
    Former Bronx borough president

    I think that when you live in an urban setting, the one treasure that you sort of appreciate and value is open space, quality, calming space, whether it’s for the tranquility that it provides you as an individual, for the activity that it provides your children and your family, for the potential for sports activities and physical activity. That green space in every neighborhood within a short distance from where you live, I think is one.

    And then the other, and I’m going to just add another, because I think it’s an important quality of life concern for me and I see it on a daily basis. If we’re going to make New York City a really quality livable city, we’re going to have to make our mass transit more aesthetically pleasing and a better experience. There’s got to be much more ability to influence that experience in New York City because so much of our work force and our daily activity relies on the quality of that experience. So I would give attention, whether it’s political pressure as a chief executive or gaining some additional control of the mass transit system.

    John Catsimatidis
    Owner of Gristedes supermarket chain

    A new idea? The one that I’m basically hung up on is making sure our streets are not given back to the hoodlums, which is not a new idea. That’s my No. 1 pet idea that I believe that people have to be able to walk around and feel safe, to be able to walk around at night and feel safe, to be able to go out at night and feel safe, to be able to park their cars or take the subway and feel safe.

    I believe that stop-and-frisk is going to go away sooner than later. Twenty-first century technology. You’re on 96th and Broadway, let’s say. And there’s a crime on 98th Street and Columbus Avenue, and the computer knows where the crime is, the computer knows where the cop is, he could get to the scene of the crime within two minutes or less. That is going to help keep New York safer. I really feel that. I don’t know why nobody else has thought about it. That would be my recommendation.

    Joseph Lhota
    Former M.T.A. chairman and deputy mayor

    One simple idea that I think could improve life in New York City is having the city at the end of each one of its subway lines in the Bronx and in Brooklyn and in Queens create park and ride. The number of people who come into the City of New York that burden our traffic problems could be significantly reduced if we just simply built parking garages and encourage more people coming into the city to use our subway system. If you go to the top of the Bronx or if you go out to Queens at the end of each one of our lines, we don’t have park and ride facilities. It is I think the most common sense approach that we can do to start eliminating some of our traffic congestion problems in the city, encouraging more people to take our subways and mass transit.

    John Liu
    Comptroller

    I don’t know if it’s considered a simple idea, but housing construction has to be a priority for the next administration. Also, I think we need tax reform. Again, I don’t know if people would consider it a simple idea, but my tax reform proposal would put more money in the pockets of middle-class and working men and women in this city.

    Instead of the flat tax that we have right now, where no matter how much you make, you pretty much pay the same rate of tax, I believe that there should be a much more progressive system of rates, where people making over half a million dollars a year now will pay a little bit more. And everybody else below that threshold will get a reduction in their New York City income tax bill.

    George McDonald
    Founder of Doe Fund charity

    I want to concentrate on cleaning up the central business districts in every neighborhood in New York City. All of those overflowing garbage cans I want to be empty and orderly. I want to remove the graffiti stickers and clean up all of the business areas outside of Manhattan.

    I would do that by expanding paid transitional work programs.

    Christine Quinn
    City Council speaker

    One simple idea that will make people’s lives easier is when we expand 311 and create a My City Hall app, where you will be able to have all of your interactions with government in one place that is easily accessible to you in a technological way. You can more easily get in touch with us in government and track everything government is doing for you and everything you need from government. So My City Hall will be that.

    William Thompson Jr.
    Former comptroller

    I think that one of the things that has been missed is health disparities in the City of New York, particularly in black and Latino communities. I think it is important that we tie health and mental health components into our schools. I think that would help to create healthier communities. As opposed to just looking at things like vision and hearing and testing for that, looking at things like diabetes and high blood pressure would allow us to address coronary disease and a number of other things early on. And I think that is one of the things that I would really like to do.

    Anthony Weiner
    Former congressman

    For too many people in this city, real compassionate health care is elusive. 1.2 million people are completely without coverage; even more are underinsured. That is a challenge that not only affects those that are not getting health care, but all the rest of us because of the drag on the economy that it creates, because of the inability of small businesses to hire, because of the high cost, because of the tax burden that we carry for covering the uninsured. It is big, but it is straightforward. Every New Yorker should have access to quality health care that doesn’t bankrupt them. I’m going to try and make that a reality as mayor.

  2. Sal Albanese

    Question From Mary Travers of Manhattan

    You will control the school system as mayor. Please give a concrete idea for the education of the public school student — not charter or private school students and not an idea that involves the classroom teacher — that should help result in a living wage paycheck for that student.

    Sal Albanese
    Former City Council member

    When I talk to parents around the city and some of our poor communities, they understand that I.Q. is not the issue, poverty is the problem. Poverty causes developmental issues. The first three years of a child’s life are pivotal and we’re not addressing it. I want to set up pediatric wellness centers in low-income communities where parents, doctors and teachers work together so when kids come into our schools at 4, 5 years old, they’re not so far behind that they never catch up. And that would go a long way toward addressing some of the root causes of our failing schools. Early intervention, birth to 3, I want to set up pediatric wellness centers in those communities so we can start working with parents and children early on. That would pay dividends.

  3. Bill de Blasio

    Question From Gregory Downs of Manhattan

    While some New Yorkers are exclusively pro- or anti-charter schools, many others, like myself, are willing to give charters a chance but disagree with the co-location system that all too often pits charter and traditional schools against each other and drains public resources. Since many other cities require charter schools to find their own locations, would you be willing to name a chancellor who will require charters to find their own space?

    Bill de Blasio
    Public advocate

    I want a chancellor who will end this conflict over co-locations. Right now I have called for a moratorium until we’ve come up with a plan that truly involves parents in schools early enough. I think that rather than requiring charters to find space outside the schools, I would say it would be my preference that charters find space outside schools. It would be my preference that when they find space in a school and they have the resources to pay for it, as some of them do, that they pay for it. But I think actually if you had a parental involvement plan that was more careful and earlier, so that there was a chance to really work through what the impacts would be, some co-locations would be deemed workable, others would be rejected and that would really reset the whole equation. When the plans were no longer sort of my way or the highway, the D.O.E. has spoken, but actually were adjusted according to the needs within the building and parental concerns, then I think that you could have some healthy and productive co-locations. Ideally, outside schools, but it’s not impossible to do it right with a co-location into a public school.

  4. Adolfo Carrión Jr.

    Question From Holly Maxson of Manhattan

    Given the inequities of taxation of real estate in Manhattan versus the boroughs, what is your plan to reduce the discrepancies in the Real Estate Tax Code across-the-board and give everyone a chance to pay their share in keeping the city financially solvent?

    Adolfo Carrión Jr.
    Former Bronx borough president

    We rely on property tax for a significant amount of our revenue. And we have made New York City a tax-friendly city for homeowners. It is lopsided toward them, and I say that as a homeowner. Many people who left the city discovered that their tax burden was so big that they wanted to return. I have family in Rockland that pay anywhere between $18,000 and $25,000 a year in property tax. They could live on City Island like I do and pay $6,000 to $7000 a year. So we’ve encouraged homeownership and that’s a good thing.

    I think where we have failed is in a number of areas. One, our co-op and condo tax. We’ve had to apply this abatement to bring back some equity or fairness to the taxes, but it’s not structural. We go back and re-approve an abatement that we give co-op and condo owners. So one, we need to create parity, equity and fairness there. If you own a co-op or a condo, that’s your home and we should be friendly to that. I think that we need to stop what I consider a pretty insidious practice or smoke and mirrors game of reassessing the value of commercial property in New York City on an annual basis and not calling it a tax. It has become a disincentive for investment in certain sectors. So you have a business, you rent space in an office building, the office building owner gets reassessed that year. The assessment goes up. And then that landlord is required to either pass on the costs or eat the costs. The landlord will pass on the costs and, of course, that adds to the cost of doing business, becomes a disincentive for job growth. People start looking at, well what are my options here, do I have to be in New York City? Might I be in a suburb of New York City. Might I take my business to Newark or somewhere else? So I think we have to stop that practice, call it a tax and be transparent about it. We can’t continue to backfill the budget gaps with that property class.

  5. John Catsimatidis

    Question From David Lelyveld of Manhattan

    Do you support the N.Y.P.D. practice of undercover surveillance of Muslims? Would you support general surveillance of other ethnic or religious communities? Under what circumstances and according to what rules and procedures?

    John Catsimatidis
    Owner of Gristedes supermarket chain

    Look, I grew up in our country here. I’m a firm believer in our Constitution and our Bill of Rights and I would say I do not support surveillance to find out who did some crime. But I would support it for one exception: terrorism that will hurt the masses. A guy who’s going to be a 9/11-type situation, a nuclear bomb-type situation. So I would say I do not support surveillance for, let’s surveil that guy because he didn’t pay his taxes.

    Proactive surveillance only if we have suspicion, big suspicion of terrorism to hurt the majority of our citizens.

  6. Joseph Lhota

    Question From Mike Rempel of Manhattan

    As Mayor, how will you use social science research to inform your decisions? Can you give an example of research you've come across that has led you to change, or at least modify, your views on some issue?

    Joseph Lhota
    Former M.T.A. chairman and deputy mayor

    I’m not sure what they mean by social sciences, other than the fact that I’ve always been offended by government officials who have decision-making by polls. Decision-making by polling is the absence of profiles in courage. Leaders should be determined based on their convictions and their views. John Kennedy’s book “Profiles in Courage” needs to be read by everyone running for office. Or anybody who wants to be a leader. You have to have convictions and then go forward. Decision-making based on polls is not the way to run a government.

  7. John Liu

    Question From Sarah Feldman of Queens

    What are you going to do about the decreased funding in libraries across all the boroughs? The Queens Library, for example, might have its doors closed in the Ridgewood, Queens, area where I live. Isn't that the one thing we should not take away from people is free education and reading?

    John Liu
    Comptroller

    I agree with that sentiment and as part of my people’s budget, I have proposed a plan with the associated revenues that would allow all of the libraries in New York City to stay open 7 days a week. The fact of the matter is that the cost of allowing libraries to open 7 days a week is in the tens of millions of dollars, a drop in the bucket in the realm of the city’s $70 billion annual budget, and is always subject to this ridiculous budget dance that occurs year after year after year that introduces uncertainty and therefore instability in our library system. We should make it stable, cut them out of the budget dance and give them the additional funding to do so. And I’ve identified the revenues as part of my people’s budget. Go to peoplesbudgetnyc.com.

  8. George McDonald

    Question From John Albin of Manhattan

    Rather than grandstand about ending it, please provide more realistic and honest answers about what you think are appropriate uses and limits of the practice of stop-and-frisk.

    George McDonald
    Founder of Doe Fund charity

    The appropriate use of it is obviously if a police officer sees specific suspicious conduct, then they have an obligation to investigate that. What I don’t want to see is the distrust between the community and the police. And we have to work toward getting people jobs and create opportunity for them, rather than continuing the practice of throwing minority children up against a wall and searching them.

  9. Christine Quinn

    Question From Melissa Vollbrecht of Manhattan

    Young, old, single, families. Housing is a nightmare for most New Yorkers; can anything be done to help us sleep in peace in an apartment or home that doesn't devour half our monthly income?

    Christine Quinn
    City Council speaker

    I agree that housing is one of the most enormous problems we have right now in the city. And what we need to do, what I’m going to do when I’m mayor, is fight to get the city control over its rent laws again, so we can protect rent-stabilized tenants, keep those units. I want to build 40,000 new middle-income housing units over the next 10 years, keep the production of housing the mayor has planned and expand for lower-income units as well. I want to make sure we take the 100,000 distressed housing units out there in the city, make them livable, hold the slumlords or the banks accountable, get that money back for New Yorkers. I also want to create for homeless folks a real program where they can get rent subsidies to get out of the shelters. I wish that there was one easy answer. There isn’t. It’s a puzzle and we’re going to have to keep aggressively putting the pieces in it, so we’re adding more housing at different income levels across the city. I’m also looking at the idea of a property tax cap to preserve affordable housing in neighborhoods where it’s a little economically challenging to build new affordable housing.

  10. William Thompson Jr.

    Question From Sandi Lusk of Bronx

    What do you intend to do about the skyrocketing number of homeless families in N.Y.C., and how would you come up with a sane, humane, cost-effective way to house them?

    William Thompson Jr.
    Former comptroller

    I think as we look at the rise of homelessness, and I think now we probably have over 50,000 people who are homeless in the City of New York. The things that have not been done that we saw that worked before: Things like legal services that prevented homelessness, looking at vouchers and creating priority for homeless families and Section 8 vouchers. Things like putting the money that we have that’s been sitting on the side for the Housing Authority back in renovation, making sure that we renovate and bring those units online. All of that could go into reducing homelessness. I think that we’ve gone away from that. That helped to keep homelessness down in earlier years of this administration. We need to go back and do some of that also. And then part of it is we have to build additional housing. I’ve spoken about that earlier, the need to construct new housing and as we look at affordable housing particularly for poor families. I think whether it’s 80-20 (People receive tax breaks to be able to build. They have to provide 20 percent of the units that are affordable.) or 70-30, I think the opportunity to create more affordable units will drive homelessness down. And last but not least, if we don’t tie social services in to families who have become homeless, once they find apartments, we need to maintain the services to them. That will keep them in apartments and keep them from coming back into the homeless population again.

  11. Anthony Weiner

    Question From David Sangalli of Manhattan

    What are you going to do to balance the city's pro-development population boom with increases and improvements in infrastructure (e.g., no hospital on the Lower West Side of Manhattan, overcrowding of public schools, high-priced new housing and gentrification displacing older, poorer populations from affordable housing)?

    Anthony Weiner
    Former congressman

    We have a structural challenge that goes well beyond infrastructure. The housing itself is falling apart. Public housing has fallen into terrible neglect because of neglect by the federal government. And we have a problem that the present rise in property values is making it impossible to build affordability. Incentives that we’ve had simply don’t keep up. One thing that we have to be careful not to do, though, is to have a zoning policy that is so far-leaning toward housing that we carve out any ability to have reasonable middle-class jobs. Right now if you want to have a back office space for your big business and you have a choice between Jersey City and Long Island City, you’re going to Jersey City because they figured out the math of reserving portions of their real estate for job creation, not just all for housing.

    I’ve suggested taking the traditional 80-20 program and making it 60-20-20. The 60 percent now makes about as much profit as the 80 percent used to and does give you room to do middle-class housing. But we are a growing city, and in the mayor’s 2030 plan, he has projected that there will be another million people in the city. We have to not only have a place for them to live, but a place for them to work and to live comfortably.
