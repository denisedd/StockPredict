    Donald Trump’s Convention: Day 1

    JULY 18, 2016

    Arguments, provocations and observations from Times Opinion writers.

    2. Cleveland’s Odd Opening
      By Frank Bruni

      CLEVELAND — The theme of the evening was “Make America Safe Again.” The headline speaker was Melania Trump. I have a better chance of winning an Olympic gold in gymnastics than of connecting those dots.

      But why should Donald Trump’s coronation be any tidier than his campaign? He indulges his whims. He goes with his gut. And the opening of the Republican National Convention here was a mirror of the man: visceral, rambling, sporadically mesmerizing, occasionally baffling, brimming with contempt for Hillary Clinton and beset by divisions within the G.O.P.

      It wasn’t the star-studded, gee-whiz show that he once promised, and this, too, reflected Trump. His sales pitch is grand, even when the product is anything but.

      Trump’s presidential campaign has been like no other in the modern era, and in the context of its eccentricity, Monday’s doings were pretty tame. Most of the staging was humdrum. Most of the speakers were scripted and succinct.

      But there was oddity nonetheless, starting just after dawn.

      Paul Manafort, Trump’s de facto campaign manager, used a string of morning appearances to denounce John Kasich, the governor of Ohio, for not endorsing Trump, calling that calculation “dumb, dumb, dumb.” He thus focused attention on all of the prominent Republicans who were skipping this convention rather than on all of the prominent Republicans who did show up. How is that smart, smart, smart?

      And Melania’s remarks were a reminder of the way in which a man who has had three families is somehow being presented to voters as the ultimate family man.

      “Donald is intensely loyal,” she said, though there’s plenty in his biography to suggest otherwise.

      “Donald intends to represent all the people, not just some of the people,” she added, though there’s nothing in the way he won the Republican nomination to give voters any assurance of that.

      Her 15-minute testimonial will be followed in the nights to come by speeches by all of his children except the one still in grade school. Indeed, his brood has been so present and peripatetic during this campaign that I imagine them as some contemporary political analogue to the Partridge family, clattering around the country in a gilded jet instead of a multicolored bus.

      Melania skips more than a few of those trips: She hasn’t played an especially public role in Trump’s campaign. That added a drumroll of excitement to her appearance on Monday night, though her words themselves quickly frittered it away.

      Indeed, the most interesting aspect of what she had to say came hours afterward, as more and more people began to notice and plumb striking similarities between a few of her sentences and a few of those in Michelle Obama’s convention speech in 2008. Both spoke of parents who impressed on their children that “your word is your bond.” Other phrases and thoughts from Obama’s speech recurred in Melania’s.

      For the most part, though, Melania simply stated over and over again what a one-of-a-kind talent her husband is and what an amazing president he’d be. Her language didn’t vary much, her details were few and her evidence, to the extent that she provided any, wasn’t particularly compelling. She’s his perfect match, not just in love but in vagueness.

      We learned nothing important from her, but we learned something important about him when he introduced her. It was an unusual move, coming into the arena like that, and it stirred speculation among observers — and fear among his supporters — that he’d pull a stunt like he did when he monopolized the microphone at Mike Pence’s big unveiling, letting his sidekick know just how far to the side he’d be kicking.

      But Trump spoke for mere seconds, then directed the spotlight toward Melania and vanished. This wasn’t proof of gallantry or humility, but of a discipline that usually eludes him and of a lesson learned. And that was Monday’s greatest oddity of all.

      I invite you to follow me on Twitter at twitter.com/frankbruni (@FrankBruni) and join me on Facebook.

    3. The Alt-Right Gets Its News
      By Elizabeth Williamson

      CLEVELAND — Andra Lance scolded me for not having heard that Bill Clinton, while he was Arkansas governor, had visited “rich hotshots who want to run the world” promising them secret, special favors from his, and now his wife’s, administration.

      “As a journalist, you really need to educate yourself,” said Ms. Lance, 63, flashing her youthful grin. She isn’t sure if she heard that Clinton story from Infowars, Breitbart.com, “The Ben Shapiro Show” or Louder With Crowder. “If you’re not reading these, you’re only getting one side,” she said.

      Breitbart was one of the sponsors of the America First Unity Rally for Donald Trump at Settlers Landing Park in downtown Cleveland on Monday. Sitting in the sunshine on the grass overlooking the Cuyahoga River, about 200 Trump supporters took in five hours of conspiratorial affirmation.

      Most liberals, who have their own like-minded news sites, have never heard of these websites. But they’re where the alt-right gets much of its news. They’re also where Mr. Trump’s campaign got the Star of David artwork and the spurious black-on-white crime statistics for his tweets.

      It’s from these sites that Mr. Trump’s supporters get the sense that something disturbing is happening. Here’s the “Ben Shapiro Show” podcast lineup on Monday:

      Ep. 151 — Every Time a Cop Dies, Obama’s Approval Rating Goes Up

      Ep. 150 — President Uniter Meets With Race Riot Fans!

      Ep. 149 — Obama Speaks in Dallas, Is a Terrible Person

      Ep. 148 — Obama Blackmails Cops, Then Stands on Their Graves

      Ep. 147 — Racist, Lawless Obama Lies About Racism, Cops

      “I’m not saying they’re right about everything,” Ms. Lance said. “But they are right about a lot of things.”

      Some rally attendees said they’d learned from these sources that Hillary Clinton plans to eliminate the Constitution, that most American slave owners were black people, that America is “really the last free nation, because Europe has been taken over by Muslims.” At the rally, they learned from Roger Stone, the Trump adviser, that Bill Clinton has “raped and bitten women.” Wait. Did he say bitten?

      “I never heard that before,” said Dennis Ragle, a retired F.A.A. air traffic controller standing beneath a shade tree. He gets his news from his friends’ shared posts on Facebook. I asked him what he thought Mr. Trump meant when he said again on Monday that President Obama’s “body language” when he speaks about police shootings suggests “there’s something going on.”

      “I’m not much of a conspiracy theorist,” he said. “But there could very well be something going on.”

      Elizabeth Williamson is an Editorial Board member of The New York Times covering national politics.

    4. A Malediction, Not a Benediction, for Trump’s Convention
      By Nicholas Kristof

      A benediction is a blessing, from roots meaning “good” and “words” — but a pastor gave a benediction at the Republican National Convention Monday that was staggeringly tasteless and politicized, provoking controversy on the internet and coming across more like a malediction.

      “Our enemy is not other Republicans,” Pastor Mark Burns, from South Carolina, told the convention hall, “but is Hillary Clinton and the Democratic Party.”

      “Father God, in the name of Jesus, Lord, we’re so thankful for the life of Donald Trump,” Reverend Burns declared. “We’re thankful that you are guiding him, that you are giving him the words to unite this party, this country, that we together can defeat the liberal Democratic Party.” It went on along those lines; you can watch it on YouTube.

      Reverend Burns is an African-American televangelist whom Time magazine has described as Trump’s “top pastor” (not that there’s much competition).

      Pastors on all sides of the aisle sometimes get carried away by their politics or ideology, but I can’t remember ever hearing a minister give a benediction that described a political rival as the “enemy” and was so nakedly polarizing: Somehow Jesus’ message morphed from “love your neighbor” to “Democrats are the enemy.” God should sue the Republican National Convention for defamation.

      Perhaps the wisest response to this kind of effort by politicians to co-opt God came from another Republican. In 1865, in his Second Inaugural Address, Abraham Lincoln declared soberly of the North and South in the Civil War: “Both read the same Bible and pray to the same God, and each invokes His aid against the other. It may seem strange that any men should dare to ask a just God’s assistance in wringing their bread from the sweat of other men’s faces, but let us judge not, that we be not judged. The prayers of both could not be answered. That of neither has been answered fully. The Almighty has His own purposes.”

    5. Outside the Convention, South By Southwest for the C-List
      By Emma Roller

      The first person I recognized when I arrived at the Cleveland Airport to cover the Republican National Convention was Willie Robertson, the “Duck Dynasty” star, who made the list of celebrities speaking in support of Donald J. Trump this week.

      The first person I recognized when I got to downtown Cleveland was Don King, the 84-year-old boxing promoter, who did not make the cut of official speakers. He was engaged in a shouting match under the awning of the Marriott Hotel.

      When I came upon the scene, all I could tell was that two men were shouting at each other at very close range. Then I realized the elderly man wearing a bedazzled jean jacket with the founding fathers on the back was Mr. King.

      The Republican National Convention so far is like South by Southwest for washed-up entertainers. People not granted access to the Quicken Loans Arena itself float from event to event, stopping to congregate in a mass around any vaguely recognizable C-list celebrity they see. Shortly before Scott Baio was slated to give a prime-time speech, Mr. King walked down Hudson Avenue with a small entourage.

      “This is fantastic! We are fighting for a better America!” Mr. King told no one in particular.

      The man who had been grilling Mr. King in the doorway of the Marriott Hotel once again pressed him on his past, specifically his manslaughter conviction.

      “You killed a guy,” the interrogator from the Marriott standoff said.

      “Yes, absolutely,” Mr. King said absent-mindedly, before he registered the comment. “What? Honestly, I accept a deep sympathy for the loss of a human being, but not for being human.”

      He continued walking down Huron Road, destination unknown.

      “Don King! Don King! I love you!” a male Trump supporter yelled.

      A female Trump supporter stopped Mr. King to take a selfie with him. “I’m sorry, Baby. Look at me, look at me, look at me,” she said, as if she were taking a photo of a puppy.

      I decided to embrace the enveloping abyss and ask Don King a semi-serious question: What did he make of the report that Reince Priebus did not want him to speak at the convention?

      “I think he’s been lost in a capsule, a time capsule. You know that guy — what’s that guy, he needs to go back in time? What’s his little name?” Mr. King said. “He must be Rip Van Winkle. That’s what it is. He is Rip Van Winkle! He’s been asleep for 20 years because he don’t know what time it is!”

      It was the most astute political commentary I’d heard all day.

      Emma Roller (@EmmaRoller), a former reporter for National Journal, is a contributing opinion writer.

    6. Donald Trump’s Two Views on the Iraq War
      By Carol Giacomo

      To hear Donald Trump talk, Hillary Clinton’s 2003 vote in favor of the Iraq war disqualifies her for the presidency.

      “In short, Hillary Clinton’s tryout for the presidency has produced one deadly foreign policy disaster after another,” Mr. Trump said in a policy speech in June. “It all started with her bad judgment in supporting the war in Iraq in the first place.”

      Of course, if he believed this, he’d have to drop out of the race, too.

      In a 2002 interview, radio host Howard Stern asked Mr. Trump if he supported the impending invasion of Iraq. He replied: “Yeah, I guess so.”

      His hypocrisy was on full display on “60 Minutes” on Sunday night, when he claimed to have no trouble with his vice-presidential running mate, Gov. Mike Pence of Indiana, a former congressman, for having supported the Iraq war.

      Asked about the disparity, Mr. Trump did not even flinch. “I don’t care,” he said about Mr. Pence’s pro-war position.

      The vote took place a “long time ago,” and Mr. Pence “is entitled to make a mistake every once in a while,” Mr. Trump added.

      But Mrs. Clinton is not?

      “No. She’s not,” he replied.

      As for his own past position on the war, he’s giving himself a pass.

      Carol Giacomo is an Editorial Board member of The New York Times covering foreign and defense policy.

    7. Three Speeches to Watch
      By Ross Douthat

      Many of the speeches at the Trump convention promise to be interesting mostly in their awkwardness. (Watch as Paul Ryan pretends that his party is nominating Jack Kemp! Watch as Chris Christie enacts the final act of “Doctor Faustus”!) But anyone interested in the legacy of Trumpism as a movement, rather than just a one-election-cycle cult, should pay close attention to three speakers.

      First, Senator Tom Cotton of Arkansas, speaking tonight. Without going the full Christie, the freshman senator has steered closer to Trump than most of the politicians in his youthful cohort. His profile in Washington has been highly ideological and very hawkish, but his positions on immigration and crime are very Trumpian, as is his Arkansan constituency. If he has presidential ambitions (not really an if), the populist path blazed by this year’s nominee might look attractive — so watch to see if his speech tries to blend Trumpishness and traditional conservatism, and how well such a marriage works.

      Second, Ivanka Trump, speaking Thursday. The jokes about Trump considering his daughter as a running mate were (probably) just that, but it’s clear that Ivanka and her husband, Jared Kushner, are the closest thing to a superego in the Trump campaign, and it’s easy to imagine the father’s ascent encouraging ambitions in his most impressive child. It’s a bit harder to imagine Ivanka playing Marine Le Pen to Donald’s Jean-Marie: A jet-setting New York socialite (and convert to Judaism) is an unlikely leader for an aggrieved heartland nationalism. But in a world where Trump can be the G.O.P. nominee, all manner of things seem possible, and her speech might drop a hint as to whether she feels any political temptations.

      Third, Peter Thiel, also speaking Thursday. The venture capitalist and Gawker nemesis has kicked up controversy in Silicon Valley by agreeing to be a Trump delegate and now a convention speaker. But if you’ve followed Thiel’s public-intellectual work, which mixes libertarianism, pessimism and futurism, the association isn’t that surprising. He’s not a populist (far from it), but he thinks the developed world is stuck in a stagnationist loop that desperately needs to be disrupted or escaped — and maybe he thinks that sometimes you need to embrace a disruptive start-up even when it comes in the unlikely form of Donald Trump. His speech won’t matter for the 2020 Republican primary, but it’s more likely to be strange and memorable than anything else said from the podium this week — and far more likely to be intellectually influential than any Republican politician’s halfhearted defense of their party’s nominee.

      Ross Douthat is an Op-Ed Columnist for the Times.

    8. Social Media vs. Regular Media: The Codependent Death Match
      By Virginia Heffernan

      In the media, who can tell the margins from the mainstream anymore?

      Video coverage of the Republican National Convention this week will be dizzyingly decentralized, with social media and regular media locked in a codependent death match — and everyman anchors, each with her own agenda, reporting or commenting on the event via social media in every conceivable digital dialect.

      And it’s those competing dialects — among journalists, protesters, delegates, ideologues and the far-flung convention audience — that threaten to tilt into cacophony. Riots in the street have been predicted by some; riotous exchanges in the media are a certainty.

      From amateurs using social media, expect “gotcha” moments, or efforts to invent those moments, as well as the kind of “reverse surveillance” that has recently allowed and even incentivized the filming of misdeeds by police, protesters and campaign workers. From the networks, look for a defensive crouch. They’re fiercely guarding institutional authority by shooting video as if this convention were media business-as-usual, keeping their eyes on the podium and on benign cutaways (unless news they deem news breaks among the audience or protesters).

      While Facebook Live, the hugely popular live-streaming service, will be available as a platform for visual commentary to any of Facebook’s 1.65 billion active monthly users, it also will feature C-Span’s coverage front and center. This means it’s not only offering polyglot and populist coverage but also monopolizing the cameras known for greatest comprehensiveness, beige-tone and neutrality.

      Twitter is streaming CBS’s video, so the young and rambunctious service is now synced up with the Tiffany Network, which has authoritatively, even arrogantly, covered conventions (first as radio) for nearly a century. To witness CBS’s god’s-eye perspective streaming through Twitter’s vox populi interface is sure to generate cognitive dissonance.

      The Republican National Convention’s own robust app is live-streaming video from Google, using 360-degree cameras for, the app promises, “an immersive experience.” The oligopoly that is Google serving as back end for the G.O.P.’s self-coverage? Can anyone imagine ABC, say, playing vendor for the Republican Party in covering the convention? Public airways — and their F.C.C. mandate to provide balanced coverage — have all but disintegrated.

      Between cameraphone fragments and unlikely network-digital bedfellows, anticipated “chaos” in Cleveland this week may be mirrored, if not aggravated, by the thousands of skirmishing cameras, each with its own perspective.

      If, not long ago, observers were bemoaning the end of spontaneity inside the party convention halls (the absence of back-room dealing, the lack of suspense over the nominee), this year it’s fear about spontaneity from an unruly mob that has informed the anticipatory convention coverage. And that coverage has, of course, stoked anxiety — which has been amplified, refracted and stoked by social media.

      Temperatures in Cleveland were in the 80s today, and are expected to mostly rise this week, to a high of 91 on Thursday as the convention reaches its crescendo. As the nation’s wireless bloodstream courses with video, beware of overheating devices — and tempers.

      Virginia Heffernan, a former television critic and internet columnist at The Times, is the author of “Magic and Loss: The Internet as Art.”
    9. 28 Years of Dropping Red, White and Blue
      By Liriel Higa

      Treb Heining is the G.O.P.’s go-to guy for balloon drops. Cleveland will be his eighth Republican National Convention, and he has also orchestrated and advised the Democrats.

      On Sunday, he took a few minutes to talk to me:

      Q: What’s the most number of balloons you’ve used at an event? How many will there be in Cleveland this year?

      A: The most that I’ve used is more than one million, but that’s when we were going for world records. As far as the political conventions, I think the most we ever did was more than 200,000, and that was in 1992 down in Houston. In Cleveland this year we’re over 100,000.

      Q: The 2004 balloon drop at the Democratic National Convention, which you were not responsible for, was a failed balloon drop. What went wrong?

      A: I don’t know the exact particulars; I feel bad for the people that were involved.

      Q: How far in advance do you prepare for a balloon drop at the conventions?

      A: We meet with the organizers months in advance; this came maybe six months in advance. The balloons were inflated here last Friday, and they remain up in the ceiling now until Thursday when they fall. The whole process of inflation and rigging is all done within an eight-hour period. They’re very high quality balloons, American-made product, so the longevity is very good.

      Years ago, I developed a system when I required large amounts of labor, I would go to local high schools — primarily the music departments because I was a band geek myself back in high school. We give them a special shirt, we feed them, we pay for their buses, and at the end of the day we give them a nice honorarium that they can use for musical instruments.

      Q: How many students were involved in blowing these up?

      A: One hundred and twenty students and 20 adult chaperones.

      Q: You’re consulting in Philadelphia for the D.N.C. this year, but you’ll be here in Cleveland for the drop?

      A: Oh yeah. I call the drop. It’s almost like playing a piano. It’s not just a cue, “go!” and they all fall down. They’ll come down in wave after wave after wave. There will probably be more than 20 different cues.

      Q: Do you have to deal with cleaning them up?

      A: We get to make the mess, but we don’t have to clean up.

      Liriel Higa is a staff editor in The Times’s Opinion section.

    10. The ‘Security Moms’ Who Could Vote for Trump
      By Judith Shulevitz

      Here are two words to listen for at the Republican National Convention this evening: “kids” and “murdered.” The leitmotif of “Make America Safe Again,” as tonight’s program is billed, is dead children.

      Among the 25 prime-time speakers listed is a mother, Mary Ann Mendoza whose son was killed by an undocumented immigrant driving drunk the wrong way down an Arizona freeway. Another, Jamiel Shaw, lost his son, a high school football star, to another undocumented immigrant who had just been released from jail for offenses that probably should have gotten him deported. Patricia Smith’s son died in Benghazi, Libya. The lineup also includes two siblings — the brother and sister of a Border Patrol agent, Brian Terry, fatally shot in 2010 on the Arizona-Mexican border by Mexican gangsters wielding an American gun they’d picked up from the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives’ much-criticized “Operation Fast and Furious” program.

      Mothers, this is your night. If you think that Donald Trump couldn’t possibly appeal to female voters, given remarks he has made about breasts, Megyn Kelly’s menstrual blood and women being “disgusting pigs,” think again.

      The Republicans hope to hold on to America’s “security moms” — the white, married, suburban women concerned about national security who were credited with pulling the Republican Party rightward in the wake of Sept. 11. “Many G.O.P. women may be willing to overlook Trump’s derogatory statements because they believe he is the only candidate capable of keeping America safe,” wrote Melissa Deckman, the author of “Tea Party Women: Mama Grizzlies, Grassroots Leades, and the Changing Face of the American Right,” in The Washington Post a couple of months ago.

      Kellyanne Conway, a Republican pollster who was recently hired by Trump’s campaign, agrees. Asked by Katie Couric what Trump could do to capture women voters, she said: “Keep talking about security, affordability, fairness and ethics. I call it the SAFE acronym.” In a Fox News interview this month with six self-identified “security moms,” the women stressed the importance of “naming the enemy” — jihadists — controlling immigration and helping them protect their children.

      Trump doesn’t need the majority of female voters, which he could never get, anyway, given his extraordinarily high unfavorability ratings. The race is close enough that he needs only to peel off some. It’s not crazy to think he might succeed: According to a CNN/ORC International poll last December, women were more likely to disapprove of Barack Obama’s handling of terrorism than men were.

      Melania Trump, one of tonight’s headliners, is lovely, but unlikely to touch the hearts of women terrified of terrorists and illegal immigrants. That’s why you’ll hear so much about these young martyrs.

      Judith Shulevitz (@JudithShulevitz) is the author of “The Sabbath World: Glimpses of a Different Order of Time” and a contributing opinion writer.

    11. What’s Going On? Racism
      By Andrew Rosenthal

      If Donald Trump actually intends to use his big convention week to present a saner, more inclusive image of his candidacy for president, an idea that is funny at best, he has sure gotten off to a bad start. On “Fox and Friends” this morning, Trump doubled down on his outrageous insinuation that President Obama is somehow sympathetic to or even in league with terrorists and cop killers.

      Asked about the president’s response to the killing of three police officers in Baton Rouge, La., Trump said: “I mean, you know, I watched the president and sometimes the words are O.K.,” Trump said. “But you just look at the body language. There’s something going on. Look, there’s something going on and the words are not often O.K., by the way.”

      To his credit, the host of the program, Steve Doocy, pressed Trump to explain what he meant. But of course, that’s not how Trump operates. He puts ridiculous claims and accusations out there, sometimes obliquely, and refuses to take responsibility for them.

      “There’s just a bad feeling, a lot of bad feeling about him,” Trump said. “I see it, too. There’s a lot of bad feeling about him. We have a country that has not been like this since I can remember it.”

      What bad feeling, apart from the one among those who hate Obama because he is African-American? What kind of country does Trump mean? Who knows?

      The man who will be nominated as the Republican’s presidential candidate this week has done this before. After the Orlando terrorist massacre, he also implied that Obama was on the side of terrorism. “There’s something going on,” he said. “It’s inconceivable. There’s something going on.”

      There is something going on, indeed. Trump and his allies are running a campaign of racism, fear, xenophobia and social division that is horrifying, even by the standards of the current Republican Party. It has been turning itself over to these forces for decades now. Nothing that Trump says this week — if he indeed says anything unifying, moderate or conciliatory — can change that.

      Andrew Rosenthal is an Op-Ed Columnist for the Times.

    12. Why Conventions Still Matter
      By David Leonhardt

      Once — in the days of Lincoln, the Roosevelts and Eisenhower — political conventions actually chose the nominee. Today, they feel like long infomercials.

      Yet it turns out that conventions still matter, more than many people realize.

      The conventions are the first time that many Americans tune into the presidential campaign. That’s especially true for swing voters, most of whom don’t vote in the two parties’ primaries. The conventions may be infomercials, but they’re infomercials that millions of people watch or otherwise pay attention to.

      Political scientists in recent years have expended great effort arguing that much of modern campaigning isn’t very important. While we in the news media hype speeches, gaffes and vice-presidential selections, most of these purported game-changers have little effect on a campaign’s outcome.

      Conventions, however, are the exception. Even most political scientists agree that they matter. They matter so much that it has been almost impossible to win the presidency in modern times without emerging from the conventions with a lead in the polls. By the time the conventions are over, most voters have made up their minds. So when the Democrats depart from Philadelphia in 11 days, we will have a strong sense of this campaign’s likely path.

      The key research on the topic has been done by Robert Erikson of Columbia and Christopher Wlezien of the University of Texas. They lay it out in a highly readable little book called “The Timeline of Presidential Elections.” Erikson and Wlezien point out that polls conducted before the conventions are unreliable. Many losing candidates, including Ross Perot, in 1992, have held early leads.

      But emerging from the conventions with a lead is a big deal. The closest thing to an exception over the last 60 years is Ronald Reagan, who was merely tied with Jimmy Carter immediately after the 1980 conventions. (George W. Bush trailed Al Gore modestly after the 2000 conventions, but, of course, also went on to lose the popular vote.)

      The typical pattern is for both candidates to get a boost from their own convention, but for the boosts to be different in size. And once the convention is over, any subsequent changes in the polls — yes, even after debates — tend to be small or fleeting. “Debates don’t have anywhere near the same effect” as conventions, Erikson told me.

      Bill Clinton may be the best example of the conventions’ importance. He did such a good job of introducing himself to voters at the Democrats’ 1992 gathering in New York — and of hammering George H. W. Bush, the incumbent — that he rose 15 points in the polls and overtook Bush and Perot. By the time Clinton left New York, Americans could imagine him as their president, and he never trailed again.

      The task facing Donald Trump in Cleveland is arguably harder than that of any other recent candidate. More than 60 percent of Americans have an unfavorable view of him, which helps explain why he has rarely cracked 40 percent in head-to-head polls with Hillary Clinton.

      For Trump to get closer to 50 percent, he will first need to solidify the support of Republican voters. The likely absence of many top Republicans from Cleveland — among them the Bushes, Mitt Romney and John McCain — won’t help.

      Even more difficult, Trump will need to win over swing voters who helped elect President Obama. Somehow, Trump will have to persuade them that his string of offensive remarks about women, Muslims, Latinos, the disabled and others don’t define him. He’ll have to make himself seem presidential.

      When the Democrats gather in Philadelphia next week, Hillary Clinton will have an easier job, because she has a small lead in the polls. But she still has work to do. Above all, she will need to remind voters of the qualities that made them appreciate her more at other points in her career than they do today.

      Given Clinton’s advantage and Trump’s chaotic convention planning, he seems unlikely to have a great next two weeks — which would be a relief to all of us who are alarmed by the Trump phenomenon. But whatever your feelings, you shouldn’t dismiss the conventions as a sideshow. Trump is, if nothing else, a skilled performer, and he has a chance to receive more focused, positive attention in Cleveland than he ever has. The next four nights represent probably his last best chance to reintroduce himself to the American public.

      David Leonhardt, who will begin an Op-Ed column later this year, is writing a daily Opinion post during the conventions.

    13. The Whole World Is Reading

      Before we even got to the city of Cleveland, we had a lot to say about the state of the Republican Party. Ross Douthat and Reihan Salam proposed “A Cure for Trumpism.” Emma Roller told us how everything she learned about politics in college, she was untaught by Professor Donald Trump. Peter Wehner wondered whether liberals were right about his party all along. He also had some questions about the theology of Trump, such as it is. Gail Collins used her vast knowledge of American political history and Indiana specifically to talk about the selection of Mike Pence, both the medium and the message. Frank Bruni got something out of Trump’s hair. Tom Edsall was thinking about how we got here in the first place and how strong Trump’s support might prove among college graduates. J.D. Vance explored why Trump’s anti-war stance resonated with Republican voters and Stanley Fish wrote about why professors shouldn’t claim that they know more about Trump than anyone else.
