    Morning Agenda: Lawyers in Line of Fire at Delaware Courts

    By Amie Tsang
    July 13, 2016 July 13, 2016
    Morning Agenda: Lawyers in Line of Fire at Delaware Courts By Amie Tsang July 13, 2016 6:07 am July 13, 2016 6:07 am

    LAWYERS IN LINE OF FIRE AT DELAWARE COURTS First investment banks came under fire for the roles they played in billion-dollar buyouts, and now there is a case that could extend liability to the lawyers in buyouts, Steven Davidoff Solomon writes in Deal Professor.

    The complaint arose from the $2.2 billion buyout of the ExamWorks Group, which contends that lawyers at Paul Hastings, a law firm in Los Angeles, were in cahoots with management and the investment banks to sell the company at a below-market price. ExamWorks, a medical examination company, vigorously denies this.

    Daytona Beach Police and Fire Pension Fund, a shareholder of ExamWorks, is suing the company’s directors, as well as the private equity firm leading the buyout, Leonard Green & Partners. It says Paul Hastings failed to run the process appropriately.

    Management and directors were participating in the buyout. Companies usually establish a committee of independent directors to guide the process from the beginning, but ExamWorks was slow to do this.

    The board still included management in the buyout discussions and even after the founders were excluded from the special committee, they appear to have regularly participated in meetings.

    At the same time, Reinaldo Pascual, a partner at Paul Hastings, continued to represent the board while representing management in negotiating the buyout arrangements. He also represented the special committee and the company itself.

    The lawsuit contends that the special committee failed to bargain effectively, negotiating an increase of 5 cents from the top of the firm’s indicated range, and no competing bids emerged during the short 25-day “go shop” period, although some private equity firms expressed interest.

    The price being paid is below ExamWorks’ 52-week stock market high of $44.09 and only a 4 percent premium over the closing price the day before the deal announcement, albeit 37 percent over the price when Leonard Green’s first bid came in.

    In the past, when there were questions about how a sale was run, lawyers were largely out of the line of fire. But this case threatens to open the floodgates and expose lawyers to the same type of conflict liability and heat that investment bankers are under. Paul Hastings is not a defendant, but the complaint indicates that it may become one.

    This is a lesson in managing conflicts, Mr. Davidoff Solomon writes. The best practice is to be like Caesar’s wife – above suspicion.
    A FOUNDER OF HYPERLOOP ONE CLAIMS HARASSMENT IN LAWSUIT Hyperloop One, the company trying to prove that a very high-speed train from San Francisco to Los Angeles is possible, is mired in legal infighting between the founders of the company and other former senior executives, Michael J. de la Merced reports in DealBook.

    Brogan BamBrogan, who helped found the company, and three other former executives, sued Hyperloop One claiming they were harassed and wrongfully fired by Shervin Pishevar, the other founder, and other senior managers.

    The lawsuit claims breach of contract, defamation, unlawful infliction of emotional distress and assault.

    Mr. BamBrogan has also sought a restraining order against Afshin Pishevar, who was Hyperloop One’s chief legal officer. Mr. BamBrogan accuses Afshin Pishevar, Shervin Pishevar’s brother, of placing a noose in his office chair when they both worked there.

    Rick Jennings, a spokesman for Hyperloop, said in an emailed statement, “Today’s lawsuit brought by former employees of Hyperloop One is unfortunate and delusional. These employees tried to stage a coup and failed. They knew that the company was aware of their actions, and today’s lawsuit is their pre-emptive strike. The claims are pure nonsense and will be met with a swift and potent legal response.”

    Hyperloop One has drawn prominent executives from the world of technology and announced an $80 million investment round in May. But the lawsuit claims that it was run as the personal fief of Shervin Pishevar and Joe Lonsdale, one of the directors.

    BLYTHE MASTERS STEPS DOWN AS CHAIRWOMAN OF SANTANDER CONSUMER USA Blythe Masters has quit as chairwoman of Santander Consumer USA after less than a year, The Financial Times reports.

    Her departure from the company, a subprime car loans provider, adds to the uncertainty surrounding the Spanish bank’s operations in the United States. Santander’s business in the United States was the first bank to fail the Federal Reserve’s stress tests for three consecutive years.

    Ms. Masters declined to comment on the move, but people familiar with her thinking told The Financial Times that she was finding it hard to allocate time to the role because of her other commitments.

    Ms. Masters, best known for helping develop the credit default swaps market while at JPMorgan, is also full-time chief executive of Digital Asset Holdings, which focuses on using the technology underpinning Bitcoin to track and settle financial assets.

    The need to travel for Digital Asset Holdings clashed with the demands of overseeing Santander Consumer USA, which regulators expect to be almost a full-time job, people familiar with her business told The Financial Times.

    She will switch to a new role advising Santander on blockchain. She will also join its international advisory board and become a director at Openbank, its online lender.
