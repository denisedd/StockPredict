    Master of Performance:

    Adrian Grenier

    After more than a decade portraying one of television’s most popular bros, Adrian Grenier is making a career out of promoting environmental issues in his own right, and serving as the first-ever social good advocate for the technology company Dell.

    Road to Stardom

    During eight HBO seasons and one appearance in a feature film as a member of the cast of “Entourage,” Adrian Grenier charmed millions of viewers as Vincent Chase. He was a rising movie star with just enough low-key charisma to lead members of his Queens, New York, hometown squad around Hollywood on an always funny, sometimes tragic quest for parties, women and fame.

     It’s a gold star entry on the 40-year-old Brooklyn resident’s lengthy roster of acting credits in projects that range from indies like John Waters’ “Cecil B. Demented” to blockbusters like “A.I. Artificial Intelligence,” “The Devil Wears Prada,” and the action thriller “Marauders” co-starring Bruce Willis. 

    But his career diversification hasn’t just been in film. In fact, he’s shown a certain relentlessness when it comes to expanding the diversity of his professional success with work behind the camera, in music studios, executive boardrooms and all over the web. He says: “It is all born of the same spirit of creation and storytelling and communicating ideas.” He has founded a film production company, Reckless; performed in two bands — Kid Friendly and The Honey Brothers — and continues to break new music talent with his indie recording label and music incubator Wreckroom.

    Branching Out

    His broad range of creative passions may not include much sleep or downtime, but over the years, Grenier’s resume has become an impressive case study in leveraging a successful entertainment career with a passion for social responsibility. He’s invested his fame wisely in bringing attention to his efforts to help raise awareness of issues surrounding climate change and preservation of the environment. He has produced and co-hosted the green-focused lifestyle series “Alter-Eco” and co-founded Shft.com, a web destination for content encouraging sustainability through art, design and gastronomy. “You look at the magnitude of the problems facing us,” says Grenier.  “I think we have to think big and act boldly. We can’t afford to be small.”

    His eco-focused work was noticed by the tech company Dell, and in early 2015, representatives approached him with a proposal to partner to champion healthier, more sustainable choices and actions for consumers and businesses alike through digital storytelling and advocacy.  In March 2015, Grenier was named as Dell’s first-ever Social Good Advocate, a job that gives him the opportunity to help Dell advance the company’s 2020 Legacy of Good goals and develop new joint programs leveraging technology for social good and advocating for the circular economy movement, including the launch of a feasibility study for using ocean plastics in Dell packaging. “It really is something that goes above and beyond any agreement,” says Grenier. “It really is a shared value, a shared vision and the commitment to manifest ideas around the world.”

    Just Cause

    It wasn’t long before Dell and Grenier partnered with the rideshare company Uber to launch “NYC Tech Takeback” to make it easier for New Yorkers to responsibly recycle their used technology through the Dell Reconnect program, a partnership with Goodwill Industries. They also collaborated to create “Cry Out: The Lonely Whale Experience,” a virtual reality film in which viewers get to simulate swimming alongside a whale in a three-minute excursion that involves encounters with other marine life, noise pollution and plastic ocean debris. 

    The VR experience was created on Dell VR technology, feeding an audience appetite for the rich simulation of real life experiences while growing awareness of an environmental issue. “To have technology meet our ability to think and act is so important,” he says. “The speed and performance of these machines is really impressive and only getting better.”  “Cry Out: The Lonely Whale Experience” has toured the globe, including its debut at Art Basel in Miami, the United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP21) in Paris, Consumer Electronics Show in Las Vegas, South by Southwest in Austin, National Association of Broadcasters in Las Vegas and many more, helping to build a global community of ocean advocates.

    The Tech Connection

    Grenier launched the Lonely Whale Foundation in December of 2015 in conjunction with the debut of the VR film. It’s a non-profit that honors a lost whale once heard making calls at 52Hz — a frequency that isn’t noticed by other whales — leaving the gentle sea giant to live in total isolation. He and his team hope that the story of the whale, also named 52Hz, will encourage people to consider more deeply the harm that ocean pollution has on the animals that live in it. Grenier also launched a Kickstarter campaign that helped finance production of a full-length documentary on 52Hz, and the film is currently being edited on Dell technology. “I want to help translate the often abstract messaging about the problems that are facing the ocean through a character that speaks to all of us,” says Grenier. “It’s a story of connection and unity.”

    Adrian Grenier has said that when it comes social and environmental advocacy, he has an entrepreneurial spirit. He is open to working with new tools and resources to improve life for people and animals, and also the environment. One of those tools is technology, which helps in the creation of innovative systems for getting the word out on important causes and offering ways for people to turn that awareness into action.  He says through his work with Dell, he has found a partner with similar values that supports his progressive, creative approach to technology as well.  “Dell has really been introducing me to great ways they’re helping artists, communicators and people who are trying to make the world a better place,” he says. “I started creating my own businesses and my own non-profits and these led to the work that I’ve been doing and continue to do with Dell. We share the same vision of making the world a better place and using technology to do so. Not just doing business as usual, but actually creating a new model for business in the future.”
