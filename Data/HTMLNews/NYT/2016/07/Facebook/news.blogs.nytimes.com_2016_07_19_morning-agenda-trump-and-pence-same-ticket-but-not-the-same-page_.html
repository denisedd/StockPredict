    Morning Agenda: Trump and Pence: Same Ticket, but Not the Same Page

    By Amie Tsang
    July 19, 2016 July 19, 2016
    Morning Agenda: Trump and Pence: Same Ticket, but Not the Same Page By Amie Tsang July 19, 2016 6:09 am July 19, 2016 6:09 am

    TRUMP AND PENCE: SAME TICKET, BUT NOT THE SAME PAGE If there is one thing consistent about the business views of Gov. Mike Pence of Indiana, it is that they are often at odds with those of Donald Trump, Andrew Ross Sorkin writes in DealBook.

    Mr. Trump’s views are not always on the record in the same way as Mr. Pence’s votes in Congress, but appearances in the news media indicate that the two are far from being in lock step.

    As a congressman, Mr. Pence opposed the bills that turned out to have the biggest repercussions on Wall Street and American industry – the bailout of the financial industry, known as the Troubled Asset Relief Program, and the rescue of the auto industry.

    Mr. Trump, on the other hand, said in 2008 that the bailing out of the banks was “sad, but, probably, it’s something that has to get done, because your financial system is most likely going to come to a halt if it does not.” He also said later, “I do agree with what they’re doing with the banks. Whether they fund them or nationalize them, it doesn’t matter, but you have to keep the banks going.”

    When it came to the auto bailouts, Mr. Trump was also opposed to Mr. Pence. “I think the government should stand behind them 100 percent,” he told Fox News. “You cannot lose the auto companies. They’re great. They make wonderful products.”

    Mr. Pence’s donors have not always been Mr. Trump’s supporters either. The billionaire Koch brothers have been long-time supporters of Mr. Pence, but Mr. Trump has been unable to persuade the family to support him.

    They don’t disagree completely on business. They both want to undo the Dodd-Frank regulatory reform law and want to lower individual and corporate taxes.

    However, the real practical obstacle created by Mr. Pence’s addition to the ticket is that he might make it harder for Mr. Trump to raise money from the business community. Because Mr. Pence is a sitting governor, donations from Wall Street fall under an obscure Securities and Exchange Commission law meant to prevent pay-for-play efforts for state pension funds, according to OpenSecrets.org.

    “The rule prevents ‘S.E.C.-registered investment advisers’ from contributing more than $250-$350 to state or local officials who could select the firm that would manage a state or local pension fund, or some part of it,” the organization’s website said. “That means most hedge funds and private equity firms – their PACs, their executives, their fund managers and probably their investor relations staff – can’t give to the ticket.”

    But, as Mr. Trump already said on Sunday night, “I don’t care.”

    And he is still struggling to unite his own party for the Republican convention, as Jonathan Martin and Patrick Healy report in The New York Times.

    SOFTBANK SHARES PLUNGE AFTER DEAL ANNOUNCEMENTShares in SoftBank plunged in Tokyo after it announced a deal to take over a British chip designer for $32 billion dollars, Bloomberg reports. Its stock dropped about 10 percent – the biggest decline since 2012. This came after shares in Sprint fell 5 percent on Monday over concerns that the company would get less support from SoftBank.

    The acquisition would make financial sense only if SoftBank more than quintupled the operating profit of the chip designer, ARM, Dominic Elliott writes in Breakingviews. Even with the rise of driverless cars and web-enabled industrial and domestic appliances, that seems a stretch.

    To achieve a 10 percent return on its investment – ARM’s own estimate of its weighted average cost of capital – SoftBank would need to increase operating profit from Â£500 million last year, or about $660 million, to Â£2.9 billion, or about $3.8 billion, using ARM’s effective tax rate last year of 18 percent, Mr. Elliott estimates.

    Cost savings are usually used to justify a deal this size, but the two companies have no overlapping businesses, so any additional value would need to be created from nebulous cross-selling opportunities.

    ARM is best left for the irrational, the exuberant or the desperate, Mr. Elliott writes.

    The deal has at least given Britain some cause for optimism after its decision to leave the European Union threatened to quash deal activity.ARM, however, is a special case, as Leslie Picker explains.

    The company is relatively immune to a slowdown in the British economy because it only generates about 1 percent of its revenue from Britain. It also does not require much in the way of raw materials as it designs chips and then sells the intellectual property. Its employees are spread out across the world. The deal is also unlikely to run into significant regulatory opposition or lead to visible cost cuts.

    The tie-up has also led to a reunion of Morgan Stanley alumni, Anita Raghavan writes in DealBook . Jeffrey Sine, who helped found the Raine Group, was the lead advisor. He first got to know SoftBank in the mid-1990s when working for Morgan Stanley. He advised on SoftBank’s acquisition of the publishing company Ziff-Davis and helped take it public later. Raine Group was also given the assignment of spearheading SoftBank’s acquisition of a controlling stake in Sprint.

    Mr. Son has also had Alok Sama, another former Morgan Stanley banker, working for him as chief financial officer. Mr. Sama took on the additional title of president when Nikesh Arora left this summer. He brought on board Alex Clavel, another managing director from Morgan Stanley, to work on the ARM acquisition.

    Mr. Sine also knew the team at Robey Warshaw, the London investment banking boutique that is advising SoftBank on the deal. SoftBank needed a well-connected British banker to lay the groundwork for what would be the largest inward investment into Britain by an Asian company.

    YAHOO’S REVENUE SLUMPS Yahoo revealed just how badly its business was deteriorating on the same day that it accepted the final bids to buy its core business, Vindu Goel reports in The New York Times.

    Its revenue in the second quarter fell 15 percent, after excluding accounting adjustments, and its operating profit plunged 64 percent. The company also acknowledged that Tumblr, its biggest acquisition under its current chief executive, was now worth only one-third of the $1.1 billion that Yahoo paid for it in 2013.

    Still, investors were more interested whether its email, news and web services would finally be sold and at what price. But Marissa Mayer, the chief executive, had little say on the topic. She offered no timeline for a decision.

    The flailing business will make it difficult to get top dollar for the company. According to the research firm eMarketer, Yahoo will get about 1.5 percent of the world’s digital ad revenue this year, down from 2.1 percent last year. By comparison, Google is expected to command 30.9 percent of the market and Facebook is expected to garner 12 percent.

    Analysts expect the final bids to come in at $3.5 billion to $6 billion, including Yahoo’s land and patents.

    Ms. Mayer noted that one bright spot for the industry, video advertising, was beginning to undergo price drops in the face of a glut of inventory as Facebook, Twitter and others churn out more live video and clips. “There’s so much video, particularly mobile video, coming online that there is some price compression,” she said.
