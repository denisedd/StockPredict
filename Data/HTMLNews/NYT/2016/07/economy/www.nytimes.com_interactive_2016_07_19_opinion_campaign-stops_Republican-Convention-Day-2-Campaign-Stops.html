    Donald Trump’s Convention: Day 2

    JULY 19, 2016

    Arguments, provocations and observations from Times Opinion writers.

    2. All About Hillary
      By Frank Bruni

      CLEVELAND — By saying almost nothing about Donald Trump on Tuesday night, Paul Ryan said it all.

      Trump isn’t the star of his own convention. Hillary Clinton is.

      What really animates the Republicans gathered here is their antipathy toward her, not their embrace of him. Speaker after speaker makes the case against her, not the one for him.

      And a weird impression is taking hold: They’re filling the minutes and running out the clock with all the bad that they can dredge up about her because there’s not enough good to plumb in him.

      He’s not their shining hero. He’s just their sole alternative.

      “Only with Donald Trump and Mike Pence do we have a chance of a better way,” Ryan said, and it’s worth paying close attention to a few of those words. Only. Better. This was the vocabulary of resignation, not of exultation.

      Without hatred of Clinton, the delegates had … what? Not Trump, not on Tuesday night. He had gone back to New York City — he’s always happiest in a tower with his name on it — though he beamed in via a Jumbotron to say a quick hello.

      He predicted victory in November. He vowed — wait for it! — “to make America great again.”

      He asked a question: “Are you having fun?” The response lacked energy. I took it as a no.

      About that promised greatness: If Trump can’t ace four days in Cleveland, how’s he going to ace four years in Washington?

      He keeps telling us what a paragon of competence and purveyor of wonder he is, but there’s scant evidence of that here so far, and the mess surrounding Melania Trump’s remarks is only the half of it.

      He promised the sun, the moon and Hollywood stars. He gave us Scott Baio.

      The speeches ran late on Monday. They started late on Tuesday.

      And so far, they’ve failed utterly to do the one thing they must: sell Trump to those voters who are still skeptical about him.

      Chris Christie followed Ryan, and spent so much time detailing Clinton’s sins that there was little left for Trump’s virtues.

      Ben Carson followed Christie, and lavished most of his peculiar oratorical energy on Clinton, whom he linked to Saul Alinsky and, by extension, Lucifer. You had to hear it to believe it, and you had to assume that Carson’s were yet another set of remarks not thoroughly vetted by the crackerjack Trump operation. Carson mustered no such grandiloquence for Trump, who had to make do with Carson’s leftovers.

      What a rich twist. At his own coronation, someone who insists on adulation is being turned into an afterthought. It’s not much of a testament to his management skills. It’s not much of a testament, period.

      Frank Bruni is an op-ed columnist for The Times.

    4. Time for an Exorcism
      By Nicholas Kristof

      The Republican National Convention has echoed with all kinds of accusations against Democrats, but perhaps the oddest came when Dr. Ben Carson spoke to the arena Tuesday evening and suggested that Hillary Clinton may be … a devil-worshipper.

      I had to listen to the tape again to make sure I wasn’t hallucinating. But no. Carson notes Clinton’s long interest in Saul Alinsky, the leftist community organizer, cites an Alinsky book mentioning Lucifer, and then suggests that Clinton reveres Alinsky who acknowledges Lucifer. If you don’t quite get the connection, don’t worry—no one else does either. But Carson’s implication seemed to be that Clinton is an acolyte of Satan.

      Whoah.

      I wrote Monday about the convention’s scurrilous benediction, in which a pastor prayed and portrayed Clinton as “the enemy,” and we’re all familiar with how some Republican voters think President Obama is a secret Muslim. But a political convention speaker suggesting that the other party’s presumptive nominee may be an agent of the devil? I’m speechless.

      Frankly, I’d say it’s time for an exorcism of demons—and not from Hillary Clinton.

      Nicholas Kristof is an Op-Ed columnist.

    5. Trump Stands on the Shoulders of Roger Ailes? If So, Look Out
      By Nicholas Kristof

      Donald Trump is rising, and Roger Ailes is tumbling — and their trajectories are deeply interwoven.

      Ailes’s tenure as head of Fox News appears to be ending, just as the Republican Party he shaped is meeting here in Cleveland for its convention. And it’s a tribute to Ailes’s vision that today’s G.O.P. has been galvanized, prodded and molded by Fox News.

      Indeed, Trump is the Republican nominee perhaps in part because Fox News and other prominent right-wing commentators weakened the control of Republican Party bosses. Fox News certainly didn’t create Trump, and he had many disputes with it and especially with Megyn Kelly, but Fox created space that Trump filled. I disagree profoundly with much of Fox News’ reporting, but it’s impossible to deny its importance as a social and political force in America.

      Yet Ailes’s apparent downfall also reflects a larger weakness as well: If he had vision, he also suffered from myopia.

      The ugly sexual harassment of which Ailes is accused was common a generation ago and too often was accepted as boys-will-be-boys behavior. But times have changed, and he apparently continued in the 2010s conduct that is no longer winked at and is now often (unfortunately, not often enough) taken very seriously. Ailes was stuck in the past, and now he apparently will end his career in disgrace as a result, if the reports are correct.

      The Republican Party suffers a similar myopia. It continues to behave as if America were in the 1970s, and its nostalgia leads it to antagonize blacks, Latinos and Muslims, as well as many whites uncomfortable with what feels to them like bigotry. In the 2000 Republican convention, George W. Bush was careful to put black faces on the podium almost nonstop, so that people joked that convention coverage looked like Black Entertainment Television, because Bush knew that he needed to come across to American centrists as tolerant and open-minded. In contrast, the 2016 convention is mostly whites all the time.

      Republican leaders have talked for many years of the need to adapt to changing values and changing demography. But so far they haven’t managed to change, any more than Ailes did — and so just as Ailes’s rise was a boon for the Republican Party, so his downfall is a harbinger of trouble ahead, of a political party that blindly marches on without regard to changing times. As Ailes topples today, I’d bet on the G.O.P. tumbling tomorrow.

      Nicholas Kristof is an op-ed columnist for The Times.

    6. Ben Carson Sees an Upside to Plagiarism
      By Emma Roller

      CLEVELAND — On Tuesday evening, a group of black Republicans gathered in the C’est La Vie Restaurant and Lounge in Cleveland to celebrate Donald J. Trump’s ascendance to the top of the Republican ticket. (As the French say, that’s life.)

      A recent poll found that zero percent — yes, zero — of black respondents in Ohio and Pennsylvania said they would vote for Mr. Trump over Hillary Clinton in November.

      Outside of the C’est La Vie shindig, Omarosa Manigault — of “Apprentice” and “Celebrity Apprentice” fame — spoke to reporters. She is the very recently announced director of African-American outreach for the Trump campaign. After a testy exchange about the 0 percent poll, she admitted that Republicans do have a problem reaching black voters, but she thinks that is changing.

      “The movement has already started, where Donald Trump is bringing folks who have never been involved in this party here to this convention to be a part of the movement that is making America great again,” she said.

      After Ms. Manigault departed, Ben Carson popped out to the lobby to address the reporters he loves to hate. He admitted Republicans need to do more to reach out to black voters, but argued that Democrats have done little to improve life for African-Americans.

      “It’s a great opportunity right now for the Republican Party to reach out and talk about ways that you empower people,” he said.

      Asked about the suggestion that Melania Trump’s speech on Monday night was partially plagiarized from Michelle Obama’s 2008 convention speech, Mr. Carson said journalists shouldn’t be so critical.

      “Instead of always looking for something negative to say, they should be saying, ‘Do you realize that Melania Trump and Michelle Obama have the same philosophy?’ ” he said. “That means something wonderful for America, that Democrats and Republicans can grasp and elevate the same ideas. That means there’s hope for us.”

      Also on hand at this gathering of black Republicans? Herman Cain, the Godfather’s Pizza magnate and 2012 candidate in the G.O.P. presidential primary. He emerged to tell reporters that he is happy for Mr. Trump’s success.

      “The voice of the people is what caused him to get the nomination for the Republican Party, period,” he said.

      Inside the event, an intimate group of black Republicans — including some who had not previously run for president or appeared on television shows produced by people running for president — milled about. I found Eugene Craig, a vice chairman of the Maryland Republican Party, whom I’d first met last year at a campaign event for Senator Rand Paul at a historically black college in Maryland.

      “Are you planning to vote for Donald Trump?” I asked him.

      “You know, I’m bound to support the Republican nominee. As the vice chair of the Maryland Republican Party, I’m bound to support the Republican nominee,” he said.

      “That doesn’t make you sound very enthusiastic,” I said.

      “Well, I will say this. There are some things I want to see out of Donald Trump, absolutely. I think he has to come out strongly in favor of criminal justice reform,” he responded, adding that he was heartened by Ms. Manigault’s addition to the Trump campaign.

      As Ben Carson would say, this is a wonderful opportunity for the Republican Party.

      Emma Roller (@EmmaRoller), a former reporter for National Journal, is a contributing opinion writer.
    7. Outwitting the Trump Brand
      By Elizabeth Williamson and Alex Thompson

      CLEVELAND — Donald Trump, whose fortune comes in large part from renting out his name, appears to be losing control of his brand to a horde of Cleveland souvenir hawkers.

      Mr. Trump earns money by putting his name on everything from hotels and casinos to steaks and wine. Street vendors in Cleveland earn money by putting Mr. Trump’s name on T-shirts, coolers, key chains, bobbleheads, sundresses and beer koozies.

      The official G.O.P. convention shops inside and outside the Quicken Loans Arena — but within the security perimeter — primarily sell hats and T-shirts with the official Donald Trump campaign logo. The Republican Party has tried to ban entrepreneurs from selling official-looking Trump merchandise, because the profits from the party’s own sales benefit its campaign fund.

      So much for Tuesday’s convention theme: “Make America Work Again.”

      The Future Female Leaders booth next to the arena can’t sell Trump merchandise, so it sells everything but. Its name began as a Twitter account, set up by Amanda Owens for conservative young women. The women in the booth, who wear red skirts printed with tiny elephants, do a brisk business in what might be called creative conservative apparel. The biggest sellers are tank tops with the slogans “Ain’t No Party Like the Grand Ole Party” and “PARTY like it’s 1776.”

      But you can’t keep a bootleg T-shirt vendor down for long. Walk farther from the arena and you will see people hawking T-shirts with the convention logo, as well Mr. Trump’s name and face. You can even buy apparel with the widely ridiculed, now-discontinued Trump-Pence logo.

      Delshaun Jackson sells his bootleg Trump T-shirts from a pop-up tent on Superior Avenue. But they’re not selling as well as his own design, a shirt that reads “RNC-CLE: I was here,” which he figures will be a big hit if riots break out.

      Mr. Jackson is no Trump supporter. He says the man is “so hateful that he’s running on sending everybody back across the border.” Besides, Mr. Jackson said, he did a lot better with Cleveland Cavaliers merchandise after they won the N.B.A. championship last month than he is doing with the famous Trump brand.

      Elizabeth Williamson is an Editorial Board writer and Alex Thompson is a news assistant.

    8. Try Plagiarism: It’s Really Underrated!
      By Andrew Rosenthal

      After a day of hearing everyone with a microphone handy talking about Melania Trump’s lifting of a passage from a speech by Michelle Obama, I had a tune running though my head.

      In 1953, Tom Lehrer, the Harvard mathematician turned satirical songwriter, wrote a song about a mathematician, Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky (you can listen to it here), in which he dealt with the thorny problem of plagiarism. Or, rather, he dispensed with it.

      Lobachevsky, it seems, said the secret to success at mathematics is to plagiarize. “Only be sure always to call it please … research.”

      What really jumped out after hearing Melania Trump was the line in the song about the immortal Lobachevsky’s first book. “Every chapter I stole from somewhere else,” the lyric goes. “Index I copy from old Vladivostok telephone directory.”

      That was 63 years ago. Seems like it was the final word.

      Andrew Rosenthal is an Op-Ed columnist for The Times.

    10. Watching the Convention as a Video Game
      By Virginia Heffernan

      The theme in Cleveland on Monday was safety, which meant speaker after speaker conjured obscure but urgent dangers from which a Trump administration would presumably save us. The assertion by the former Navy SEAL Marcus Luttrell that “your war is here” was a case in point. Another was Rudy Giuliani’s war whoop: “You know who you are, and we’re coming to get you!”

      This kind of saber-rattling and phobic speech may have seemed familiar to the snark-virtuosos on Twitch, the gigantic platform for players of (mostly) video games, which is streaming the convention this week, the company’s first foray into news and politics. Twitch considers the livestream a public service to “the millions of U.S. voters who call Twitch home,” most of whom, according to Quantcast, are childless Caucasian men under 24 living in lower-income households. The site’s chat section is known for insult comedy, bluster and bellicosity.

      A Twitter user called @FlakeJay instantly saw why a gaming site might be the right home for the Cleveland proceedings: “They’re livestreaming the R.N.C. on Twitch because our political system is a giant fighting game.” Some of the convention speakers did seem to strike the same note as apocalyptic video games. In fact, the intro to Overwatch, a recently released shooter game beloved of Twitch members — ”As the world teetered on the brink of anarchy, a new hope arose” — could have served as a paraphrase of Monday night’s proceedings.

      About 28,000 people tuned into the convention on Twitch on Monday, said Brian Petrocelli, the company’s product marketing manager. And they did not watch in silence. Memes, custom emoji, insider tropes and emphatic repetition are the lingua franca of Twitch, and apart from the occasional link to a news site, much of the chat was esoteric. Twitch closely monitored the chat for impropriety, Mr. Petrocelli said. He described the expurgated commentary as “clean-spirited and cheeky.”

      The house specialty at Twitch is demented threads of what seems like sleeptalking by a collective lizard brain. Around 7:30 p.m., while Melania Trump spoke at the convention in Cleveland, Twitch commenters on the convention were posting tone poetry like this: “BREXIT,” “EU GRILL,” “RIP CHAT,” “spy,” “SALT,” “THIRD WIFE,” “putins spy,” “d port,” “dis contree,” “Communism is bad says bimbo,” “lib mods.”

      “Lib mods,” perhaps, was a reproof to moderators seen to be deleting pro-Trump posts. But maybe it meant something else.

      At the same time, other Twitch members were invited to stream the convention on their own channels, and there they could host more pointedly ideological chats. On a channel run by rthe_donald, a Twitch member called Str8UpGankster riffed on Mr. Trump’s threat that his proposed wall be built higher each time the idea is criticized. “Next stop: The White House! The Trump train just got 10,000 m.p.h. faster!” “Crooked Hillary’s prison sentence just got 10 days longer!”

      The pugnacious language of Twitch is enlightening. It syncs up naturally with the scattershot rage in the convention site, the Quicken Loans Arena. Unlike the network pundits, the Twitch chatters didn’t try to force the off-kilter speeches to conform to reasoned analysis. Instead, they repaid the convention’s tone in coin.

      In the caricaturing lens of young gamers, for example, Ms. Trump, who was born in Slovenia, was cast reflexively as Evelyn Salt, the possible Russian mole and heroine of “Salt,” the 2010 thriller starring Angelina Jolie. Ms. Trump’s Eastern European inflections won no more subtle a response than “Commie!” and “Putin’s spy!”

      For those who wonder where the fury and fear at the convention comes from or how it registers with young voters, Twitch chat may be more useful than cautious commentary by professionals who don’t share it.

      Virginia Heffernan, a former television critic and internet columnist at The Times, is the author of “Magic and Loss: The Internet as Art.”

    11. #NeverTrump’s Last Fight
      By Ross Douthat

      As always with Donald Trump, one fiasco quickly overshadows its predecessor, so we’re talking today about Melania Trump plagiarizing Michelle Obama rather than the tumult on the convention floor late Monday afternoon, in which Reince Priebus and Co. put down a last-ditch foray by anti-Trump delegates with maximal hamfistedness and plain parliamentary abuse.

      The hamfistedness was unnecessary. The foray was always going to be defeated. The effort to dump Trump at the convention, while honorable, had exactly the same problems as the effort to stop him in the primaries.

      First, it lacked the necessary leadership. Like any coup plotters (just ask the Turks), the delegates who opposed Trump needed some support within the party apparatus for their effort to have any chance. There are plenty of notable anti-Trump Republicans — senators, governors, the party’s last two presidents and its most recent presidential nominee. But except for Senator Mike Lee, who led the floor fight yesterday, they were all far away from the convention. Meanwhile the party leaders who could have cracked the door to a coup — Priebus and Paul Ryan, above all — had made their peace with nominating an authoritarian buffoon. So nominate him they will.

      Second, the coup attempt lacked a clear and unifying alternative. This, too, was a leadership problem, because nobody wanted to play the part. (Ted Cruz is aiming for 2020, John Kasich is aiming for some sort of post-partisan holiness.) But even if someone had stepped up, they wouldn’t have been able to command the necessary support, because it was clear from the primaries that the party’s activists and establishmentarians prefer their long-running feud to unity against Trump’s hostile takeover.

      Here it’s fitting that the anti-Trump effort yesterday was entangled with an inside-baseball struggle over the design of the 2020 primaries. Even as Trump trashes the party, the establishment and the grass roots can’t quit their penny-ante battles, and the establishment especially can’t help itself from mobilizing against the Real Threat — which is always and forever Cruz.

      In the end, with the party this divided, the only imaginable path to a convention coup required both Cruz and Kasich to stay in the race even after Indiana, to continue to present themselves as candidates even in the face of a Trump delegate majority, and for them to work together to persuade leaders in the party apparatus to open the convention for a free vote. At which point they might have divided the not-Trump delegates between them across multiple ballots until, in exhaustion, their respective factions reached some kind of deal.

      In other words, the coup scenario required leading Republican politicians to show sustained courage and creativity for the sake of their party and their country, and other leading politicians to respond to Trump’s abnormal rise by abandoning party business as usual and leaping into the unknown.

      And a Republican Party that had such leaders would not have found itself in this position in the first place.

      Ross Douthat is an op-ed columnist for The Times.

    13. Rudy Giuliani’s Fear Factor
      By Andrew Rosenthal

      Way back in 1992, people were shocked when Pat Buchanan hijacked President George H. W. Bush’s re-election convention in Houston to declare war on those who didn’t share his right-wing Christian values.

      “There is a religious war going on in our country for the soul of America,” he said. “It is a cultural war, as critical to the kind of nation we will one day be as was the Cold War itself.”

      Buchanan was right in a way. That cultural and religious war has come to define American politics, for the worse. His speech showed the depths to which the Republican Party had fallen in practicing the dark art of the politics of fear and division. But the Republicans, it turned out, had a lot farther to fall.

      This week, I felt almost nostalgic for kinder, gentler Pat Buchanan as the Republicans opened a national convention that has adopted the most right-wing platform in history and will soon anoint the most unqualified candidate for president in memory.

      For President Bush in 1992, fear and division were a sideshow. Now they are the main event.

      On the convention’s opening night, Rudy Giuliani, the former mayor of New York, gave a speech that was raw anger and hatred. Giuliani’s tirade swamped whatever Trump hoped to accomplish by having his wife talk about what a wonderful guy he is.

      Waving his arms and shouting frequently, Giuliani declared: “We must not be afraid to define our enemy. It is Islamic extremist terrorism.”

      The former mayor, who has turned the tragedy of Sept. 11 into a career, was picking up on a favorite Republican theme — that not using the phrase he used or an alternative, “radical Islamic terrorism,” somehow suggests a lack of commitment to combating terrorism.

      It’s absurd, of course, but the idea is to attack the “other” and still claim there is no bigotry in doing so.

      “Failing to identify them properly maligns decent Muslims around the world,” Giuliani said, without explaining how, exactly. “It also sets up a fear of being politically incorrect that can have serious consequences.”

      Trump likes to make the same claim that people who object to his attacks on Mexicans, Muslims, women and others are being “politically correct.” That is not political correctness. It is calling out bigotry and racism for what they are.

      Giuliani attacked Hillary Clinton over the Obama administration’s decision to help depose the Libyan dictator Muammar el-Qaddafi. He said, rightly, that Libya is now in chaos. But he conveniently left out that President George W. Bush’s decision to wage war against Saddam Hussein and Iraq was the gigantic blunder that set up most of the subsequent horrors in that part of the world.

      Giuliani’s speech led nowhere in terms of actual policies or ideas, only to this rather chilling statement: “If they are at war against us — which they have declared — we must commit ourselves to unconditional victory against them.”

      I can think of a couple of conditions: Basic human decency and traditional American democratic values.

      Giuliani’s speech was effective. It whipped up the crowd in the convention hall and, no doubt, Trump supporters watching on television.

      It just would have been better delivered from a balcony, or at the head of a torch-bearing mob.

      Andrew Rosenthal is an op-ed columnist for The Times

    15. Populism Without Racism
      By David Leonhardt

      Let’s take a break from plagiarism and look ahead to Tuesday night’s convention. As the Republicans prepare for a night devoted to the economy, it is worth debunking the myth that the party has been doing better lately with working-class and lower-income voters.

      Not true. President Obama trounced Mitt Romney in 2012 among households making less than $50,000 — roughly 60 percent to 40 percent, according to exit polls. It was a larger margin than the one by which Bill Clinton won the under-$50,000 vote in 1996. And Hillary Clinton leads Donald Trump today among under-$50,000 households, doing better than she is among higher-income voters.

      Why the confusion, then? Republicans have indeed improved their performance among white working-class voters. These voters may fit the classic blue-collar stereotype, but the stereotype becomes a little less accurate every year. Whites make up a shrinking part of the working class, just as they make up a shrinking part of the country. Even as Republicans have fared better with working-class whites in recent years — thanks mostly to shifts in the South, as Nate Cohn of The Times has noted — the party has fared worse with other groups.

      The main thing I’ll be watching for tonight is whether Republican are in the early stages of grasping this situation.

      Tuesday’s lineup of speakers — under the theme “Make America Work Again” — is certainly eclectic. It includes a soap-opera star, the president of Ultimate Fighting Championship and the general manager of Trump Winery. Sprinkled among the entertainment, however, will be many top Republicans like Paul Ryan, Mitch McConnell and Kevin McCarthy. They will each offer clues about whether Republicans understand the problem that Donald Trump has exposed.

      Namely, there is a large gap between the party’s recent agenda and the needs of working-class voters. (For more details, see Ross Douthat and Reihan Salam’s cri de conservatism.) The agenda hasn’t had a very good record of lifting incomes over the past 25 years. Perhaps in reaction, many Republican voters this year preferred Trump’s economic heresy — inchoate as it may have been — on issues like trade and Social Security.

      Of course, Trump’s populism has come wrapped in racial appeals: “Mexican rapists,” “my African-American,” the Muslim ban, China’s “rape” of “our country,” the online anti-Semitism that he has not disavowed. That makes it hard to know whether the populism or the racism has played a bigger role in Trump’s nomination victory.

      It’s tempting for many liberals to believe that conservative populism must necessarily be tinged with race. But it’s wrong, as Douthat and Salam note. There is no shortage of ways that Republicans can try to appeal to working-class voters of all races — starting with tax cuts for the nonrich, such as a payroll-tax cut.

      Tonight, I’ll be looking for signs that any leading Republicans are intrigued by such a shift. The happy reality is that race-based appeals are likely to become less and less effective as a national strategy, thanks to the country’s increasingly diverse population. But it’s also a complicated reality for a Republican Party that has long won votes through cultural appeals to white America while pursuing trickle-down economics.

      For those Republicans who have one eye on a post-Trump future, the question is which parts of Trumpism to keep.

      David Leonhardt, who will begin an Op-Ed column later this year, is writing a daily Opinion post during the conventions.

    18. Why the Melania-Michelle Echo Matters
      By Frank Bruni

      CLEVELAND – We may never know how consciously the author of certain sentences in Melania Trump’s speech on Monday night used language from Michelle Obama’s convention remarks eight years ago. Plagiarism sometimes happens by accident.

      But the unmistakable echo of Obama’s words in Trump’s is a bad turn for her husband and his campaign. Below are four explanations why, along with one reason that it falls well shy of a disaster.

      If Donald Trump can’t manage the details of a campaign or convention, how can he run the country?

      Trump is constantly telling us how incompetent everyone who’s been steering the government is and how masterfully he’ll take the wheel. He’s incessantly crowing about what a brilliant business he manages.

      But his campaign keeps contradicting that. Aides come and go. He steps on his message and sullies what should be his best days. He badly bungles the rollout of his vice presidential pick, turning a potentially big media moment into the source of eye rolling and laughter.

      And now this. In a normal, thorough, disciplined political operation, someone is scouring all the major speakers’ scripts for any potential landmines, and plagiarism is a biggie. In a normal, thorough, disciplined political operation, the person helping a speaker of Melania’s stature prepare his or her remarks has a level of professionalism that greatly diminishes the possibility of what happened.

      Trump is once again cast as the disengaged captain of a ragtag, amateurish team.

      It becomes the prism through which the rest of the convention is seen.

      Trump’s many detractors and the thousands of journalists chronicling this convention were on watch for proof that Trump’s coronation had been cobbled together sloppily and at the last minute, and the hubbub over the speech—which was the climax of the event’s opening night—confirmed such a narrative.

      That watch now becomes a high alert.

      It throws Trump and his senior aides even further off balance and off script during a week when they should be projecting utter command.

      This wasn’t just any speaker. This was Trump’s wife. The derision being heaped on her remarks is something that will burrow deep under his tenderly sensitive skin. It will turn an already-defensive candidate even more so. And it will leave his aides fumbling for the smartest response.

      That fumbling was evident early Tuesday morning, when Paul Manafort, Trump’s de facto campaign manager, wholly dismissed the talk of plagiarism, saying: “This is once again an example of how, when a woman threatens Hillary Clinton, she tries to demean her and take her down.”

      There is no sign whatsoever that Clinton had a hand in the detection of strangely familiar lines in Melania’s speech, and the assertion that Clinton feels threatened by Melania is somewhat less than persuasive.

      It’s bound to rattle a surrogate who was already a hesitant recruit.

      Melania has been less visible and vocal than presidential nominees’ spouses typically are, a clear sign of some discomfort at the microphone.

      After Monday night, that discomfort is bound to increase, and it could become more difficult than ever for the Trump campaign to turn to her as a surrogate who can cover some of the ground that her husband can’t.

      It’s just a few sentences on one night by a person who’s not asking to be trusted with the nuclear codes.

      Journalists’ relentless focus Tuesday morning on Melania’s speech could quickly come to look like overkill to voters who aren’t so fond of the media in first place. They may wind up being more troubled by what they deem the bullying of Melania than by any ineptness in the Trump campaign.

      Besides which, there are three more nights of developments to overtake this one, and three and half months of cutthroat campaigning that’ll turn it into a distant memory.


      I invite you to follow me on Twitter at twitter.com/frankbruni (@FrankBruni) and join me on Facebook.

    20. There’s Something Going On With Melania’s Speech
      By Elizabeth Williamson

      CLEVELAND – It was odd enough that Slovenian-born Melania Trump’s tribute to her husband and the value of her foreign upbringing was the crowning moment of an evening otherwise devoted to murders of children by immigrants and the ever-present threat of foreign-born terrorism.

      But Mrs. Trump’s speech during the Republican National Convention’s opening night seemed, pundits said immediately afterward, a bit remote. Mrs. Trump mentioned earlier in the day that she wrote it herself, but it was so devoid of personal detail that it could have been about anyone’s family. Turns out, some of it was from Michelle Obama’s family.

      Jarrett Hill, an unemployed journalist watching Mrs. Trump’s speech on his computer in a Los Angeles Starbucks, had a Eureka moment.

      He’d been inspired by some of these same passages in Michelle Obama’s 2008 convention speech. So Mr. Hill looked up the speech and started tweeting.

      Mr. Hill’s tweets went viral, retweeted more times than Mr. Trump’s reaction to his wife’s speech. At about 2 a.m. The Trump campaign issued this statement:

      “In writing her beautiful speech, Melania’s team of writers took notes on her life’s inspirations, and in some instances included fragments that reflected her own thinking. Melania’s immigrant experience and love for America shone through in her speech, which made it such a success. -Jason Miller, Senior Communications Advisor.”

      It’s a shame Mrs. Trump’s team of writers didn’t dig deeper into history. The Trump convention’s dark and ominous tone last night has prompted comparisons between Mr. Trump and Richard Nixon. Mr. Nixon’s wife Pat was the first potential First Lady to make a formal convention speech on behalf of her spouse, in 1972. By using fragments of Mrs. Nixon’s thinking as a template for Mrs. Trump’s “life’s inspirations,” at least Mrs. Trump’s speech writers would have been humanizing a guy from the same party. And Mrs. Nixon’s 44 year-old speech is tougher for enterprising fact-checkers like Mr. Hill to find on the web.

      Elizabeth Williams is an Editorial Board writer.

    22. The Power of Plagiarized Words
      By Judith Shulevitz

      The cheap joke about the apparent plagiarism in Melania Trump’s speech is that once again, a white woman has done a cover of a black woman’s original material. It’s a theft, but also the sincerest form of flattery, underscoring how Michelle Obama has become a first lady worthy of imitation.

      In cases like this, it’s useful to compare the contexts in which the words appear. One difference jumps out: Mrs. Obama’s words have been proven to be true, while the truth of Ms. Trump’s remains to be seen. Eight years in office have shown that the Obamas’ parents did teach them to treat people with dignity and respect even when they disagree with them. Ms. Trump’s parents may have taught her the same thing, but she has never publicly objected to the low, nasty tone of the Trump campaign.

      But the parallels in the circumstances in which the two speeches were given are even more instructive. When Mrs. Obama and Ms. Trump stood up to speak on the first nights of their husbands’ conventions, they both had to convince the American public that they were fit to be first lady. Mrs. Obama had to overcome her image as an angry black woman scornful of America. She had to find a way to take back her confession that, as her husband won primary after primary, “For the first time in my adult life I am proud of my country.”

      For her part, Ms. Trump had to show that she, a rather reserved woman despite her modeling background, could speak without hesitation to a national audience. She had to be forceful, but not too forceful. She had to serve as an advocate for her husband but also, as first ladies are supposed to do, soften his harshness.

      Both women had to establish their very American, up-by-their-bootstraps credentials. In 2008, reporters cited the very lines that Ms. Trump seemed to have taken from Mrs. Obama as proof that her story was one of blue-collar origins, hard work and essential decency, not of Ivy League privilege. (Both Obamas had been mocked for their elite educations; Mrs. Obama graduated from Princeton.)

      For her part, Ms. Trump also had to show that she was a child of poverty, not privilege, however rich she may be now.

      Both women did what they had come to do, at least at first. And that, too, is notable, proving the power of those plagiarized words.

      Judith Shulevitz (@JudithShulevitz) is the author of “The Sabbath World: Glimpses of a Different Order of Time” and a contributing opinion writer.
