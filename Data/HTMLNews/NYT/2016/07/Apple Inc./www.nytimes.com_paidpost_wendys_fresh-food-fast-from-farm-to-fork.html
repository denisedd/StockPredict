    PAID FOR AND POSTED BY WENDY’S

    Fresh Food Fast

    From Farm to Fork

    As the sun rises over the long, hooped rows of blackberry bushes on John Navarro’s farm, there is little sign in the stillness of what’s to come.

    Within minutes, the 65-acre family-owned ranch in Watsonville, California, bustles with dozens of workers, moving from bush to bush, busily picking blackberries.

    From there, boxes of berries are whisked away on flatbed trucks to be meticulously checked for quality, cooled to the perfect temperature and then finally loaded onto refrigerated trucks to head to their final destination: atop a heap of lettuce, strawberries, white-meat chicken and feta cheese on Wendy’s Summer Berry Chicken Salad.

    The blackberries – and the salad they’re featured in – reflect the Dublin, Ohio-based restaurant chain’s commitment to using fresh, seasonal ingredients. The sweet and savory salad – which includes red apple chips and light blackberry vinaigrette and clocks in a diet-respecting 390 calories – took more than two years to come to fruition.

    John and Peter Navarro

    John Navarro and his brother, Peter, independent growers based in Watsonville, California, adjusted their crop to help California Giant meet Wendy’s unprecedented request for fresh, seasonal blackberries. Growing berries for Wendy’s seasonal salad, he said, has had a “huge” impact on the family-owned business.

    “We have the satisfaction of growing a product we know already has an outlet,” says John, whose father and uncle came to the U.S. from Mexico in the 1950s and, in the early ‘60s, became Watsonville’s first Hispanic growers to own their berry farm. “As a grower, that’s your main worry.”

    “Consumers are smarter about their food and more demanding of great flavors and high-quality ingredients; food that’s grown with great credentials,” says Wendy’s Chief Communications Officer Liliana Esposito. “Bringing quality food and flavors to consumers, whether that’s in a salad or on a sandwich, has been a core of Wendy’s vision since day one.”

    The new seasonal salad is an example of the burgeoning farm-to-fork, fast-food trend. It’s getting big these days as diners increasingly expect fresh, quality ingredients.

    Alissa Rumsey, a spokesperson for the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, said the trend of people “realizing the benefits of fresh, high-quality, seasonal food “extends beyond the grocery aisles and into restaurants.” 

    As a result, she says restaurants increasingly are putting an emphasis on “serving seasonal fruits and vegetables, whole grains and quality protein sources.” 

    Restaurants like Wendy’s, that focus on providing fresh, quality ingredients, are attracting customers who increasingly are scrutinizing their on-the-go food choices. 

    “I think it’s very important to eat things as fresh as possible,” says Jenny McCoy, a Brooklyn, New York, pastry chef and author who says she makes sure to choose restaurants that serve fresh, seasonal ingredients “because the flavor is so much better.”

    Prospero Lopez Diaz

    Harvesters like Prospero Lopez Diaz begin work on the Navarros’ ranch at 6 a.m. Diaz has been picking berries for eight years. He says that, while speed is important, picking berries with care is paramount. Every flat of blackberries can be traced back to the worker who picked them.

    “It’s quality control. From the moment that berry’s starting to ripen you want to get it in the box at the optimal time,” independent grower John Navarro says. “It’s all based on our harvesters’ skill — they know which berry goes in the box.”

    Other restaurant chains may be scrambling to catch up with consumer demand, but Wendy’s commitment to quality and freshness has been ingrained in the chain since the beginning. When Wendy’s launched in 1969, founder Dave Thomas was determined to use only fresh – never frozen – North American beef in Wendy’s hamburgers. “We’ve never changed that,” says Joe Loiacano, Wendy’s Quality Supply Chain Co-op, Inc. senior purchasing manager for fresh and agriculture-related products. 

    But because fresh beef “requires a significantly quicker turn” than the frozen patties many competitors use, Loiacano explains, Wendy’s laid out a distribution network to ensure deliveries multiple times a week to each of its restaurants. 

    Wendy’s restaurants order only enough ingredients to get them through a few days, until the next delivery arrives. This ensures that all food is served fresh and at peak quality.

    That frequency of delivery and fresh-food supply chain has evolved with the company. It also has enabled Wendy’s to use fresh produce – such as romaine and iceberg lettuce, tomatoes and berries – in its more than 5,500 restaurants in the United States. The produce is delivered whole and then washed, cut and prepared by hand onsite. 

    “Really it comes down to the core value that hangs on every Wendy’s restaurant in the world, which is that ‘quality is our recipe,’” Esposito says. 

    The focus on freshness has enabled Wendy’s to take a pioneering role in rolling out everything from freshly prepared salads (it was the first fast-food restaurant to offer its customers a salad bar in 1979) to baked potatoes (which it launched as a french fry alternative in 1983) to seasonal salads with straight-from-the-farm strawberries in 2011.  

    Karim Elsayed

    Every morning, the restaurant crew arrives early at Wendy’s to wash berries, chop lettuce and prepare produce for customers’ meals. Produce is delivered multiple times a week to the more than 5,500 Wendy’s restaurants and arrives “in a fresh state, whether that’s a whole head of lettuce or a whole tomato,” says the company’s chief communications officer, Liliana Esposito.

    “Our salads are made every day in every Wendy’s restaurant with hand-sliced lettuce, tomatoes and strawberries,” Esposito says. Blackberries, however, “are included whole.”

    Ensuring freshness isn’t easy. Take, for instance, the decision by Wendy’s to feature blackberries, along with strawberries, in this summer’s seasonal salad. 

    Initially, when Wendy’s research and development team came up with the recipe for the Summer Berry Chicken Salad more than two years ago, there was great excitement internally. 

    After all, customers had responded with overwhelming positivity to its two previous summer salads that featured strawberries: Berry Almond Salad and Strawberry Fields Salad. And early tests that were conducted in the spring and summer of 2014 in Buffalo, N.Y., Shreveport, La., and Salt Lake City, Utah, showed that customers would equally enjoy blackberries in their salad. 

    But the team realized that sourcing the fresh berries would be a big problem: There simply weren’t enough fresh, high-quality blackberries available from North American growers to meet the estimated 106 million blackberries Wendy’s would need.

    And finding blackberries to meet the demands of more than 5,500 restaurants is no easy feat, Loiacano says: “In addition to the quantity, the quality of the berry also has to meet our specs on size and how it was grown. We’re invested in quality standards of these berries from the day they are planted. ”

    Given the brand’s commitment to quality, Loiacano told the Wendy’s team that getting enough blackberries that meet the company’s size and taste specifications would take at least another year. The nationwide launch of the salad, which was initially slated for the summer of 2015, would have to be delayed.  

    Then, after a more than two-year process, Loiacano and his team found enough berries of Wendy’s quality to meet the company’s volume needs by working with several different blackberry growers. 

    These growers were willing to adjust their crops by pruning them so they produced fruit at the right time and by planting new blackberry bushes. But they then had to wait a year for those bushes to mature so that, finally, Wendy’s could provide customers with another industry first – the first fresh, seasonal blackberry salad from a quick-service restaurant that takes advantage of one of the great tastes of summer.

    ​

    Cindy Jewell, vice president of marketing for California Giant, a family-owned Watsonville, California, berry company that worked with its growers to meet Wendy’s blackberry requirements, says the willingness of its independent growers to adjust their crop for a single customer was unprecedented.

    The upside for growers, she says, will extend beyond this summer: “Once people see blackberries in a Wendy’s salad, the writing’s on the wall that it’s going to be a new consumer trend and that there’s going to be a higher consumer demand for blackberries moving forward.”

    Wendy’s customers may not be aware of all the work that went into getting the fresh, seasonal ingredients for the Wendy’s Summer Berry Chicken Salad – from the farmers who plant the blackberry bushes to the pickers who harvest them to the restaurant workers who wash the berries, chop the lettuce and place a warm, white-meat chicken breast on top. 

    But Esposito says when they try the new, limited-time-only salad, with its great-taste-of-summer berries, they’ll surely enjoy the fruits of all that labor.
