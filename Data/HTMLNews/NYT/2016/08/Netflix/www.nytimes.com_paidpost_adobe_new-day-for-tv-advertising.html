    New Day for TV Advertising

    Marketers find ways to reach TV viewers who skip commercials

    Marketers find ways to reach TV viewers who skip commercials

    Debbie Molesworth is proof that TV ads can still grab the attention of people who do everything they can to avoid them. Molesworth watches mostly commercial-free programming, including Netflix, DVDs and On-Demand TV. She uses a DVR to fast forward past ads when she watches recorded shows. And she checks social media on her smartphone when commercials pop up during regular programming.

    Yet, during a week’s worth of shark shows on the Discovery Channel, Molesworth was drawn to a Dunkin’ Donuts ad that featured a guy in a shark costume asking viewers to post photos on social media. Molesworth went out and bought a half-dozen doughnuts before posting a photo of herself indulging in a doughy pastry to Twitter with the hashtag #DDSharkWeek.

    “I’m not someone who eats doughnuts at all,” Molesworth said. But the ad piqued her interest anyway because it was relevant to people who are interested in the surrounding shark programming and it encouraged them to actually engage with the commercial’s content.

    Despite the fear among major network execs and marketers that TV viewership is dying, people are still watching TV. In fact, although TV watching is slowly declining, American adults still watch an average of over five hours each day, according to Nielsen Research.

    But there is a more pressing threat for advertisers: Consumers have changed the way they watch TV. People increasingly are using streaming services and other technologies to skip or ignore the 30-second commercial that has become the cornerstone of TV advertising since Bulova aired the first paid spot in 1941.

    When it comes to skipping video advertisements, 29 percent report that they do so because they are simply not interested in the product, according to a recent survey by Adobe Digital Insights, the research arm of Adobe.

    These trends have made it clear that advertisers must find other ways to get their message through to consumers. As a result, companies are making ads that are more relevant to consumers and more effective for marketers.

    Campbell Foster, director of product marketing at the technology company Adobe, said companies are urgently trying to find ways to reach ad-adverse viewers with fun, creative and personalized content.

    Making Ads More Relevant

    What’s one big way in which TV advertisers are trying to reach consumers who are increasingly avoiding commercials? They’re working harder to make sure their ads are more relevant to specific groups of people so that they’re reaching the viewers who are most likely to be interested in their brands and/or products.

    In fact, they’re using the very same digital technologies that are changing viewing habits to target consumers – just like they do online. For instance, some set-top boxes have Internet-like targeting capabilities that give advertisers the data they need to focus their ads on specific demographics.

    Here’s how it works: Companies target audiences, using variables such as income, ethnicity and household size. Then, they work with cable providers to make sure their ads go to those so-called “addressable” homes in their target.

    This is a growing area for advertisers. Last year, set-top boxes representing about a third of American households were “household addressable.”

    In theory, this technology gives advertisers the chance to show ads that are more relevant to customers. For instance, when a car company did an addressable TV ad for its luxury sedan, it combined sales and other data to choose 1.7 million households — ones that had a car-buying rate of 60 percent higher than the general population — to view the ads.

    The ad worked. The car-buying rate for those households was more than 60 percent higher than that of a demographically similar group who did not see the ads.

    These advancements in digital marketing have enabled advertisers to do a better job of targeting their ads, and consumers have noticed. Another survey by Adobe Digital Insights revealed that 46 percent of consumers think that advertisers have gotten better over the past two years at showing ads that are valuable or compelling.

    More Engaging Ads

    Even when marketers aren’t able to target a specific group with their ads, they’re making sure the content of their commercials is generally more engaging to viewers.

    Some are integrating their brand and products – and sometimes the commercials themselves – into regular programming.

    In a unique move, Pepsi struck a deal with Fox last season to give its flagship drink a starring, repeat role in a three-episode plot of the popular prime-time drama Empire. As part of the story line, a character on the show participated in a competition before landing an endorsement deal with Pepsi.

    The deal included a commercial that played off of the story line, starring the singing character drinking a soda in a New York City subway on his way to perform at a concert.

    S. Adam Brasel, an associate professor of marketing at Boston College, said that this is an example of smart marketers knowing that it’s necessary to look for seamless, relevant ways to integrate their brands into programming. Viewers will be turned off otherwise, he said.

    “You don’t want viewers to feel like they’re being beat over the head with advertising,” Brasel said.

    Other companies are making TV commercials that are more like entertainment — and less like advertising — with the same high-quality production and celebrity-headlined experiences that draw viewers to traditional TV programming.

    Among the other major differences in this new brand of ad: They’re designed to just bring overall brand awareness. In fact, the ads don’t always have a direct connection to the company’s products. And in most cases, they’re designed to hold viewers’ attention much longer than 30 seconds.

    For example, GRAMMY Awards viewers last year got treated to a live, four-minute Imagine Dragons performance during a commercial break. The rock band’s performance was a TV ad by Target Corp., but it didn’t promote any products.

    And earlier this year, Target produced a similar ad, featuring singer Gwen Stefani. The ad, which aired during a four-minute commercial break, was the first music video created live on TV.

    Still, other companies are making their content more entertaining by actually getting consumers to engage with the ad. They’re doing this with more cross-channel promotions that include several different platforms, including TV and social media, to engage viewers on all of their screens. The Dunkin’ Donuts ad is an example of this.

    This is smart for advertisers. It’s an opportunity to reach the growing number of viewers who are distracted while commercials are on because they’re using multiple devices while watching TV. In fact, according to the Deloitte Digital Democracy Survey, Millennials are doing an average of four additional activities while watching TV, including surfing the web, text-messaging and checking social media.

    “Everyone’s media multi-tasking these days,” Brasel said. “Everyone is watching TV and having their smartphone or computer out.”

    The Future: Ads You Don't Mind

    TV advertising is starting to look more like web advertising, with more advanced software and data-mining technology that affords advertisers the ability to tailor ads to a more specific group of customers. And by all accounts, it will continue to move in that direction.

    As the way people view TV continues to shift, experts say it will be more imperative for advertisers to continue to work to reach the best customers for their products on any screen the consumers are watching.

    Indeed, the youngest generations of viewers – and consumers – continue to change their viewing patterns. The Deloitte survey found that 19-to-32-year-olds estimate that they spend more of their TV content time watching streaming video than watching live programming. And that time spent watching commercial-free subscription services over live TV is only expected to increase.

    Advertisers that want to reach their customers will have to continue to think of creative ways to get their brand messages in front of them. For TV viewers, that will mean more advertising that is fun and entertaining, not to mention more likely to be the products, services and brands that they already like. In the long run, experts say the change will make advertising more enjoyable for viewers.
