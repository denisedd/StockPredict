    How digital technology is changing the sports viewing game

    How digital technology is changing the sports viewing game

    Kayla Harrison distinguished herself as a serious athlete in middle school. In her teens, she rose to the top of her sport in the U.S. By 22, she became the first American to win an Olympic gold medal in judo.

    Despite her accolades, the Middletown, Ohio, native hasn’t been very visible in her home country because most judo matches aren’t aired on TV in the U.S.

    But technology is quickly changing that: Judo is among the 41 sports of the summer games in Rio de Janeiro that people are able to watch anytime, anywhere, on almost any device. And while fans are watching judo matches, they’ll be able to view Harrison’s stats on sports apps like NBC Olympics and NBC Sports Live Extra.

    The summer games are the latest examples of how sports TV watching is going digital, completely transforming into an immersive viewing experience. Websites, apps and technologies like TV Everywhere — a term for the authenticated viewing of broadcast shows from channels people subscribe to on cable or satellite networks — enable fans to have a deeper, more engaged and interactive experience with sports.

    The new technologies also enable people to discover and become rabid fans of sports that they previously didn’t have access to, like archery and canoeing. And that means they’re interacting more with the athletes of those sports.

    Take judo, for instance. Since the sport’s matches aired on multiple devices during the summer games in 2012, Harrison’s fan base has grown. She now has over 15,000 Twitter followers and nearly 21,000 Instagram followers.

    “The greater visibility, more TV time and the more athletes who become household names, the better for the sport,” Harrison said.

    This year, NBC will broadcast and/or live stream 6,755 hours of the summer games in Rio on desktops, smartphones, tablets and connected TVs.

    The world’s stage

    The game has changed for TV sports broadcasting. Previously, the network that pays for the rights to broadcast the summer games would bolster ratings by airing only the most popular events for each country.

    This year, for the second time in history, NBC will broadcast and/or stream 6,755 hours of live coverage of the summer games across desktops, smartphones, tablets and connected TVs. Sports fans can see all the events in the summer games, not just the popular ones.

    It’s not just a win for TV watchers. It’s a win for networks, too.

    The move to digital has boosted viewership. Overall, the summer games in London attracted 4 billion global viewers over 17 days. In the U.S., there were 219.4 million TV viewers — the most ever.

    Along the way, some sports achieved newfound popularity. At one point, an average of 1.5 million people were watching archery competitions on cable during daytime hours. That was larger than the audience that tuned in to any other sport that was aired on cable at that time, including basketball.

    The viewership gains show the potential for technologies like TV Everywhere apps, including WatchESPN. Those apps are fast approaching mainstream use. This authenticated TV viewing encourages people to keep their cable subscriptions by allowing them to stream the TV they already pay for.

    TV Everywhere is rapidly changing viewership habits and the way people consume content, according to a report by Adobe. In fact, TV Everywhere viewership has more than doubled in the past year, up by 107 percent year over year, according to the report, which was compiled by Adobe Digital Insights, the research arm of Adobe.

    “Technology has the power to connect consumers to the sports they love, yet many media companies are challenged with providing people with a personalized and engaging viewing experience across screens,” said Campbell Foster, Adobe’s director of product marketing.

    Everyone is watching

    Technology that makes it possible to watch sports content on multiple devices has created new sports fans. And if the fans are any indication, sports like judo will continue to grow in popularity.

    Sam Carter, a sales manager at Odyssey Marketing Group in Cheyenne, Wyoming, became a huge fan of judo because of the technology that’s enabled him to watch judo matches. He's planning to watch the judo matches in Rio, too.

    “I am guilty of creating a cult following at our business for Olympic judo,” he said. “At the office in the break room, I would watch Olympic judo matches. Next thing I knew, they were hooked just like me.”

    Fans aren’t the only ones taking notice of the newfound celebrity of athletes in up-and-coming events, such as weightlifting and trampoline. Marketers should be, too, experts say.

    Since many people will be watching from their mobile phones or tablets, with some simultaneously watching TV, marketing experts say it gives companies a big opportunity to do some creative marketing.

    To be sure, the trend toward viewing sports events across multiple screens makes it more complex for companies to find effective ways to reach customers. As a result, some companies will need to use multi-platform marketing strategies to target them.

    It’s essential that marketers consider this because people aren’t just watching on their set-top boxes anymore. In fact, the Adobe Digital Insights study found that 40 percent of U.S. consumers are watching sports on mobile devices.

    AS TV GOES, MARKETERS GO

    Marketing experts say that companies are starting to find ways to take advantage of the 360-degree viewing of sports.

    For instance, during the 2012 summer games, a mobile-phone company created a campaign to help fans stay keyed-in through several technologies while watching the games. It created an app that gave fans updates on events, including schedules and winners. It also had a Facebook app that let fans see what others were saying about the games on social media. Additionally, a blogger at the company posted updates.

    Marketing experts say that smart marketers will think of interesting ways not only to take advantage of the more immersive sports-viewing experience, but also to target fans of newly popular sports.

    “So many companies want to have a dog in the race,” said Carlos Scott, who owns a sports marketing and public relations firm that represents several gold-medal athletes. “With the advent of apps and technology, there are companies that could create some serious buzz, even with something like trampoline.”

    Jerry Solomon, a Boston-based sports agent who’s represented big names in figure skating and tennis and now represents Harrison, said marketers can do more with top athletes in newly popular sports. He said those sports don’t have the parameters that are sometimes in place in other sports, so companies can think of marketing ideas that have never been done before.

    “In a sport like judo, you sort of have a blank canvas you can work with to create things that are new and different,” Solomon said. “You can be more creative.”

    A DIGITAL FUTURE FOR SPORTS

    As digital technology improves and viewers have access to more ways to watch sports, the rise in viewership of all sports — and newly popular sports, in particular — will likely continue.

    Digital will not only continue to drive viewership of the summer games. It will also boost fan engagement by providing more content and ways for enthusiasts to participate in the sports they love.

    “Consumers are always finding ways to watch what they want, when they want,” said Adobe’s Foster.

    The ability to watch sports wherever and whenever is good for fans and the sports they love. It’s also beneficial for the top athletes in sports that are gaining popularity because they are getting more recognition.

    The growing interest in judo has been good for Harrison, who overcame adversity as a teen to rise to the top of her sport. She now runs an organization that helps abused children and says that judo’s greater visibility in the U.S. has meant a lot to her as well as to the sport.

    “As long as you have a good message and a good product — in this case, it’s me and the sport — I think the opportunities will come, especially if you are getting more visibility,” Harrison said.
