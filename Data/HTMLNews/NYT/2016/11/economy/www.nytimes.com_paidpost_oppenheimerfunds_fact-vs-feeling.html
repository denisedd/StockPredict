    Fact vs Feeling

    Know Your Emotions Today, for a Better Tomorrow

    Wake up on the wrong side of the bed this morning?

    If you did, you might end up settling for a lot less throughout your day. This is because your feelings often dictate your short-term decision making.

    Dr. Jennifer Lerner, professor of public policy and management at Harvard, has studied how emotions can affect financial decision making. In her work, Dr. Lerner has found that compared to those who’ve recently experienced emotions like disgust or neutrality, those primed for sadness by watching a somber video were more likely to pay higher prices and be more impatient when it came to choosing lower cash rewards in the near term opposed to greater rewards in the long term.

    Lerner’s conclusions suggest key insights into how emotions affect our financial decisions. Her anecdotal accounts of the experiments reveal something else, too. It’s perhaps not only the emotion itself that leads toward poor decision making, but our inability to recognize how those emotions affect us.

    "In general, people have very little awareness of how emotions affect their decisions."

    Lerner’s conclusions suggest key insights into how emotions affect our financial decisions. Her anecdotal accounts of the experiments reveal something else, too. It’s perhaps not only the emotion itself that leads toward poor decision making, but our inability to recognize how those emotions affect us.

    “We’ll ask participants afterward, ‘Is it possible that your feelings from that movie influenced how much you paid for this coffee mug?’” Lerner says. “They say, ‘No. Don’t be silly. That would be irrational.’ They absolutely, firmly deny — and are even offended — that we ask about any kind of carryover (of their emotions from the video). In general, people have very little awareness of how emotions affect their decisions.”

    When it comes to long-term investing, where emotions are proven to have a very real impact on our financial health, that can be a disquieting thought. To address these conflicting forces of emotion — the way we think we feel versus what we actually feel below the surface — OppenheimerFunds embarked on an unprecedented study, the Optimist Index, to use biometrics not only to identify this disparity but to demonstrate how remaining optimistic (and being aware of it) is often a safer path to financial stability in the long run.

    Emotions and Long‑Term Investing

    In recent years, the topic of decision making has been one of great interest for those working in and crossing over among a variety of fields of academic study: economics, neuroscience, psychology, biology and now biometrics. Known as behavioral economics or neuroeconomics, scientists are gaining greater and more profound insights into how emotions function in determining our courses of action and, more specifically, what we do with our money. While individual conclusions may differ, most agree on a simple premise: There’s no escaping our emotions — sooner or later they will affect how our brains and bodies make decisions.

    “You can’t make a decision without first experiencing some kind of emotional impulse in your brain,” says Dr. Lisa Kramer, who specializes in behavioral finance at the University of California San Diego, Rady School of Management, and at the University of Toronto, Rotman School of Management. “We need to step back from the idea that we can even make decisions in the absence of emotion. I think it’s more important to try to understand the way in which emotions do influence decisions, and then try to put in safeguards to prevent our emotions from causing us to make a catastrophic mistake in our portfolios.”

    To further understand these influences, Rom Brafman, psychologist and co-author of “Sway: The Irresistible Pull of Irrational Behavior,” gives the following example: On a recommendation from his financial advisor, Brafman recently invested in new mutual funds. After checking his account, he saw that his investment was going in the wrong direction. Immediately, Brafman says, his mind started racing.

    “I saw that I was down a couple hundred dollars and automatically started having bad feelings about my financial advisor — should I stop listening to him?” Brafman says. He had to remind himself that this was a long-term investment, not a one-off bet. “I had to say to myself, ‘Rom, you have to understand that over the long haul it’s probably a good investment. You shouldn’t be judging this on a day-by-day basis.’”

    Indeed, humans have evolved to think and see patterns more in the short term than the long term. We feel losses much more profoundly than we feel gains. In fact, the emotional impacts of a loss are felt, on average, two and half times more profoundly than the same quantity of gains, says Brafman.

    “Instead of recognizing an overall pattern, and knowing that you need to stay the course on certain investments, people who are too emotionally reactive will instead constantly react to subtle changes in the market,” says Lerner. “The reason is that the emotion system is doing exactly what it has evolved to do — interrupt us and call our attention to potential threats and opportunities. If you don’t have the kind of expertise that says, ‘OK, I'm alerted — a market fluctuation happened today, but we don't necessarily need to abandon our long-term investment,’ then you can get into trouble.”

    Know Your Feelings, Find Your Opportunity

    Throughout the course of long-term investments, conditions, outlooks and even our very emotions are bound to change. Fostering an optimistic outlook is one way to stay steady during trying times. As Dr. Kramer puts it, being aware of our emotions and avoiding the urge to make decisions during emotionally charged moments can contribute toward a more balanced approach to financial health.

    “You’re going to have a set of risk preferences that vary less over time,” she says. “You’ll be more consistent in the kind of decisions that you’re making for your portfolio.”

    Through the Optimist Index research, OppenheimerFunds used biometric technology — sensor-based measurements of subtle physical activity such as facial expression, pulse and skin moisture that change with emotional state — to assess the disparity between how we say we feel and the physical cues our bodies exhibit when experiencing different emotions. The study looked at how investors of all demographics reacted to three key investment themes relevant to long-term investing, Aging, Emerging Markets and Innovation, and asked investors how they thought these themes will impact the long-term global economy.

    The study found that not only do risk appetites vary between demographics, but also there’s a difference between how optimistic participants said they were versus how they measured using biometric technology.

    With Age, Comes Wisdom and Opportunity

    As people age, their risk appetites can shift, especially in a world that’s changing so rapidly in population, technology and other factors.

    “The fact is that as people get older, they’re approaching — and eventually passing — their retirement age. They worry about having enough money to be able to continue to pay the bills,” says Dr. Kramer. “The stakes become even higher, as do the emotional reactions to blips in the market. The market crashes; my nest egg is now half the size it was. The reaction becomes that much more drastic.”

    Interestingly, this bias was represented most profoundly in those approaching retirement, who measured having higher levels of optimism than self-reported, perhaps due to uncertainty regarding the political climate, according to the OppenheimerFunds study.

    Across a number of factors concerning aging, respondents were surprisingly more optimistic about retirement than expected given the projected higher costs associated with health care. When asked about the cost of health care and its impact on the global economy, those approaching retirement age at 55 measured far higher levels of optimism than reported, perhaps indicating that retiree fears are exaggerated. This was further corroborated by a subsequent spike in optimism at 65, an age when many have already entered retirement. Put another way, most pre-retirees have faith that their investments will serve them well into retirement despite current concerns about rising costs and an aging population.

    For instance, health care spending by those aged 60 and older is projected to grow by $1.4 trillion by 2030.¹

    George Evans, CIO, Equities and portfolio manager of Oppenheimer International Growth Fund understands these concerns. He also sees opportunity. His investment strategy is to “ignore the short term noise, because when you invest for the long term you see it through.”

    Rising Global Optimism

    Despite representing lucrative and reliable investment opportunities, people often let their bias get in the way of making more rational decisions about diversifying their portfolios with foreign investments.

    “Our gut instinct drives us to be very nervous about investing abroad,” says Dr. Kramer. “People tend to invest in what they know, and most people actually have way too much invested in their home country. We need to try to overcome that sense of comfort that’s driven by our emotions, to seek exposure to the risks and the opportunities that are outside of our comfort zones. That includes emerging markets. Lacking that exposure in the portfolio can be very harmful in the long run.”

    This bias was most palpably seen in an older demographic of investors, compared to millennials who, despite having an optimistic outlook about emerging markets to begin with, were measured as having an even more positive outlook on emerging markets with biometrics, the OppenheimerFunds study found.

    Millennials reported less optimism than was measured when asked about investment opportunities abroad. Even though they reported being less optimistic, they likely measured higher because of their global perspective afforded to them by being the first Internet generation.

    When asked about the impact emerging markets will have on the world economy and their long-term investment potential, millennials were shown to be particularly optimistic. Perhaps due to cultivating a more global perspective as the first generation native to the Internet, millennials measured much more optimism than reported compared to non-millennials. Non-millennials, however, may still harbor feelings of skepticism toward globalized commerce despite being aware of its apparent economic benefits. This may suggest that deep-seeded anxieties over the unknown can be mitigated through facts — such as the growth of global markets — but never entirely leave us.

    For instance, over the last 50-plus years, global gross domestic product, or GDP, has exploded from $1.3 trillion to $70 trillion, with the United States making up a smaller share of the world’s economic activity.²

    That’s just one indication that it’s time to take a global perspective, says Heidi Heikenfeld, portfolio manager of Oppenheimer Emerging Markets Innovators Fund. “You have to learn from the companies themselves,” she says. “You need to be immersed in the culture ... to put it in the context of their local market.”

    Innovating for a Brighter Future

    Improvements in technology can be a double-edged sword for long-term investors. On the one hand, they now have up-to-the-minute information on how markets and investments are performing. On the other hand, too much information can lead to panicked and premature decision making.

    “Now, we’ve got information coming from an uncountable number of sources. It’s just beyond our brain’s wiring to process this information. That’s a real downside of technology,” says Dr. Kramer. “But technology also offers some remedies. Now we’ve got technology that allows us to delegate decision making at a low cost.”

    For those with greater assets, the additional savings afforded by technology innovations may do less to counterbalance the potential rewards, the OppenheimerFunds study found.
