    Mixing Money With Morals
    The Growth in Environmental, Social and Governance Investing

    They worry that global climate change has led to rising sea levels and the hottest temperatures on record. They want to stem overuse of the earth’s resources. And they feel compelled to put pressure on companies that violate workers’ rights.

    That explains why Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) investing has become so popular in the past couple of years. Wealthy investors are flocking to financial products that take into account the ethics, social responsibility, corporate governance and environmental records of the companies they invest in.

    “There’s a huge increasing interest in responsible investing,” said Janine Guilliot, director of capital markets policy and outreach for the Sustainability Accounting Standards Board, a San Francisco organization working to develop ways companies can report their sustainability efforts.

    ESG investing is growing as more investors adopt the philosophy that investments should not only create value, but also reflect their values. Just as some consumers may avoid shopping at companies with large carbon footprints or poor workplace safety records, investors are harnessing their power to influence positive changes when picking their portfolios.

    And while there once was a fear that ESG investing could be a costly decision, investors now see growing evidence that they don’t have to sacrifice good returns for good deeds. On the contrary, according to a 2015 analysis by the University of Oxford and Arabesque Asset Management, 80 percent of academic studies found that the stocks of companies with “good sustainability practices” do better than other stocks.

    Combining Money
    And Social Conscience

    Investors are demanding companies prove they can do more than deliver big returns. They’re also looking to focus their investments on companies that are well positioned to avoid ESG risks in the long term.

    Take the Center for Humans and Nature. The nonprofit was founded in 2003 by the late philanthropist and printing heir, Strachan Donnelley, a self-described “fly-fishing philosopher” who saw the need for an organization dedicated to exploring the relationships between humans and nature. During his life, Dr. Donnelley crafted the Chicago organization’s mission: To bring scientists, scholars and others together to discuss ethical solutions to global issues like climate change and ask questions such as, “What does it mean to be human?”

    These “trusted thinkers” discuss big issues and share ideas in blogs, articles, videos and at events.

    In May of last year, the Center’s board of directors challenged itself to have the organization's significant reserve funds invested in a way that aligns with the nonprofit’s values. This is not a simple proposition: The Center relies on returns from this reserve for its annual operating funds, so it needs those investments to be profitable. But it also must not pour money into industries that contribute to climate change.

    “As an organization that is working at the moral crossroads of these social and ecological challenges, we have taken a look at the implications of climate change,” said Brooke Hecht, the Center’s President. “We want to do everything we can to divest our portfolio from the fossil fuel economy and invest in companies that are working toward a clean energy future.”

    Value Meets Values
    While some investors with ESG investing strategies exclude companies that are affiliated with business practices that violate their principles — like the production of tobacco products or cluster munitions — increasingly investors are adopting an investment philosophy geared towards a portfolio of companies best positioned to navigate the environmental, social and corporate governance risks of their respective industries.
    “Investors are asking increasingly sophisticated questions of the companies in their portfolios,” said Emily Lawrence, a Senior Product Specialist focused on ESG investing at Chicago-based asset management firm Northern Trust.
    Whatever their goals, investors should obtain guidance from an experienced financial firm. In an effort to achieve its goals for ESG investing, the Center for Humans and Nature turned to Northern Trust, which has managed the Center’s reserve funds since it was founded.
    Northern Trust began working with the group after it signed a commitment to stop new investments in the top 200 fossil fuel companies, drop oil and gas companies from investment portfolios and invest a portion of their portfolio funds in the clean energy sector. Northern Trust created a mandate for the Center and developed a detailed screening system to find investments that fit the Center’s commitment.
    “It was not only avoiding those companies that have high carbon emissions, but also trying to make sure we own companies that are really focused on generating alternative energy, preventing pollution and furthering sustainable water solutions,” said Sally N. Giegerich, Senior Investment Officer at Northern Trust Wealth Management.
    This strategy helps meet the Center’s investment needs in an ethical way, said Hecht, who added that: “Northern Trust listened deeply to what we needed.”
    Northern Trust, which has a nearly 30‑year history in ESG investing and recognizes the value of being a good corporate citizen in its own operations, has formed an ESG Council. The group consists of investment professionals with expertise across multiple sectors, to help its asset managers advise their clients on the most suitable strategies for them.
    Northern Trust also has developed research and products that represent a broad range of ESG goals, from its white papers exploring green bonds and navigating regulations to its World Quality Low Carbon solution, which emphasizes maximum exposure to high-quality companies while reducing a portfolio’s carbon footprint.
    “We have specific strategists who are partnered with clients to help understand their goals and match them with the products and the strategies that are the best fit,” Lawrence said.
    Giegerich adds that investors considering incorporating ESG investing into their investment philosophy don’t have to worry about changing their strategy overnight.
    “We usually like to do it gradually as clients get more comfortable with what we’re doing and as we fine tune what elements are important to their mandate,” she said.
