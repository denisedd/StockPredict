    November 4, 2016

    Are Voting Booth Selfies Fun or Dangerous?

    Debaters

    • Richard L. Hasen

      Richard L. Hasen, a professor at U.C. Irvine School of Law, writes the Election Law Blog. He is also the author of "Plutocrats United" and is on Twitter.

    • Elie Mystal

      Elie Mystal is an editor for Above the Law and the legal editor for the More Perfect podcast on WNYC. He is on Twitter.

    Why Not? People Pay Attention to Pictures.

    Elie Mystal 3:20 AM

    Pictures or it didn't happen. A ballot selfie is just the logical extension of that ironclad rule. A picture is an act of speech, and given our modern reliance on social media sites to disseminate all human information, a picture is among the only speech acts people will actually pay attention to.

    Or at least "like."

    I would support allowing people to livestream themselves explaining their choices from inside the polling booth, if I thought it would inspire more people to vote.

    The people most inclined to take ballot selfies are exactly the kind of social media "influencers" for whom documenting their vote is more powerful than any stodgy, written endorsement. The people following Justin Timberlake on social media might not be reading the Tennessean, scanning to see if J.T. has weighed in. But they will notice his ballot selfie on Instagram, and they may be inspired to bring participation back.

    According to the Pew Research Center, U.S. turnout in the 2012 election was 53.6 percent. That's embarrassing. That's why we can't have nice things. Let's agree that the "I Voted" sticker is not the most effective way to shame Americans into performing their most basic civic duty. I would support allowing people to livestream themselves explaining their choices from inside the polling booth if I thought it would inspire more people to actually vote.

    Given the importance of the speech act, the state needs a compelling reason to take it away. But let's remember, right now, it's perfectly legal to tell everybody who you voted for. It's also perfectly legal to lie to everybody about who you voted for. In this election especially, I expect there will be a lot of the latter. A ballot selfie is just a neat way to show you're not a hypocrite.

    I wish Paul Ryan had taken one.

    The state shouldn't prevent people from being proud of who they voted for, just for the sake of those who are not.

    Ballot Secrecy Should Override the Need to Take a Pic

    Richard L. Hasen 3:20 AM

    Once upon a time in America there was no secret ballot. Parties printed ballots in different colors, and people even marched to the polls, ballots in hand. Then, one by one, as states adopted the “Australian” (secret) ballot at the beginning of the 20th century, turnout dropped by around 7 percent. And one reason turnout dropped is because voters could no longer be coerced or bribed into voting a particular way.

    A law barring the photographing of ballots may seem strict, and an affront to freedom of expression, but it is one which is necessary to promote election integrity.

    It’s the same thing when it comes to voter fraud. Voter fraud is very rare, especially the impersonation fraud (one person claiming to be another) which Republicans claim necessitates voter ID laws. But when voter fraud does occur, the most common example is with the sale of absentee ballots. In one 1996 county commissioner’s race in Georgia, for example, the campaigns of the two candidates set up tables at opposite ends of the county courthouse and had an auction for absentee ballots. Absentee ballots can facilitate fraud because the buyer can verify how someone voted.

    The secret ballot assures that no one can verify how someone voted despite what they might say. A law barring the photographing of ballots may seem strict, and an affront to freedom of expression, but it is one which is necessary to promote election integrity.

    In current times, the idea seems to be that, as Elie said, you need a pic or it didn’t happen. I get the impulse and I don’t want to be a spoilsport. But there are a ton of ways to express your support for voting for or against a candidate without taking a picture of the one thing which can serve as proof of how you voted and thereby open the window up for vote buying or coercion from your employer, spouse, religious leader or union boss. As with laws against electioneering keeping people away from the polling place, some limitations on free expression help promote other democratic values.

    We Have Laws Against Voter Fraud and Intimidation

    Elie Mystal 3:20 AM

    Whoa, how did we go from a shameless attempt to get noticed on social media to a dystopian vote buying regime? Donald Trump hasn't won anything, yet. The mere fact that ballot selfies could be used inappropriately doesn't mean that they will be.

    We have laws against voter fraud, and laws against voter intimidation. Rick, you know that those laws generally work. You know that we should be more worried about people who seek to suppress voting than we are about actual voters using their power unethically.

    If ballot selfies are used to encourage young people to vote, I'm O.K. with them. If they're used to subvert the disenfranchisement inherent in the Electoral College, I'm in love with them.

    It seems to me that the amazing benefit (or great danger) of ballot selfies is that they can be used to facilitate "vote swapping." Vote swapping is when you live in a safe state and you "pair" or "trade" your vote with a person supporting a nonviable, third-party candidate who lives in a battleground state. On Facebook, I myself suggested pairing my vote with a friend in Nevada: I would cast his ridiculous protest vote in New York, he would cast his vote for the candidate I want to win in a state that matters. We could record our votes and put them on Facebook, to ensure no funny business.

    Vote swapping is totally legal. But in New York, it is illegal to take a picture of my vote. So I can literally trade my vote, so long as I don't prove it.

    If ballot selfies are used to encourage young people to vote, I'm O.K. with them. If they're used to subvert the disenfranchisement inherent in the Electoral College -- potentially bringing about the needed revolution in our national elections -- I'm in love with them.

    If they're used to intimidate voters or buy votes, I'm confident that we can bring the abusers to justice, without harshing on a millennial's freedom of expression.

    Let’s Just Celebrate With the “I Voted” Stickers

    Richard L. Hasen 3:20 AM

    My 20-plus years of studying elections and election law has convinced me of the virtues of the secret ballot. If we are really concerned with the integrity of elections, as we all should be, we should do what we can to make sure people cannot prove how they’ve voted. We know vote buying and coercion were problems before the secret ballot, and we know that they can be a problem with absentee ballots. There’s no reason to believe things would go better with ballot photography.

    If we are really concerned with the integrity of elections, as we all should be, we should do what we can to make sure people cannot prove how they’ve voted.

    The argument that ballot selfies can facilitate vote swapping is another reason to oppose them — not to support them. Unlike Elie, I am not convinced such swaps are legal, even though one court has held that a particular vote trading site was protected by the First Amendment.

    Back in 2008, I noted that Ben and Jerry’s, in an act of civic virtue, promised free ice cream on Election Day to everyone who showed an “I Voted” sticker. But this ran afoul of a long-running federal prohibition on vote buying. I was called a “crotchety spoilsport” for raising this argument, as Elie seems to treat me now, but my fear was that payment for turnout — especially directed to certain areas only — could be the basis for more worrisome vote buying. The Ben and Jerry’s story ended happily, with the ice cream company responding by giving free ice cream to voters and nonvoters alike, including kids.

    So let’s celebrate the electoral process with selfies — at the polling place, with our “I voted” stickers, and with words saying who we support or oppose. Let’s just not give the tools for those with less civic motives to mess up our process along the way.
