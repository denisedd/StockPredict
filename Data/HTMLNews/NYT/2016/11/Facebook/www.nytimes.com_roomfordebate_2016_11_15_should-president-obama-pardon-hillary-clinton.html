    Should President Obama Pardon Hillary Clinton?

    Debaters

    • Brian C. Kalt

      Brian C. Kalt is a professor of law and the Harold Norris faculty scholar at Michigan State University. He is the author of “Constitutional Cliffhangers: A Legal Guide for Presidents and Their Enemies."

    • Stephen B. Presser

      Stephen B. Presser, the Raoul Berger professor of legal history emeritus at Northwestern University's Pritzker School of Law, is the author of the forthcoming "Law Professors: Three Centuries of Shaping American Law."

    Pardon Her, the Voters Already Have

    Stephen B. Presser 3:20 AM

    The United States Constitution gives the president complete discretion in exercising the pardon power, as for example, President Gerald Ford did for President Richard Nixon, in order to spare the nation the divisive ordeal of seeing a chief executive who had, after all, twice been voted into office by the American people, further disgraced and punished.

    There is a similarly powerful argument that a person, such as Hillary Clinton, who may have won the popular vote for president by an electorate well aware of her purported misdeeds connected with her use of a personal server for government emails when she was secretary of state, should not be subjected to prosecution for conduct that most of her fellow Americans seem to have condoned. This was conduct that at least one of her predecessors as secretary of state also, though to a more limited extent, engaged in.

    F.B.I. Director James Comey has already determined that the server issue did not meet the tests of criminal prosecution. The Clinton Foundation investigation is still ongoing.

    ​There are two other powerful reasons for sparing a now fractious American people the protracted agony of a criminal proceeding of this nature. One is that F.B.I. Director James Comey has already determined that the server issue did not meet the tests of criminal prosecution, and the other is that a jury, which is likely to include persons sympathetic to Hillary Clinton, would not be likely to convict in any event.

    Ironically, pardoning Clinton, because it would, after all, taint her with the possibility of having behaved in a criminal manner, would be something that would also make her opponents feel vindicated, much as Nixon’s detractors have maintained that if he hadn’t engaged in criminal conduct, there would be no need for Ford to have pardoned him. A pardon on the server issue would be a prudent one.

    A pardon for Hillary Clinton would, however, run counter to our core belief that no person is above the law. And while that argument isn’t persuasive in the ambiguous case of her operation of a private server, it would not be appropriate to pardon her for any misconduct in connection with the more alarming allegations regarding the “pay for play” activities of the Clinton Foundation, as detailed in Peter Schweizer’s book "Clinton Cash," and in the reporting of The New York Times and other news media outlets. The F.B.I. investigation of those activities is, apparently, still ongoing, and should be allowed to run its course.

    Pardoning Her Would Reinforce Bad Precedents That Others Would Follow

    Brian C. Kalt 3:20 AM

    President Obama should not pardon Secretary Clinton regarding her email server. Doing so would reinforce bad precedents, and might not work anyway.

    Presidents have the pardon power because, unlike federal prosecutors and judges, they are accountable to the electorate. Legally, the pardon power is very broad; the incentive to avoid political controversy is the biggest check on it.

    Lame-duck pardons are legally valid, but politically illegitimate. When we approve expansive power in the service of causes we love, we enable the use of that power for causes we detest.

    Lame-duck pardons in high-profile cases are thus problematic. They are legally valid, but politically illegitimate. When a president is leaving office, after the election to choose a successor, political accountability is at its lowest ebb. This made Bill Clinton’s last-day pardon of Marc Rich outrageous. So too George H.W. Bush’s pardons of six Iran-Contra figures after his reelection defeat. These pardons were historically unusual — if President Obama wants to pardon Hillary Clinton, these dishonorable acts would be his leading precedents. If Obama decides against it, he would join George W. Bush in burying these precedents under another layer of restraint.

    When we approve expansive power in the service of causes we love, we enable the use of that power for causes we detest. Those who want President Obama to prevent President-elect Donald Trump from pursuing his extraordinary promise to incarcerate his opponent should pause to picture Trump at the end of his own term, using this precedent to pardon his own favored allies, unaccountably, to shield them from future prosecution.

    To be sure, Donald Trump could push his powers to their legal limits regardless of past practices. But the issue is not legal. Again, lame-duck presidents legally can issue high-profile pardons. Rather, the issue is political. Pardoning Clinton would normalize the practice and dull the (already weak) incentive for future presidents to exercise self-restraint.

    President Obama might argue that the Rich and Iran-Contra pardons preempted or overruled federal prosecutors, judges and juries, while a Clinton pardon would solidify F.B.I. Director James Comey’s conclusion that no charges were warranted. But would that argument satisfy those who disagreed with Comey? As Stephen notes, even without a pardon Hillary Clinton is unlikely to be convicted. But if she would be — if the Trump administration would not stop until it got a conviction — then an Obama pardon would not end things. A pardon would launch congressional hearings, accusations of perjury in those hearings, criminal investigations of that, and so on. The payoff for a Clinton pardon might thus be too small to justify its costs.

    Damon Winter/The New York Times

    President-elect Donald Trump said in an interview on “60 Minutes” that he had not decided whether to prosecute Hillary Clinton. This comes despite claims during his campaign that he would appoint a special prosecutor to look into her use of a private email server when she was secretary of state, as well as repeated calls during his rallies to “Lock her up.”
