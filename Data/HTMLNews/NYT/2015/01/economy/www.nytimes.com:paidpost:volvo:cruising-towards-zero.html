    CRUISING TOWARDS ZERO

    CAN WE ELIMINATE AUTO-ACCIDENT FATALITIES?

    Note: Crash scenes simulated for illustration purposes.

    As safety technology gets smarter, vehicles are getting better at avoiding crashes before they happen and minimizing harm when they do. The goal of eliminating accident deaths — once thought impossible — could be in sight.

    Note: Crash scenes simulated for illustration purposes.

    Elizabeth Malsin has good reason to be passionate about the goal of zero car fatalities.

    In 2004, the real estate developer was driving her Volvo Cross Country station wagon over a bridge in Portland, Oregon, when the driver of an S.U.V. lost control and hit her head on.

    “All the airbags went off, and the seatbelts held me in,” recalls Malsin. “In another car, I easily could have died. Now I feel really good about having bought a safe vehicle, and about being around to see my little boy grow up.”

    Malsin’s experience is far from unique. Per mile driven, fewer passengers are dying on U.S. roads than ever before.

    Between 1954 and 2013, vehicle miles traveled in the United States — a statistical estimate of the total distance covered by all the nation’s vehicles — grew roughly sixfold. Yet over that same 59-year span, the number of auto-related deaths actually fell slightly, from 33,890 to 32,850. Deaths per motor vehicle crash have fallen as well, by about half since 1975, according to the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety.

    “Progressively more stringent crash-safety standards, beginning in the late 1960s, have done wonders to protect occupants in vehicles of all types and sizes” in both single- and multi-vehicle accidents, says Sam Abuelsamid, a senior research analyst at Navigant Research.

    Changes on a variety of fronts have combined to improve auto safety. Over decades, better road design has improved intersections, on ramps, off ramps and other infrastructure. But a big share of these safety gains can be credited to the advanced materials and technologies being built into cars.

    New vehicles carry a dazzling array of both active and passive safety systems. From multiple airbags to adaptive cruise control, collision warning systems and lane-keeping assistance, modern cars are designed to be on the alert for danger and react instantly when it occurs.

     

    Stronger, Lighter

     

    Stronger, Lighter

    Reinventing the way a car is built is devilishly complex. Engineers must balance conflicting goals, such as adding strength to improve safety while cutting weight to boost fuel economy.

    To accomplish both, engineers across the industry are turning to once-exotic materials, including tough-but-light carbon fiber, aluminum and ultra-high-strength steel. These advanced materials “maintain the passenger compartment while dissipating crash energy,” Abuelsamid says. Meanwhile, airbags and seat belts have “helped to keep occupants in the vehicle, where crash structures can provide the best protection.”

    At Volvo, ultra-high-strength steel — the strongest used in the car-body industry — is the material of choice. The 2016 XC90, a seven-seat S.U.V., uses about five times more of the metal than the first-generation XC90 did. And a uniquely designed passenger cage absorbs energy upon impact while maintaining its structure, which protects occupants.

     

    Actively Safer

     

    Actively Safer

    Volvo’s efforts to protect passengers go beyond what regulators require for five-star safety ratings. Its Accident Research Database, a trove of accident-analysis findings the company has been building for more than 45 years, provides real-world insights to guide those efforts. The database now includes information on 40,000 crashes in Volvos, involving 60,000 occupants.

    Like other carmakers, Volvo also studies U.S. NHTSA test standards. But its focus on real-world data helps push the company’s safety goals further, says Magdalena Lindman, a Volvo technical expert for traffic-safety data analysis. “We do our own in-depth analysis of these accidents,” Lindman adds. “If we have knowledge of the past, it’s easier to predict the future.”

    Of course, reducing the number of crashes in the first place is the most effective way to cut auto fatalities, says Thomas Broberg, a senior technical advisor for safety at Volvo. Studies have found that human error is a factor in roughly nine out of 10 accidents, so auto makers have started to make safety technologies more active, to enable them to interact with drivers and their vehicles.

    Sleep-deprived drivers pose an all-too-common danger. A National Sleep Foundation poll found that a third of adult drivers — more than 100 million people — have dozed off while behind the wheel. And one in six deadly accidents involves drowsy driving, according to the American Automobile Association

    Technology can’t yet keep you from falling asleep, but it can step in to lower the risk. And if you do doze off, it can soften the consequences.

    Volvo’s 2016 XC90 has a feature called Driver Alert Control, which uses pattern-recognition software — monitoring the car’s progress between lane markings — to notify the driver if his or her driving pattern is erratic. When triggered, the system sounds an alert and displays a dashboard message recommending that the driver take a break. Using GPS, Rest Stop Guidance directs the driver to the nearest place to pull over.

    The car is also designed to intervene to prevent accidents. To lower the risk posed by an inattentive or sleepy driver in your rearview mirror, radar scans for possible rear-end impacts. If necessary, the car flashes warning lights at the driver behind you. If the system senses that a collision is imminent, it tightens the safety belts and hits the brakes to minimize harm.

    Other big auto makers are on a similar route, exploring features like lane-deviation monitoring and pre-collision warnings.

     

    Protecting pedestrians

     

    Protecting pedestrians

    For drivers, policymakers and the public, the next frontier in automotive safety is lowering the risk to those outside the car. While auto-accident fatalities have been declining in the U.S., pedestrian deaths are going up. In 2012 (the most recent data available), 4,743 pedestrians were killed by cars, according to NHTSA.

    To address this, the Volvo XC90’s City Safety system uses a mix of forward-looking cameras and radar to I.D. vehicles, pedestrians or cyclists and applies the brakes if it senses that a crash could occur. The system also reduces the risk of turning into the path of an oncoming vehicle.

    In the event of an impact, there are ways to mitigate harm to a pedestrian. Volvo was the first carmaker to introduce an external airbag in the V40 and V40 Cross Country (vehicles not sold in the U.S.) to help protect pedestrians. In a collision, sensors trigger an outside airbag that’s mounted near the windshield to cushion the blow if, as sometimes happens, a pedestrian is thrown onto the hood and into the windshield. The system also raises the rear of the hood four inches, giving the metal more depth to dent, thereby absorbing more of the pedestrian’s impact.

    The pedestrian airbag technology was specifically developed by Volvo for a smaller vehicle with a low stance and short hood. Larger vehicles have a different stance and geometry enabling collisions with pedestrians to be mitigated in other ways.

    In Europe, region-wide regulations have ensured wide adoption of these pedestrian-protection systems, and U.S. auto makers are moving in that direction as well. Other big auto makers have debuted pedestrian-safety systems and the pace will quicken further if, as some expect, NHTSA follows through with federal regulations requiring them.

     

    Driver distraction

     

    Driver distraction

    In an increasingly connected society, our vehicles have become places where drivers want to stay linked to the wider world. But that desire can pull focus from the task at hand: driving. So manufacturers are looking for ways to minimize the distraction factor.

    Volvo approached this challenge by studying the in-car habits of drivers, with a special focus on the touchscreen, which is quickly becoming the most widely-adopted user interface.

    By developing a familiar, intuitive interface, enhanced by voice interaction, Volvo aims to strike a just-right balance by offering a simple, nondistracting way to access the most relevant apps — navigation, music and other streaming media. This upgraded interface is new in the 2016 XC90.

    Towards zero

     

    Towards zero

    Can these advances come together to lower deaths to zero?

    Volvo thinks so. By decade’s end, its Vision 2020 plan aims to deliver cars in which nobody will be killed or gravely injured. “We believe that in the future, cars should not crash, but if they do, no one will be seriously harmed,” says Volvo’s Broberg.

    In the not-too-distant future, the combination of better design and advanced materials, along with active safety systems and car-to-car communication, could transform auto fatalities into a concern that’s rapidly disappearing in the rearview mirror.
