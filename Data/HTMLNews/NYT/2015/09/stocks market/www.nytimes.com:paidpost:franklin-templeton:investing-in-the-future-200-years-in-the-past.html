    Investing in the Future 200 Years in the Past

    Illustrations by Rob Dobi

    More than 200 years after winning a revolution and building a powerful nation, the U.S. founding fathers continue to make a difference in people’s lives. But one is touching lives even more directly — thanks to his financial savvy rather than his politics.

    Benjamin Franklin — the founder, statesman and philosopher immortalized for his inventions, diplomacy and experiments with electricity — established two small investments in his will that are still funding the educations of 21st-century students. More than 225 years after his death, those investments continue to provide a valuable lesson for today’s investors.

    This lesser-known Franklin story builds on the simple advice about savings and thriftiness that he offered as a printer and the author of “Poor Richard’s Almanack.” It’s a story that illuminates his deep understanding of the power of compounding — an investment strategy of reinvesting interest and other gains over time.

    ...Waste neither time nor money, but make the best use of both.

    ...Waste neither time nor money, but make the best use of both.

    Benjamin Franklin

    His foresight stemmed from a connection with money throughout his life. While he lacked it growing up, he became a successful printer of currency and accumulated enough wealth to retire early. That opened other opportunities in science, politics and statesmanship. He even played a pivotal role in obtaining financing from France for the Revolutionary War.

    “He was frugal. He was cautious. He was observant. He was pretty reluctant to incur debt,” says Page Talbott, president and CEO of the Historical Society of Pennsylvania. “But he didn’t mind lending to other people so that they could be successful.”

    History

    Franklin expanded his printing business beyond newspapers to money. “The printing of currency coincided with his interest in many disciplines,” Talbott says. With his friend, Joseph Breintnall, they created a specialized way to print that hindered counterfeiters.

    This innovation contributed to Franklin’s financial success. It allowed him to get currency printing jobs in many of the colonies, essentially creating franchises.

    He was so well off that he was able to retire at the age of 42, says Gordon Wood, the Alva O. Way University Professor at Brown University. “He was one of the most wealthy men in Philadelphia, if not all of the colonies.”

    Franklin felt very strongly that if you had sufficient wealth, you should retire and devote yourself to the public good, Wood says.

    And that’s exactly what he did. He helped create the first fire department, one of the first libraries and the American Philosophical Society. He was also the first person to chart the Gulf Stream. In his 70s, Franklin became minister to France and famously helped draft the Declaration of Independence and, later, the U.S. Constitution.

    The Will

    Toward the end of his life, Franklin wanted to give back to Boston and Philadelphia, the cities that he called home, and to those who looked up to him — the apprentices and tradesmiths of America. When he died April 16, 1790, he left 1,000 pounds sterling (roughly $4,500 at the time) to each city — but that money came with one big stipulation.

    Each sum had to be placed in a trust and invested for 100 years, after which around 75 percent could be used to help tradesmen and fund civic projects. The rest was to be invested for another 100 years, at which time it would be given in full to the cities.

    After the first 100 years, thanks to compounding interest, the trusts in Boston and Philadelphia were worth almost $400,000 and $100,000, respectively, prior to any disbursements. (The amounts differed due to each city’s financial strategies and investment paths.) By 1990, the remaining amounts had grown to $4.5 million for Boston and $2 million for Philadelphia, as a result of compounding. Twenty-five years later, a fraction of that money still remains, which is used to continue Franklin’s wishes.

    Much of what is left goes to scholarship funds in both cities for students pursuing trades. In Boston, the first disbursement of about $300,000 was used in part to create the Benjamin Franklin Institute of Technology (then called the Franklin Union) with the help of Andrew Carnegie, in 1908. In 1993, it received the final disbursement of more than $4.5 million. The school now uses some of that money to give out a scholarship for students in need, just a fraction of the more than $2 million that it gives out annually to students.

    Industry, perseverance, and frugality make fortune yield.

    Industry, perseverance, and frugality make fortune yield.

    Benjamin Franklin

    In Philadelphia, Franklin's investment went to the Philadelphia Foundation, a non-profit community organization that helps fund educational opportunities and improve the lives of those in Southern Pennsylvania. The Philadelphia Foundation is helping students like Hakim Ben-Abdou become an electrical engineer — a trade close to Franklin’s heart. “It’s great how 200 years later I’m able to go to school thanks to all the money he saved up all those years ago,” he says.

    While the gift has provided for many students in Boston and Philadelphia, Anthony Benoit, president of the Benjamin Franklin Institute of Technology, understands that it won’t last forever.

    “What we really count on now is current-day Franklins — the other people who have worked hard and have invested in ways that allow them to be generous to the next generation.”

    Investing

    A modern-day Benjamin Franklin isn’t necessarily an innovator and statesman. People can follow in his footsteps simply by investing for what’s next — whether it’s for a personal goal or the well-being of future generations.

    In fact, many modern investors can take advantage of the same strategy that paid off for Franklin: compounding. The strategy is as simple as reinvesting the interest, dividends and other gains delivered by your principal investment.

    “It’s so important because you are staying in the market and having the clock work for you,” says Dan O’Lear, executive vice president of Franklin Templeton Investments. “Time is on your side.”

    Asset Allocation

    By investing in a mix of equity and fixed income, and reinvesting all returns, the initial amount grew by more than five times.4

    One could have significantly boosted their investment if they were willing to take on additional risk.     The money left in Franklin’s will had 200 years to grow. It stayed invested. “It’s a snowball effect of interest on top of interest, plus principal, that gives you the compound returns,” says O’Lear.

    Past performance does not guarantee future results. Asset allocation does not assure a profit or protect against market losses.

    Today, investors also have the added benefit of asset allocation, or putting your money into a mix of different asset classes, such as equity or fixed income. This mix helps investors reduce risk when the market is in flux while still offering the potential for earning returns.

    “Asset allocation allows you to have some of your money invested in the top performers while also ensuring you don't have all your money in the worst performers,” says O’Lear. Or to put it more simply: “You’re not putting all your eggs in one basket.”

    “It may make you feel more comfortable about remaining invested, which allows you to take full advantage of compounding, because if you are invested and staying with it, your investments and your patience can work to your benefit,” he says.

    O’Lear says that the lessons from Franklin can easily be applied today when it comes to investing practices. “It’s mind-boggling when you see how compounding can work for you.”
