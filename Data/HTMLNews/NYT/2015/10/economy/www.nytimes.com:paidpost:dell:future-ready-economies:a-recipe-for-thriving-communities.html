    A Recipe for Thriving Communities

    The 2015 Strategic Innovation Summit: Enabling Economies for the Future gathered leaders at the forefront of innovation to discuss what makes cities future ready.

    In Boston, sensors were installed on school buses so parents can track the whereabouts of their children’s rides during storms. The 400‐year‐old city is also working on another mobile app that collects data from drivers’ smartphones to alert road crews of pot holes. And it’s made battling City Hall easier with apps for fighting (and paying) parking tickets.

    Across the country in Riverside, Calif., more than 30 years’ worth of infrastructure projects were completed in just five years, thanks to innovative technology and creative financing. The Los Angeles suburb also set up classes outside classrooms to teach residents how to write code, hosts hackathons and is working with an insurance company to fund the planting of more trees.

    Innovations like those in Boston and Riverside are easy to spot, but many cities are finding them hard to reproduce. To identify ways to jumpstart the process, municipal officials, entrepreneurs, technology executives and scholars gathered on the campus of Harvard University to discuss lessons from the world’s most innovative cities.

    “Our goal is to simply understand how our communities and cities can play a role in enabling an innovation economy of the future,” said Professor David Ricketts, who chaired the 2015 Strategic Innovation Summit: Enabling Economies for the Future. The event, sponsored by Dell, was hosted by the Technology and Entrepreneurship Center at Harvard.

    Developing an Action Plan

    The benefits of rethinking how government and services work are clear. Innovations like those in Boston, Riverside and other leading cities are leveraging technology and new ways of thinking to make communities more attractive places to live. That’s attracting more businesses that need the workers. As the population and economy grow, more ideas and more innovation follow. It’s a self‐ perpetuating cycle that’s defining the modern thriving community.

    At the summit, officials highlighted several examples of different innovations around the world but stressed merely copying an app or service isn’t enough. But common themes emerged. All innovative cities adopted a methodical approach to innovation that fosters a culture of experimentation. They also looked outside traditional government frameworks to avoid bureaucracy. They found and worked with partners both inside and outside government on shared visions. And they adapted ideas from companies and cities to suit their own needs.

    In several cases, the path to innovation started with a newly elected mayor who wanted to realize campaign promises and modernize basic city services.

    Ted Smith, chief innovation officer for Louisville, Ky., said his department was set up after a businessman was elected mayor and wanted his administration to do more than just put out fires and provide basic services. The mayor borrowed a page from business playbooks and created a position that was responsible for finding and implementing initiatives.

    “It’s the one‐throat‐to‐choke school of management,” Smith said. “If you have to get something done and you don’t have someone accountable, it doesn’t get done.”

    At first, there was little assistance from city coffers. Smith’s team looked to partner with like‐ minded philanthropies to implement ideas. In 2012, Louisville was awarded $5 million from the Bloomberg Foundation to improve services by collecting and analyzing data.

    The city is now studying whether it can prevent heroin addiction by analyzing pharmacy records for people who have been denied prescriptions for painkillers — an indicator of someone who might turn to street drugs.

    “Innovation shouldn’t be special. It should be day-to-day. Innovation can be little things.”

    Smith said his group focuses its projects on areas that need improvement, and they’re often found in rankings where the city fares poorly.

    “That’s where the headroom is — there’s no organization chasing it,” he said.

    Data collection and analysis also play a role in Burlington, Vt., where Beth Anderson serves as chief innovation officer. Like Boston, Burlington is making it easier for people to interact with government for basic city services. But her office will analyze those interactions for patterns, which will then be studied to improve apps and other tools even more.

    “Innovation shouldn’t be special. It should be day‐to‐day,” she says. “Innovation can be little things.”

    Nurturing a Culture of Experimentation

    Innovation, however, can be a risky endeavor, but several speakers at the summit identified ways to mitigate the risks. Boston’s Urban Mechanics Project, which worked on numerous popular apps for tracking buses, potholes and improving interactions with City Hall, treats each project as an experiment.

    “It’s a very iterative process,” said Nigel Jacob, the group’s co‐chair. “We’re talking to people constantly and getting user feedback.

    Photo: Griffin Greenberg

    “Then there’s a pause at the end of experimentation where we sit down and look at what we learned,” he says. “Did it work? If it did, how do we scale it? If it’s failing, we shut it down as quickly as possible.”

    His office also creates a safe place for city departments to experiment. If a project succeeds, the department gets all the credit. If it fails, Jacob’s group steps in and takes the blame. “It’s important to provide a safe place for experimentation,” he said.

    But even successes can bring challenges. If something new succeeds, it’s likely to displace legacy systems and sometimes even livelihoods, Ricketts said.

    “We need to embrace change but realize that disruptive innovations have an ugly twin, which is disruptive obsolescence,” he said. “We have to understand that's part of the innovation process, and part of what we need to plan.”

    Role of Technology

    Cities might be reluctant to experiment since they’re already facing significant headwinds ranging from a general mistrust of government to shrinking budgets. Most also wrestle with laws and regulations that were enacted before technology accelerated the pace of life and business.

    Technology, though, also provides solutions. After all, computers are now ubiquitous, storage is practically free, radio‐equipped sensors cost just pennies and potential collaborators are easy to work with, regardless of location. None of this was possible or affordable just five or 10 years ago, said Glenn Wintrich, Dell’s Innovation Lead.

    “We now have this confluence of technology where it's not only viable and feasible but also inexpensive,” he said. “That's really key for cities.”
