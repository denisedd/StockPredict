    Study: San Jose Metro Area Leads U.S. in Future Readiness

    Future-ready metropolitan areas in the U.S. rank high across key human capital, commerce and infrastructure categories.

    The San Jose metropolitan area, which gave birth to the high‐tech industry and continues to be a growth engine today, is the top future ready economy in the U.S., according to a new survey that attributes the region’s strength to the engagement of residents and innovation by its businesses and governments.

    San Jose’s neighbor to the north — the metro region that includes San Francisco and Oakland — ranked No. 2. The nation’s capital, Washington, D.C., Boston and Austin, Tex., rounded out the top five regions in the Dell Future Ready Economies Model.

    Approach to Future-Ready Index

    The survey defined future readiness by examining three categories: the engagement and education of residents, innovation in commerce and government, and the state of the region’s infrastructure. About 40 data points were analyzed to calculate the rankings.

    The categories were determined during a conference on the campus of Harvard University that explored the nature of innovation and attempted to find common themes among communities that are well‐known for their innovative programs. City officials, entrepreneurs, scholars and technologists from around the world contributed to the discussion at the 2015 Strategic Innovation Summit: Enabling Economies for the Future gathering, which was sponsored by Dell.

    One theme was how cities, once innovation takes hold, find themselves in a virtuous cycle. Educated and engaged citizens are drawn to the communities by jobs, cultural activities and social opportunities. More businesses move in to take advantage of the talent. And more people relocate, bringing even more ideas, innovation and economic vitality.

    “The new city is about attracting people, and that's where the innovations come from,” said Karen Campbell, senior consultant at IHS Economics, which conducted the survey on behalf of Dell. “It's about getting a mix of people there who can bring new perspectives and new ideas.”

    Bay Area: A Hotbed of Innovation

    The San Jose area has been experiencing the innovation cycle for decades. Just 50 years ago, it was known largely for its orchards, sleepy suburbs and proximity to universities.

    But after World War II, those schools were hotbeds of innovation attracting people well‐versed in technologies such as the newly invented transistor. Students and professors alike started companies that attracted more people and ideas. Transistors led to integrated circuits, which led to microprocessors and storage chips. An entire industry emerged to harvest the power of silicon chips through hardware and software.

    In the IHS study, San Jose and its surrounding communities scored well above the average of all the ranked cities in what the researchers called human capital, particularly the strength of its labor force and the educational achievements of its residents. It ranked the highest, by far, in science, technology, engineering and math occupations per capita.

    In the commerce category, which captured data on business and local government innovation, San Jose topped all other cities. These results were driven by high scores for overall productivity, innovation/investment and growth.

    The San Jose area, however, fared relatively poorly for infrastructure. Though its ranking for high‐ speed Internet availability was high, its numbers slipped due to traffic congestion, highways, public transportation and energy accessibility.

    San Jose’s infrastructure “gives other metro areas an opportunity to move ahead,” Campbell said. “They can attract people with the good infrastructure that they might be building.”

    The No. 2 region, San Francisco‐Oakland, ranked slightly lower in terms of human capital and infrastructure than San Jose. Its commerce rating was weighed down by lower growth forecasts, while high housing costs affected human capital, Campbell said.

    “San Francisco is not expected to grow as much. Maybe they're not as up‐and‐coming as some of the newer metros,” she said. “They're becoming more of a mature future ready economy. “

    “The new city is about attracting people, and that’s where the innovations come from ... new perspectives and new ideas.”

    Innovation Across the U.S.

    No. 3 Washington achieved the highest score among ranked cities for human capital — the result of a highly educated young workforce. Its ranking was also boosted by cultural attractions, such as the Smithsonian museums, and its high placement on “best places to live” lists.

    Other ranked regions and key drivers of their rankings:

    Boston‐Cambridge, No. 4, is the home of Harvard University, Boston University and other elite schools. Not surprisingly, this provided one of the highest scores in the area of human capital, specifically the education levels achieved by residents.

    Austin, No. 5, is well-known for music and high tech, but its ranking was driven by forecasts for its continued economic growth. In fact, its gross metro product (a metro-area analog to national GDP) outranked San Jose, Washington and San Francisco.

    Raleigh, N.C., No. 6, is part of North Carolina’s Research Triangle, which includes major universities and technology companies. The region’s human capital score, particularly in the area of resident education, was well above the average of other cities. Its infrastructure more than compensated for a below-average commerce ranking, in part due to a very high score for its relatively uncongested highways.

    Seattle, No. 7, another tech hotbed, posted an above-average human capital score, driven primarily by a highly educated workforce and higher-than-average voter participation rates.

    Denver and surrounding communities, No. 8, ranked highly thanks to the commerce categories, especially the region’s growth outlook and small business job gains. The area also fared well in the area of transportation and energy accessibility.

    Portland, Ore., No. 9, ranked above average in terms of human capital thanks to a high employment rate and voter participation. The region’s low commerce rating was balanced by an above-average ranking in infrastructure, particularly in the area of transportation.

    Dallas-Fort Worth, No. 10, ranked highly in commerce largely due to an economic growth forecast that’s second only to Austin. Its human capital and infrastructure ratings were below the average of the other cities analyzed in the study.
