    Republican Candidates on the Economy and Social Programs

    By WILSON ANDREWS and THOMAS KAPLAN UPDATED November 10, 2015

    The contenders for the Republican presidential nomination have proposed numerous tax overhauls and have largely voiced opposition to raising the minimum wage. They are split on how to deal with Social Security and Medicare.

    Others advocate a flat tax or a tax on consumption. Ben Carson wants a flat tax of 10 to 15 percent, based on tithing in the Bible, while Rand Paul has proposed a 14.5 percent flat tax and Ted Cruz has proposed a 10 percent flat tax. Mike Huckabee wants a national sales tax of 30 percent on purchases.

    Many of the Republican candidates have made the argument that raising the federal minimum wage of $7.25 will cost jobs.

    Mr. Carson has said that “probably it should be higher,” but he backed away from that stance during the Nov. 10 debate.

    John Kasich has said any increase should be “reasonable” and undertaken at the state level.

    Mr. Huckabee and Chris Christie, who has said he is “tired of hearing about the minimum wage,” have said that the focus on increasing the minimum wage is misplaced.

    Jeb Bush, Marco Rubio and Mr. Kasich have expressed support for the deal.

    Several candidates have questioned the Obama administration’s negotiating abilities, including Mr. Christie, Carly Fiorina and Donald Trump.

    Mr. Huckabee said he “wouldn't trust Obama to buy a car off Craigslist,” let alone negotiate a sweeping trade deal.

    Mr. Paul has said he wants to read the agreement before taking a position.

    Mr. Huckabee and Mr. Trump have vowed to protect Social Security.

    Mr. Cruz supports creating private retirement accounts.

    Mr. Christie would eliminate Social Security benefits for the wealthiest retirees.

    Mr. Bush and Mr. Rubio advocate a “premium support” system in which the government would provide a fixed amount to beneficiaries to purchase private health plans or traditional Medicare.

    Mr. Bush and Mr. Christie would increase premiums for beneficiaries with higher incomes.

    Mr. Carson had suggested replacing Medicare with health savings accounts, but now has backed away from that position.

    On the Budget Deal

    Several of the candidates have spoken out against the deal between President Obama and Republican congressional leaders.

    Oppose the Deal

    cruz
    huckabee
    paul
    rubio

    Mr. Cruz has called the deal a “complete and utter surrender” that amounts to “a slap in the face to conservatives.”

    Mr. Huckabee, who has positioned himself as a defender of Social Security, has said he is “appalled” that the deal reallocates money between Social Security trust funds.

    Mr. Rubio called the agreement “severely flawed” and said it “postpones tough decisions until after the next election.”

    Mr. Paul has vowed to filibuster the budget deal, calling it “exactly the opposite of what every conservative Republican in America wants.”

    Mr. Carson said that as president, he would not sign a budget that raised the debt ceiling. But on this budget deal, he said “it’s time to move on.”
