    5. I. A World Like Our Own

    “Good fantasy always mirrors the world we live in, usually commenting on something that is immediately recognizable to readers and threatening to them.”

    TERRY BROOKS
    Author of the “Shannara” novels

    EXTENDED AUDIO

    “Good fantasy always mirrors the world we live in,” says Terry Brooks, the author and mastermind behind the “Shannara” novels, soon to debut as a live-action television series, “The Shannara Chronicles,” on MTV. “Usually,” says Brooks, it’s by “commenting on something that is immediately recognizable to readers and threatening to them as well as to the writer.”

    In the MTV series, which draws from Brooks’ best-selling “The Elfstones of Shannara,” the talismanic tree known as the Ellcrys is dying, which has opened the Four Lands — the setting for Brooks’ adventures — to an invasion by demons from a parallel world.

    “Most of my books talk about personal responsibility for yourself or for others,” says Brooks, whose story uses the Ellcrys Tree to symbolize the natural world that sustains our planet today. “It is a common worry for us — that we might lose what we have through neglect or deliberate mismanagement and foolish choice — and this is what is happening to the elves.”

    “Most of my books talk about personal responsibility for yourself and others ... about choices that must be made where the choices are difficult and perhaps damaging.”

    EXTENDED AUDIO

    If that story sounds familiar, it’s by design. According to a study by The Lancet, over the coming century people’s exposure to extreme rainfall will rise fourfold and to drought threefold. Couple that with rising ocean levels from glaciers melting at alarming rates and we have the recipe for disasters that will radically alter the Earth. The stakes, like those for the adventurers in “Shannara,” could not be higher.

    Although fantastical, “The Shannara Chronicles” takes place on our own planet after nuclear war has decimated society as we know it. In place of science, magic has arisen and races of elves and men must fight the demons to sustain peace in the land.

    While J.R.R. Tolkien’s “The Lord of the Rings” trilogy is often viewed as a reflection of the horrors of the world wars and, more recently, the Harry Potter series recast teenage angst through the lives of Muggles and wizards, “Shannara” serves as its own sort of parable. Brooks’ novels — which have appeared 23 times on The New York Times' best-seller list since the late 1970s — offer a multifaceted commentary on the destruction of the natural world.

    The conflict sets the scene for one of the biggest fantasy series to be adapted to television. It also presents a stark allegory for present-day concerns. “A rather large segment of the elven population does not believe that this threat of the destruction of their world is real,'' says Brooks. “Other people in other nations don't even know it is a problem. There is a lot of denial.”

    II. Today’s Four Lands

    “If you embrace the reality of environmental destruction fully and carry it through to its logical consequences, one of those consequences is another war.”

    TERRY BROOKS
    Author of the “Shannara” novels

    EXTENDED AUDIO

    As becomes clear from the decayed images of a fallen Space Needle, the Four Lands of “The Shannara Chronicles” are Brooks’ imagined version of the Pacific Northwest, albeit ravaged by the passing of millennia and a global war. “If you embrace the reality of environmental destruction fully and carry it through to its logical consequences, one of those consequences is another war, this one fought over water and food and shelter and land,” says Brooks.

    “If these resources are placed at risk to a sufficient degree, then people will start to fight over who is responsible and entitled to what is left,” he says. In Brooks’ world, nature and magical forces have now taken control, having swallowed up cities and reshaped the landscape.

    While this postapocalyptic world won’t likely come to pass — at least not the elves and magical parts of it — the Pacific Northwest of today is changing alarmingly quickly. According to John Rybcyzk, professor and chair of the department of environmental science at Western Washington University, global warming is already causing hotter summers and decreased snowfall during winter.

    “You want to live in a place where people practice a lifestyle you support. On environmental issues, this was a good fit.”

    EXTENDED AUDIO

    “We’re likely going to see much more flooding in the winter because [water] is not stored and not being released gradually as the snowpack melts,” says Rybcyzk. “It’s going to come down as a tremendous dump of rain.”

    According to Brooks, the natural world had been on his mind throughout writing the novels, and Seattle embodied the landscape that his characters are fighting to preserve. “This part of the country is so beautiful, and its look and feel felt so evocative to me as a writer,” he says.

    Brooks decided to stay in Seattle after meeting the woman who would become his wife and the location proved fertile ground for a story partially defined by environmentalist undercurrents. “You want to live in a place where people practice a lifestyle you support,” he says. “On environmental issues, this was a good fit.”

    III. An Uncertain Future

    “There is definite evidence that sea level rise has increased over the last few decades.”

    JOHN CALLOWAY
    Graduate Program Director
    Department of Environmental Science
    University of San Francisco

    The predicted changes to the Pacific Northwest’s climate are minuscule compared to what is already happening elsewhere around the world. Unlike in the '90s, when The New York Times asked, “Just how urgent is the problem of climate change?” global warming is no longer an abstract fear — we can literally see it happening all around us.

    “Hurricane Sandy and other extreme weather events really brought home climate change to people in a way that they hadn’t felt before,” says John Oppermann, the executive director of Earth Day Initiative, an organization whose goal is to raise awareness of environmental issues including climate change.

    John Callaway, a professor and graduate program director for the department of environmental science at the University of San Francisco, says he sees the effects of global warming in the wetlands of the Bay Area. “I’ve been ... looking at increased flooding rates associated with increased sea level,” he says. “There is definite evidence that sea level rise has increased over the last few decades.”

    Climate Change: Past, Present and Future

    • 1972

      "THE GREENHOUSE EFFECT"

      The meteorologist John Stanley Sawyer publishes “Man-made Carbon Dioxide and the ‘Greenhouse’ Effect,” which accurately predicts global warming and summarizes what scientists know so far.
    • 1997

      CLIMATE CHANGE BECOMES A NATIONAL CONCERN

      The New York Times asks the question: “Just how urgent is the problem of climate change?” and the Kyoto Protocol is adopted by 192 countries.
    • 2014

      The U.N. Issues Warning on Climate Change

      A United Nations report states that “continued emission of greenhouse gases will cause further warming and long-lasting changes in all components of the climate system, increasing the likelihood of severe, pervasive and irreversible impacts for people and ecosystems.”
    • 2015

      Greenland Is Melting Away

      The New York Times reports on the alarming rate at which Greenland’s ice sheets are melting. In December, 195 nations reach a landmark accord, committing to cut the emission of planet-warming pollution.
    • 2050

      The Cost of Rising Tides

      A study published in the journal Nature predicts that flooding could cost coastal cities $60 billion a year by 2050. A recent report by the Environmental Justice Foundation predicts climate change could cause there to be 150 million "climate refugees.”
    • 2100

      The Poles Continue to Melt

      Global oceans continue to rise as the century comes to an end. In the Puget Sound region, models show an increase by 11 to 38 inches. According to National Geographic, one plausible scenario could put much of the tip of Florida under water.
    • 2500

      A Different World

      While various models predict different outcomes, there’s little doubt the world will be a much different place. Mass extinctions, reshaped coastlines and radically altered weather systems will create an Earth much different from our world today.

    In the series, the unlikely companions — Allanon, the last druid, Wil Ohmsford, the last of the heroic Shannara bloodline and Amberle Elessedil, an elf with a fraught relationship with her own people — are fighting to revive the Ellcrys Tree, which represents the life force of the Four Lands. But for contemporary humans, battling global warming isn’t quite so straightforward.

    “Globally, we need to figure out a way to reduce the amount of carbon in the atmosphere back down to 1970 levels,” says Rybczyk. “The only way that’s going to happen is by stopping the use of fossil fuels, use alternative forms of energy that don’t put carbon into the atmosphere.”

    Even if humans do somehow manage to reduce emissions to such levels, some scientists fear it may be too late to reverse the damage. According to the recent New York Times report, “Greenland Is Melting Away,” if Greenland’s ice sheets continue to melt at predicted rates, sea levels will rise by as much as 20 feet. The stark reality is that global climate change can be mitigated, but not reversed.

    IV. A Better Tomorrow

    “I was captivated by the idea that failure to pay sufficient attention to what is happening around us allows for things to happen that we don't intend.”

    TERRY BROOKS
    Author of the “Shannara” novels

    EXTENDED AUDIO

    Though the road ahead is tough, there’s still hope. Mike Dieterich, an environmental scientist and LEED (Leadership in Energy & Environmental Design)-certified professional who has advised governments and private enterprises on green energy issues, is focusing on creating a greener world by incorporating smarter technology into urban planning. Recently, he advised the Algerian government on creating a greener economy. “I talked about incorporating renewable energy and passive design strategies,” says Dieterich.

    Dieterich believes new technologies will spur economic growth and save lives in the future. “If we build in a smart way right now, we’re going to be saving lives [and] we’re going to be saving property,” he says.

    Concerns also surround the social cost of climate change, especially for people in developing nations. “In the wealthier parts of the world we have these safety nets, we have the resources to shift to address that,” says Oppermann. He says we need to set up international policies that can help people who will inevitably be displaced by rising sea levels and severe weather.

    Brooks hopes his story can serve as wake-up call to those still on the fence about climate change. “I was captivated by the idea that failure to pay sufficient attention to what is happening around us allows for things to happen that we don't intend,” he says.

    As for the real-life Four Lands, Rybcyzk speculates that the Pacific Northwest may not fare quite as badly as other parts of the world. “Compared to some other places, we may be relatively better off,” says Rybcyzk. “We don’t have an extremely cold climate or an extremely hot climate, so our energy needs could be less. We have the option to use hydroelectricity here, where other places in the world do not. We might see more and more people come to the Pacific Northwest as a refuge from climate change.”
