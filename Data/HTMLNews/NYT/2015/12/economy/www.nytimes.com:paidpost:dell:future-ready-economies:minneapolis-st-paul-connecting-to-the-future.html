    Minneapolis-St. Paul: Connecting to the Future

    Policies and programs that foster inclusion and connections build a strong economy that is well-positioned for continued growth.

    Watch the video below.

    The Minneapolis-St. Paul region spends much of the winter in a deep freeze, but its economy burns hot all year, recently outpacing national averages in unemployment, income and wage growth. It’s also home to Fortune 500 companies, research institutions and cultures from around the world.

    The success is built on a civic philosophy of inclusion and connection — two factors that propelled the Twin Cities to No. 12 in the Dell Future Ready Economies Model. The region’s infrastructure, which connects people with jobs and other opportunities, was cited as the best among the 34 U.S. metro areas surveyed.

    "When you look at the ingredients for communities that will continue to be successful going forward, we’re as well positioned as any community in the world," says Shaye Mandle, CEO of LifeScience Alley, a health care technology trade association.

    The Green Line light rail project, for instance, was designed from the start to improve access to jobs and spur development. Just six months after starting operations in 2013, ridership hit forecasts that had been set for 2030. The project has also led to an estimated $3 billion in new development along its 11 miles and 23 stations.

    "We understand the relationship between smart infrastructure and the economic development that follows," says Brian Lamb, general manager of Metro Transit.

    The Neighborhood Development Center provides other types of connections by providing financing and training to immigrants and others who might not have access to opportunities. Its entrepreneurs return $73 million annually to the regional economy.

    "One thing that stands out for me in training was that business is not just about making money but it's also about community," says Abdirahman Ahmed, owner of Safari Restaurant and Event Center.

    The region has long been a melting pot of cultures, with Scandinavian and Lutheran immigrants settling in Minneapolis, and French, Irish and German Catholics in St. Paul. Today, the Twin Cities has the largest Somali population in North America as well as a large Hmong population.

    Despite harsh winters, Minneapolis-St.Paul continues to attract people and new ideas, which make the region even more appealing to businesses. The virtuous cycle is a common trait among future-ready cities.

    "It’s the hardest place to recruit people to, but it’s also the hardest place to recruit people from," says Melodie Bahan, vice president of communications of ArtSpace, an organization that helps local artists find affordable space. "Once people get here, they don’t want to leave."
