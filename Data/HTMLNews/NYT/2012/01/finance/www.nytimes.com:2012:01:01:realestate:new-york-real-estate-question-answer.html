    Setting a Date for a Board Meeting

    Q At our last co-op meeting, we were about to start voting for board members. But the meeting was abruptly adjourned when the board president realized that because we hadn’t voted in several years, all current board members were up for re-election and the ballots did not reflect this. A short time later, we were given just six days’ notice of the new meeting date. What can shareholders do?

    A Sandor Krauss, a Manhattan co-op and condo lawyer, said the state’s Business Corporation Law requires that formal notice of the annual meeting be served upon each shareholder no more than 50 and no fewer than 10 days before the meeting. If a meeting starts and is then adjourned, and the date of the follow-up meeting is announced at that meeting, it is not necessary to give any minimum notice of the new meeting date.

    In this case, Mr. Krauss said, the new date was not announced at the first meeting, so the board failed to comply with state law requiring at least 10 days’ notice. If a board was elected at the second meeting, any shareholder who wishes to contest the validity of the vote can sue in court to nullify the election.

    Mortgages, Sponsors and Condominiums

    Q I have heard that if a co-op has a high percentage of sponsor-owned apartments, that could limit the shareholder’s ability to qualify for a mortgage under Fannie Mae guidelines. Is the same true for a condominium?

    A Oded Ben-Ami, a senior loan officer for Sterling National Bank in Great Neck, N.Y., said that for Fannie Mae to approve financing in a new condominium project, at least 70 percent of the units must be sold (or under contract to be sold) to “owner-occupiers.” Moreover, no single person or business entity, other than the sponsor, may own more than 10 percent of the units in the project.

    Alterations for the Disabled

    Q I have been a tenant in a rent-stabilized apartment for almost 30 years. I have had a succession of injuries and am unable to pull open any of the doors in the building, including the door to my apartment. I have tried to sublet so I can take time to recuperate, but the landlord has not been responsive to my prospective subtenants. Is the landlord required to alter the doors so I am able to get in and out?

    A “Under various antidiscrimination laws,” said Glenn H. Spiegel, a Manhattan real estate lawyer, “landlords are required to make a ‘reasonable accommodation’ for people with qualifying disabilities.” That requirement has been interpreted to mean that modifications presenting no undue hardship on a property owner must be made, but the tenant may be required to pay for that work.

    If the parties cannot agree on how to handle the situation or who will pay for it, the tenant should consult with a lawyer to determine whether a discrimination lawsuit or complaint with the appropriate human rights agency is an option.
