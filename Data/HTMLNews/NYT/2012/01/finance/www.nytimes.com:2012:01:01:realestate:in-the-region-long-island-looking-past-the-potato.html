    Looking Past the Potato

    By MARCELLE S. FISCHLERDEC. 29, 2011

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    ON a trip to Italy last year, Carol Festa and Tom Geppel of Mattituck savored the pecorino and ricotta in Tuscany. Back home on the North Fork, motivated by an increasing involvement with the sustainable farming and local food movement, they leased farmland and bought 13 hardy Icelandic sheep.

    “The intention is to make cheese,” said Ms. Festa, who spent many years in banking and finance. In late November, she and her husband jumped at the chance to buy a former potato farm on 27.5 undulating acres on Cox Lane in Cutchogue.

    “It was the perfect opportunity,” Ms. Festa said of the $1.075 million purchase from the nonprofit Peconic Land Trust. She and Mr. Geppel are taking cheese-making classes and learning to raise sheep. Their certified organic operation will eventually include wool products and meat.

    The new sheep farm is the latest of a growing number of diverse livestock operations on the North Fork, an area once best known for potatoes and, in recent years, for farm activities like “pick-your-own” orchards and fields.

    John v. H. Halsey, the president of the Peconic Land Trust, said there was a “growing interest in food production.” And Joseph M. Gergela III, the executive director of the Long Island Farm Bureau, said that of the 650 farms in Suffolk County, including horse farms, specialty crops, vegetables and horticulture, just over a dozen primarily raise livestock.

    Because of that activity, he said, he is exploring the possibility of some type of processing facility that might be of interest to local farmers.

    The number of duck farms has dwindled to one, but Browder’s Birds, a chicken farm in Southold, started in 2010, selling all its chickens the first two seasons and providing eggs to local farmers’ markets in the winter. For the last 10 years, Catapano’s Dairy Farm in Peconic has been making and selling goat cheese and skin-care products made with goat’s milk. At McCall Wines, a vineyard and 100-acre farm in Cutchogue, a herd of purebred Charolais, French beef cattle, graze in pastures alongside the grapevines. In Bridgehampton on the South Fork, Art Ludlow, a third-generation potato farmer, switched to making artisanal cheeses and has a herd of dairy cows. In Riverhead, bison are being raised for their low-fat, low-cholesterol meat; they graze pastures at the North Quarter farm.

    Mr. Halsey said the trust had acquired the Cox Lane farm property in 2007, paying $950,000 to protect it from development until a buyer committed to agricultural production could be found.

    Simultaneously, it sold the development rights on the farmland to the Town of Southold. The sale completed the trust’s “intended conservation cycle” and replenished its revolving fund to acquire other parcels that can be recycled for agricultural use. The process is part of its “Farms for the Future” initiative to provide both established and new farmers with access to affordable farmland. Over the last decade, despite the downturn, even protected farmland has been beyond the reach of most farmers.

    Chris and Holly Browder lease land from the Peconic Land Trust to grow their certified organic, pasture-raised meat chickens.

    “Farmland is very expensive on the North Fork, even if the development rights have been sold,” Mr. Browder, a former corporate investment banker, said in an e-mail message. Even so, “It’s our goal to own a farm here, so we’ll see what the future holds.”

    So far, with their successful sales, it is looking bright. This spring they intend to triple their laying flock, adding a few lambs and sheep as their “lead herbivore to mow the grass for the chickens,” and expand their line of spice rubs and dredging flour for frying.

     Stephen Searl, the Peconic Land Trust’s manager for the North Fork, cited two other parcels that the trust is trying to sell: a vegetable farm on Oregon Road in Southold and a defunct vineyard in Cutchogue. In both cases, development rights were sold to Southold Town through a community preservation fund.

    Preserved farmland covers 25.7 acres at the Festa place; the remaining 1.83 acres, considered a development area, are home to a 1930s farmhouse, a two-car garage, two side-by-side barns, and two additional outbuildings.

    Though the farmhouse is leased and the new sheep farmers plan to commute from their home five miles away to tend their flock, Ms. Festa hopes the sheep farm will provide the children with “a business that will give them the opportunity to stay in this community and have a business they can be part of.”

    Besides providing the community with local food, Ms. Festa said, their goal is to give their children, Max, 7, and Olivia, 11, a work ethic and an opportunity to “be involved in an active pursuit.”

    Instead of playing video games or running from a lacrosse game to a soccer match, they can “feel productive and feel like they are contributing to something greater in life.”

    Olivia, for instance, looked at the various breeds with her father and helped make a “decision on what sheep they wanted based on the genetics.”

    It is now mating season, and these sheep are known to bear twins and triplets and “multiply quickly,” Ms. Festa said. “If all goes well, we should have quite a few lambs running around in the springtime.”
