    Nila Do and Joshua Simon

    Vows

    By LINDA MARX MARCH 30, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    FOR Nila Do a good time is eating grilled chicken wings (she once ate 52 at one meal during her college days), playing soccer or flag football and watching University of Florida sporting events.

    She was 18 and a journalism student at Florida when she first met Joshua Simon, a 6-foot-1, athletic student who was pursuing a degree in finance. He was about as memorable as the water boy on the Gators’ sidelines.

    “I had zero impression of Josh other than he may be shy and too nervous to talk,” she said.

    They were with a small group of students at a Gainesville sports bar, and she didn’t exactly pique his interest either. “I thought she was sort of snobby,” remembered Mr. Simon about the slight, but not particularly delicate 5-foot-2 Ms. Do.

    About nine months later, in 2004, they met again at the Gainesville house of mutual friends. Mr. Simon was playing pool when Ms. Do overheard him talking about soccer. Since she had spent the previous summer studying Portuguese in Rio de Janeiro, Ms. Do, now 28 and the managing editor of nine regional magazines for the Gulfstream Media Group in Fort Lauderdale, blurted, “I was in Brazil when they won the World Cup!”

    “Whoa, that’s pretty cool,” said Mr. Simon, now 29, who played a lot of soccer and is the youngest of the three sons of Paz Simon, a nurse who was raised in the Philippines, and Dr. Arnold J. Simon, a West Palm Beach internist and geriatric specialist.

    Ms. Do, who also has two brothers, and Mr. Simon began talking and soon discovered how pervasive sports was in their lives. They began seeing each other about three times a week, always with a group of mutual friends, who were as into music as they were sports.

    “I thought Josh was a really good and kind-hearted guy, but we were friends at first,” she said. She even tried to set him up with one of her girlfriends. (“Too flashy,” he said.)

    “Slowly, organically, Nila and I realized how much we enjoyed each other,” he said of that time in early 2004. “I loved having deep conversations with her about religion, race and ethics. She challenged me intellectually. I had not found that before in anyone.”

    Ms. Do, who was born and raised in the Jacksonville, Fla., area, thought Mr. Simon was witty, modest, cute and caring, not a man who was full of himself. She wasn’t looking for a relationship, but they began to hang out daily. He would call her every night when she got off work at a sporting goods store.

    “There was no romance at the beginning,” she said, “until I started to enjoy the attention he gave me.”

    By May 2004, she became frustrated that he seemed to have no inclination to ramp up their relationship. The impatient Ms. Do, who that month had earned her journalism degree in three years, decided to make a move.

    “I made a sexual advance toward Josh but he rejected me,” she said. “It was such an embarrassing moment. I thought he wasn’t into me, which made me want him more.”

    Mr. Simon said he “cared about her and was attracted to her, but it was bad timing.” He was leaving in September for three months of study in Madrid and did not want to start an intimate relationship only to put it on hiatus until he returned. “I felt this was the right thing to do,” he said.

    The casual, if not nearly platonic, dating continued for a few weeks until Ms. Do left for Ann Arbor to watch her brother graduate from the University of Michigan. Mr. Simon was surprised by how much he missed her.

    When she returned, she gave him a Michigan T-shirt, at which point he grabbed her and kissed her — their first romantic, and definitely passionate, kiss. “Even though I didn’t want to get serious before I left for Spain, I did,” Mr. Simon said.

    Ms. Do cried every night for two weeks before his departure. Three months apart seemed unbearable. “I missed him so much that I lied to my parents and flew to Madrid to see him for 10 days,” she said. “I said I was going to Miami.”

    Her mother, Tien Do, a retired school teacher, and her father, Nho Do, a journalist, are immigrants from Vietnam. They thought it inappropriate of their daughter to show outward affection for a man in their presence. But others were beginning to see a perfect fit.

    “Nila is a tomboy and the best companion for Josh,” said Jeffrey Simon, a brother of Mr. Simon’s. “She is the sister version of a brother. She makes him very happy.”

    Ms. Do decided to pursue an M.B.A. so she could be in Gainesville when Mr. Simon returned from Spain to finish his last year of undergraduate study in finance. But Ms. Do helped him see that his heart wasn’t in it.

    “I loved medicine growing up in my dad’s shadow, but it was intimidating thinking I could be as good as dad,” he said. “She told me to go for the top, that I could do whatever I want.”

    In September 2005, he began studying toward a master’s in biomedical science at Florida Atlantic University in Boca Raton and was living nearby with his parents. Having received her M.B.A. that May, Ms. Do decided to move again. She went to Miami to take a job editing the alumni magazine for the medical school at the American University of the Caribbean in Coral Gables, Fla. There was still no specific plan to marry, though he knew it was inevitable.

    “She supported me, listened to me, and even moved down to South Florida for work to be there for me when I was studying,” Mr. Simon said. “That won me over.”

    Shortly after Ms. Do started her job with Gulfstream Media, in March 2006, the couple rented an apartment together in Fort Lauderdale. They played soccer and they joined a coed flag football team. At home at night, they made music — she is a classical pianist; he plays the saxophone. They traveled to Costa Rica and Canada, tackling adventure sports like white-water rafting and zip-lining.

    But they constantly disagreed over when to marry. “I felt extremely unsettled,” she said. “I’m driven and needed a plan.”

    He finally told her to “stop asking” about a wedding. They didn’t break up, but the issue remained a frustration.

    “Josh is cool, calm and long-term goal oriented,” said Grace Mercurio, Ms. Do’s maid of honor and a friend of Mr. Simon’s from the University of Florida. “He wanted to wait until he was more financially independent before proposing. Nila wasn’t thrilled about that.”

    Last March 16, Mr. Simon, who is now a fourth-year student in osteopathic medicine at Nova Southeastern University in Fort Lauderdale with graduation and a paid residency on the horizon, bought a dozen red roses and prepared a dinner of fresh salmon and asparagus. The normally laid-back Mr. Simon rushed through the meal and kept asking Ms. Do if she had finished.

    Before she could guess what he was about to do, Mr. Simon fell to his knee and proposed. Her nearly seven-year wait was over. “All of the years waiting made me love and appreciate him even more,” she said.

    In the two hours before the ceremony began on March 24, Ms. Do grew anxious — not about their wedding, but rather the Florida men’s basketball team, which was about to play Louisville for the right to advance to the N.C.A.A. Final Four.

    “Tipoff was 4:30 and the wedding was to begin at 6:30,” she said. “We were checking our smartphones for the score the entire time we were shooting portraits.”

    Mr. Simon checked the score one last time, then took his spot with his groomsmen.

    “The game ended around 6:40,” Ms. Do said. “What a way to start the wedding.”

    The Gators lost, but the couple’s disappointment faded quickly as Loretta Burbridge, a Florida notary public, led them in their vows before 105 people at the historic Bonnet House Museum and Gardens in Fort Lauderdale. And finally, the couple officially began their lives together.

    “Every day is a new adventure for her,” Mr. Simon said of his new, but still impatient, bride, “she keeps me from doing too much laying around. She gets me out of my comfort zone.”
