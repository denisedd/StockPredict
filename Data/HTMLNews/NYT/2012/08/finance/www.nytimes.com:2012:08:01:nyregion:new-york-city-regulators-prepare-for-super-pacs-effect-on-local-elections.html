    In 2013 Races, New York Prepares for ‘Super PAC’ Effect

    By DAVID W. CHENJULY 31, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    New York City campaign regulators are girding for a wave of independent spending in local campaigns next year, saying that moneyed interest groups and wealthy individuals are watching the “super PAC” phenomenon in the presidential campaign this year and preparing to adapt it for local use.

    The city will, for the first time, require disclosure of independent expenditures — spending that is not coordinated with a campaign. It said its tracking of the finances of such groups, which it plans to post on the Web, would be among the most detailed anywhere, including not only contributions and spending, but also an archive of advertisements run by the groups and information about their backers.

    City campaign finance officials said, however, that the Citizens United case and a related Supreme Court decision on Montana — both of which struck down limits on campaign spending by corporations and unions — made it clear that the city could only document, and not limit, independent spending on elections.

    “The Citizens United decision started a sea change in the culture of money in politics that will ultimately affect every level of government all across the country,” said Amy M. Loprest, executive director of the New York City Campaign Finance Board. “Outside spending in city elections was increasing even before Citizens United, and we expect the trend will only accelerate in the 2013 elections.”

    Over all, 59 major elected offices — mayor, comptroller, public advocate, the City Council and the borough presidents — are on the ballot next year, and in about half of the races, including the one for mayor, there will be no incumbent.

    And there are a large number of wealthy people, labor unions and business groups with both the means and the motivation to get involved in local campaigns. Chief among them is the departing mayor, Michael R. Bloomberg, a billionaire who already finances groups seeking to tighten gun laws, persuade more states to legalize same-sex marriage and fight global warming. The mayor’s top aide in Albany recently joined the staff of a group, StudentsFirstNY, that will seek to preserve key elements of Mr. Bloomberg’s education agenda even after he leaves office, and that group immediately got involved in a local Congressional race.

    Mr. Bloomberg’s spokesman said the mayor, a political independent, was not thinking about establishing his own super PAC, but there are many other potential independent spenders in New York’s campaigns. In July, a group of women active in national politics formed the first lesbian super PAC, LPAC, and in an interview, a leader of the group said they were interested in the potential mayoral candidacy of Christine C. Quinn, the City Council speaker. If elected mayor, Ms. Quinn, a Democrat, would be one of the most powerful and prominent gay elected officials in the country.

    “Our goal is to build a network, and it would be foolish for us not to look at Chris Quinn,” said Sarah Schmidt, the group’s chairwoman and treasurer, whose board includes Emily Giske, a lobbyist and close friend of Ms. Quinn. “We’ve been talking about this from the very beginning.”

    A prominent labor leader, Michael Mulgrew, president of the United Federation of Teachers, said he was “definitely open to conversations” about a local super PAC, “especially when you see that people on the other side who don’t like us” were willing to spend heavily to influence campaigns.

    “It’s a tool that somewhat tries to alleviate this money that’s pouring into politics,” he said.

    Of course, if the unions do get involved in the mayoral campaign, or other city races, that could provoke a campaign finance arms race of sorts, said John A. Catsimatidis, a supermarket magnate who is considering a mayoral run next year as a Republican.

    “It’s got to be a level playing field with the unions, and I’m sure they’ll be matched dollar for dollar,” he said. “So yes, I think it could be very important next year.”

    There has been a slow but steady increase in independent spending on local campaigns. In 2009, the Real Estate Board of New York raised more than $500,000, primarily from developers, to defend Democratic Council candidates who were facing opposition from people backed by labor unions or the left-leaning Working Families Party.

    In San Francisco last year, outside groups spent $2.6 million — up from $30,000 in the previous election — to influence the mayor’s race, with most (both for and against) going to the eventual winner, Mayor Edwin M. Lee.

    And in the June Congressional primaries in New York, a Democratic candidate from Queens, Assemblywoman Grace Meng, cruised to victory, thanks in part to more than $100,000 in spending by two outside groups, Women Vote! (an offshoot of Emily’s List) and the Humane Society. In another local Congressional primary, two independent groups — the Campaign for Primary Accountability and Campaign for Our Future — spent about $42,000 in an unsuccessful effort to defeat Representative Charles B. Rangel, a Democrat, while the New York League of Conservation Voters spent about $20,000 to support him.

    There is a huge potential upside for candidates who are aided by independent spending. New York has a voluntary campaign finance system, embraced by almost all candidates other than Mr. Bloomberg, that sharply limits spending by participating campaigns. But New York is also an expensive media market. As a result, campaigns can usually afford only a limited amount of television advertising on their own.

    But there is also a downside. Some candidates view the super PAC phenomenon as unseemly, or do not wish to be associated with the agenda of an outside interest group. Earlier this year, for example, Assemblyman Hakeem S. Jeffries, a Brooklyn Democrat running for Congress, asked StudentsFirstNY to cease campaigning on his behalf. (The group nonetheless spent $11,000 to support Mr. Jeffries, who won his party’s primary.)

    The issue of independent spending could be especially delicate for Bill de Blasio, the public advocate, who is looking at a run for mayor. Mr. de Blasio, a Democrat, has been an outspoken critic of the Citizens United ruling, but has also been closely aligned with labor unions.
