    The Art of Making Beer: Not Just for Professionals

    By SUSAN M. NOVICKMARCH 2, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    PORT JEFFERSON, N.Y.

    DOZENS of bottles of craft beer line one wall of Brewtopia of Port Jeff, the beer-themed general store that opened in the Harbor Square Mall here in April. More than 90 kinds of ales, lagers, porters and stouts represent microbreweries from around the world.

    Not only are they sold to the store’s thirsty clientele, but they also serve as inspiration for a growing number of people who make their own beer, said Mark Romano, 49, who owns Brewtopia with his partner, Carolyn Coddington. The couple previously owned the Loyal Dog Ale House in Lindenhurst, which they sold about a year and a half ago to start their new venture, Mr. Romano said.

    Brewtopia stocks beer-making kits, brewing equipment and raw ingredients like grain, yeast and bitters, as well as flavorings like orange and lemon peel, juniper berries and chicory root.

    “There are four basic ingredients: hops, malt, yeast, water,” Mr. Romano said. “If you can make toast, you can brew beer.”

    Alan Talman, 53, the owner of Karp’s Homebrew Shop in East Northport, as well as the hardware store, Karp’s Hardware, in which it is situated, has been selling home-brewing supplies and equipment since 1994. He has seen a sharp rise in interest on Long Island in the past few years, he said.

    “It used to be a single-male-household hobby,” said Mr. Talman, who now sees more couples brewing together.

     “These are people who bake bread on Thursday and brew beer on Friday,” he said. “They’re looking for stuff that’s interesting to serve to their friends.”

      Interest in the topic extends all the way to the White House, Mr. Talman said: When the Obamas served their chefs’ honey ale at a Super Bowl party last year, the writer of the Obama Foodorama blog called Mr. Talman for background on home brewing.

    Some hobbyists become so adept that they parlay their beer into a business. Michael Philbrick, 36, who recently taught a brewing class at Brewtopia, is one of them. He began as a home brewer in 2001, and opened the Port Jeff Brewing Company, a seven-barrel, 1,000-square-foot production center, in October.

    Newcomers to home brewing, like Christopher Petty, 24, of East Setauket, who shops at Brewtopia, often begin with an inexpensive kit and follow the instructions for cooking, fermenting and carbonating the beer.

    “It takes some trial and error, and things do go wrong,” Mr. Petty said. “You learn what to do next time.”

    Since a batch of homemade beer typically yields five gallons, many hobbyists brew in groups so they can share the experience and the product. Such is the case with Rocky Point Artisan Brewers, which began in 2006 as a home-brewing partnership between Donavan Hall and Mike Voigt. They later added a third partner, Yuri Janssen; all live in Rocky Point.

    They often brew with a group of friends — “a stable of five or six others,” Mr. Hall, 43, said — and they plan to continue that tradition even if, as they hope, the brewery is licensed to sell its products commercially in the coming year.

    Continue reading the main story

    “A lot of people get involved in home brewing because they want to be connected,” Mr. Hall, 43, said. “Beer is a great equalizer. It creates community.”

    Mr. Hall is also a founder of Long Island Beer and Malt Enthusiasts, an organization formed in 2007. With about 300 members, the group holds monthly meetings with guest speakers and local food producers, as well as beer and Scotch tastings.

    One former hobbyist who has become a professional “nanobrewer,” as those who make beer in very small batches are called, is Paul Dlugokencky, 52, of Centerport, a home brewer since 2003. “I just fell in love with brewing,” he said. “It’s almost like being a cook or a chef.”

    In 2008, Mr. Dlugokencky established Blind Bat Brewery, initially making 10 gallons at a time. He now has a three-barrel brewing operation in his 350-square-foot former garage, a separate building behind his house. Its signature beers include Hellsmoke Porter (made with grains smoked over apple wood and alder wood) and Long Island Potato Stout (in which local organic potatoes are added to the mash).

    Although Mr. Dlugokencky still works in publishing at the American Institute of Physics in Melville, he hopes to start brewing full time this year as the business grows. He sells his products wholesale to bars and restaurants and retail at beverage stores and farmers’ markets.

    Mr. Philbrick, the owner of the Port Jeff Brewing Company, has made the transition to full-time brewery entrepreneur. He offers eight beers on tap in his nautical-themed tasting room in Port Jefferson Village. Tastings are free; no beer is sold for consumption on the premises, but all can be bought for takeout by the “growler,” a recyclable 64-ounce glass jug.

    Most of Mr. Philbrick’s signature beers are based on his home brew recipes, like his Schooner Ale, Oatmeal Stout and Port Jeff Porter, flavored with local honey.

    At least 10 percent of the customers who walk into his tasting room are home brewers, he said, and they often bring him their own beers to taste. “Generally all brewers, whether it be a home brewer or a pro brewer, like to share information,” he added.

    Mr. Philbrick is to participate in the Spring Craft Beer Festival at the Nassau Veterans Memorial Coliseum in Uniondale on March 10. Volunteers from the Long Island Beer and Malt Enthusiasts will be there, too, offering samples of more than a dozen home brews.

    “You no longer have to be professional to brew great beer,” said Richard Thatcher, 47, of Bellport, a founder of the Enthusiasts. “So many people come up to me and say, ‘Where can I buy this?’ And I say: ‘You can’t. But if you have some time, I can show you how to make it.’ ”
