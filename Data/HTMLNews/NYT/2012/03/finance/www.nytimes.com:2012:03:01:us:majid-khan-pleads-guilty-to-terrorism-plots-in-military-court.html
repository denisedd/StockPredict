    Testimony on Al Qaeda Is Required in Plea Deal

    By SCOTT SHANEFEB. 29, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    FORT MEADE, Md. — Majid Khan, who joined Al Qaeda after graduating from high school outside Baltimore, pleaded guilty on Wednesday in a deal requiring him to testify against Khalid Shaikh Mohammed, the architect of the Sept. 11, 2001 attacks, and other terrorism suspects.

    At a hearing in the military court at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba, Mr. Khan, 32, admitted to plotting with Mr. Mohammed in 2002 to blow up gasoline tanks in the United States, agreeing to assassinate the Pakistani president and delivering $50,000 that was used to finance the bombing of a Marriott hotel in Jakarta, Indonesia, in 2003, killing 11 people. The hearing was broadcast at Fort Meade on a live video feed.

    A document entered in the military court added one striking detail to Mr. Khan’s story: In early 2002, when Mr. Khan’s family was discussing his possible marriage to the daughter of a well-connected Pakistani general, Mr. Khan spoke secretly with Mr. Mohammed about sneaking a suicide bomber into the wedding if Pervez Musharraf, then the president, was a wedding guest. The marriage did not take place, and the scheme went nowhere.

    Mr. Khan did not meet Mr. Mohammed until 2002, and is not charged in the Sept. 11 plot.

    Under a plea agreement, Mr. Khan, a Pakistani native who had lived legally in the United States, will face a maximum sentence of 19 years in a military prison starting Wednesday if he cooperates with the government, in addition to the almost nine years he has already been detained. But sentencing was postponed for four years, and Mr. Khan could argue for a lesser sentence in 2016.

    “I’m making a leap of faith here, sir,” Mr. Khan told the military judge, Col. James L. Pohl, with a nervous laugh. “That’s all I can do.”

    One of Mr. Khan’s lawyers, J. Wells Dixon, later repeated allegations that his client had been tortured at a secret Central Intelligence Agency prison overseas. “He was tortured very badly,” Mr. Dixon said, calling on the government to acknowledge its responsibility as Mr. Khan had acknowledged his own.

    But when Mr. Khan mentioned the C.I.A. during the hearing, the authorities at Guantánamo cut off the video feed and sound for fear that he might reveal classified information.

    The plea appeared to be an important step forward for the government’s troubled, decade-long attempt to convict and sentence important Qaeda figures for Sept. 11 and other attacks.

    Under rules revised by Congress in 2009, the military commissions, as the tribunals are known, resemble regular federal criminal trials far more closely than the original version authorized by President George W. Bush in 2001, though some civil libertarians say they fall short of that standard.

    Brig. Gen Mark S. Martins, the chief prosecutor at Guantánamo, told reporters after the hearing that he believed the case would help demonstrate to the world that military commissions, now under new rules, can provide a full measure of justice. “This system is fair,” he said, saying it was a “false choice” to claim that only military or civilian trials could provide an adequate outcome.

    In an interview from Guantánamo, Andrea Prasow, senior counterterrorism counsel at Human Rights Watch, called Mr. Khan’s plea “a tremendous win for the government.” She said it allows prosecutors to claim an important conviction, potentially will make it easier to prosecute others — and does not force the government to discuss the alleged mistreatment of Mr. Khan while in C.I.A. custody.

    Based on military documents, it appears Mr. Khan could be a valuable witness against Mr. Mohammed; Riduan Isamuddin, more commonly known as Hambali, the former leader of Al Qaeda in Southeast Asia; Ammar al-Baluchi, Mr. Mohammed’s nephew; and possibly others. Whether he could offer testimony relevant to the Sept. 11 attacks is unclear.

    Wearing a dark suit, white shirt and tie, the husky Mr. Khan wore wire-rimmed glasses, neatly trimmed hair and a small chin beard, not the full beard favored by many other Qaeda members, including Mr. Mohammed.

    He spoke confidently, addressing the judge repeatedly as “sir,” in barely accented American English, a reflection of six years in the United States, where he moved with his family in 1996 as a teenager. He graduated from Owings Mills High School in a Baltimore suburb in 1999 but never took American citizenship.

    Asked by Colonel Pohl whether he believed it was in his interest to plead guilty to all the charges — including murder, material support for terrorism, spying and conspiracy — Mr. Khan replied in a loud voice, “No doubt, sir.”

    Mr. Khan’s defense team, including a military lawyer, Lt. Col. Jon S. Jackson, and two civilian lawyers, Mr. Dixon and Katya Jestin, sought unsuccessfully to keep secret some parts of the seven-page plea agreement, saying the passages might put at risk unnamed people overseas. The prosecutors, also a military-civilian team, included Courtney Sullivan of the Justice Department and Lt. Col. Michael Hosang and Lt. Nathaniel Gross.

    But Colonel Pohl said the fact that Mr. Khan was now cooperating with the government was “basically common knowledge” and that the redactions requested would not reduce any risk of retaliation against others, presumably relatives or friends in Pakistan, including Mr. Khan’s wife and daughter.

    As Colonel Pohl led Mr. Khan through each of the many facts asserted in the charging document, he sometimes seemed rattled or confused by the legalese.

    After consulting his lawyers, he accepted the judge’s explanation that he could still be part of the hotel bombing conspiracy, even though he had been captured in Thailand in March 2003, five months before the attack.

    Likewise, he eventually accepted that he was legally considered to have been part of a conspiracy with the founder of Al Qaeda, Osama bin Laden, even though “I’ve never met him, obviously.”
