    Who Needs Characters to Be Likable?

    ‘Bachelorette,’ by Leslye Headland, Gets Wider Release

    By BROOKS BARNESAUG. 31, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    LOS ANGELES

    “BACHELORETTE” is a smart, chainsaw-edge comedy about friendship and partying past your prime that features an exceptional Kirsten Dunst as the wedding-party equivalent of Hannibal Lecter. It’s darkly hilarious, and you’ll love it.

    “Bachelorette,” written and directed by Leslye Headland, is a snotty, unfunny mess, a crass attempt to force-feed “Bridesmaids” to an art-house audience. Gay guys will probably like it (gee, thanks, Variety), but film fans with taste will recoil.

    So which is it?

    Big studios are in the business of parading pretty cinematic floats: safe, uncontroversial, consumable on a global scale. But independent films — those like “Bachelorette” that cost about $3 million to make and showcase a specific artistic vision — are supposed to be fought over. Starting a “cultural conversation” not only gets you noticed, it’s also a crucial part of what directors are trying to achieve; messy is O.K. as long as there are strong ideas to chew on.

    “Bachelorette,” which co-stars Lizzy Caplan and Isla Fisher, had its premiere at the Sundance Film Festival this year. Would Ms. Headland have preferred universally gushy reviews? Probably. “Bachelorette” is her baby. But is this first-time director also at ease with the acutely mixed reception it did receive? Totally.

    “The point is to start a discussion, not solve a problem,” Ms. Headland, 31, said over lunch. “Look at ‘Virgin Suicides.’ Look at ‘Fight Club.’ You don’t think back on those movies and think, ‘Aww, everyone just loved and adored them.’ There were people who absolutely hated those movies. And that’s fine because it means they challenged the audience.”

    She added, “I wanted to make a comedy that was really confrontational right out of the gate, which is going to make some people uncomfortable.”

    As is often the case with Sundance movies, she has also had the chance to make changes, including re-editing, and fleshing out the characters.

    The frustration, Ms. Headland said, hacking into a fried green tomato, involves the manner in which cultural discourse is increasingly boiled down to sharp blacks and whites. RottenTomatoes.com, the review aggregation site, declares movies “fresh” or “rotten,” with no middle ground and sometimes only a critic or two making the difference. A growing number of moviegoers skip professional reviewers altogether in favor of their Twitter feeds, where nuance is next to impossible.

    “See it. Skip it. See it. Skip it,” Ms. Headland said, tucking her streaked hair behind both ears and making a face. “Depressing.”

    Her unruffled demeanor about the Sundance reaction may reflect how grateful she is for what “Bachelorette” has already brought her. She wrote it as a play in 2007 — four years before the release of “Bridesmaids,” ahem — and, under the direction of Trip Cullman, the production became a smash hit Off Broadway in 2010. Hollywood doors have already opened; Will Ferrell and the veteran screenwriter Adam McKay produced the film version of “Bachelorette” and are working with Ms. Headland to develop other television and movie ideas.

    “We love that Leslye is this great big ball of energy: intrepid and wicked smart, with a loud and powerful comedic voice,” Mr. McKay said.

    Only a few years ago Ms. Headland, a motormouth with a warm, thunderous laugh, was working as an assistant to Harvey Weinstein, when he was running Miramax. (She playfully lampooned him in a play, “Assistance,” which had a successful run at Playwrights Horizons this year.) On Sept. 7, Mr. Weinstein’s new label, Radius-TWC, will simultaneously release “Bachelorette” in 20 cities and via cable and satellite on-demand systems.

    Radius released “Bachelorette” in mid-August on iTunes to start building buzz, and the comedy quickly became the online store’s No. 1 movie rental. It centers on the reunion of four friends, one of whom, the zaftig and humdrum Becky, is getting married. Becky (Rebel Wilson, who played one of Kristen Wiig’s weird roommates in “Bridesmaids”) is progressing deeper into adulthood, but her prettier, more put-together friends (at least on the outside) are stuck somewhere between college and their 10,000th line of cocaine. James Marsden, Kyle Bornheimer and Adam Scott star as randy groomsmen.

    “Girls don’t normally get to act this way on film,” Ms. Dunst said in a telephone interview. “The drug use in particular is unusual. So that’s one reason I wanted to do it. Plus, it was really fun to play the bitch for once.” Ms. Dunst’s character gets high, has sex in a strip club bathroom and fires insults with machine-gun efficiency.

    Ms. Headland’s unvarnished depiction of women comes amid a boomlet of similarly themed movies and television shows. Aside from “Bridesmaids,” which took in more than $288 million at the global box office, there was Diablo Cody’s “Young Adult,” staring Charlize Theron as a spoiled, alcoholic writer. HBO has “Girls,” the raw dramedy about four Brooklyn women and their friends. But the backers of “Bachelorette” don’t love those comparisons, in part because they worry it makes Ms. Headland’s movie come across as derivative and narrow.

    “It ghettoizes us in an unfair way,” said Tom Quinn, co-president of Radius. “I can relate to Leslye’s movie in a way I couldn’t relate to ‘Bridesmaids.’ ” Mr. Quinn and his Radius counterpart, Jason Janego, acknowledge “Bridesmaids” as one comparison, but also point to movies like “The Hangover” and “Heathers” as reference points.

    People who saw the stage version of “Bachelorette” may have a hard time picturing Ms. Headland’s material as a movie at all. The play was set entirely in one room and functioned mostly as a dark character study. “I never would have imagined it as a movie,” said Mr. Scott, who saw the stage version during its New York run. “Now it doesn’t feel like it was ever written as a play. Usually you can tell when material is adapted, but Leslye really opened it up.”

    How did Ms. Headland, who studied directing and acting at New York University, go about reworking “Bachelorette” as a movie? “This makes me sound a little gross,” she said, “but I actually wrote them simultaneously. The play would have made a terrible movie. Even in the first incarnations of the screenplay it was about making something very different.”

    An agent told her that she needed a sample screenplay and so, as she was writing the play, she also worked on a cinematic version, adding more male characters, warming up the humor and moving plot more front and center. For instance the movie has a lengthy chase-type sequence involving Becky’s soiled wedding gown and the efforts of her self-absorbed friends to fix it. (Well, they try to help, in between using the frilly garment to clean up semen.)

    In 2008 Ms. Headland, living in Los Angeles and working at a video rental store, learned that her “Bachelorette” screenplay had made the Black List, an independent ranking of the year’s best unproduced scripts. Ms. Headland, raised by strict religious parents in Maryland and Connecticut, became known overnight in Hollywood for a foul-mouthed, drug-fueled movie project. “The resounding response was ‘great voice, very funny, no one is ever going to make it,’ ” she recalled.

    It wasn’t because the film was too R-rated or even because it was R-rated and about women — or at least that’s what studio executives told her. “I kept getting told that none of my characters were likable,” Ms. Headland said. “Likable? That had never occurred to me. Yeah, these women do some bad things, but I like them. And even if they weren’t likable, why does that make them uninteresting?”

    With independent financing from BCDF Pictures and Mr. Ferrell and Mr. McKay’s producing clout, Ms. Headland was able to bring her deeply flawed characters to the screen as she envisioned, despite her lack of directing experience. Now audiences get to decide how much likability matters. “Visceral responses are good,” Ms. Headland said, circling back to her earlier theme. “Go for it! Fight it out amongst yourselves!”
