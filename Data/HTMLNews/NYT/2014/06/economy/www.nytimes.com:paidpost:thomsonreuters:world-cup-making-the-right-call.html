    World Cup: Making the Right Call

    To pick the tournament’s best economic bets, it pays to ignore conventional thinking and dig a little deeper

    By Adam Aston

    When it comes to predicting the World Cup, many fans stick with tried-and-true bets. Going into the games, Brazil was the short-odds favorite to nab its sixth world title. Argentina’s Lionel Messi — the greatest soccer player in the world — was the early pick for this tournament’s top scorer (graphic).

    But savvy sports fans know to look past the obvious options. Unexpected twists can turn routine games into legendary matches and overlooked players into new superstars.

    That’s why, in an era when practically every play is statistically tracked, some fans are scanning those data for clues to a smarter call. Perhaps Brazil will be off its game, or Messi’s golden boot will tarnish.

    At trading desks and in newsrooms around the globe, market watchers face a similar challenge in evaluating Brazil’s economic prospects.

    Sinking forecasts

    It seems like a no-brainer, for example, that the makers of World Cup-related stuff — from TVs to beer to shoes — are likely to see sales rise regardless of the games’ final victor.

    After two years of anemic growth for big TV brands, they’re optimistic that the games will boost global sales by a third or more. In Brazil, wholesale orders of flat-panel LCD TVs surged by 51 percent in the first quarter, year on year, according to NDP DisplaySearch.

    Similarly, Anheuser-Busch InBev, the world’s biggest brewer and parent of Budweiser, Stella Artois, and Corona, expects the games to boost 2014 beer sales by up to two percentage points in Brazil, the world’s third-largest beer market.

    Still, despite early optimism that the World Cup would give Brazil’s economy a steep lift, sentiment has since turned negative.

    Including public spending and indirect and induced effects — such as increased tax revenue, higher wages and jobs growth — the games will deliver 142 billion reals, or $64 billion, in additional economic output, according to Ernst & Young. Last year, the government had predicted a boost to full-year GDP of 0.5 percentage points.

    Yet rising inflation — prices in Brazil have ticked up 6.2 percent this year — and high infrastructure costs have dampened the expectations of both economists and the public. By May, the consensus forecast for the games’ impact on GDP had been more than halved, to just 0.2 percentage points, according to a Reuters poll.

    Counter indicators?

    Dig a bit deeper, though, and it turns out each of these examples offers a reminder that near-term consensus thinking can obscure long-term insights.

    Take TVs. The World Cup effect, in some markets, may be nothing more than a shift in the timing of spending as buyers rush to upgrade sets that were already due for replacement rather than representing real growth in total sales. During World Cup years in the U.K., according to market research firm GfK, then slump after the games end.

    Brewers may face a similar hangover. Given the World Cup’s unequaled visibility,top brands pour cash into massive marketing efforts that can wipe out the profits from added sales. In the first quarter, InBev’s sales in Brazil surged by 11 percent, year on year, but sales and marketing costs expanded even more, by 17 percent, driven in part by World Cup spending. Core profits were $3.88 billion — $100 million short of consensus — according to a Reuters poll.

    What, then, are the counter-intuitive signs of Brazil’s economic prospects?

    To be sure, there’s historical precedent that the World Cup and other big events themselves don’t guarantee a boom. Consider the centennial Olympiad in Athens. The 2004 games left the government saddled with billions in debt and an array of white-elephant sports facilities. As Greece’s economy toppled into crisis starting in 2010, Olympics-related debt came under special scrutiny.

    For advanced, open economies like Greece’s, “such events are almost all wasteful,” Andre Rose, an economics professor at the University of California at Berkeley and co-author of a study on the Olympiad’s effects for the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER), told Time.com. Debt could end up being a problem for Brazil as well. The federal government and public banks are borrowing nearly two-thirds of the games’ $11 billon price tag.

    Beyond the noise

    Yet history is not destiny. And quarterly metrics are a poor measure of the structural benefits that can flow from the spending surges associated with big sporting events. Economists reckon that investments in roads, public transit, and other infrastructure generate roughly $2 in additional GDP for every dollar invested.

    These gains are notoriously slow to manifest, however, since it can take years for businesses and consumers to adjust — whether that means switching to freight-rail delivery thanks to new tracks or moving to a new home because of upgraded transit availability.

    In this context, Barcelona’s experience after its Olympic games offers an alternative to Athens’ struggles. The Spanish city used the 1992 Summer Games as a catalyst for infrastructure investment and urban renovation that lifted the Mediterranean city out of an era of post-industrial stasis. In the period from its first bid to hosting the games, “Barcelona would go from depression to economic boom,” concludes Autonomous University of Barcelona economist Ferran Brunet’s assessment of the games’ impact.

    Brazil’s World Cup spans a much larger footprint (map) so it could deliver an even bigger boost. And as with Barcelona, the government hopes that pouring capital into airports, roads, and other infrastructure will spur growth in multiple regions across the country.

    Trade is another, somewhat surprising, long-term beneficiary of welcoming worldwide sports competitions. Rose’s NBER study found that within a decade or so of the event, hosts of the Olympics and the World Cup see long-term gains in total trade on the order of 30 percent. These high-profile international celebrations, he concludes, tend to be pivot points, after which governments turn away from closed, regulated economic policy toward more open, liberal trading regimes.

    Brazil is certainly ripe for reform. The country currently ranks in the bottom quintile of trade openness, according to the International Chamber of Commerce — the lowest-positioned member of the G20. Brazil’s bureaucracy is also legendarily bloated. It can take more than four months and a dozen procedures to register a business. This no doubt contributes to Brazil coming in at 116 among the 189 countries in the World Bank’s survey of “ease of doing business.”

    Visibility is currency

    Whether or not the international attention provided by the World Cup spurs Brazil toward trade reform over the long run, there’s further evidence that a higher global profile can deliver near-term benefits to host economies. In a recent research note, Goldman Sachs found that when comparing the equity-market performance of World Cup victors to runners-up, the winner outperforms global markets, on average, by 3.5 percent in the month following its win. That margin fades over time, however. By a year or so later, runners-up take the lead, on average, with returns exceeding those of the victors’ post-triumph month.

    Brazil may not win 2014’s World Cup, of course. And while losing would be a bitter disappointment, it wouldn’t necessarily be a recipe for any long-term demise in soccer — or in the markets.

    The first time Brazil hosted the games, in 1950, its team made it all the way to the final. More than 174,000 fans — the most in World Cup history — watched the match, which Brazil lost in the final minutes, to Uruguay, 2-1.

    Instant analysis might have been dismissive of Brazil’s future potential. Yet the trauma seemed to steel the nation’s resolve. In its next two appearances in the finals, in 1958 and 1962, Brazil came from behind to triumph.

    The takeaway? In sports, as well as in markets, short-term prognostications may not yield savviest bets. More disciplined training can produce winning teams. And deeper thinking can make it easier to ignore conventional wisdom and make smarter decisions.
