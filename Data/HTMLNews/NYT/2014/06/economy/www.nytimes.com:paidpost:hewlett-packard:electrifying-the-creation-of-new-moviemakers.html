    Electrifying the Creation of New Moviemakers

    To cultivate emerging film talent, the Sundance Institute is turning to web-based technologies

    By Jaydine Sayer

    At a time when media companies conduct massive market research to pick and promote even low-budget projects, there’s at least one organization that’s still choosing projects the old-fashioned way — picking great, original and unique stories.

    Content is still king at the Sundance Institute, the nonprofit founded in 1981 by Robert Redford to support independent film and theater artists. The Institute is also the force behind the eponymous film festival held every January in Utah.

    “We believe it’s the powerful, authentic stories that end up resonating with audiences and take them places they didn’t even know they wanted to go,” says Keri Putnam, executive director of the Sundance Institute.

    That doesn’t mean the nonprofit shuns the type of cutting-edge technology you’d find at big film studios. In fact, Sundance embraces — even pursues — new media platforms and innovations to help engage audiences for its films, documentaries and even new-media products that defy definition.

    While Sundance prioritizes the artistic and social impact of its films over their commercial promise, it’s quick to adopt new technologies that help create passionate audiences willing to spend money.

    Crowdfunding as a tool and a barometer

    “Kickstarter, Indiegogo and other innovative funding platforms are really transformative for independent projects of a risk-taking nature, the kind we support at Sundance,” says Putnam. “We see people not only raising money for projects; they also develop an early fan base around their work.”

    These crowdfunding sites serve a dual purpose — to fund films and to market them. When a film comes out, supporters often spread the word about a project, acting as ambassadors for it, Putnam explains.

    In particular, Kickstarter is making major waves in the film biz. Seven Kickstarter-funded films have been nominated for Academy Awards. Roughly 10 percent of the films at this year’s Sundance Film Festival were funded by Kickstarter, and nearly $215 million has been pledged to more than 14,000 independent film projects since the site’s 2009 inception. Sundance was an early collaborator with Kickstarter and, through its curated page, has worked with filmmakers to raise funds for more than 220 films.

    “Crowdfunding sites are directly connected to what we call the ”˜maker economy,’ and it’s growing momentum exponentially,” says Sarah DaVanzo, chief cultural strategy officer at the trend-spotting agency Sparks & Honey.

    “What’s cool about Kickstarter-like sites is that they match people who have a goal with people who want to be part of something innovative,” adds DaVanzo. “So two needs are being met, and we see no signs of the trend slowing down.”

    At least one big studio has also used Kickstarter as a barometer for audience interest. Last year Warner Brothers tested the viability of a “Veronica Mars” film by challenging creator Rob Thomas to prove fan interest via a $2 million fund-raising goal on Kickstarter. The goal was met and the movie was debuted earlier this year.

    Amazon is also getting in on the crowdsourcing game. In early 2014, the online retail giant asked customers to choose which of 10 original-content pilot television shows it should produce by leaving comments and ratings online.

    DaVanzo says participating in crowdfunding gives people a chance to do something creative, even if only in a monetary way. “People want to be part of something special,” she says.

    “Kickstarter is a place where you can begin to talk meaningfully about audience demand,” says Putnam. “You can immediately see if there is an audience out there responding to what you’re saying,” she says, “the artist can then talk directly to that audience and find out what it is they are excited about and why. And then the artist can run toward that if they choose.”

    Supercharging digital distribution

    Distribution of films is a huge challenge for Sundance, especially since many of its artists still hope for a big deal with a professional film distributor. When those deals don’t happen — not everyone gets tapped by Harvey Weinstein — Sundance turns to its powerful partners.

    Creating partnerships with media companies who excel at distribution is one tactic Sundance takes to help. The Institute has deals in place with Netflix, iTunes and Hulu, among others, to provide access for its filmmakers to make their films available for download or streaming, a growing trend.

    According to a report by Nielsen, consumer transactions for subscription streaming increased 10 percent in 2013, while video-on-demand transactions grew by 6 percent — the second consecutive year that at-home entertainment spending has risen.

    The Institute is also forging partnerships with emerging companies with innovative technology tools: “Companies that offer a way to stream your film, market your film, talk to your fan base, sell your merchandise, book a theatrical distribution tour — all in one place,” says Putnam.

    Two companies, Tugg and Gathr, are already helping artists connect with viewers who want to go to the cinema to experience a film. These companies use crowdsourcing to book theaters around the country, so artists can essentially plan an entire theatrical campaign, on demand, directly with fans.

    Riding the e-wave

    It’s hard to champion new technology if you don’t know about it.

    “We have a team dedicated to consuming digital content and thinking about the ways audiences are receiving and talking about it,” says Putnam. But it’s not just this team that fosters discovery of new technology — it’s part of Sundance’s culture.

    “All the curious, creative people at Sundance use new tools to think about content all the time,” she says. “We made it a value to bring these conversations into the office.”

    Artists themselves can teach organizations a lot about new technology. For instance, artists including Nonny de la Peña, Danfung Dennis and Chris Milk used the Oculus Rift, a 3-D virtual reality headset, in their work exhibited at the film festival’s New Frontier program in recent years. Sundance Film Festival was one of the first places audiences could experience stories in this medium. New Frontier is a platform that fosters projects that push the boundaries of definition — often innovative, multi-platform media concepts, games or installations that cross boundaries between film, art, music, performance and technology.

    “The earliest creative work with Oculus started at Sundance,” says Putnam. “The technology was prototyped with de la Peña at the New Frontier exhibition at the 2012 festival. Dennis and Milk have also demonstrated what virtual reality — which is often associated with video games — could become in an artist’s hands.” In March, Facebook bought Oculus VR for $2 billion, potentially providing the technology a far larger platform.

    For all the work Sundance does to educate artists about the brave new world of digital media, the flow goes two ways. Tech-savvy artists and art-minded technologists have never had a more fertile moment to inspire one another and push the boundaries of creativity.
