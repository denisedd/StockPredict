    What Kinds of Energy We Use and What We Use it For

    The rectangles represent the primary fuels the U.S. consumes, before they are refined into useful forms — before coal is combusted to make electricity, for example. In this rendering, known as a Sankey diagram, the colored paths lead from left to right, showing how the energy flows to different sectors. Thicker paths represent a larger share of that energy type. To make it possible to compare outwardly different energy sources — how a barrel of oil compares to the output of rooftop solar panels — all energy types are converted into a common measure: BTUs, or British thermal units.

    In the middle section, the focus shifts to how the U.S. uses primary energy. Larger rectangles show which parts of the economy are the most energy intensive. Industry, for example, is far more energy hungry than households or businesses. Electricity is a special case since it is a medium, not a kind of fuel. Thus, natural gas or wind can all be transformed into electric power, which is then consumed to power computers, drive motors and the like.

    Powering the U.S. economy requires just shy of 100 quadrillion BTUs, or “quads”, of energy, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration (E.I.A.). Less than half of that total is transformed into useful services, such as making steel or propelling planes. The remainder — over half of primary energy consumption — is wasted. This “rejected energy” results from countless points of inefficiency, such as the heat a light bulb gives off rather than light.
