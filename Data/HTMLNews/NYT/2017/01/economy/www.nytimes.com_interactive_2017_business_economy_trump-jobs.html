    Should Trump Get Credit for All Those Jobs?

    By THE NEW YORK TIMES UPDATED May 2, 2017

    President Trump regularly lauds his ability to bring back American jobs, an argument he made during his campaign for the White House and has continued since taking office. Below is a look at the companies that have changed plans, announced new ones or stood their ground after catching the president’s attention. Related Article

    The equation here appears simple. President Trump takes aim at a company for moving jobs out of the United States. Soon after, the company says that it will be investing billions back home, creating or saving scores of jobs.

    Ford, Toyota and General Motors were such targets, and subsequently — and very publicly — announced plans to invest in domestic production.

    General Motors is sending Mexican made model of Chevy Cruze to U.S. car dealers-tax free across border. Make in U.S.A.or pay big border tax!

    — Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) January 3, 2017

    But it is not always so simple. In some cases, the announcements tell only part of the story. On the day of Mr. Trump’s inauguration, G.M. followed through on different plans it had announced several months earlier: It cut about 2,000 jobs at plants in Michigan and Ohio. Ford’s moves will have little net effect on American jobs because of restructuring it is doing elsewhere.

    One big reason so many companies have moved jobs overseas is that the economics of manufacturing have vastly shifted. Carrier, which makes air conditioners and furnaces, had planned to close two Indiana factories and move roughly 2,000 jobs to Mexico, where labor is far cheaper.

    After meeting with Mr. Trump, though, Carrier said it would keep about half of the jobs. One inducement for the company is $7 million in incentives from the state, which happens to be where the Vice President Mike Pence is from.

    For some companies, the new president has meant the possibility of more favorable operating conditions, and has given them the occasion to tout projects that they had been weighing — or even had already been developing.

    Fiat Chrysler, for example, said before the president’s inauguration that it would create 2,000 jobs as part of plans to invest $1 billion in its plants in Michigan and Ohio — plans it said were under consideration long before Mr. Trump won the presidency.

    It's finally happening - Fiat Chrysler just announced plans to invest $1BILLION in Michigan and Ohio plants, adding 2000 jobs. This after...

    — Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) January 9, 2017

    Intel, the world’s largest computer chip manufacturer, said in early February that it would invest $7 billion to build a new factory in Arizona. The new factory had been under consideration for years, but Intel’s chief executive, Brian Krzanich, noted that the tax cuts and deregulatory policies that the president proposed had prompted the company to move forward with the plans.

    The German chemical giant Bayer said it would invest $8 billion in the United States and create 3,000 high-tech jobs, according to Sean Spicer, Mr. Trump’s press secretary. Bayer has been facing intense scrutiny from antitrust regulators in Washington and Europe over its plan to buy Monsanto for $57 billion. The announcement came about a week after the chief executives of the two companies met with President Trump and made their case for the deal.

    Some Asian companies have also announced plans to hire more workers in the United States. Infosys, one of India’s leading tech outsourcing companies, said that it would hire up to 10,000 Americans to serve its clients in the United States. Others, like the Alibaba Group of China and SoftBank of Japan, have similarly made vows to hire large numbers of Americans.

    President Trump’s influence has not saved everyone’s jobs. United Technologies owns Carrier, which announced that it would preserve some of its jobs in Indiana instead of moving them to Mexico. But 700 or so workers at another United Technologies factory, in Huntington, Ind., are losing their jobs. They are not alone.

    Rexnord of Indiana is moving to Mexico and rather viciously firing all of its 300 workers. This is happening all over our country. No more!

    — Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) December 3, 2016

    Rexnord, a ball bearing maker, disclosed plans in October to leave Indianapolis for Mexico soon, despite criticism from Mr. Trump and threats to impose steep tariffs on companies that move production abroad.
