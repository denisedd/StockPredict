    TODAY’S RESEARCH,
    TOMORROW’S SOLUTIONS

    How our brightest minds are combating the world’s biggest challenges

    Since its founding in 1851, the University of Minnesota has become one of America’s premier research universities, with a faculty that includes professors from every facet of the academic spectrum. Part of UMN’s success lies in its interdisciplinary spirit: discoveries have ranged from Norman Borlaug’s disease-resistant wheat and the first bone marrow transplant by Robert Good to Karen Hsiao Ashe’s landmark Alzheimer’s research and Bin He’s mind-controlled robotic arm. Today, UMN’s professors are working to redefine the way we think about global challenges — including closing the opportunity gap, protecting human rights, ending addiction and abolishing hunger. Though there’s no panacea (it’s going to take corporations, the government, educators and communities working together, says Megan Gunnar, director of the Institute of Childhood Development at the University of Minnesota), faculty members are making the world a better place — one advance at a time.

    DR. KELVIN LIM

    END ADDICTION

    Addiction — particularly opioid addiction — has become a national health crisis. According to the recent surgeon general’s report, “Facing Addiction in America,” 20.8 million people, or 7.8 percent of the population, have a substance use disorder. To combat substance abuse, scientists must better understand the brain.

    Kelvin Lim’s interest in the brain wasn’t originally addiction. “I was interested in the brain because of my interest in consciousness and meditation,” he says. As a medical resident at Stanford University in the mid-’80s, Lim — now the vice chair for research at the Department of Psychiatry at the University of Minnesota Medical School — was exposed to magnetic resonance imaging, which was then just emerging. “This imaging technology gave us a new and exciting window to examine the brain,” he says.

    It soon became clear that this technology — a functional MRI measuring blood flow and brain activity — could shed light on the conditions that create addiction and help with treatment. “We were motivated in thinking about how the brain recovers,” he says. “Let’s say someone enters treatment. [It’s] estimated that 80 to 90 percent of people relapse within a year of their treatment. Are there ways we can better understand that? And perhaps develop better treatments?”

    Functional MRIs can help identify parts of the brain associated with addiction. “One of the things we were interested in is how does the brain recover when people stop using drugs?” says Lim. “How does that relate to their future use?” Lim explains how a brain scan can show how much communication is occurring between the reward center and the decision-making parts of the brain.

    The stronger the communication, the higher the chance of a patient staying abstinent six months later. “This was such a fascinating and exciting discovery,” he says. “It provided us with new avenues for thinking about who is at highest risk for relapsing.”

    Lim’s work in understanding addiction is just one area that’s being studied at the University of Minnesota. Some researchers are also exploring a neural pathway that might serve as an “off switch” to drug-seeking behavior, while others — such as UMN professor George Wilcox — are investigating nonaddictive painkillers that are effective but have minimal to no exposure to the brain. “If you can reduce the number of brains exposed to these drugs, then I think we could have a win,” Wilcox says. Together, the hope is to create a multipronged approach to battling addiction.

    In the future, this could mean targeting the brain’s pathways with electrical stimulus or medications — possibly preventing the deaths of many of the 44 people who die every day from prescription drug overdoses in the United States.

    DR. SAMUEL MYERS

    PROTECT
    HUMAN RIGHTS

    “I want to empower people in our community to help solve problems,” says Samuel Myers, director of the Roy Wilkins Center for Human Relations and Social Justice at UMN. “The most vexing and persistent problem is that of racial, ethnic and economic inequality.”

    The approximate number of children who were reported to be abused or neglected in 2014.

    Source: Children’s Bureau

    Though Myers’s career has been spent in the world of social justice, his background is in economics. Using econometrics — which combines statistics and math to study economic systems — Myers has changed the way researchers and officials view human rights issues by identifying complex problems other methodologies might miss.

    According to Myers, approximately 3.9 million children were reported as abused or neglected in 2014. Among those, about 15 African Americans per 1,000 children in the U.S. were confirmed victims of maltreatment, while about eight whites per 1,000 children were substantiated victims of maltreatment. “What’s missing is the clear articulation of the relationship between the solution and underlying problem,” he says. Myers’s research shows that much of this disparity is attributable to racial discrimination and racialized beliefs of child protective service workers, and not due to actual racial differences in child abuse and neglect. He sees these disparities as another manifestation of a human rights dilemma. One solution he offers: training child welfare workers to see hidden bias to help improve outcomes for all children.

    Myers has studied disparities in arrests, incarceration and homeownership among minorities. “What, specifically, about these differences ... is the underlying core problem?” he asks.

    The desire to empower people to solve human rights abuses is shared by other researchers at the University of Minnesota. Their work ranges from collecting data on current abuses to studying historical precedents of how societies addressed past human rights problems.

    “The core idea behind human rights is that every individual has a claim to human dignity and to fairness,” says Professor Barbara Frey, director of the Human Rights Program in UMN’s College of Liberal Arts. Frey’s work pushes governments to uphold these standards. “The major challenge in the field of international human rights is compliance,” she says, “getting governments to comply with the obligations that they’ve already agreed to.”

    Myers believes local discriminatory practices and human rights abuses should be viewed through the larger lens of United Nations positions against racial discrimination and xenophobia. “The dilemma is that by viewing racial inequality solely through the lens of local laws ... we miss an important opportunity to reenvision solving some of the problems of inequality through an international human rights perspective,” he says.

    In Minnesota, Myers has found a community where the results of his work can be put into practice through legislation protecting disadvantaged groups. “The thing that attracted me to Minnesota was the fact that this is a place where you can actually accomplish something,” he says. “The goal is to build something that is global, something that’s international, but in a compact sphere.”

    DR. VIPIN KUMAR

    ABOLISH HUNGER

    “The population is increasing,” says Vipin Kumar, a Regents professor of computer science and engineering at UMN. In a few decades, the seven billion people in the world are expected to grow to nine or 10 billion. “When you have such a large increase in population, you have to figure out how to feed them and feed them in a sustainable way,” he says.

    According to the World Food Programme, 795 million people — roughly one in nine — do not have enough food. Kumar’s background in machine learning and data mining has given him a unique ability to understand the intersecting worlds of food production, water, energy and climate change. “There are hundreds of satellites scanning the earth every day, taking pictures and [feeding] computer models that simulate the environment of the earth,” he says.

    Kumar’s work explores the ways in which climate change and deforestation are related to food production. For example, expansion of agriculture to meet rapidly growing demand for food and biofuels worldwide has led to massive deforestation in the tropical forests of South America and Southeast Asia. Resulting greenhouse gas emissions from deforestation exacerbates climate change, which has huge implications for water and food security for the world. Increasing temperatures are expected to degrade crop yields and changing precipitation patterns are already disrupting agricultural production. Kumar’s work has used data to track how much of the Amazon rain forest is being turned into farmland, which poses huge ecological concerns long-term.

    Kumar’s research is intertwined with the work of his colleagues. For example, assistant professor Candice Hirsch, a faculty member in the Department of Agronomy and Plant Genetics at UMN, works with a team studying how plants can yield more food and lessen losses caused by global climate change. “We use a lot of big data in different ways,” she says. “We use all that information to model how plants are adapting to changing climates” and how to help mitigate some of the risks that come with extreme environments.

    One takeaway from both Hirsch and Kumar is that hunger is a holistic problem and must be addressed as such. The harmful effects of a change in one area often has implications in others. “The ultimate aim is to be able to produce information that allows stakeholders to figure out relationships between food, energy, water and climate change,” says Kumar. “All of these things are so interlinked that you cannot study them ... in isolation.” By studying these connections, we can help combat hunger by understanding and addressing its web of causes, rather than just treating the individual symptoms.

    Because of the enormous data sets Kumar and other UMN professors work with, students at the University of Minnesota play a critical role in the research. A large team is needed to work on this because the algorithms have to be developed for a variety of problems, says Kumar. “Students are very excited about working on these problems because it allows them to develop new solutions in computer science that can be used in many, many other places.”

    DR. MEGAN GUNNAR

    CLOSE THE
    OPPORTUNITY GAP

    According to the National Center for Education Statistics at the Department of Education, Hispanic and African American students have 12- and 16-percent lower graduation point rates than white students. But the many causes for these gaps are hard to pin down. Megan Gunnar, director of the Institute of Childhood Development at the University of Minnesota, has spent most of her life trying to close this gap. “We use the term ‘opportunity gap’ to go beyond just achievement in school,” she says, “to recognize that there are many opportunities that economically disadvantaged children miss out on outside of school that disadvantage them academically.”

    Hispanic and African American students have 12- and 16-percent lower graduation rates than white students.
    Source: Council of Economic Advisors

    At UMN, professors are working to close opportunity gaps in many different ways, from studying testing methodologies to the biology of stress. Gunnar’s work deals with the latter. She knew that animals neglected in infancy showed signs of stress throughout their lives, even if they were later well-cared for — and she suspected that children experiencing neglect and deprivation might display some of the same difficulties, even after they had been transferred to economically and emotionally supportive homes.

    Gunnar now believes a more accurate view of deprived and neglected children — including those whose mothers experienced economic and emotional stress while pregnant with them — can provide insight into what’s needed for children to succeed. The problem might lie in the overproduction of the stress hormones such as cortisol, a steroid that has powerful effects on the way the body functions and the brain develops. “I study how children’s social relationships with their parents and other supportive caregivers help them regulate stress-reactive hormones and protect their developing brain,” she says.

    Also working to close opportunity gaps is Michael Rodriguez, a professor at UMN’s Department of Educational Psychology, who focuses on standardized testing — specifically the need for children of underserved communities to have benchmarks reflecting their specific circumstances.

    Diverse Communities, Different Needs

    “How did we know if programs were really effective and meeting goals and helping kids?” he says.

    His goal is to build a stronger understanding of the role of social and emotional learning in youth development. After observing the use of common educational tools across communities, including Latinos, Hmong and American Indians, Rodriguez felt that universal testing wasn’t accounting for persistent challenges being faced by diverse communities.

    Rodriguez seeks to close opportunity gaps by tailoring testing benchmarks “to the unique learning needs and context and experiences of kids in different communities.” In so doing, he hopes to pay more attention to social and emotional characteristics in learning. Schools in communities with high poverty levels and large immigrant populations tend to have the least resources, says Rodriguez. “When we provide spaces and opportunities in schools for kids to grow socially and emotionally, students can begin to understand how to manage their stress and trauma, and then begin to attend to the goals of being in school.”

    The University of Minnesota has played a big role in the progress that Rodriguez and his colleagues, like Gunnar, have made to close opportunity gaps. In addition, researchers are pursuing other paths, ranging from achieving a better understanding of the development of foundational learning skills to investigating ways to more quickly diagnose autism in children.

    “I’m fortunate to be surrounded by people that are committed to equity and really want to make a difference,” Rodriguez says. “They take the extra step in translating that research into practice.”
