    How to Jump-Start Your Career

    SEPT. 22, 2017

    Hi. I answered your questions about how to get your career moving in the right direction. Whether you're starting your job search or looking to jump-start your career, I hope I can offer some helpful advice from the more than 500 C.E.O.s I've interviewed in the course of my career.

    Adam Bryant

    Adam Bryant has conducted hundreds of interviews with C.E.O.s about leadership for the Corner Office series he created. He is also the editorial director of live journalism at the Times.

    Your Questions, Answered

    Changing Careers

    +

    I've been working as an administrative assistant for about three years now, and I would like to change my role to marketing or event coordination. How would I ask my company if there is a way to do that within the company?

    Chelsea

    There are probably official channels through your company’s H.R. department that you should explore, but you should also start doing some reporting through unofficial channels to learn about how the various departments operate. Is there a colleague you could reach out to for a coffee to learn more about how those units work and whether there are any openings (or ones coming up)?

    In effect, you need an ally in the department (the person doesn’t to be highly placed) who can give you insider information. That way, when you approach the head of the department for a coffee or a meeting, you can show that you’ve done your homework, and position yourself in a way that clearly shows how you can start contributing the moment you start. Also be willing to volunteer to help. Perhaps they could use an extra pair of hands at an event? That will signal your genuine interest.

    If you want to start looking for this type of job outside your company, don’t box yourself in and apply only for jobs that already match your experience. Everybody has to get experience at some point. You just need to convince somebody to bet on you, and you’ll do that best by showing authentic and passionate interest in what they do, with a real focus on what you can do for them, rather than what they can do for you.

    +

    My husband is in a job that he dreads. His confidence has taken a hit, too. What are the best things he can do to network and job search when self-esteem, energy and will are at a low?

    Leigh

    My sympathies. Doing work you dread is hard on your soul, and how you feel starts coloring everything else in your life. You can feel trapped and demotivated. Job-hunting reminds me of that expression about running: The first step is the hardest.

    Your husband just needs to go for a low-stakes, easy win: like a lunch meeting or two with people he already knows, with a focus on new avenues rather than dead ends. He needs a spark, a glimmer of possibility, and that will come through human connections, and will improve his view on the world. Once you start, it gets easier and the momentum builds.

    +

    My contract with a company in the textile industry is three years long, but I would like to jump into the food and beverage industry after this. What's your suggestion? I have three years as a sales and accounting coordinator. What would be the best way to enter a new industry without any related experience?

    Rebecca C., Nicaragua

    First step is to start asking people who are already in your network if they know people who work in those industries. Could they make an introduction just so you could ask some questions? The challenge in making a shift like this is, how do you turn yourself from an industry outsider to an insider? How do you get experience when you don’t have any? And the best answer is to start sounding like an insider, so you understand the key companies in the industry, and the broader strategic shifts and directions for the businesses.

    All that requires knowing people in that industry. You can read articles (and you should), but there is no substitute for friends, allies and acquaintances who are in the business. They can tell you where the opportunities are, and how to position yourself so there is a logical narrative for your background and why it would make sense in their industry.

    +

    I'm in consulting but it doesn't feel like I'm learning actual skills. I want to eventually go to business school and work in business development and strategy. What should I be doing to set myself on this path?

    Anonymous

    Sounds to me like you have your answer! Consulting can provide a crash course in understanding different industries and learning how to quickly develop models, frameworks and solutions. But it is also understandable that you might want to go deep into one job. Business school is certainly one route. Have you come across people who are doing the kind of job you ultimately want to do? Why not reach out to them and buy them lunch or coffee to pick their brains? What are their backgrounds? Did they need business school? You need to hear different perspectives and stories that will ultimately inform your decision about what’s best for you.

    +

    What's the best way to market myself and my 'transferable skills' when trying to leave a niche field within the nonprofit, cultural world?

    Kris, Massachusetts

    Is there a specific kind of job you want to move into? If so, you need to take a blank sheet of paper (I know, I know. I’m old school, but some work is best done away from a screen.) and start breaking down the underlying skills and work in your current job, and how those can be applied in the new job. It’s like you’re building an argument, but you have to be able to back up the argument with specifics. “This is what I did, this was the result.”

    Show that you moved a needle or two. It’s about having a positive attitude. If it’s authentic and you can make a convincing case, that attitude is infectious. And employers love it because you’ll bring that positive attitude to everything you do (and you’ll be fun to work with).

    Mindy Grossman, the chief executive of Weight Watchers, told me she has a simple framework for people: There are Tiggers and Eeyores.

    A lot of people prefer to be around Tiggers.

    +

    How do you know it's time to move on to a next job? Do you make a parallel move to re-energize?

    Sam, New York, NY

    If you’ve done your current job well, contributed to the team and learned a lot from it, and you’re feeling like you want to shed your skin and do something new, then it’s time to start looking.

    Everyone is different — some people are happiest on a steep learning curve all the time, while others like cashing in on all the expertise they’ve built up. No judgments either way, but you have to know yourself.

    I think parallel moves can be great. One popular career metaphor is that it’s like building the foundation of a pyramid; the broader the base, the more stable the structure. I even think that people should be willing to take a pay cut for the right opportunity to get your foot in the door or set your career in a new direction.

    Last piece of advice on this: It’s always better to run to something (an exciting new opportunity) rather than just doing anything to escape a job you don’t like.

    +

    I have been a stay-at-home mother out of the "workforce" since 1994. During this time I have been the most underpaid employee, with the greatest benefits. I morphed from a woman running a $17-million cosmetics business into a mother who multitasks 24/7. I've run benefits, championed fundraisers and built an impressive resume, albeit not for a major corporation. Most importantly, I know what my passion is. How would you dive back in?

    Alison, New York, NY

    Seems like the easiest transition would be to build on all the work you’ve done with fundraisers and benefits. Those are marketable skills, and a lot of jobs require those talents. It sounds like you’re the kind of person who can make things happen and move the needle in whatever you do! I think you could make a compelling case to an employer. Plus, not everyone has run a $17 million business before.

    +

    How do you find a balance between a job that inspires, and one that pays the bills?

    Leigh

    It’s hard to find that perfect sweet spot, isn’t it? Those tradeoffs you mention gave rise to that expression I’m sure you’ve heard: Some people live to work, and others work to live. In other words, some people look to find meaning and fulfillment in their job, and others just do the job to pay the bills so they can pursue their passions outside of work.

    No judgments either way! But you have to know yourself.

    Is there a different line of work you really want to move into? Any career transition requires some sacrifices and extra work. It’s part of a larger conversation we all have to have with ourselves from time to time: What do I really want, and why? Those are big questions, but always worth wrestling with.

    Improving Your Current Career

    +

    I've always wanted to go back to graduate school but my current job is going well. How will I know it's the right time to go back to school?

    Ashley, Idaho

    I would pause before going to graduate school at this point, based on what you’ve shared. If your current job is going well, and you’re learning and growing and feel like there is runway in front of you, do you need to give that up?

    After all, there are real costs in attending graduate school — the tuition and the opportunity cost of lost wages. If you see a clear benefit to advancing your career through graduate school -- and you know precisely what you want to get out of it -- then by all means, go for it. Or if it’s a possibility, could you do grad school in addition to your day job? I would just think very hard before giving up a job that is going well.

    +

    I've been at my job for almost three years. I enjoy the work I do and find that my work is meaningful. But I can't help but wonder where all this is leading to. How do I figure out a broader vision for my life and work toward it?

    Enelle, Singapore

    I think a lot of people hit a similar point in their careers, sometimes more than once. The only answer is deep, serious and extended reflection, maybe with a blank sheet of paper or two (no screens!).

    Start thinking hard about what you like to do and want to do, and what kind of work would make you feel fulfilled and challenged. The answer will be as unique as your fingerprint. What kind of lifestyle do you want? How much money do you feel you need? What does an ideal day look like for you? What kind of work makes you feel like you’re in a state of flow, where your skills and talents match the task at hand? What gets you excited, and lifts your heart rate just a little bit, when you think about doing it? You don’t need a hard and fast answer, but you do need a direction to start moving in.

    Jeff Weiner, the chief executive of LinkedIn, was particularly thoughtful on this when I interviewed him:

    You can’t realize your goal if it’s not defined. It sounds so simple but it’s true. So the most important piece of advice I can give folks who are coming out of school, even people who’ve already begun their career, is to know what it is they ultimately want to accomplish. And if they don’t, there’s absolutely nothing wrong with that, but start thinking about what it is, because once you know it, the moment you know it, you begin manifesting it. You manifest it in explicit ways by virtue of knowing and then pursuing it, and you manifest it in implicit ways — just in the way you talk, in the way you think and the things that you say to others and the people you attract to yourself.

    And if you don’t know the answer to the broader question, my advice is to optimize for two things: passion and skill, not one at the exclusion of the other. You have to optimize for both.

    +

    I am an introvert and I feel like I am skipped over for management positions. What can an introvert do to make opportunities?

    Anonymous

    I’m an introvert myself, so I can very much relate to your question. Two pieces of advice: First, always let your work speak for itself. If you are great at your job, that will mean more than anything else, and hopefully your bosses will notice.

    But you also need to push yourself a bit to raise your profile. Otherwise, the show ponies of the world will get more attention than a workhorse like yourself. I’ve interviewed a lot of C.E.O.s for Corner Office who are introverts, and they typically say that you have to muscle through your introversion and put yourself out there a bit more. But you also have to do it in a way that feels authentic and natural to you. Maybe that’s in one-on-one meetings over coffee or lunch, rather than trying to be the life of the party during a happy hour after work. It’s hard — trust me, I know — but you’ve just got to do it.

    +

    My boss, who is a charismatic, bright and experienced professional, is running his department ragged. Everyone on his team is exhausted because he says yes to every request. What exacerbates the situation: All the meetings we have do not leave much time for “real work.” How do I tell him to slow down?

    L.

    Well, it can’t just be you who tells him to slow down (he might not take it well). But what if the team suggested that you have some kind of off-site (even if it’s in the office, over beers or pizza after work). Just sounds like you need a time-out to have a discussion about prioritization. What if you suggested a Monday morning check-in just to set priorities among all the competing demands? As Jim Collins, the author of “Good to Great,” has said many times, “If you have more than three priorities, you don’t have priorities.”

    Right now, it sounds like everything is equally urgent, and maybe all the meetings are kind of providing cover for the fact that there is not a clear plan for delivering the results people need. Maybe you can even take a light-hearted approach: “Can we have a meeting about our meetings?”

    As long as you focus all your feedback on how to help him win better, rather than what he’s doing wrong, he should be more open to the feedback.

    +

    I have started a new position as a V.P. with a company I consulted with for a few years. I have five people on my team. I have always been a collaborative, successful colleague but am failing as a leader. What can I do to be a better leader, build consensus and inspire them to believe in the work and what we're trying to accomplish?

    M.B.N., Texas

    Feels like it’s time for an offsite, whether it’s a literal or metaphorical one. Tell the team you feel it’s time for a reboot, and ideally get people out of the office for a distraction-free and honest discussion. Ask them to come prepared to share some ideas so that you don’t have to worry about awkward silences when you start asking for opinions.

    Some suggested questions to start the discussion: What are three things that are working well, and three things that are not? What’s slowing our momentum? How can you, as their leader, help them more? What three things are frustrating them the most, and what are their suggestions for fixing them?

    If you keep the discussion focused on solutions, you’ll avoid an extended gripe session.

    And then — here’s the key step — if you can act on a few things and make some concrete changes, they’ll sit up and take notice. Then you may notice they’ll start to feel engaged a bit more.

    If you need a bit of inspiration for the kind of challenge you’re facing, I would point you to my interview with Amy Errett, C.E.O. of Madison Reed.

    +

    I work at a fairly large company. What's the best way to meet people outside of my team or department and expand my network?

    Ranu, New York, NY

    The best tip: Can you find people in your company, maybe through a LinkedIn search, who went to the same school as you? Or grew up in, or were born in, the same city? Or maybe you both worked at the same company early on in your career, long before moving to your current employer?

    A big part of people’s identity is wrapped up in those aspects of their lives, and if you reach out to them as a fellow member of their “tribe,” they’re very likely to welcome you into their circle. It’s all about finding those human connections.

    +

    I'm in my mid-20s and have worked primarily at the same small company since I started my career. I love my job and don't want to move, however, how am I supposed to know if my yearly salary, increases and raises are on par with other companies in my field and across the industry?

    Annie D., New York, NY

    You can probably learn a lot from a deep internet search about salaries for comparable jobs at other companies. But there is also the simple reality that if people don’t think you’re going to leave, sometimes you will feel taken for granted. And that might be reflected in your paycheck. That might not be right. And it may not be fair. But it’s also how much of the world works.

    So speak up and advocate for yourself, but don’t let it drive you crazy. If you like your work and your colleagues, and you’re still learning and growing, keep your eye on the big picture and the long-term. Over the course of your career, your earnings are going to be affected more by a handful of key decisions and inflection points of your career, rather than short-term raises. Plenty of other people may have different advice, but that’s my two cents.

    Starting Out

    +

    Since finishing my master's degree, I have jumped around from job to job, staying at each job for at most one year. How do I find my ideal job and jump start my career without job hopping?

    Michelle

    The job hopping would be seem to be a symptom of the fact that you haven’t found your ideal job. I’m not sure what that job is, but I would caution against using that phrase, even as you think to yourself about the jobs you’ve done and the job you want.

    Are you setting too high a bar? The grass-is-always-greener phenomenon is very real, and it’s easy to think everything will be better elsewhere. And maybe it will be. (Again, I don’t know your circumstances.)

    But you have to be open to the idea that if you made the most of your current situation, you might enjoy it more. You may surprise yourself, too. I say that as long as you’re in a job, why not make the most of it? Get to know as many people as you can, and do a really good job. That will open doors for you, rather than feeling like they’re closing around you.

    +

    I graduated in June 2016 with a Bachelor in Business Administration, and I have been working on digital marketing and Google Adwords. Now I want to go back to recruitment (where I did my first internship) but it seems hard to get my CV noticed by recruiters, because of lack of experience. What do I do?

    Eliza, Barcelona

    Another reader posed a similar question, but the short version is that you have to create your own opportunities. Use and expand your network to find people who are doing the work you want to do. Reach out to them and ask for advice (in exchange for lunch or coffee). Show genuine interest and enthusiasm, and people will usually respond well. Think of it like a reporting project, and make your own luck.

    +

    I have just graduated college with an engineering degree. What is the best way for me to get started on a great career path?

    Dakota, Indiana

    To help narrow the field, do a lot of research on the best companies in the particular field of engineering you want to pursue. If you join a company that’s growing and with a strong culture, you will learn a lot and get new opportunities to move up and build your resume. Don’t set too high a bar for yourself by focusing on landing the perfect job. Focus on getting your foot in the door of a great company.

    +

    I am 20 years old and currently finishing my undergraduate degree. What are the best ways to stand out to employers among other graduates just coming out of college?

    Gina S., New Brunswick, NJ

    Do your homework about the companies you’re applying to, and read up on the executives you’ll be interviewing with and possibly working for. Focus on what you can do for the team and the company, rather than what the company can do for you. You will stand out if you make a convincing case that this is the only company you want to work for, and why. Sounds simple and obvious but it makes a big difference.

    Resumes and Interviews

    +

    I've heard conflicting advice whether to reveal my current salary when asked. How should I answer such question when asked during an interview?

    Sandy W.

    Sandy, the reason you’ve heard conflicting advice is that, well, there is always a danger in a cookie-cutter, one-size-fits-all approach. If you’re making good money in your current job and want to set the bar at a decent minimum for any discussions, I think it makes sense to go first and say, “This is what I’m making now.” Implicit in that is that you expect a premium above what you’re making to switch jobs.

    If you think you’d be coming in at the low end, it can be smart to ask them what they typically pay for someone in that role.

    From that point on, the negotiation will depend on the leverage of each side. It’s also up to you how hard you want to negotiate. As long as you can build a clear and factual case for why you deserve X, that’s a fine conversation to have. Any work you can do ahead of time to get a sense of what the company pays is also useful for such discussions.

    The final point is about your temperament. I’ve seen some people negotiate so hard that it adds a real sour note to the start of a working relationship.

    +

    I go to a lot of interviews, the interviewers like my resume, but I'm not called to work, what am I doing wrong?

    Victor A., São Paulo

    I’m operating in the dark here, with little information about your situation or how to best advise you. But here’s a piece of advice I give to people all the time: Do your homework. Learn as much as you can about the company you’re applying to. Learn as much as you can about the job, the department you’d be working in, and the team of people who might be your colleagues. Learn as much as you can about the person who will be interviewing you.

    Employers want to know that you really want to work at their company, rather than just land a job.

    This may sound obvious but you’d be shocked at how few people do this basic work ahead of time. Hope that helps.

    +

    Tell me how a resume with gaps between jobs can still get noticed and interviewed?

    Margo N., Mount Laurel, NJ

    Whenever there are soft spots in a resume, you have to be able to talk about them in a positive way. Be completely open about what happened and why, and talk about what you learned from the experience. Many of the C.E.O.s I’ve interviewed told me that they value attitude above all else. Everybody faces challenges and headwinds. The question is, how do you deal with them? Do you own them, work through them, and learn from them? People like to work with positive colleagues. Nobody likes complainers. Barbara Corcoran had plenty to say on this topic when I spoke with her.

    +

    I'm a freelancer and got a full-time job that was great on paper but I quit after three months. I did a lot of great work in that time but left for health reasons. Should I keep it on my resume?

    Jason

    I would throw it back to you with a question: How much do you need it on your resume? If it’s important to include it to show you have that experience, and it’s worth including even if you have to go into the explanation of why you left, then sure.

    But if it’s just another example of the kind of work you’ve done in other jobs, then maybe it’s not worth it? I can’t really answer this for you, but those are some considerations as you’re thinking through this.

    +

    I left a previous job. Now that boss is giving me bad references. How do I handle that without sinking down to his level?

    Misha, New York

    If it’s inevitable that any prospective employer is going to call your previous boss, then you need to bring it up at the right moment and tell people up front what they’re going to hear when they call that previous boss. Just be matter of fact about it, and make your case. “This is why we had problems working together. This is what I tried to do to fix it. Here’s my take on why it didn’t work. And if you call these other references, you’ll discover that he’s an outlier among all the opinions about me.”

    People should appreciate your honesty. And let’s face it, everybody knows there are plenty of bad bosses out there.

    +

    I struggle with interviews. What are some tips to make sure my interviews go well?

    Dakota, Indiana

    What aspect of the process is a struggle for you? Are you worried about what questions they might ask, and feeling unprepared? There are lots of articles and books out there on some of the most frequently asked questions that you can use to prepare.

    One of the most important questions to prepare for is when the interviewer asks you: “What questions do have for me?” This is your chance to shine. Do your homework on the company and its culture, and ask really incisive questions. And learn as much as you can about the person who’s going to be interviewing you. Between LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter and Instagram, you can quickly get a sense of someone’s background and interests. That will help you connect with them at a human level.

    Final piece of advice: Ask a friend to role play with you so you can practice, practice, practice.

    +

    What is the best approach for a qualified, but older, experienced worker to stand out amongst the coveted more recent grads when interviewing for a job?

    Donna, Cobleskill

    You can’t change your age, so focus on your strengths, and sell your experience and wisdom as an advantage over recent grads. And you need to take any concerns off the table right away. People might make assumptions about your energy and willingness to learn compared to your younger peers. So hit those notes up front. Sell yourself.

    +

    I was treated poorly in my last job even though my work was exceptional, so I resigned. Now organizations I'm interviewing with focus why I left my previous job. I've always read that you should never say anything negative about your former employer, so I haven't. Yet I've now been passed over for two jobs that I should easily have gotten. Any advice?

    Sally, San Diego

    Be wary of any advice that includes the words “never” or “always.” If you feel it’s an ongoing issue in your job search, you should probably start addressing it head on in your interviews (at the right moment, of course).

    It’s all about positioning and attitude. If you can tell a simple and credible story about what happened, and talk about what you learned from the experience and how you can apply those insights in future roles, then you should be fine.

    Many interviewers (dare I say “most?”) appreciate somebody who is honest and not trying to present a gleaming, polished facade. Just own the problem and minimize the finger-pointing, talk about what you learned from it and what you’d do differently given a do-over, and people should respond well. Part of being trustworthy is showing vulnerability.
