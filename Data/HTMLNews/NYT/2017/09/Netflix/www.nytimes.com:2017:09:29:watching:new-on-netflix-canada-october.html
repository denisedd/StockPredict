The Best Movies and TV Shows New on Netflix Canada in October

by Scott Tobias

Sep. 29, 2017

From left: “War of the Worlds,” “Our Brand Is Crisis” and “Stranger Things.”Paramount Pictures; Warner Bros. Pictures; Netflix

Welcome to Watching, The New York Times’s TV and movie recommendation site.

Every month, Netflix Canada adds a new batch of movies and TV shows to its library. Here are the titles we think are most interesting for October, broken down by release date. Netflix occasionally changes schedules without giving notice. (Unfortunately, streaming information provided in our Watchlist listings applies only to viewers in the United States.)

‘Black Mass’
Starts streaming: Oct. 1

The Boston accents may be thicker than a curdled bowl of New England clam chowder, but Scott Cooper’s moody gangster thriller about the murderous exploits of James “Whitey” Bulger brings clarity to Bulger’s powerful, multitentacled operation. With sunken eyes, a rictus grin and thin silver hair slicked back to expose a jutting forehead, Johnny Depp looks like Bulger’s diseased soul peaking through to the outside, as he cuts a wide swath through the Boston underground in the ’70s and ’80s. At particular issue is Bulger’s work as an F.B.I. informant, which allows him to stay in business as his Italian-American rivals are exposed.
—

‘Our Brand Is Crisis’
Starts streaming: Oct. 1

After “The War Room” portrayed the campaign strategist James Carville as a shrewd, colorful genius, the 2005 documentary “Our Brand Is Crisis” scraped some of the luster off, detailing his firm’s attempt to meddle in Bolivian politics. This feature adaptation, directed by David Gordon Green, serves mostly as a vehicle for Sandra Bullock, playing a campaign guru who spearheads an unpopular candidate’s presidential bid and starts to feel queasy about it. The film is missing the documentary’s editorial sharpness, but Billy Bob Thornton was born to play a Carville type (as he did previously in “Primary Colors”) and Bullock is given plenty of room to work.
—

‘Patriots Day’
Starts streaming: Oct. 1

Within a three-month period toward the end of 2016, director Peter Berg released two fact-based thrillers starring Mark Wahlberg that have so much in common they barely feel like separate events. After focusing on the grit and heroism of workers trapped on an exploding oil rig in “Deepwater Horizon,” Berg takes a similar approach to the Boston Marathon bombing in “Patriots Day,” setting aside politics in an effort to champion the teamwork of authority figures and emergency medical workers in attending to victims and tracking down the perpetrators. Berg’s weakness for masculine bombast occasionally betrays him, but he excels at staging complicated events with clarity and urgency.
—

‘The Death and Life of Marsha P. Johnson’
Starts streaming: Oct. 6

From David France, the director of the superb documentary “How to Survive a Plague,” about the advocacy group ACT UP, comes another doc inspired by a foundational moment in L.G.B.T. activism. Nicknamed “the Rosa Parks of the L.G.B.T. movement,” Marsha P. Johnson was an African-American “street queen” who was present at the Stonewall Riots and was a founder of the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries, another activist group. “The Death and Life of Marsha P. Johnson” celebrates her achievements, but it also investigates the mysterious circumstances surrounding her death, which was officially labeled a suicide but always suggested foul play.
—

‘Ali G Indahouse’
Starts streaming: Oct. 12

Four years before Sacha Baron Cohen turned Borat, his Kazakh TV reporter, into a minor pop-culture sensation in “Borat: Cultural Learnings of America for Make Benefit Glorious Nation of Kazakhstan,” he gave his signature character, the hip-hop dimwit Ali G, a shot at big-screen glory. In many respects, “Ali G Indahouse” served as an example of what not to do with a Cohen creation, leading him toward the mock-documentary style that more easily accommodated his style in “Borat.” Yet it’s nonetheless a stinging sendup of a particular strain of white gangsta wannabes, borrowing its “let’s save the community center” plotting from ’80s American rap comedies.
—

‘The Kite Runner’
Starts streaming: Oct. 12

Based on the best-selling novel by Khaled Hosseini, “The Kite Runner” is an American drama that attempts to capture the simplicity and emotional directness of Iranian films with children at their center. With the majority of the dialogue in Dari — enough, in fact, to earn it a Golden Globe nomination for Best Foreign Language Film — the film follows two boys from Kabul, Afghanistan, whose friendship crosses class and ethnic lines. Opening in the mid-1970s, it sets their story against the backdrop of tremendous change in the country, including the fall of the monarchy, the war against the Soviet Union and the rise of the Taliban. The “Game of Thrones” co-creator David Benioff adapted the book for the screen.
—

‘Mindhunter’ Season 1
Starts streaming: Oct. 13

In order to catch a serial killer, you have to know how a serial killer thinks. Or at least that’s the idea before “Mindhunter,” a new thriller series based on Mark Olshaker and John E. Douglas’s book about the F.B.I.’s elite Serial Crime Unit. Executive produced by David Fincher — who has carved out a niche for himself with “Seven,” “Zodiac,” and “The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo” — the show stars Jonathan Groff and Holt McCallany as F.B.I. behavioral-science experts who apply the testimony of imprisoned serial killers to active cases.
—

‘The Meyerowitz Stories (New and Selected)’
Starts streaming: Oct. 13

Along with Bong Joon Ho’s “Okja,” which has already debuted on the service, Noah Baumbach’s new comedy, “The Meyerowitz Stories (New and Selected),” was brought to competition at Cannes by Netflix this year, where it won significant acclaim. Like “Punch-Drunk Love” and “Spanglish,” the film takes Adam Sandler out of his lowbrow comic schtick and forces him into one of Baumbach’s stories of urban family dysfunction. Sandler joins Ben Stiller and Elizabeth Marvel as grown-up siblings who try to get over their estrangement to attend an exhibition in honor of their father, played by Dustin Hoffman.
—

‘Patton Oswalt: Annihilation’
Starts streaming: Oct. 17

Stand-up specials are a dime a dozen on Netflix these days, and although Patton Oswalt’s shows are always worth seeking out — his last special, “Talking for Clapping,” won an Emmy — “Patton Oswalt: Annihilation” promises to be an unusually bracing example. After his wife, the crime writer Michelle McNamara, died suddenly in April 2016, Oswalt exorcised some of his grief through comedy, touring behind material that reckons with his turbulent emotions as well as with his obsessions with social media and the current political environment.
—

‘War of the Worlds’
Start streaming: Oct. 19

Giving H.G. Wells’s alien-invasion classic a distinctly post-9/11 twist, Steven Spielberg’s “War of the Worlds” is about how humans contend with a threat that cannot be defeated through military force or American bravado. Although Tom Cruise isn’t the most obvious choice to play a blue-collar Jersey dockworker, he acquits himself well as a deadbeat dad who happens to be looking after his two kids on the weekend aliens come to town. Equipped with incinerating lasers and impenetrable defense shields, the invaders are so powerful that survival is a more realistic strategy than counterattack. The first invasion sequence, in which the army of machines surfaces from underground, is among the best in Spielberg’s career.
—

‘1922’
Starts streaming: Oct. 20

One month after taking a solid crack at “Gerald’s Game,” Netflix returns to the Stephen King business with “1922,” an adaptation of a novella from his “Full Dark, No Stars” anthology. Combining King’s period fetishism with the rural horror of “Children of the Corn” and “Cujo,” the film stars Thomas Jane as Wilford, a Nebraska farmer embroiled in a dispute with his wife (Molly Parker), who wants them to pack up and move with their 14-year-old son to Omaha. Not going along with his wife would mean losing his land and custody of their son, so Wilford plots to kill her and coerce the boy into silence. But the film soon turns his actions into a heartland version of “The Tell-Tale Heart.”
—

‘Do the Right Thing’
Starts streaming: Oct. 20

In 1989, much of the reaction to Spike Lee’s “Do the Right Thing” focused on the climatic scene, when the racial tensions that simmer on a hot day in Brooklyn’s Bed-Stuy neighborhood finally explode into violence. While it remains a galvanizing moment — though not so galvanizing as to justify conservative warnings that the film would trigger inner-city riots — it also tends to overshadow the richness and vibrancy of Lee’s masterly slice of life. Photographed in striking color by Ernest Dickerson, the film functions as a loose, often funny survey of a neighborhood that’s both specifically detailed and a microcosm for American racial animus.
—

‘Joan Didion: The Center Will Not Hold’
Starts streaming: Oct. 27

Only a month after its premiere at the New York Film Festival, Griffin Dunne’s documentary about his “Aunt Joan” is coming to Netflix. Through one-on-one interviews and access to extensive archival footage, Dunne explores Joan Didion’s 50-year career as an essayist, novelist, screenwriter and culture critic, including her encounters with celebrities and musicians in Los Angeles and the elite literary circles of New York. “The Center Will Not Hold” draws connections between her struggles to gain a foothold as a female writer and the ways the culture evolved (and didn’t) around her.
—

‘Stranger Things’ Season 2
Starts streaming: Oct. 27

The first season of this Netflix sci-fi series about the supernatural goings-on in small-town Indiana blazed through so many ’80s references that it’s hard to imagine what it has left in the tank for Season 2. Yet there’s evidence that “the Upside Down” still threatens the major characters on the show, who are still recuperating from the trauma of monsters and lost friends. Details have been shrouded in secrecy — though Barb fans will be disappointed to learn that the Upside Down still claims her — but this season opens around Halloween 1984, which suggests that John Carpenter references will keep pace with nods to Stephen King and Steven Spielberg.
—
