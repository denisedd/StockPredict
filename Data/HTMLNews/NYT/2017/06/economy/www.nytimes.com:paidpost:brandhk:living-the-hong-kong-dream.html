    Living the Hong Kong Dream

    Ranked as the world’s freest economy, Hong Kong is an entrepreneurial boomtown, standing tall alongside the world metropolises of New York and London. With its global status, common law legal system and business-friendly economy, this success story is now primed for new waves of opportunity.

    Where business thrives

    In 1985, a young American banker named Rob Chipman arrived in Hong Kong, gazed out of his hotel window and was hooked. “There was the Hong Kong harbor in all its splendor,” he recalls. After working in New York and Latin America, Chipman had been posted to Hong Kong for a three-year stint. But when the time came for him to return to the U.S., he realized he wasn’t yet ready to leave.

    “You can’t live in Hong Kong and not at least take a stab at starting your own business,” he says. Chipman left his bank job and spent the 1990s setting up a string of relocation businesses. In 1997, after more than 150 years of British administration, Hong Kong returned to China under the “one country, two systems” principle, which allowed it to maintain its independent common law system and its capitalist system. Chipman became the CEO of Asian Tigers Mobility in 2001 and served as chairman of the American Chamber of Commerce in Hong Kong in 2011 and 2012.

    “What makes Hong Kong unique is a combination of things that starts with the mentality of the people here,” he says. “It’s in the DNA of the Southern Chinese – they are resilient, resourceful and aggressive.” With Hong Kong’s low corporate tax rate of 16.5 percent, a slew of pro-business incentives to spark entrepreneurialism and the stability afforded by the rule of law, says Chipman, entrepreneurs can make “pure business decisions” without dealing with a complex web of regulations.

    An edge in innovation

    In 2015, the government created the Innovation and Technology Bureau to drive greater investment in the industry. Funding for R&D projects, start-up incubators and legal support are readily available. “You won’t find the level of IP protection that Hong Kong has in many other developing parts of Asia,” says Chipman. Hong Kong’s start-up scene is thriving: 1,926 companies have been launched in the city up to the end of last year, a 24 percent increase from 2015.

    Start-ups in 2016

    In 2013, Michelle Sun chose Hong Kong to roll out her business idea. Having graduated from the University of Chicago, she went on to work for Goldman Sachs in San Francisco, specializing in the tech sector. She picked up coding, made a career switch to software engineer and volunteered as a coding instructor to young people in the Bay Area. The experience sowed the seeds of a business idea for Hong Kong’s nascent tech ecosystem.

    Seeing that many Hong Kong parents sought out after-school programs for their children, Sun launched First Code Academy to provide coding lessons to children from as young as five years old. “When we started, there was no one else doing what we were doing,” she says. Four years on, more than 4,000 students have enrolled, and Sun has expanded her business to Singapore and Taipei. Last year, the American Chamber of Commerce in Hong Kong named her Young Achiever of the Year.

    Large-scale vision

    Hong Kong stands to play an integral role in China’s “Belt and Road” initiative, which will create a modern-day version of the ancient Silk Road. The ambitious endeavor will connect the developing economies of Asia and Eurasia, with Hong Kong as a natural conduit. “In Hong Kong, you’re on the doorstep of China and the rest of Asia, and you have the ability to draw on skilled manpower and resources,” says Chipman.

    To improve connectivity to the Mainland of China, the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge and the Guangzhou-Shenzhen-Hong Kong Express Rail Link are under construction. The bridge, spanning nearly 30 kilometers, cuts across the Pearl River and will open up new economic possibilities in the Western Pearl River Delta region, while the rail line will deepen economic ties with the Mainland as part of the 22,000-kilometer national high-speed rail network.

    Living the good life

    “Because Hong Kong is incredibly safe and has so much to offer in terms of everything from sports to culture, children start doing very sophisticated things from a young age,” says Chipman, who lives in the Southern district of Tai Tam and sent his children to the Hong Kong International School. “Education is very seriously emphasized and valued in Hong Kong.”

    $71 billion

    In infrastructure development over the past decade

    Bars and clubs in Lan Kwai Fong and the Mid-Levels shake up New World cocktails and thump with house music into the small hours, while a short bus ride leads to lush mountains or picturesque beaches. Work is well underway on a world-class waterfront in Central, and culture enthusiasts will delight in the new Xiqu (Chinese opera) performing arts center slated to open next year in the West Kowloon Cultural District. With so much on offer, even chronic workaholics can be hard-pressed not to make time for themselves. “I spend most of my time at work,” says Sun, “but I’ve recently taken up triathlons.”

    Hong Kong’s mix of business opportunities and lifestyle amenities continues to attract – and no wonder. In an age when other global capitals may be turning inward, Hong Kong continues to burnish its “open for business” sign.
