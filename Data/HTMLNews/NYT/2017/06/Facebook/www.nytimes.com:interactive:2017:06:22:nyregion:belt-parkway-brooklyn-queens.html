    New York’s Great Scenic Ride

    By RACHEL WHARTON JUNE 22, 2017

    California has the Pacific Coast Highway; the Southwest, its historic Route 66. New York? Our great drive may be the Belt Parkway, whose 25 miles hug green space and coastline along south Brooklyn and eastern Queens. Yes, the Belt can be a traffic nightmare for those heading to Kennedy International Airport or over the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge at rush hour. But there are times — say, 11 a.m. to 3 p.m. on weekdays and early Sunday mornings — when the grandeur of the Belt reveals itself.

    1. 1. The Belt Parkway Promenade

      Neighborhood: Bay Ridge, Exit 1

      Overview: If you visit only one section of the Belt Parkway, make it the promenade: a two-mile curve along one of New York City’s best vistas, no matter which direction you’re pointed or how you got there. Everywhere is the placid expanse of New York Harbor, whose backdrop is a shifting panorama of Manhattan skyline and the Statue of Liberty, Staten Island’s steep shoreline and the looming Verrazano-Narrows Bridge. It’s all skirted by the Shore Parkway Greenway, a waterfront biking and walking path that here has an extra-wide berth for lingerers.

      Don’t Miss: The rest of the greenway, which runs in two parts. The first 4.5-mile stretch follows the water south from American Veterans Memorial Pier at Bay Ridge Avenue to Bay Parkway in Bath Beach. It resumes for another eight miles of protected path from Brigham Street in Sheepshead Bay to 84th Street in Howard Beach. In between, follow the city Department of Transportation’s recommended bike routes.

      Pro Tip: The promenade is the most accessible point of the parkway for pedestrians, thanks to several nearby subway stops and footbridges at 80th and 92nd Streets. (They offer their own excellent views, hovering over the Belt’s six lanes of traffic.) It’s even easier to get there via the new NYC Ferry, which stops at American Veterans Memorial Pier. Drivers are also in luck: There’s a free rest area just north of the bridge.

    2. 2. John Paul Jones Park

      Neighborhood: Bay Ridge, Exit 2

      Overview: There is a beautiful World War I monument here and an impressive collection of 18th-century cannonballs, hence its neighborhood nickname Cannonball Park. It also has the prime view of the bridge, its massive eastern flank rising along the park’s southern border. This is an easy detour off the waterfront for bikers, walkers and even drivers. In fact the ideal vista may be at the on-ramp to the Belt Parkway while you’re waiting at the stoplight on Fourth Avenue and Shore Road.

      Don’t Miss: The park’s giant black Civil War-era cannon, also known as a Rodman gun.

      Pro Tip: The Harbor Defense Museum is within the grounds of Fort Hamilton, New York’s only active-duty military base

    3. 3. Adventurer’s Park Family Entertainment Center

      Neighborhood: Bath Beach, Exit 5

      Overview: Formerly known as Nellie Bly, after the adventurous 19th-century reporter, this 51-year-old mini-amusement park is synonymous with summer for many Brooklynites. The long-running, low-impact rides are geared for toddlers to 10-year-olds: spinning teacups, a Tilt-a-Whirl and a mini-roller coaster. What they may lack in modernity, they make up for in charm: “People are always on Facebook, saying ‘Who remembers Nellie Bly?’ and taking pictures of the gates,” said John Sullivan, the park’s general manager. (He started working there in 1979, when he was 19.)

      Don’t Miss: If you’re a grown-up, the go-karts are your go-to ride.

      Pro Tip: The park’s immediate surroundings — which include a city Department of Sanitation garage and a B J’s Wholesale Club — aren’t so quaint, though one notable neighbor is Empire City Watersports, where you can rent a Jet Ski and cruise around the Statue of Liberty.

    4. 4. Calvert Vaux Park

      Neighborhood: Gravesend, Exit 6

      Overview: This 85-acre park on Gravesend Bay is still not quite finished, even though it has been decades in the making. For the architect and artist Bettina Johae, that’s part of its appeal. Ms. Johae leads biking and walking tours of city buildings and redevelopment projects through her company Aplusnyc, tracking changes in public spaces like Calvert Vaux Park. 

      Over the years, Ms. Johae said, this land has been used as an illegal dumping ground, a scrapyard, the Dreier-Offerman home for unwed mothers and a repository for material excavated to build the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge. Though the city has owned the land since 1962, it has been a proper park for just a few years and is now home to waterfront pathways, a model aircraft field and 36 acres of baseball and soccer fields known as Six Diamonds, including some that have been in use since 1942. 

      “It used to be called Four Diamonds,” Ms. Johae said, until the city removed enough junk from the waterfront to build two more.

      Don’t Miss: The sunken wooden barges along the park’s rustic shoreline, originally placed to prevent erosion. Head left just past the parking lots, then walk until you hit the water.

      Pro Tip: The sporting scene at Six Diamonds is most impressive at night, when the field lights are on. From here, follow the city Department of Transportation’s recommended bike routes, as there’s a gap in the greenway.

    6. 5. Emmons Avenue

      Neighborhood: Sheepshead Bay, Exit 8

      Overview: There’s a sliver of Brooklyn trapped between the Belt Parkway and the bay, creating a nano-neighborhood that is one block wide and 20 blocks long, and is a port of call for foodie types. Emmons Avenue is its main drag, lined with boats docked at the Sheepshead Bay Piers and two dozen restaurants, many of them catering to the neighborhood’s immigrants from Turkey, Russia and other Caucasus countries. Jacques Gautier, a Brooklyn chef who owns the Park Slope restaurant Palo Santo, has been traveling the Belt for decades to fish, kayak and explore, and this area has long been his go-to for lunch. His current favorite is Cherry Hill Gourmet Grocery, a 24-hour supermarket with free parking that offers prepared foods from Eastern Europe. “Almost every time,” Mr. Gautier said, “I stop there and I get stuff to take for a picnic.”

      Other options include Randazzo’s Clam Bar (known not just for clams but for its spicy red sauce) and Roll-N-Roaster, a 45-year-old roast beef sandwich spot that is the favorite of David Meltzer, a native Brooklynite, a lawyer and an active member of the Five Borough Bicycle Club. “They’ll give you a free bottle of water if you show up with a bike helmet,” he said. Follow the city Department of Transportation’s recommended bike routes until you rejoin the greenway at the western end of Emmons Avenue.

      Don’t Miss: This is the best place to eat along the Belt, worth a trip in its own right.

      Pro Tip: There are several fishing party boats available for charter along Sheepshead Bay Piers, including the Golden Sunshine, a 100-foot-long vessel that also hosts occasional three-hour ecology tours of Jamaica Bay with the American Littoral Society.

    7. 6. Marine Park

      Neighborhood: Marine Park and Gerritsen Beach, Exit 9, via Knapp Street

      Overview: At 530 acres, Marine Park is Brooklyn’s largest park. It wasn’t always such a pleasant place, particularly in the grim 1970s, but years of restoration have rewarded the city with acres of salt marsh pathways cutting through cordgrass as tall as your head. There’s a golf course, a model-airplane field, the Salt Marsh Nature Center and Wheel Fun, where you can rent a kayak or a stand-up paddle board to get an up-close look at herons and ducks in Shell Bank Creek, which flows through Gerritsen Inlet under the Parkway to Jamaica Bay. (Bikers can get there safely via the Jamaica Bay Greenway, which connects to the Shore Parkway Greenway at Flatbush Avenue.)

      Don’t Miss: The park’s brand-new bocce ball courts, on Fillmore Avenue.

      Pro Tip: Take a detour through Gerritsen Beach, a peninsula of a neighborhood along the park’s western flank. Gerritsen Avenue, lined with old-fashioned pubs and other businesses, runs parallel to the park, and dead-ends at a sandy spit of beach on Shell Bank Creek. Also seek out Tamaqua Marina at the end of Channel Avenue. It’s one of many marinas in the area you’ll spot Venice Marina as you exit the Belt but this one has a bar that’s open to the public.

    8. 7. Plumb Beach

      Neighborhood: Just east of Sheepshead Bay in the Gateway National Recreation Area, between Exits 9 and 11

      Overview: This quiet beach is a surprise to many when it pops up off the Belt Parkway’s eastbound lane, which is the only way to reach it. Formerly an inhabited island, it’s now part of the Gateway National Recreation Area and a sacred home to windsurfers, kitesurfers, kayakers and many naturalists. Plumb Beach’s shallow waters and mud flats lure horseshoe crabs, which spawn here during full moons in May and June. In the fall, foragers arrive for the tiny fruit of the beach plum, a shrub that is both abundant on and native to this beach, but surprisingly not its eponym.

      Plumb Island was named instead for the plumb lines first used to construct the streets in eastern Brooklyn. Even more confusingly, when the Department of Parks and Recreation repainted the old ranger station on the property it spelled the word “plumb” without the B.

      Don’t Miss: The exit. It’s from the eastbound lane only and essentially unmarked: The only sign says “Rest Area,” which leads directly to its free parking lot.

      Pro Tip: One of the few city beaches where leashed dogs are allowed year-round. Its shallow waters are kid-friendly, too.

    10. 8. A Gas Station, and the Gateway to the Gateway

      Neighborhood: In the buffer zone between the Marine Park Golf Course and the National Park Service’s Gateway National Recreation Area, just west of Exit 11 on the parkway

      Overview: Just west of Exit 11, for Flatbush Avenue, take note of a tiny, white-columned Mobil station, set into a traffic island between the eastbound and westbound lanes of the parkway. Still inscribed with the New York City Department of Parks and Recreation logo, it is one of the original Art Deco service stations built by Robert Moses when parkway construction began in the late 1930s.

      Don’t Miss: From here the Shore Parkway Greenway runs between the highway and the dense marshlands of the Gateway National Recreation Area, giving bikers, walkers and observant drivers an inescapable sense of the duality of their environment. “Look left, it’s terrible — you see cars and cars and cars,” said Mr. Meltzer of the Five Borough Bicycle Club. “Look right, after Plumb Beach, it’s more and more a feeling of being into the woods. All the way to Howard Beach.” He added, “It’s sort of a fascinating ride.”

      Pro Tip: For drivers, this is the quickest place to get gas (and Dunkin’ Donuts) for miles.

    11. 9. Floyd Bennett Field

      Neighborhood: Southwest of Marine Park, in the Gateway National Recreation Area, Exit 11

      Overview: The semi-wild sprawl of Floyd Bennett Field — a decommissioned airport and naval air station that is now part of Gateway National Recreation Area — can be intimidating to the uninitiated. Yet for those willing to explore, it offers a remarkable array of unconventional New York City activities. These include deep trails through the North Forty Natural Area — a native field and pond with bird watching blinds for amateur ornithologists — campgrounds that can be reserved for overnight stays, and the recently reopened Historic Aircraft Restoration Project at Hangar B, where volunteers work on antique planes like a 1952 Grumman S-2 Tracker. (Bikers can get there safely via the Jamaica Bay Greenway that connects to the Shore Parkway Greenway at Flatbush Avenue.)

      Don’t Miss: The path to Dead Horse Bay, across the street from the Aviation Road entrance. Most people call this Bottle Beach, as its shoreline is littered with leather shoe soles, twisted metal and piles of bottles, the remains of a former landfill.

      Pro Tip: Thanks to the Brooklyn Golf Center and the Aviator Sports and Events Center, there are sports of almost every kind. The Aviator center offers baseball, basketball, gymnastics, skating and soccer, but you can also play pickleball, cornhole and ninja warrior.

    12. 10. Jamaica Bay Riding Academy

      Neighborhood: Bergen Beach, the exit is unnumbered between Exits 11 and 13.

      Overview: East of Mill Basin Bridge, you’ll often spot a string of horses ambling along the shore from the 45-year-old Jamaica Bay Riding Academy. The waterfront horse trails by this Bergen Beach stable are technically part of the Gateway National Recreation Area, and include wooded coastal paths and sandy beaches. The academy offers boarding, lessons, birthday parties, summer camp and 45-minute private guided tours for walk-ins, provided they have closed-toe shoes and long pants.

      Don’t Miss: The slow curves between the riding academy and Howard Beach, which hold some of the Belt’s finest scenery. Though temporarily savaged in spots by the Seven Bridges Project — the city Department of Transportation is reconstructing most of the original parkway bridges — the highway here crosses low over basins and creeks, and at many points Jamaica Bay glitters right beside you. The panoramic scene in the other direction (Manhattan, off in the distance) isn’t bad either.

      Pro Tip: The riding academy also has a tack shop and concession stand.

    14. 11. Canarsie Pier

      Neighborhood: Canarsie, Exit 13

      Overview: There’s something stately about the 600-foot-long stretch of the Canarsie Pier, which is marked by arched front gates. It’s fitting, given its history, which is as old as New York City. A pier has stood here in some form for several hundred years, used by ferries, commercial fishing boats, yacht clubs and now hundreds of New York City residents who come to fish. The catch can include flounder, fluke, porgy and striped bass, along with the small bluefish locals call snappers. Regulars hail from several continents, casting until after midnight with help from the gooseneck lampposts ringing the pier’s perimeter.

      Don’t Miss: The opportunity to fly a kite or kayak: Like Floyd Bennett Field and Plumb Beach, the pier is home to an official National Park Service kayak launch. In the summer, rangers sometimes offer free lessons on Saturdays.

      Pro Tip: The Rockaway Parkway station of the L line in Canarsie is 1.5 miles from the pier, straight up Rockaway Parkway. Along the way, you’ll find dozens of West Indian restaurants ideal for picnic fixings at the tables along the water just west of the pier’s entrance. There are also public grills, and a food truck is usually parked in the pier’s parking lot.

    15. 12. Pennsylvania Avenue and Fountain Avenue Landfills

      Neighborhood: East New York, Exits 14 and 15

      Overview: Those new to the city may find it hard to believe the green hills along the eastbound lane of the Belt Parkway were once fetid garbage mounds. But what a difference a decade and several feet of clean topsoil can make: In 2004 the city Department of Environmental Protection planted trees, shrubs and prairie grasses, and today these two mini-mountains on Jamaica Bay are practically pastoral. This is the highest elevation on the city’s eastern edge.

      Pro Tip: This is still a drive-by view, but the National Park Service occasionally offers tours.

    16. 13. Cross Bay Boulevard

      Neighborhood: Howard Beach, Exit 17

      Overview: The Shore Parkway Greenway ends near Exit 17, but the intrepid cyclist should resist the urge to simply turn around. Instead, head into Howard Beach, a secluded neighborhood hemmed in by the parkway, the water and Kennedy International Airport. It’s home to New Park Pizza, a Queens institution offering up some of the best slices in the city since 1956 (a young Woody Guthrie was a fan). New Park holds an important place in more serious New York history, too: 30 years ago, it was the setting for a violent racial confrontation that led to a death, spurring demonstrations that divided the city and made headlines.

      Don’t Miss: Just three miles south on Cross Bay Boulevard you’ll find the Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge, a holy grail for city birders in the island neighborhood of Broad Channel. The refuge is home to hundreds of species including osprey — “the most beautiful bird to watch,” said Don Riepe of the American Littoral Society, who serves as the Jamaica Bay guardian. Though the birds were endangered in the 1970s, Mr. Riepe says there are now at least 23 nesting pairs in this part of the Gateway National Recreation Area, at least until they migrate south in September.

      Pro Tip: The A train stops less than a mile away from both New Park Pizza (Howard Beach-JFK Airport, at Coleman Square and 159th Avenue) and the wildlife refuge (Broad Channel, at East Sixth Road and West Road).

    18. 14. Plane Spotting

      Neighborhood: Ozone Park, Exit 17 through 20, on the parkway

      Overview: As the Belt Parkway skirts Kennedy International Airport, you’re so close to some runways that flashing safety lights for pilots sit along the highway just outside the Aqueduct Racetrack. Landing planes buzz the city below at squeamishly close quarters: The sensation is most intense when you are driving back toward Brooklyn after sunset, when the plane’s lights appear to be pointing right at you.

      Don’t Miss: Paying attention to the road. No photos unless you’re a passenger.

      Pro Tip: There’s no protected greenway here, but bikers and pedestrians can get the same feel from the sidewalks on the Belt Parkway overpasses.

    19. 15. Laurelton Parkway

      Neighborhood: Laurelton, Exits 23 or 24

      Overview: This is the end of the Belt — at Exit 25, it becomes the Cross Island Parkway. Exit here for a quick cruise around Laurelton’s suburban streets, many lined with Tudor-style homes. When you’ve had enough of the suburbs, make your way to 121st Avenue and 238th Street, where a tree-lined service road known as Laurelton Parkway begins, a six-block stretch past just-mowed lawns and garden apartments. At its end, you can get right back on the Belt and head back to the city.

    Correction: June 23, 2017

    An earlier version of this article misidentified the location of several landmarks. Plumb Beach is east of Sheepshead Bay, not west. The Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge is south of Howard Beach on the Cross Bay Boulevard, not east.
