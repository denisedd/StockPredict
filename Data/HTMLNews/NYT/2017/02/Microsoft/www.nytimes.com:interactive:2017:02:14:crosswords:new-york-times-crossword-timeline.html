    75 Years of Crosswords

    FEB. 14, 2017

    After the bombing of Pearl Harbor, a New York Times editor made the decision to run a crossword puzzle in the paper to offer relief to its war-weary readers. Seventy-five years later, The New York Times Crossword continues to be a source of comfort for readers. Here's how it got there.

    1. Dec. 21, 1913
      The World’s First Crossword Puzzle Sparks a Craze
      ‘The granddaddy of crossword puzzles’

      The New York World publishes the world’s first crossword puzzle by the newspaperman Arthur Wynne. It is instantly popular with readers and becomes a craze in 1924 when Simon & Schuster publishes the first crossword book.

    2. Nov. 17, 1924
      The New York Times Refuses to Join in the Fun
      ‘The utterly futile finding of words’

      An opinion column in The New York Times calls crossword puzzles “a primitive sort of mental exercise” and a “sinful waste” of time. Another opinion column four days later predicts their swift disappearance.

      Read the original opinion in Times Machine here.

    3. Dec. 18, 1941
      Bombing of Pearl Harbor Leads to Changed Minds
      ‘We ought to proceed with the puzzle’

      Lester Markel, the Sunday editor, sends a memo to Arthur Hays Sulzberger, the publisher, just two weeks after the bombing of Pearl Harbor: “We ought to proceed with the puzzle, especially in view of the fact it is possible there will now be bleak blackout hours – or if not that then certainly a need for relaxation of some kind or other.”

      Attached to Mr. Markel’s memo was a note from the crossword pioneer Margaret Farrar, who said, “I don’t think I have to sell you on the increased demand for this type of pastime in an increasingly worried world. You can’t think of your troubles while solving a crossword …”


    4. Feb. 15, 1942
      Margaret Farrar and the First New York Times Crossword
      ‘Profession and passion’

      The New York Times becomes the last major metropolitan daily newspaper in the country to offer a crossword puzzle — begun as a way to give readers a distraction from war news. It is published once a week in the Sunday magazine and is edited by Margaret Farrar, who will become the grande dame of American crosswords through 27 years at the paper. Ms. Farrar sets a new standard for crosswords by raising the quality of the constructions and by rigorous editing.

      Solve the first New York Times Crossword here.

    5. Nov. 11, 1950
      The Daily Crossword Begins

      The Times adds a daily puzzle — a 15 by 15-square crossword that is a “little brother” to the larger Sunday puzzle. The theme of the first daily puzzle is New York City reservoirs. The author is unknown.

    7. Jan. 6, 1969
      Will Weng Becomes Second Crossword Editor
      ‘Good company on lazy Sunday mornings’

      Ms. Farrar, the grande dame of crosswords, retires from The Times and Will Weng, previously head of The Times’s Metropolitan Desk, takes over as crossword editor. He adds sly humor to the clues and more wordplay to the themes.


    8. Feb. 28, 1977
      Eugene T. Maleska, the Third Crossword Editor
      ‘Exactitude with puckishness’

      Eugene T. Maleska becomes The Times’s third crossword editor. A former superintendent of schools in the East Bronx, and a lover of Latin, opera and poetry, he brings a new level of culture and seriousness to the puzzle.

    10. March 4-5, 1978
      First American Crossword Puzzle Tournament
      ‘Get ready … get set … solve!’

      The first American Crossword Puzzle Tournament is held in Stamford, Conn. Directed by 25-year-old Will Shortz, it will become an annual contest and the largest crossword event in the world.

      The 2017 American Crossword Puzzle Tournament will be March 24-26. Register to compete or just watch the finals here.

    11. September 11, 1989 and January 7, 1998
      Marriage Proposals in the Crossword
      … And we still get requests

      Solving with a loved one is a popular pastime, and sometimes those collaborations lead to marriage proposals. We know of two that ran in The New York Times.

      Jean Sherman of New York City, N.Y. proposed to Peter Sherman in the September 11, 1989 puzzle, after working with then-editor Eugene T. Maleska to get both their names, “proposes,” “marriage,” and “I do” into the crossword.

      With help from the crossword editor Will Shortz, Bill Gottlieb, a 27-year-old lawyer, proposed to his girlfriend, Emily Mindel, a 24-year-old law student, over brunch at a Manhattan restaurant in 1998.

      This was no ordinary proposal. As Ms. Mindel solved the puzzle constructed by Bob Klahn, she found her first name (18-Across: poet Dickinson), Mr. Gottlieb’s first name (14-Across: Microsoft chief, to some) and the three long answers that provided the puzzle’s theme: “Modest Proposal” (20-Across: 1729 Jonathan Swift pamphlet, with “A”); “This Diamond Ring” (38-Across: 1965 Gary Lewis and the Playboys hit); and “Will You Marry Me” (56-Across: 1992 Paula Abdul hit, with Stevie Wonder on harmonica).

      “In the back of my mind, I thought this has to be for me, but it can’t be,” she said. “It was just too coincidental.”

      Mr. Gottlieb picked up the story: “Four letters from being done, her voice is sort of quivering and she says, ‘This puzzle …’

      “I just kissed her and said, ‘Will you marry me?”’

      Ms. Mindel’s response was identical to the answer to 57-Down: “Yes.”

    12. Nov. 21, 1993
      Will Shortz Becomes Fourth Editor of the Crossword
      ‘A tradition of culture and quality’

      Will Shortz becomes only the fourth crossword editor of The New York Times. He adds contributor bylines to the daily puzzle, raises the pay, broadens the range of contributors and adds more playful themes and clues.

    14. Jan. 22, 1996
      First New York Times Crossword on the Web
      ‘Less blood, fewer erasures’

      The New York Times crossword is offered in electronic form for the first time.

      Today, solvers can play the Mini, the Variety puzzles, a monthly bonus puzzle and more online.

      Play online or take the Crosswords with you on iOS or Android devices.

    15. June 2006
      Crossword Documentary ‘Wordplay’ Opens
      ‘Crowd-pleasing entertainment and suspense’

      The crossword documentary “Wordplay” opens in theaters around the country, becoming one of the 25 highest-grossing documentaries at the time of its release and introducing crosswords to a new generation of solvers and constructors.

      “Wordplay” was directed by Patrick Creadon of O’Malley Creadon Productions.

    16. Oct. 27, 2008
      The Wordplay Crossword Blog Begins
      ‘Your thoughts?’

      Wordplay, The Times’s daily crossword blog, begins. Today it is a daily column that provides hints and tips to help readers become better solvers.

      Join Wordplay, the daily discussion of The New York Times Crossword, here.

      Follow @NYTimesWordplay on Twitter.

    18. Nov. 16, 2008
      ‘The Simpsons’ Meet the Puzzle Master

      Will Shortz and the constructor Merl Reagle appeared in ”Homer and Lisa Exchange Cross Words,” Episode 6 during the 20th season of “The Simpsons.”

      Watch the entire episode here.

    19. Aug. 21, 2014
      The Mini Crossword Is Born

      The first Daily Mini Puzzle – a quick, 5 by 5 puzzle made for the mobile age – was the brainchild of Matt Hural, then the product manager, and has been constructed daily since then by Joel Fagliano, the digital puzzles editor.

      “I remember thinking, no one is looking at this so I can kind of try just about anything,” Mr. Fagliano said.

      Today it’s one of the most popular pieces of the mobile news feed, and people compete to see if they can complete it in under a minute.

      Solve the Mini every day for free here.

    20. Dec. 18, 2016
      The Puzzle Mania Section Is Published
      The largest New York Times Crossword

      The New York Times publishes its largest puzzle: a 50 by 50 grid across a full-page spread in a special Puzzle Mania section of the Sunday paper. Families pored over the section over the holidays and over 7,000 entries were received from solvers who figured out the secret message in the Super Mega puzzle.

      Listen to Will Shortz talk about the Puzzle Mania section on the Times Insider podcast.
