    Savvy Business Leaders Are Embracing A.I. — Are You?

    IF

    your company hasn’t yet incorporated artificial intelligence (A.I.) into its business, you can be certain that it’s going to soon. A.I. is becoming more common and has the potential to impact everything from the way a company interacts with customers to how it hires employees.

    At the November New York Times DealBook conference, executives indicated in a survey that A.I. is becoming a much bigger priority. In the survey, 64 percent said they plan to invest in A.I. over the next three years.

    Still, while executives know that A.I. is critical, 35 percent said their top challenge is understanding exactly what an A.I. solution involves, while 22 percent said budget is an obstacle to implementation. Even so, more executives know that if they don’t adopt A.I., their companies are at risk of being left in the digital dust.

    To get a better idea of how these technologies are reshaping the business world, we spoke with three business leaders about their approaches to A.I.

    TAD SMITH

    President and CEO at Sotheby’s,
    the renowned retailer and auctionhouse

    Is Sotheby’s incorporating A.I.?

    We’re looking at it. It’s possible it could help us value some of the lower valued items we sell. Researching a particular item often involves finding and organizing data from the past, and A.I. could empower our specialist staff to be much more efficient in their estimating.

    When do you think A.I. might be able to help with this process?

    Within the next year or two. We’ve hired a data analyst and a data scientist, and we have a technical team ready to start automating pieces of the process.

    Do you think A.I., in general, will be as transformative as many people say it will be?

    It can be transformative. Routine manual processes are already being done in many different businesses by machines, and the price of those machines is falling rapidly. Cognitive routine processes are also moving to machine learning rapidly. You’ve seen this with banks, credit card processing and real estate decisions. There are very few businesses where some portion of the employment base is not doing some version of manual or cognitive routine tasks.

    Some larger companies may be saying “We do this the best, and we can do it the old way.” Sotheby’s is not, so is this a lesson for other big, legacy brand names?

    I struggle to come up with an industry that’s more than 10 years old that isn’t facing some version of material disruption. In March next year it’ll be our 274th birthday. You don’t last that long unless you have a very strong business model, but you also have to keep innovating. What we’re trying to do now is embrace technologies and accelerate that rate of innovation.

    Takeaway
    It’s not just scrappy start-ups that are considering A.I. Clearly, big businesses like Sotheby’s see the need to adopt this technology, too. If they don’t, they could lose their competitive advantage.

    MICHELLE BACHARACH

    CEO and cofounder at Findmine, a start-up whose A.I. algorithm is enabling fashion retailers to show customers products in a new way

    You’ve been using A.I. in your business for a while. How?

    Typically, if I buy a couch, someone shows me the couch and then leaves me to my own devices to figure out the rug, and end table, and lamps that go with it. It’s similar in the clothing world, where we usually buy just a shirt, but we all wear a whole outfit every day. So, we take all the information a brand has and, through machine learning, create new outfits out of every product in their catalogue. We use a lot of natural language processing to parse through the text associated with a product to determine information about it that may not be available to others.

    What’s it like using A.I. with other companies?

    We work with major corporations, and it is often hard for them to move fast or do anything different. So, we said let’s work with whatever data or processes you already have. Most retailers have product feeds, which is a giant file that has all their products and all their attributes listed out. We’ll get a copy. Once we get that file, the machine learning processing happens. We calculate outfits in real-time, on the fly, when a customer visits a product page online. This has made our clients happy and increased their revenues considerably.

    Are you concerned about A.I. taking people’s jobs?

    We’re not replacing jobs. We’re helping the merchandisers who hated doing this. It lets them use their skills for something only a human can do. That’s the positive promise of A.I. — it might replace some tasks, but hopefully you can shift what you’re doing to the more high-level tasks that only a human can do within your company.

    Takeaway
    As Bacharach shows, companies across sectors can find uses for A.I. It’s not just for technology companies or the Amazons and Googles of the world — even traditional retail operations can integrate this technology into their business with the help of innovative entrepreneurs.

    ANDREW STERN

    Senior Fellow at the Economic Security Project, a nonprofit working to end social inequity in the U.S.

    What’s it going to take for organizations to adopt A.I.?

    The threat they’ll be disrupted. In some organizations, like universities, change is not a friendly partner. What we can see is places just going out of business because they can’t adapt, and in some cases they don’t have the resources to adapt. But there is potential, because there are venture capitalists who can begin building organizations that can do this.

    Can A.I. really help solve big global issues?

    It can — I think around disease, for sure. But it may be dealt with by businesses instead of philanthropy. The people who really should deploy A.I. are those such as governments, but that’s not a general practice yet. They’d need to work with a company like Intel that can help speak out on the potential for disruption, help build leadership groups in the business community and then make available its skills and technology to develop solutions.

    What’s one way A.I. is empowering workers?

    Some organization are trying to figure out ways to help people that have questions related to their jobs. They see A.I. as an opportunity to answer those questions, without having workers fight their way through all the different personnel programs or H.R. people inside of a company. An organization can essentially build a set of services that are really not available otherwise, all based on A.I.

    Takeaway
    A.I. will do more than just transform companies — it will also solve big global problems. While there’s still a lot to figure out, and governments and companies need to work together, there’s plenty of potential in the power of A.I.

    It’s clear that business leaders across industries are excited about the possibility of A.I., but implementing it is no easy task. Finding the right technology partner is critical, says Brian Krzanich, Intel’s CEO. Not only is the chip giant helping customers introduce A.I. into their processes, but it’s also using A.I. to solve its own business challenges. “We are taking the lead on A.I. integration into business operations by providing flexible, scalable solutions across business scenarios,” he says. “The most forward-thinking companies are already gaining a competitive advantage through A.I.” What’s clear is that for businesses that want to stay relevant, an A.I. strategy will no longer be optional — it’ll be a condition for survival.
