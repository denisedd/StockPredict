‘The Crown’: A Look Back at Season 1 and the History Behind It

By Palko Karasz and Hannah Olivennes

  • Dec. 6, 2017

LONDON — “The Crown,” the Netflix drama about the life of Queen Elizabeth II, returns for its second season on Dec. 8. It picks up roughly where last season left off, the latest installment in a series that aims to track England’s longest reigning monarch up to the present day. (Claire Foy, who currently stars as Elizabeth, will be replaced by the older Olivia Colman next season.)

The first season spanned the period from just before the death of Elizabeth’s father, King George VI, through the early years of her own reign, portraying the challenges she faced both as queen and as a mother, wife and sister. While the dialogue is fictional, the major events depicted were based on historical fact.

As a refresher, here’s an episode-by-episode summary of Season 1 of “The Crown,” and a look at how some of these events were covered in The New York Times.

Episode 1, ‘Wolferton Splash’

Princess Elizabeth is engaged to Lieut. Philip Mountbatten, the former Prince Philip of Greece. King George VI gives Philip his royal title ahead of the marriage to his daughter and heir to the throne, Elizabeth. The wedding is held at Westminster Abbey, in great ceremony, and the Princess vows to “obey” her consort, to much controversy. The royal couple have two children, Charles and Anne. Winston Churchill is elected prime minister for the second time. Upon finding out her father is to undergo lung surgery, Princess Elizabeth returns to London with her family.

Episode 2, ‘Hyde Park Corner’

King George VI is found dead in his bed in the royal country house in Sandringham. The country is in shock, because even his doctors didn’t think the end was so near. The late king, known as ‘Bertie’ to his family, was a symbol of unity and continuity to the country. At the time of her father’s death, Princess Elizabeth, now queen, is traveling in Kenya. The trip is cut short, and Elizabeth and her husband, Philip, return to London for the funeral.

Episode 3, ‘Windsor’

Members of the royal family also return to London for King George VI’s funeral, including the king’s brother, the Duke of Windsor, who abdicated as King Edward VIII in 1936 in order to be able to marry Wallis Warfield Simpson, whose two previous marriages had ended in divorce. Queen Elizabeth starts planning her coronation, but Churchill asks her to push it back to June 1953. In return, she asks the prime minister, who was 78 at the time, to help her convince the cabinet to let her name her children Mountbatten-Windsor, after her consort’s name. But the cabinet refuses, and she is forced to maintain the name Windsor for her heirs and their descendants. She also requests that her family stay in Clarence House, but agrees to move to Buckingham Palace.

Episode 4, ‘Act of God’

Weather warnings are issued to the cabinet about a thick layer of fog that could be harmful to the population. But Churchill refuses to see the smog as an issue. With hundreds of people dying, the Queen is pressured to ask her prime minister to resign. But the fog clears just in time.

Episode 5, ‘Smoke and Mirrors’

Queen Mary, Elizabeth’s grandmother, dies. The Duke of Windsor returns to London, only to be told he could not attend his niece’s coronation. Philip is given the task of planning the ceremony, and decides to have it televised. For the first time in history, millions of people, in the U.K. and abroad, would be able to watch the Queen take the royal oath. Elizabeth II was crowned on June 2, 1953 in a ceremony during which her consort, the Duke of Edinburgh, had to kneel in front of his queen — and wife — and pay homage. The Duke of Windsor and his wife watch the ceremony on television from Paris.

Episode 6, ‘Gelignite’

The Queen’s sister, Margaret asks for her permission to marry Group Capt. Peter Townsend, whose previous marriage ended in divorce. Members of the Royal Household are opposed to it. The press reports the love affair and the public becomes fascinated with the couple. The Queen tells Princess Margaret she must wait until her 25th birthday, because of the Royal Marriages Act 1772. While Princess Margaret is in Southern Rhodesia, Peter Townsend is sent to a post in Brussels.

Episode 7, ‘Scientia Potentia Est’

Both Winston Churchill and the foreign secretary, Anthony Eden, are ill. Each tries to hide it, despite speculation. Mr. Churchill has to cancel a meeting with President Eisenhower. Mr. Eden undergoes a successful operation at a hospital in Boston. Feeling underprepared for a state dinner with world leaders, the Queen hires a private tutor to help her bolster her education.

Episode 8, ‘Pride and Joy’

Queen Elizabeth and Prince Philip depart on a six-month Commonwealth tour, leaving Princess Margaret in charge of royal engagements, including as visiting a coal mine. The Queen Mother travels to Scotland, where she decides to buy a castle.

Episode 9, ‘Assassins’

The Queen, who has a passion for horse racing, considers new horses with her racing manager, the seventh Earl of Carnarvon, whom she affectionately calls Porchey. The relationship between the Queen and Prince Philip faces tensions as the couple struggles to find the right balance between royal duties and family life. Churchill celebrates his 80th birthday. A portrait is commissioned and he unveils it after his birthday address in Parliament. But the painting portrays him as an old and frail man, which angers Churchill, but also helps him conclude that he must resign. The Queen attends his farewell dinner at 10 Downing Street. Anthony Eden becomes prime minister.

Episode 10, ‘Gloriana’

Peter Townsend's return to England sparks speculation about his engagement with Princess Margaret, who is theoretically free to marry whom she likes after she turns 25. But the cabinet, the Parliament and the Church of England all reject her request, and the Queen is forced to accept that she will have to ban the marriage despite having promised her sister that she could wed her lover. Princess Margaret announces that she will not marry Townsend.
