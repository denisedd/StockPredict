    Q&A: Holiday Charitable Giving and Taxes

    By RON LIEBER and TARA SIEGEL BERNARD DEC. 13, 2017

    We’re taking your questions on tax implications and charitable giving. Ask Ron Lieber and Tara Siegel Bernard your questions. Related Article

    Tara Siegel Bernard

    Personal finance reporter

    Ron Lieber

    Your Money columnist
    We took your questions on tax implications and charitable giving. See our answers below.

    In recent days, you’ve asked us many excellent questions about charitable giving, especially as it relates to the new tax laws.

    Before we answer, here are a few general tips. First, we’ve often found it helpful to think about giving in much the same way we think about our investments: Different pots of money have different goals. So how do you want to divide your charitable pie? Ron’s column from a few years back can help you figure it out.

    Have children in your life? Help them get in touch with their inner giver by telling them stories about your family’s history of having been helped itself. Every family has one, after all. Then, tell them about how that history helps inform how you divide your charitable budget and why you prioritize the things that you do. And here are a few more ways to give young people the gift of generosity.

    Now, to your questions.

    Essentials

    +

    How can I come up with a plan for charitable giving? How much should I donate each year?

    Start by reading Carl Richards’s useful guide, “How to Make a Personal Plan for Giving,” which provides step-by-step instructions on how to approach giving — why we give, what to give and how — and examines whether that giving is in the form of a financial donation or your time.

    How much you want and can afford to give is something only you can determine. But for financial gifts, Peter Singer’s advice, which is laid out in his book “The Life You Can Save,” provides some interesting food for thought.

    Mr. Singer, a philosopher and professor of bioethics at Princeton, tries to calculate what financially comfortable individuals would need to give to do their “fair share” — that is, how much these individuals would need to donate to ensure that the world’s poorest citizens had a chance at a decent life.

    “I wanted a number that, on the one hand, people could not say was unreasonably burdensome,” Mr. Singer said in a 2013 interview. “People who are earning more can give higher percentages, and they are still not making any real sacrifice. That was one side of the equation.”

    On the other side, he wanted to be sure the sums would be big enough to make a large dent in extreme poverty. He came up with a graduated scale, which resembles progressive income tax rates. He suggests that those who earn $105,000 to $148,000 annually contribute 5 percent of their gross income each year, for instance, while individuals or families earning $148,001 to $383,000 would donate 5 percent of the first $148,000 and then 10 percent of the next $235,000.

    The sliding scale goes up progressively; at the top, those making more than $10,700,000 annually would give gradually greater percentages until they were donating a third of all income over $8.8 million. Mr. Singer’s website has a calculator that will do the math for you.

    +

    Will the new tax plan take effect Jan. 1, 2018? Are there any concerns that it's being implemented so quickly?

    Daniel Packer, Los Angeles

    Most of the provisions in the tax plan will take effect at the start of the new year — and many of them expire after seven years.

    The legislation did move through Congress quickly, which left many taxpayers scratching their heads and wondering how much deep thinking went into writing the bill — and whether it could have any unintended consequences. Only time will tell.

    Regardless, individual taxpayers have little time to plan for the changes, especially if they wanted to put into effect last-minute strategies that might save them some money on their coming tax bill. That’s why it’s probably not a bad idea to book some time with your accountant before the year ends, to see if there’s anything you can do.

    One thing to consider before year-end: Bunching of contributions. The idea here is to pile donations into, say, alternating years if it gives you a better chance of itemizing your deductions that year and thus getting more than the standard deduction. Opening a donor-advised fund, which is a kind of personal foundation, can help. We explained these strategies in a recent article.

    Tax Reform

    +

    I see articles about what people’s average annual individual contributions are by income, but I still don't understand how much one should give to maximize the tax benefits as related to your income. We think we're giving a lot, but it all seems relative. How does one figure it out?

    Melissa, Medford, MA

    The reasons people give to charity are highly personal, and any financial benefits would depend on an individual’s unique situation: their income, whether they itemize their tax returns and what deductions are available to them, among other things. We wish we could offer a hard-and-fast rule, but it’s best to discuss this with a tax professional.

    You can find a certified public accountant through the American Institute of Certified Public Accounts, a trade organization that has a list of state resources, which includes links to local accounting societies.

    +

    How is the new tax bill going to affect charitable donations to 501(c)(3) organizations? I work for a nonprofit in California and we have not been able to figure out what to tell our donors.

    Tim Wilson, Oak Hills, California

    The new tax bill does indeed impact charitable donation strategies, for you and your donors. For more, see the answer to our next question.

    +

    If the new tax plans pass in Congress, what will that do to the deductibility of charitable donations? Given that fewer taxpayers will itemize if the plan passes, are charities worried that giving will decrease?

    Elizabeth Luna, Shrewsbury, MA

    Nonprofits are worried that the tax bill will cause fewer people to make charitable donations because, for many taxpayers, those contributions will no longer be deductible. Here’s why: The tax bill roughly doubles the standard deduction — to $12,000 for individuals and $24,000 for married couples filing joint returns — which means that fewer people will itemize their tax returns. And only people who itemize can take advantage of the charitable deduction.

    According to the Tax Policy Center, more than 46 million filers would be expected to itemize in 2018 under current law, but it estimates that the number would drop to less than 20 million.

    “The main reasons people give are personal — they want to give back to society, they are motivated by their faith, and it's simply part of American values and culture,” Steve Taylor, United Way Worldwide’s senior vice president and counsel for public policy, said in an email. “However, there is consensus among academics and economists that the tax incentive allows people to give more than they would otherwise give.”

    And that’s why charities are nervous. Who will people favor if they do cut back their contributions some? We are about to find out.

    +

    Is the Qualified Charitable distribution for people 70½ and older renewed each year by Congress? If so, does that mean this option may not be available in 2018 if Congress does not renew the act?

    Alice, New York City

    A few readers asked questions about qualified charitable distributions. Please read the answer to our next question for more details.

    +

    For persons turning 70½ in 2018 and becoming subject to mandatory individual retirement account withdrawals, does the tax bill alter the treatment of funds withdrawn from an I.R.A. and donated directly to charity? Under current law such funds are not deemed to be included in the taxpayer's income when withdrawn from an I.R.A. but also not deducted as a charitable contribution — the transaction is treated as a wash.

    GBustin, Pennington, NJ

    First, a little background: A qualified charitable distribution occurs when an individual over the age 70½ takes what would otherwise be a taxable distribution but instead sends it directly to a qualified charity, free of tax.

    It also satisfies the so-called required minimum distributions that individuals must use when they reach age 70½. So if your required minimum distribution is $10,000 — and you make a $5,000 donation to charity from your individual retirement account — you would have to withdraw another $5,000 to satisfy those rules.

    The tax bill did not make any changes to this strategy. In fact, it may become even more appealing in the future, according to Michael Kitces, a financial planner and tax expert, now that fewer people will be permitted to itemize their tax returns.

    These rules were made permanent at the end of 2015, according to Tim Steffen, director of advanced planning at Baird Private Wealth Management, so you don’t have to worry about year-to-year changes.

    Charitable Giving

    +

    Is there a reliable, comprehensive source where we can verify whether an organization qualifies as a charity to which donations are tax-deductible? Sometimes I doubt a claim of such. Especially when the website does not offer reassurances.

    Evelyn, Odenton, Maryland

    The Internal Revenue Service maintains a database that can answer your question, so start there.

    If you’re also looking for more qualitative reviews of how the charities run themselves, try Charity Navigator, which uses a four-star system to rate charities. Then there’s BBB Wise Giving Alliance, CharityWatch, GuideStar and GiveWell, a group that uses evidence-based results and makes just a handful of recommendations each year.

    These pieces provide greater detail on how to research charities.

    +

    Should the charitably minded accelerate 2018 gifts into 2017? Seems that a donor-advised fund is a good way to do this, especially with appreciated securities.

    Bill Lloyd, Geneseo, NY

    Donors are certainly considering this option, as nonprofit organization weigh the potential effects of the tax code changes. See the answer to our next question for a fuller discussion.

    +

    I serve on the board of a 501(c)(3) alumni group — we've just launched our annual appeal — should we encourage alumni to give before the end of the calendar year so they don't risk not being able to benefit from making a charitable donation? Many thanks.

    Susan Lennon, Hartford, CT

    Now that fewer people will be itemizing their tax returns, many financial advisers are working with clients to come up with ways to get the most bang for their charitable buck this year. That includes using donor-advised funds, which we mentioned up above (with a link there to an article that explains them in detail).

    Deb Kriebel, a certified financial planner with Pinnacle Advisory Group, said some of her clients have decided to double up on their donations to donor-advised funds this year. “A couple of them said, ‘You know, I was going to give $5,000 this year and next, so I am going to just go ahead and give $10,000 this year,’ ” she said, adding that clients can choose to have a bigger impact in one year, or spread the contribution over two years (or more, if they choose).

    +

    Is there a way to use funds from a 403(b) retirement account as a charitable contribution? Could some of the retirement account funds be placed in an I.R.A. for that purpose, making the required minimum distribution at age 70 from the I.R.A.?

    Virginia, Williamsburg VA

    Qualified Charitable Distributions (Q.C.D.) can only be made from an I.R.A. You could do a rollover from a 403(b) to an I.R.A. in order to have your assets in an I.R.A. and do the Q.C.D., but the rollover must happen in the prior year if you want any of the Q.C.D. to satisfy your required minimum distribution, according to Scott Dauenhauer, a certified financial planner with Meridian Wealth Management.

    Before doing the rollover, be careful that you are not missing out on any guarantee or investment option that you wouldn’t otherwise have access to outside of the 403(b) and make sure you’re not paying something called “surrender charges” on your money in the 403(b) if you do a rollover. Also, keep in mind that any distribution has to go directly to the charity.
