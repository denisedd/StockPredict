    The Most-Read Food Stories of 2017

    By THE NEW YORK TIMES UPDATED December 20, 2017

    Arguably, 2017 was the year of the multicooker. Anything Instant Pot was popular with readers of the Food section. In January, Melissa Clark tried one (that article is No. 2 on this list), and in the fall, she shared more recipes for it (No. 6). But two articles, published in mid-December, became late additions to the list, as they reported on inappropriate sexual behavior in the restaurant world. What follows is the most-read Food stories and columns in 2017, along with some of the most popular reader comments.

    1. 15. Is New York’s Best Pizza in New Jersey?
      Pete Wells reviews Razza in Jersey City.

      In September, our dining critic crossed the Hudson River, and named Razza Pizza Artigianale in Jersey City home to the region’s best pizza, citing a sauce “which is less acidic and bitter than many, and about as sweet as possible without tasting sugary.” Local ingredients like a buffalo mozzarella made from Sussex County water buffaloes helped, too.

      “What is perhaps most refreshing of all is that the NYT restaurant critic has ventured off the island to a pizza joint in Jersey. Not all of his readers are able to mortgage the house for a dinner. I don’t object to the reviews by Mr. Wells of those swell establishments. In fact, I expect it in the paper of record. But it’s nice to see a normal place every once in a while.” RML, New City
    2. 14. Five Common Wine Myths, Debunked
      Eric Asimov dispels common wine misconceptions.

      Five of Daniel Boulud’s sommeliers held a dinner this year to counter enduring wine shibboleths. Among them? The proper temperature to serve wine and fixations on fine vintages. “I was drawn to this dinner because, as with anyone who writes or talks about wine regularly, I am intimately familiar with all sorts of widely held yet mistaken notions,” our wine critic said. “They are worth discussing, as these fallacies can be significant barriers to getting the most pleasure out of drinking wine.”

      “I would ask to include a sixth common wine myth: ‘Fine Rating Fixations.’ I have met too many people who will only buy fine rated wines that critics deem outstanding. This may be a fine strategy for investors — those who buy great wines with the intent of reselling for a profit — but for people who simply want to enjoy good wines, it is a mistake, and sometimes a costly one.” Rafael Mauaccad, São Paulo
    3. 13. The Single Most Important Ingredient
      Samin Nosrat with a lesson on proper salting.

      Before she joined The New York Times Magazine as an Eat columnist, Ms. Nosrat was extolling the importance of proper seasoning in the Food section. This article, an excerpt from her book “Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat: Mastering the Elements of Good Cooking,” covered the three main kinds of salt and the nuances of salting.

      “You can have the freshest, most decadent, most expensive of ingredients, but if you don’t get the balance of the salt right in a dish, you might as well have bought it at the corner convenience store.” JA, Michigan

      Recipes: Buttermilk-Marinated Roast Chicken | Caesar Salad

    4. 12. How Erin French Made a Tiny Maine Town a Dining Destination
      Tejal Rao profiles a chef who does things her own way.

      From her small, idiosyncratic restaurant in Freedom, Me., Ms. French has found success and a cult following with her own approach: open, intimate and personal.

      “Grateful for this article about women empowering and caring for each other; we need more such stories in a time when some powerful, public males demean women on a daily basis. And I love how Erin’s Mom — and maybe whole family — helps to sustain this dream.” Pamela Mercier, St. Paul

      Recipes: Halibut Niçoise | Chilled Golden Beet and Buttermilk Soup

    6. 11. The Hippies Have Won
      Christine Muhlke on how the counterculture is winning the war for the dinner bowl.

      Vegetarianism and veganism are on on the rise; and kale, the bacon of the clean-eating moment, is now routinely heaped on salad plates across the land. Products and ideas surrounding eating, health and wellness that seem like a 1970s flashback have gone mainstream.

      “As the trends described here work their way into the mainstream, and more and more people are opened up to the universe beyond animal products, it’s inevitable that the movement will continue to gain momentum. While veganism might never be universal, I’d like to believe that it will eventually become the new norm, leaving the animal eaters as the outliers. The world will certainly be a better place for it.” JR, State of Shock

      Recipe: Pan-Griddled Sweet Potatoes With Miso-Ginger Sauce

    7. 10. Traces of Controversial Herbicide Are Found in Ben & Jerry’s Ice Cream
      Stephanie Strom reports on a chemical found in the pesticide Roundup.

      In July, the ice cream brand joined the list of food companies that are grappling with trace amounts of glyphosate in their products. The herbicide is the main ingredient in the popular consumer pesticide Roundup, which is widely used in agriculture.

      “As a biologist and toxicologist I continue to be astonished at the collective shrug with which many people and government regulators respond to news about artificial chemical contamination of our environment and, not incidentally, our food supply. Biodiversity is crashing at what may be an irreversible and is certainly an alarming rate - the biodiversity that sustains our agriculture from pollinators to soil microorganisms. If you don’t care what’s in your and your children’s food, it might be time to at least consider that your food supply depends on non-human organisms we are killing off or severely harming with intentionally applied pesticides and chemical pollutants from our industries, farms, and cities. Human health is not the only thing at risk here, but we ultimately don’t escape the consequences of what we are doing to the planet either.” Susan Meadows, Santa Fe, N.M.
    8. 9. Chef Sean Brock Puts Down the Bourbon and Begins a New Quest
      Kim Severson profiles the chef behind the Husk restaurants.

      After the chef Sean Brock went to rehab, he came back with a new mission: teaching people in the restaurant business how to ask for help. “Suffering is suffering,” Mr. Brock said. “It doesn’t matter if you are addicted to porn on the internet or you’re codependent or you’re addicted to gambling or if you’re addicted to ‘The Real Housewives of Atlanta.’ You’re suffering, and that’s what gets us into trouble.”

      “After 35 years in the business I didn’t recognize myself. The business didn’t make me angry, impatient, or unable to see other’s needs. It just brought out the worst parts of me and allowed them a place to flourish. Only after stepping back did I realize this business that was my life didn’t allow me to grow because of how it is but mostly because of who I was. Only now am I learning about those other parts of me that were prized by many. I now understand life (work) is not only about getting it right, and right now. It’s about taking care of people. That includes yourself first and foremost.” KB, Fairfax
    9. 8. Mario Batali Steps Away From Restaurants Amid Sexual Misconduct Allegations
      Four women alleged that the chef had touched them inappropriately.

      Earlier this month the celebrity chef Mario Batali, one of the country’s most high-profile restaurateurs, announced he was  stepping away from the daily operations of his businesses and the daytime program he co-hosts on ABC, “The Chew,” amid allegations of sexual misconduct.

      “I worked in a variety of restaurants in the ’80s and ’90s. At that time harassment was an everyday thing for women workers; it was the culture and few thought to report the disrespect of wolf whistles, suggestions of a sexual nature or the casual brushing up against a woman’s body with a wiggle of the eyebrows and a smirk on the face of the males. If you needed your job, as so many of us did, you ignored it and moved on. If the culture has not changed since then, it is more than time for it to do so.” Vicki, Florence, Ore.
    11. 7. When Jack Daniel’s Failed to Honor a Slave, an Author Rewrote History
      Clay Risen with one woman’s mission to bring recognition to a whiskey pioneer.

      When Jack Daniel’s faltered on its vow to honor Nearest Green, a slave distiller who taught Jack Daniel how to make whiskey, Fawn Weaver sprang into action. “It was jarring that arguably one of the most well-known brands in the world was created, in part, by a slave,” said Ms. Weaver, an African-American real estate investor and author.

      “The story of Fawn Weaver is the true story of America. Ms. Weaver saw an historical ‘mistake’ made by a whisky company in the Deep South, and she didn’t just sigh and silently say to herself, ‘just another proof that racism is still alive and well.’ Instead she got up and did something about it, and made all of our lives richer in the process. Her obvious patience, persistence, and success in this story, in the face of what probably looked like quite daunting odds in the beginning, is a story of some of the very best that America truly is.” Edmund Malik, Salinas, Calif.
    12. 6. To Transform Your Weeknight Cooking, Turn On the Instant Pot
      Melissa Clark falls in love with a multicooker.

      A multicooker is dinnertime convenience in stainless steel form — an appliance that combines an electric pressure cooker with a slow cooker, electric steamer and rice cooker — and for our Good Appetite columnist, there is no other single gadget that can make weeknight cooking easier.

      “I’ve tried similar things, they just take all the fun out of cooking. There’s no real satisfaction in cooking a meal with one of these things. The fun is in the process; a dash of this, a dash of that, a taste test, adjustments, the wonderful sizzle and sputter of the oil as you toss in an ingredient, the satisfaction of not only watching but actually causing a dish to come together from disparate ingredients into something that is much more than the sum of its parts. There’s something very empowering about cooking, and using a gadget instead takes that out of the equation entirely.” A, W

      Recipes: Pressure Cooker Sticky Tamarind Baby Back Ribs | Pressure Cooker Coconut Curry Chicken | How to Use an Instant Pot

    13. 5. The 400-Degree Thanksgiving
      Sam Sifton and Melissa Clark formulate a one-day plan for cooking Thanksgiving.

      This blueprint for cooking an entire Thanksgiving meal over the course of one day in an oven and on four burners above it proved to be very popular with readers.

      “We had the best Thanksgiving dinner using this. Thank you NYT!!!!! We cooked for four people and it worked perfectly. We started an hour earlier at 7 a.m. and got everything done on schedule. Had we done some prep the day before, we could have started at 8 a.m. and been fine. The recipes are terrific and all worked as advertised. Easy to prep, and cooking everything at 400 degrees made things very simple in the over. My kids are tough with food, and they ate everything and had seconds. Great Thanksgiving!!!!” Michael Roban, Los Angeles
    15. 4. An Internet-Famous Cookie Worthy of Baking in Real Life
      Julia Moskin finds another reason to get excited about chocolate chip cookies.

      A technique that creates thin chocolate chip cookies, with crisp ripples that run to the edges, finds Instagram fame.

      “My grandmother, my mother, other family members and I have been using the pan-banging technique for many types of cookies for years — at least since the 1940s and probably long before that. So this is not news to us. And, it works just fine without chilling the dough or making the dough balls large or using chopped chocolate (although using good chocolate is always a great thing).” Jennifer Farrand, Midwest

      Recipe: Giant Crinkled Chocolate Chip Cookies

    16. 3. Ken Friedman, Power Restaurateur, Is Accused of Sexual Harassment
      Julia Moskin and Kim Severson’s investigation into sexual misconduct at the Spotted Pig.

      Julia Moskin and Kim Severson spent months interviewing former employees of Ken Friedman, the owner of the Spotted Pig, a West Village restaurant helmed by the chef April Bloomfield. The women said that Mr. Friedman had subjected them to unwanted sexual advances: groping them in public, demanding sex or making text requests for nude pictures or group sex. Within hours of the article’s publication, his company announced that Mr. Friedman had decided to take an indefinite leave of absence from the management of the restaurants.

      “I worked at the Spotted Pig for a couple years long ago, when a lot of this was happening. I witnessed it first hand. We all did. And then we would complain about it to each other afterwards, but that was all. It’s strange how only when you see it in print 12 years later does it actually seem real and the gravity of it truly sinks in. People were getting hurt, every day. I feel sick with shame. I never said anything or did anything about it.” Jonathan Stan, Brooklyn
    17. 2. Why Do Cooks Love the Instant Pot? I Bought One to Find Out
      Melissa Clark takes the Instant Pot for a test drive.

      The electric pressure cooker is the kitchen gadget of the moment. In January, Melissa Clark looked at what it does (and doesn’t do) well.

      “I have an Instant Pot, and really like it for specific tasks. I don’t do a lot of ‘dump a meal into the pot’ recipes which some folks love. Most frequent things that I make are yogurt, steel cut oats, quinoa, mashed potatoes, and fajita-seasoned chicken thighs. I use the slow-cook function to make pinto beans. Risotto is grand in the IP. I have made some great soups, like butternut squash soup and black bean soup, and I’ve had good experiences making baby back ribs. My observation from spending some time in the IP Facebook group is that disappointment seems to come when folks try to do things that just aren’t amenable to pressure-cooking. The IP hasn’t replaced my stove, but it is one more useful tool. I probably use it 3 to 4 times per week.” Hannah K., Lexington, Ky.
