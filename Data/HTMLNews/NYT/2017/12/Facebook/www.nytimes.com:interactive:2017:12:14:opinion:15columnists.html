    Columnists’ Book Club

    DEC. 14, 2017

    We asked our writers to give each other reading recommendations. Here's what Paul Krugman, Gail Collins, Ross Douthat and others proposed.

    From: Paul Krugman
    To: Michelle Goldberg

    I was going to suggest something worthy and enlightening, but being worthy and enlightening is our day job, so maybe some escapism is in order? On the other hand, one can’t completely ignore the times we live in; I find that my best literary escapism comes from novels that resonate with the shadows over our lives, pretty much the same way that sad songs seem to help when you’re having personal hard times.

    So, Michelle, if you haven’t encountered the novels of Alan Furst, I’d like to give him a plug. He has a long series of “Night Soldiers” novels — they’re thrillers, but more cerebral than shoot-’em-up — set in the late ’30s and early ’40s, pretty much roaming around a Europe shadowed by dark forces that have once again become all too familiar, but lightened, of course, by the fact that we know how it turned out.

    Of these, I think the best is “Dark Star,” involving a journalist turned reluctant spy — a typical Furst protagonist, cosmopolitan, living in and loving Paris but being caught between multiple worlds and forces.

    Furst’s novels have given me a lot of pleasure and relief. I hope they do something for you.

    From: Bret Stephens
    To: Gail Collins

    Gail, We spend a lot of the time discussing — and mostly agreeing on — the subject of America’s 45th president. For moral and intellectual relief, I recommend Ron Chernow’s new biography of our 18th, “Grant.”

    Ulysses Grant was Donald Trump’s opposite in nearly every respect. Understated. Underrated. Unadorned. Loyal to his soldiers, his president, his wife. A champion of African-Americans. Compulsively honest. Literate. Magnanimous. A supposed failure with a hidden genius for war — as opposed to a supposed success with a hidden string of failures.

    Grant was also an alcoholic who won the Civil War even as he struggled with his addiction. The current president is a teetotaler who behaves like a drunk and may yet be driving us toward another civil war.

    The book is about 1,000 pages long. I devoured it in a week. In these gloomy political times, isn’t it good to be reminded of the stuff real leaders are made of?

    From: Nicholas Kristof
    To: Roger Cohen

    I’m pleased to present Roger with the cover from a hardback of Proust’s “À la Recherche du Temps Perdu.” Not the book itself, mind you, but just the paper slip cover.

    Roger already is instantly impressive the moment he opens his mouth, because that British accent always leaves us Americans feeling inadequate. It gets worse when Roger meanders off into his fluent French, German, Spanish, Italian or Portuguese, but all this is evident only when he speaks. So with Proust in French under his arm, he’ll be able to disarm people without even saying a word. Sources will collapse into a quivering mass and tell him what he wants. Granted, I did briefly have second thoughts, for my own view is that Proust is so unreadable that it would be uncharitable to give a friend a novel of his, but that’s why I’m simply presenting Roger with a Proust cover. Now, Roger, you can conveniently place the cover over whichever thriller that you are actually reading.

    From: David Brooks
    To: Frank Bruni

    Dear Frank,

    You’ve probably read them, but this strikes me as the perfect season to go back and read some of Calvin Trillin’s hilarious food books. They remind one, in these shadowy times, that the world can be savory and amusing, and still worth rising out of bed for. I’ve got three yellowing copies of them on my bookshelves and they certainly stand the test of time: “American Fried: Adventures of a Happy Eater,” “Alice, Lets Eat: Further Adventures of a Happy Eater” and “Travels with Alice.”

    Trillin is a master of the good first sentence. Here are a few of the ways he starts off his chapters: “Now that it’s fashionable to reveal intimate details of married life, I can state publicly that my wife, Alice, has a weird predilection for limiting our family to three meals a day.” “I once wronged the state of Kentucky, but compared with the Kentucky Fried Chicken people I am innocent.” “My friend Fats Goldberg, the pizza baron, has been slim enough to be called Larry for years, but I still think of him as Fats Goldberg. So does he.”

    Trillin’s columns delighted me when I was first going into journalism. If not for Trillin I might never have become a columnist. I once mentioned this in an email to him. Trillin, a lefty who used to write for The Nation, responded, “Let’s keep that fact between ourselves.”

    From: Ross Douthat
    To: Charles Blow

    For Charles, who has distinguished himself even among our roster of anti-Trump columnists with his stress on this presidency’s links to white supremacism, I will follow the principle of “know thine enemy” and recommend two somewhat different books that discuss how white identity politics has gained ground in contemporary America. Two of them are recent: “Kill All Normies,” Angela Nagle’s portrait of the online culture war, and “Making Sense of the Alt-Right,” by George Hawley, an academic historian of conservative thought. Neither of them will change your mind about the nature of the threat, Charles, but they will enrich your understanding of how identitarian extremism found a new foothold on the right.

    From: Michelle Goldberg
    To: Maureen Dowd

    Maybe this is a little bit on the nose, but I have to recommend Michelle Dean’s “Sharp: The Women Who Made an Art of Having an Opinion,” a delicious cultural history that comes out in April. It brings together some of the most influential social critics of the 20th century, including Dorothy Parker, Mary McCarthy, Hannah Arendt, Susan Sontag and Joan Didion, and shows how these glamorous iconoclasts forged their singular careers. Dean makes the convincing argument that women’s voices — if not necessarily feminist ones — did far more to define the last century’s intellectual life than we realize. She writes, “The longer I looked at the work of these women laid out before me, the more puzzling I found it that anyone could look at the literary and intellectual history of the twentieth century and not center women in it.”

    From: Andrew Rosenthal
    To: Ross Douthat

    For Ross, a book I read in college that changed my view of American history: Gabriel Kolko’s brilliant “The Triumph of Conservatism,” about the way lawyers for American corporations hijacked and rewrote the laws that were supposed to put some restraints on megabusinesses and instead made sure nobody who did not belong to the club would have a chance. The “conservative” right has been following that playbook ever since.

    From: Gail Collins
    To: Paul Krugman

    Paul, I know you’re a great cat fan. In fact, you debuted on Twitter wearing a T-shirt with a cat-with-Trump-face. These days the art of Cat Trump has grown a great deal, and you deserve an updated version. Or maybe a cat walking across a wall. (To show the permeability of borders.) Or just a fat cat on a big pile of cash? I couldn’t find a cat whose national security adviser pleaded guilty to lying to the F.B.I. But there are still several shopping days until Christmas. In the meantime, check out @furrrrguson, a 12-pound cat who snorts and thinks he’s superior to dogs.

    From: David Leonhardt
    To: David Brooks

    David, you pointed out in your Thanksgiving column that America needed a new national narrative. For inspiration, you pointed readers to Abraham Lincoln’s second inaugural — a “great sermon of redemption and reconciliation.”

    My reading recommendation for you is the latest volume of the “Oxford History of the United States,” which starts not long after that inaugural speech. It’s called “The Republic for Which It Stands,” by Richard White of Stanford University, and it covers this country’s history from 1865 to 1896. I’m in the midst of it now, and I’ve often thought of your column while I’ve been reading it.

    The Times’s review of the book captures part of why I think you’ll be drawn to it: White focuses on “‘the common’ — the striving middle- and working-class America of shops and neighborhoods, churches and trade unions,” as Sean Wilentz, the reviewer, wrote. “In mostly unsung ways, they expanded the public good.”

    By the way, if you enjoy the book, it may point you to hours upon hours of reading pleasure. This is the ninth volume published so far in the series, and several of the others are truly excellent.

    From: Maureen Dowd
    To: Andrew Rosenthal

    As Robert Mueller opens up every Russian nesting doll of the Trump administration, down to that last hidden one, I’m sending Andy all 744 pages of “The Romanovs: 1613-1918,” by Simon Sebag Montefiore. The dynasty was, according to the author, “blood-spattered, gold-plated, diamond-studded, swash-buckled, bodice-ripping and star-crossed.”

    Sounds like another dynasty we know.

    As Olga Grushin wrote in her New York Times review: “The Romanovs inhabited a world of turmoil and excess, ‘a world where obscure strangers suddenly claim to be dead monarchs reborn, brides are poisoned, fathers torture their sons to death, sons kill fathers, wives murder husbands, … barbers and peasants ascend to supremacy,’ and more and more. Endless visions of horror, splendor and absurdity — chopped-off heads soaked in vodka and courtiers traveling with their own portable gardens, children roasted and eaten in pogroms and wedding palaces carved out of ice, courtiers made to dress as chickens and sit clucking for hours and carriages pulled by bears — crowd one another so closely that the sheer concentration of history can become overwhelming.”

    One character at court is described by the author as a “shark that can clean its gills only by eating more.” Does that ring a bell in the Trumpian era of turmoil and excess?

    I’ve been trying to persuade Andy since the Republican convention to use his Russian language skills and sources to dig up the truth of what really happened between Trump and the Russians.

    I’m hoping reading about another gold-plated, conspiracy-minded dynasty will spur you to head to Red Square, Andy.

    From: Frank Bruni
    To: Nicholas Kristof

    My gift to Nick is random, and it’s a cheat, because it’s really a gift to all, an excuse to plug a poet who recedes too far from view and a poem of hers that so many readers never viewed at all. Nick, I present to you “Mad Girl’s Love Song,” by Sylvia Plath.

    It’s a villanelle, which, despite Dylan Thomas’s best effort, is an underused, underexposed form: a musical sleight of poetic hand. It’s overwrought and (per the title) madly romantic, like so much of Plath’s work.

    And it really is right for you, in this sense: You have done such a spectacular, necessary job of chronicling epic suffering on grand canvases. But some of the most wrenching drama happens on the more intimate terrain of a tortured individual’s heart. That’s Plath’s terrain, and that’s her heart, which beats so wildly and vulnerably in “Mad Girl’s Love Song” that you just know it will give out too soon.

    From: Charles Blow
    To: David Leonhardt

    David, I’m a Martin Luther King Jr. devotee. When I was a boy growing up in the South in his wake, this Southern black man of towering intellect, soaring oratory and piercing moral clarity was to me a beacon in the darkness, a template for a life lived with purpose. I still can’t get enough of reading about him. So I recommend “Redemption: Martin Luther King’s Last 31 Hours,” by Joseph Rosenbloom. (It will not be published until March, but I have an advance copy that I can lend you.) The granular detail of this slender volume is immersive, humanizing and demystifying.

    From: Roger Cohen
    To: Bret Stephens

    Bret, I’ve tried a lot of ledes for this but you just keep moving so fast, and as you know I’m a sedentary type, so I’ve had to discard them all.

    The world is so messed up these days, so full of problems, so earnest about everything, you’ve got to laugh out loud sometimes, so how about reading Paul Beatty’s “The Sellout,” a book that proves even race (but perhaps not gender) can be treated with mind-bending hilarity.

    But perhaps it’s really wrong to lighten up in this day and age. I mean, apart from all the people in the Middle East waiting for peace, there are all the people waiting in vain for the New York subway, and that’s not easy either. There’s nothing like good old-fashioned reporting, and that’s what Dan Ephron did to produce his very fine book “Killing a King: The Assassination of Yitzhak Rabin and the Remaking of Israel.” It’s fascinating how avoidable and at the same time how inevitable the assassination was. With time it has become clear that Yigal Amir succeeded — a terrible outcome.

    Too serious! Way too serious. Part of Bret just wants to get off to the beach. He wants his toes in the water and a Pabst Blue Ribbon in his hand (or maybe a Corona, given that Mexico City upbringing).

    Here’s what I want you to read: William Finnegan’s “Barbarian Days: A Surfing Life.” It’s just beautiful. And George Orwell’s “Burmese Days.” You won’t put it down and you’ll feel the reek of empire. And of course Malcolm Lowry’s “Under the Volcano,” a love story — no, the love story — of your Mexico. Happy holidays, Bret.
