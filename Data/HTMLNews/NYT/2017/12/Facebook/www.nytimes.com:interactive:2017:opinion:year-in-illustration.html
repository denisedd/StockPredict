    The Year In Illustration 2017

    By THE NEW YORK TIMES DEC. 28, 2017

    Every year, The Times commissions thousands of illustrations from artists around the globe. Here are some of the most notable from 2017.

    Every year The New York Times commissions thousands of original illustrations from independent artists around the globe. Artists are asked to distill the most compelling aspects of stories and create a powerful experience. They often have just hours to make images that move, provoke and enrich the act of reading.

    During years of tumult and uncertainty, illustrators use the medium’s inherent flexibility to comment on a rapidly evolving social, political and economic landscape. When a single subject dominates the news, artists find new ways to elaborate on it without succumbing to dull repetition. They clear away the noise and get to the point, using pen, pencil, brush, stylus, cut paper, clay, motion and raw code to produce indelible images that resonate.

    Below is a selection of notable art The Times published in 2017.

    Shakespeare’s Hot Mess: What We Can Learn From Falstaff

    Artist: Armando Veve

    “A maximalist celebration of Shakespeare which covers two books dedicated to showing Hamlet's and Falstaff's eternal flaws and conflicts as alive as ever in the modern moment.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    Exciting Microbe Research? Temper That Giddy Feeling in Your Gut
    (On desktop, move your mouse to interact with the illustration. On mobile, tilt your phone.)

    Artist: Vince Mckelvie

    “This was one of a series of interactive illustrations we decided to commission in WebGL. What would the world look like without JPEGs?” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    Blame Technology, Not Longer Life Spans, for Health Spending Increases

    Artist: Erik Blad

    “Creating humor in drawings is hard. These two examples delight us with their combination of 3D animation and illustration.” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    The Upshot

    How I Lost Weight and Learned to Love Thanksgiving Again

    How to Do Broadway

    “A year of Trump-related articles requires a year's worth of Trump-related imagery. How do we avoid repeating the same images over and over? Brilliant illustrators to the rescue.” — Nathan Huang, art director

    The Problem With Participatory Democracy Is the Participants

    Artist: Matt Chase

    “A clever and playful illustration of ‘armchair politics.’” — Alexandra Zsigmond, art director

    When ‘Conservatives’ Turned Into Radicals

    Artist: Derek Brahney

    Democrats, Please Get Ready to Lose

    Artist: Ben Wiseman

    Erick Erickson: The Fantasy of Impeachment

    Artist: Adam Maida

    “Good photoillustration requires careful selection and precise alteration of an existing image. Here, Adam makes it look easy, modifying an unusual angle in an unexpected way—an inspired solution during a year of many Trumps.” — James Datz, art director

    My Supercharged, Tricked Out, Bluetooth Wheelchair Life Force

    Artist: Dadu Shin

    “Our weekly Disability series is illustrated by the same artist — an opportunity to explore one subject from many angles (unlike our daily commissions.) Because this essay was lighter than most, the editor asked Dadu to have fun with it. He did.” — James Datz, art director

    11 Years Old, a Mom, and Pushed to Marry Her Rapist in Florida

    Artist: Anna Parini

    “Anna Parini demonstrates how powerful an illustration can be when it comes to sensitive topics such as rape and child marriage. Sometimes the most effective approach is to depict the cry for help in a child’s eyes.” — Nathan Huang, art director

    Therapy for Sexual Misconduct? It’s Mostly Unproven

    Artist: Benedicte Muller

    “Sexual harassment is a big topic in the news right now. This illustration depicts the perpetrator in therapy. Benedicte's clever use of overlapping grays creates a ghostly portrayal of the perpetrator's demons and victims.” — Cathy Gilmore Barnes, art director

    How Doctors Fail Women Who Don’t Want Children

    Artist: Bianca Bagnarelli

    Why Nobody Cares the President Is Lying

    Sketching the Bill Cosby Trial

    Artist: Lauren Tamaki

    “Courtroom drawing has a long visual history; we wanted to try to refresh this practice. Lauren attended the trial and made these sketches from life, sending them to us shortly after they were made. These images tell a very different story of the trial than one told in photographs.” — Alicia DeSantis, art director

    What a Fraternity Hazing Death Revealed About the Painful Search for an Asian-American Identity

    New York Stories

    Margaret Atwood on What ‘The Handmaid’s Tale’ Means in the Age of Trump

    Artist: Eleni Kalorkoti

    “Margaret Atwood reflects on the myriad reasons for The Handmaid's Tale's modern appeal. Eleni Kalorkoti's interpretation used the popular iconography of Atwood's writing to illustrate how the distance we've come since 1984 isn't nearly as far as it ought to be.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    How to Stay Sane During a Solar Eclipse

    Artist: Geoff McFetridge

    How to Have More Fun Than Usual Today (print only)

    Artist: Kelsey Dake

    Microbes, a Love Story

    Artist: Davy Evans

    An Ancient Cure for Alzheimer’s? | The Planet Can’t Stand This Presidency | Racism Is Everywhere, So Why Not Move South?

    Artists: Eleanor Davis, Eiko Ojala, Cannaday Chapman

    The Lawless Presidency | Let Black Kids Just Be Kids | Why ‘Skinny’ Obamacare Repeal Is a Terrible Idea | Why Waiters Drink. And Why It Matters.

    Artists: Brian Stauffer, Jillian Tamaki, Harry Campbell, Joan Wong

    From Push-Ups to Bralettes: Victoria’s Secret Revamps

    Artist: Luci Gutierrez

    How Many Pills Are Too Many?

    Artist: Scott Gelber

    “Hypnosis has been a popular theme over the past century. Scott Gelber applied similar thinking when creating this infinite-loop animation.” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    You Know Your History? These Podcasts Aren’t So Sure

    Artist: Tracy Ma

    “We decided that all of the illustrations in our reoccuring Amanda Hess columns should have motion. But they also have to be bold and graphic enough to hold a print page.” — Alicia DeSantis, art director

    Yes, Your Sleep Schedule Is Making You Sick

    Artist: Icinori

    “This animation is based on a phenakistoscope disc from the mid-1800s, the first widespread animation device to give an illusion of fluent motion and a precursor to the GIF.” — Alexandra Zsigmond, art director

    As Workouts Intensify, a Harmful Side Effect Grows More Common (as seen in NYTimes Snapchat)

    Artist: Eden Weingart

    “To inject a bit of humor into this piece on the risks of hyper-intense exercise, I wanted to illustrate the mismatch between our workout desires and what our bodies can actually handle.” — Eden Weingart

    A Czech Astronaut’s Earthly Troubles Come Along for the Ride

    Artist: David Jien

    “Fleeing marital problems in space is no easier than confronting them on earth. Because in space you have to contend with giant mutant spiders, which may or may not be specters conjured by your own guilt.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    The Civil Rights Stories We Need to Remember

    Artist: Najeebah Al-Ghadban

    “A catalog of injustices under the Jim Crow system as told through the life and persecution of Caliph Washington.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    I Am a Member of the Muslim Brotherhood, Not a Terrorist

    Artist: Mark Pernice

    “For a text written by an author while imprisoned in solitary confinement, Mark played with our visual perception to make a powerful statement about subjective/objective truths (and earned himself a Society of Illustrators gold medal for it).” — James Datz, art director

    America Made Me a Feminist

    Artist: Cristiana Coureiro

    “Cristiana Coureiro's striking surrealist collage utilizes images culled directly from the text — such as the “little bug” (a nickname for women in the Czech Republic) and the “hidden stiletto knife” (a metaphor for French women's sexual power).” — Alexandra Zsigmond, art director

    Drop the Bluster on North Korea

    Artist: Linda Huang

    “Diplomacy in Trump times — Cold War symbols of a hot line and a missile convey the dangers and the possibilities of Washington's confrontation with North Korea. As is often the case, this editorial responded quickly to the news, so Linda produced this from sketch to final on the same day (in about six hours).” — Sarah Williamson, art director

    Time for Congress to Investigate Mr. Trump’s Ties to Russia

    Artist: Justin Renteria

    The Silence Breaks (print only)

    Artist: Rachel Levit

    “Rachel Levit called in one week after the Weinstein news broke, and she wanted to respond to the avalanche of sexual violence allegations that followed. The art says it all with remarkable conceptual clarity.” — Andrew Sondern, art director

    Byrd Bath: Seven Provisions That Could Disappear From the Senate Health Bill

    Artist: Luis Mazon

    “Using the GIF as a tool, Luis Mazon enables the viewer to experience an illusion of movement.” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    Silicon Valley Is Not Your Friend

    Artist: Yoshi Sodeoka

    To Tweet Is Human, to Delete Divine

    Artist: Scorpion Dagger

    “This animation started out as a personal piece by James Kerr (Scorpion Dagger), who creates hilarious GIFs using Renaissance paintings. He added Trump's head and robe to fit the article.” — Alexandra Zsigmond, art director

    Nuclear War Doesn’t Seem So Funny After All

    Artists: Klaas Verplancke (illustration) and Arevik d'Or (animation)

    The High Price of Failing America’s Costliest Patients

    Artist: Jody Barton

    “Jody Barton's drawing uses the desktop's white negative space to extend the artwork’s narrative.” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    White Nationalism Is Destroying the West

    Artist: JooHee Yoon

    Try These ‘Love Hacks’ to Fix Your Marriage

    Artist: Pawel Mildner

    “Pawel sketched several playful ideas to illustrate this article. The one we loved best showed a mess of distractions that come with having a family, which leave too little time for the married couple to focus on each other.” — Cathy Gilmore Barnes, art director

    A Fictional (So Far) History of the Second American Civil War

    Artist: Sophy Hollington

    “What would distinguish a second civil war from the first one? Fossil fuels and corporate interests! Sophy's approach entertains a potential future dystopia as a possibility for our present. Thanks a lot, Sophy.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    We’re Sick of Racism, Literally

    Artist: Angie Wang

    My Trip to the DMZ

    Artist: Christoph Niemann

    “An experimental 360º Sketchbook about my tourist trip to the border between North and South Korea for The New York Times Magazine.” — Christoph Niemann

    What Not to Eat: “The Case Against Sugar”

    Artist: Hudson Christie

    “Sugar is delicious. Sugar is deadly. The goal was to hold these competing thoughts in the same space without siding with one to the exclusion of the other.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    In the Shadow of a Fairy Tale

    Artist: Ben Giles

    The Liver: A “Blob” That Runs the Body

    Artist: Guyco

    “Within the human body, the function of the liver is secondary only to that of the brain. Visually, the liver is little more than a ‘blob.’ Guyco found an elegant and visually pleasing way to illustrate the inherent value of this ‘blob.’” — Cathy Gilmore Barnes, art director

    Colson Whitehead on George Saunders’s Novel About Lincoln and Lost Souls

    Artist: Jason Holley

    “Reconciling Abraham Lincoln, his deceased son, the Buddhist interpretation of limbo and all accompanying sorrows of the Civil War in a single image is impossible unless you call Jason Holley.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    The Return of the Techno-Moral Panic

    Artist: Jon Han

    Why Hollywood’s Most Thrilling Scenes Are Now Orchestrated Thousands of Miles Away

    Artist: Giacomo Gambineri

    People Have Limited Knowledge. What’s the Remedy? Nobody Knows

    Artist: Janet Hansen

    “The text argues that we're really not all that smart. We know far less than we think we do as individuals because we treat knowledge in the minds of others as if it were our own. Janet zeroed in on a classic symbol of erudition and swiftly, deftly, cut it down to size.” — Matt Dorfman, art director

    How New York Is Turning Food Waste Into Compost and Gas

    Artist: Joren Cull

    “Joren Cull pushes the graphic novel format, allowing animation to flow from one quadrant to another.” — Antonio De Luca, art director

    Facebook Live Drawing Series: How to Doodle (Almost) Anything

    Artist: Jon Burgerman

    “The New York Times Live Drawing Series engages a live Facebook audience in an interactive experience with illustrators and artists. Each week artists create work around a given theme, with viewers offering comments and questions as they watch. Our editors then pass them on, and the artists respond in real time. Check out more of the series: What’s in Your Warm-Weather Wardrobe?, What Is the Future of Identity? and What's Your Mood?” — Antonio De Luca, art director, and Josephine Sedgwick, producer

    From Voyeur to Friend

    Artist: Vincent Mahé

    “Vincent Mahé split this illustration into two panels to emphasize the voyeuristic divide between seer and seen.” — Alexandra Zsigmond, art director

    Arundhati Roy’s Return to the Form That Made Her Famous

    Artist: Yann Kebbi

    “In which characters from unrelated movements, conflicts and lower castes foster solidarity and survive amid strangers at an anti-corruption protest in Delhi. The crayons dragged across the cover tell you everything about the emotional, physical and systemic violence at the root of the story.” — Matt Dorfman, art director
