    Is Trump Driving the Stock Market Rally?

    By RAYMOND FISMAN and ERIC ZITZEWITZ DEC. 14, 2017

    A live feed of President Donald Trump speaking is projected on the floor at the closing bell of the Dow Industrial Average at the New York Stock Exchange on November 15, 2017 in New York. Bryan R. Smith/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

    “Highest stock market ever!” President Trump boasted last week. By his own reckoning, Wall Street’s rally since his election is proof of the economic potency of his presidency.

    But is Mr. Trump really delivering what investors expected? To answer that question, we assembled a set of Trump Policy Indexes. They offer a measure of Wall Street investors’ beliefs about the impact of his policies on businesses.

    Our general index, the Trump Bulls and Bears, tracks two portfolios of stocks: the Bulls (like Morgan Stanley), which investors initially expected would benefit disproportionately from Mr. Trump’s election; and the Bears (like HCA, a hospital chain), for which they expected his election would do relative harm.

    Until late November, the date of the last available data, the index showed that Mr. Trump had been largely frustrated in helping the firms and industries he was expected to — in short, he may not have had as big an impact on the market as he claims for himself. The Bulls were, on average, not far ahead of the Bears — perhaps investors had unrealistic ideas of how easy it would be for Mr. Trump to push his agenda and how effective he would be at doing it.

    But in the final days of November, with Mr. Trump and Republicans on the verge of a tax cut bill, the index rallied — not to the optimistic levels seen early in Mr. Trump’s term, but back above its pre-election level.

    In our policy graphs, which measure whether markets expect Mr. Trump to follow up on his campaign talking points, important events are noted, like the Senate's rejection of health care repeal. Sometimes these move markets, but at least as often they do not — many political events are well anticipated by investors and their effects factored in well ahead of any politician’s announcement or bill’s passage.

    To be clear, these indexes do not measure whether what Mr. Trump is doing is good or bad for the economy as a whole, but rather his effect on corporate profits.

    Raymond Fisman is a professor of economics at Boston University. Eric Zitzewitz is a professor of economics at Dartmouth.

    America First index

    The market expects Mr. Trump, whose saber rattling on trade continues, to help firms in import-exposed industries, presumably by disrupting free-trade agreements.

    The America First Index has often moved in the opposite direction from other components of the Trump Bulls index. Our theory: Perhaps protectionism is a substitute for the rest of Mr. Trump’s agenda. If he is unable to move his agenda through Congress, he may be more assertive on trade — which he can do unilaterally. (The index has also been affected by the decline in the United States dollar since April.)

    For-Profit Indexes

    The most striking Trump-era stock boom has been for the for-profit prison companies CoreCivic and Geo Group. The spectacular rise of their stocks was probably fueled by anticipation of a reversal of the promise by Eric Holder, the former attorney general, to end prison outsourcing, as well as expected demand for immigration detention.

    For-profit education has similarly done well — beating the market by an impressive 20 percent — possibly because companies expect less oversight or expansion of school choice or both.

    The Energy Indexes

    Mr. Trump may have pitched himself as the savior of coal country, but the market agrees with most energy analysts: Not even he can save coal.

    The Alternative Energy Index has shown more promise. In the minds of many investors, whether or not government subsidies continue, wind and solar have a future.

    Health Care Indexes

    Investors are split on the impact of health care reform efforts. They are bearish on hospital stocks, perhaps a sign that Republican actions will result in more Americans who are uninsured and can’t afford to pay their bills.

    But investors are bullish on insurance companies, which can pick and choose profitable markets.

    ‘Too Big to Fail’ Index

    Mr. Trump’s economic team has drawn heavily on Wall Street and now includes a Wall Street-linked chairman of the Federal Reserve, along with a Wall Street-connected Treasury secretary, Council of Economic Advisers chairman and bank regulatory appointees. Perhaps as a result, the index has surpassed the Trump Bulls portfolio.

    Hotels Index

    Given Mr. Trump’s involvement in the hotel business, we present one final, tantalizing pattern: the sustained post-election hotel-industry stock boom. This is not the result of a broader rally among companies that benefit from a low minimum wage — Restaurant and Leisure Indexes have not performed nearly as well. Why the hotel rally? We’re open to suggestions.
