12 Shows We’ll Be Talking About in November

by Noel Murray

Nov. 2, 2017UPDATED Nov. 3, 2017

Rachel Brosnahan in “The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel.”Amazon

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation website. Sign up for our thrice-weekly newsletter here.

The major networks spent the past two months trotting out new shows and bringing back favorites, so now it’s time for the cable outlets and subscription streamers to get back into the game, debuting series they hope viewers will binge through the holidays. Amazon, Hulu and Netflix all have especially strong November lineups, including adaptations of another Marvel comic and another Margaret Atwood novel, and the return of the master TV writer and producer Amy Sherman-Palladino.

In fact, impressive names are all over the credits of this month’s new shows, from the indie film legends Spike Lee and Steven Soderbergh to the reliable comedy heroes Seth Rogen and Maria Bamford. The next few weeks will also include a welcome influx of distinctive women’s voices, with new efforts by the veterans Sarah Polley and Amy Seimetz and by the relative newcomer Frankie Shaw.

Here’s what we’ll be talking about:

Netflix
‘Alias Grace’
Debuts: Nov. 3 on Netflix

For viewers craving more of the Canadian literary giant Margaret Atwood after Hulu’s Emmy-winning adaptation of her dystopian novel “The Handmaid’s Tale,” Netflix is offering a different side of the writer with this six-part mini-series, based on a piece of Atwood’s historical fiction. Directed by Mary Harron, written by Sarah Polley, and starring Sarah Gadon (all Canucks to the core), “Alias Grace” is about a real-life mid-19th-century Irish immigrant who was convicted of murdering her boss. Though not at as fantastical as “Handmaid,” the new show is equally concerned with how women who serve privileged masters take care each other — or fail to.

‘The Girlfriend Experience’
Debuts: Nov. 5 on Starz

Although producer Steven Soderbergh gets a lot of credit for the TV version of “The Girlfriend Experience” (probably because he directed the movie it’s based on), the show is really the work of the writer-directors Lodge Kerrigan and Amy Seimetz, who in Season 1 collaborated to tell the story of a promising law firm intern who’s moonlighting as a call girl. The creative team returns for the 14-episode second season, handling two entirely new, separate story lines: Seimetz follows a high-end prostitute (played by Carmen Ejogo) in the witness protection program, and Kerrigan looks at an escort (Louisa Krause) who gets embroiled in a political blackmail scheme.
—

SMILF (2017) | Official Trailer | Frankie Shaw SHOWTIME SeriesVideo by SMILF
‘SMILF’
Debuts: Nov. 5 on Showtime

A full-on auteur project for actress Frankie Shaw, this raunchy, semi-autobiographical sitcom is written and directed by its creator, who also stars as a single mother (that’s the “SM” in “SMILF”) trying to make it in Hollywood. Like Pamela Adlon’s FX show “Better Things,” “SMILF” takes an honest look at the difficulties of balancing family, a personal life and a career in the entertainment business. Shaw, however, plays someone who’s young and struggling, with a much different perspective on what her future might hold.
—

DamnationTV
‘Damnation’
Debuts: Nov. 7 on USA

USA Network continues its evolution into a prestige TV mainstay with this unusual historical drama, created by the poet, cultural critic and former “Longmire” staff writer Tony Tost. Set in Depression-era Middle America, the series follows the violent clashes between laborers and corporate goons, orchestrated by a mysterious rabble-rouser and a well-paid strikebreaker. There’s no clear analogue for “Damnation” on television right now, which makes it intriguing.
—

The Long Road Home | National GeographicVideo by National Geographic
‘The Long Road Home’
Debuts: Nov. 7 on NatGeo

The ABC News reporter Martha Raddatz turned what she saw in the field during the Iraq War into the book “The Long Road Home,” which NatGeo has adapted into an eight-part mini-series. It has some impressive production values for a network not exactly renowned for scripted programming. Jeremy Sisto, Michael Kelly, Kate Bosworth, Jason Ritter and Sarah Wayne Callies play men and women who struggle to make it through each day — on the battlefield and on the home-front — so that they can be reunited when the fighting is done.
—

Lady Dynamite - Season 2 | Official Trailer [HD] | NetflixVideo by Netflix
‘Lady Dynamite’
Debuts: Nov. 10 on Netflix

Comedian Maria Bamford brings back her one-of-a-kind Netflix sitcom, which fictionalizes her life as a restless entertainer with a sunny but fragile disposition. In Season 2, the “Lady Dynamite” version of Maria settles into a regular job, working for a subscription streaming service. The new episodes promise to strike the same tone that made the first season so special, with its bright look and wacky digressions that mask the heroine’s shaky mental health.
—

Hulu
‘Future Man’
Debuts: Nov. 14 on Hulu

If nothing else, Hulu’s new time-travel comedy deserves a look because it contains the last work by the character actress Glenne Headly, who died unexpectedly this year after filming five episodes of “Future Man.” But the show also has a solid creative pedigree, with the “Superbad”/“Preacher” partners Seth Rogen and Evan Goldberg executive producing, and the “Hunger Games” hero Josh Hutcherson starring as a nerdy custodian who may be the only person who can save the world.
—

Search Party: The All New Season Premieres November 19! [TRAILER] | TBSVideo by TBS
‘Search Party’
Debuts: Nov. 19 on TBS

One of last year’s happiest surprises, the darkly comic mystery “Search Party” delivered a first season filled with great performances (especially by the lead, Alia Shawkat), along with keen observations about young New Yorkers and their increasing sense of disconnection from their friends and their work. Although the original ten-episode run reached a fairly satisfying resolution, these characters are rich enough to support whatever lies in store in Season 2.
—

Hulu
‘Runaways’
Debuts: Nov. 21 on Hulu

The Marvel TV Universe expands to Hulu with this adaptation of the cult favorite comic book, about a group of teenagers who band together against their supervillain parents. The original series was written by Brian K. Vaughan, whose “Y: The Last Man” and “Saga” are among the most beloved comics of the 21st century. The television version is being shepherded by Josh Schwartz and Stephanie Savage, who previously worked together on “Gossip Girl” and “The O.C.” — and who know a thing or two about angsty adolescents and sinister adults.
—

Godless | Official Trailer [HD] | NetflixVideo by Netflix
‘Godless’
Debuts: Nov. 22 on Netflix

“The Girlfriend Experience” isn’t the only Steven Soderbergh production arriving to TV this month. He’s also been working with his “Out of Sight” screenwriter, Scott Frank, on Netflix’s western mini-series “Godless,” about a New Mexico settlement that’s governed almost entirely by widowed women. Frank (who wrote and directed) has assembled a fine cast for this rugged frontier drama, with Jeff Daniels playing a vengeful outlaw, Jack O’Connell as his wayward cohort, Scoot McNairy and Sam Waterston as rival lawmen and Michelle Dockery and Merritt Wever as part of the power structure in the cursed town of La Belle.
—

Netflix
‘She’s Gotta Have It’
Debuts: Nov. 23 on Netflix

In 1986, Spike Lee rocked American independent cinema with his exuberant comedy “She’s Gotta Have It,” which celebrated the busy sex life and joyous spirit of an artsy New Yorker named Nola Darling. Lee’s new Netflix series revisits his roots, with the newcomer DeWanda Wise playing an updated version of Nola, navigating three suitors in a world that’s changed a lot in the past 30 years. It will be interesting to see if Lee can still be fresh, competing in a medium that includes shows like “Insecure” and “Broad City.”
—

The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel - Official Trailer [HD] | Amazon VideoVideo by Amazon Video
‘The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel’
Debuts: Nov. 29 on Amazon

Creator Amy Sherman-Palladino (“Gilmore Girls” and “Bunheads”) tries something new with her latest project: a period dramedy set in and around the late 1950s New York stand-up comedy scene. Rachel Brosnahan stars as a housewife who starts telling jokes on stage — and soon becomes a sensation — after her husband leaves her. Sherman-Palladino brings her usual fast-paced, funny dialogue to a story that partly echoes the experiences of pioneering comics like Joan Rivers, and partly speaks to what it’s always been like to be a woman in showbiz.
—

Correction: November 2, 2017

An earlier version of this article misidentified the director of the pilot episode of “Damnation.” The director is Adam Kane, not David Mackenzie.
