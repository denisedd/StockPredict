    How Florida Launched a Technological Revolution

    It’s not a stretch to say that the future of aerospace and related IT commerce is in Florida. Ask Frank DiBello and he’ll agree. As president and CEO of Space Florida, the state’s spaceport authority, he’s charged with attracting private-sector aerospace firms and the IT businesses that support them. “As the space commerce industry evolves, we expect to be the global leader, able to partner with other nations to achieve a new kind of space economy,” says DiBello. These goals may seem like a moon shot, but if Florida’s legacy of innovation is any indication, such a trajectory is right on the mark.

    Past: An Early Launch Pad

    When the skies over Cape Canaveral filled with a crackling and mighty roar on July 24, 1950, no one guessed how common such an occurrence would become. The bellowing thrust from the Bumper 8 rocket as it pushed spaceward marked the first launch from Florida’s Space Coast.

    For the nascent space race, Florida provided the perfect launchpad for getting humans and robots into orbit and giving industries a boost. Those industries supported NASA’s space missions and became an unprecedented economic engine: New cities were built, new freeways were laid and educational institutions thrived. Iconic space missions from Apollo to the space shuttle galvanized the nation’s place in aerospace history.

    When NASA’s space shuttle program ended in 2011, many wondered about the future of the state’s aerospace sector and its associated IT industries. “We had, at the height of the shuttle, about 15,000 employees. Everyone knew there would probably be layoffs coming,” recalls Lisa Malone, former director of public affairs at NASA's John F. Kennedy Space Center.

    Present: A Modern Day Leader

    Pair the end of the shuttle program with miles of white sand beaches, sapphire blue waters, tropical climate and a general appeal to holidaymakers, and it’s easy to think that Florida’s space days might be over, replaced by a healthy tourism economy.

    Malone thinks those assumptions are premature, though, and that the Space Coast is in the middle of a renaissance, centered on one-of-a-kind facilities to process launch vehicles. “This is the only place on this continent where humans are launched into space,” says Malone. Couple that with the fact that Florida’s universities are among the top national producers of STEM (science, technology, engineering and math) degree graduates and you have a formula for success.

    The state has developed a strategy to attract companies in sectors that build on its strong aerospace legacy. New industry has more than replaced the 9,000 jobs that were lost after the shuttle program ended. “We are no longer just launching rockets, we’re building the rockets that we launch here,” says DiBello. Already private spaceflight companies such as SpaceX, Blue Origin and Virgin Galactic and most of the world’s best-known aerospace and IT brands have a presence here.

    These are just a few of the things that attracted OneWeb, a startup backed by European aerospace giant Airbus. In March the company broke ground on the world’s first facility for high-volume satellite production at Exploration Park, adjacent to the launch facilities at the Kennedy Space Center. The production and launch of satellites are secondary to OneWeb’s overall mission, though, which is to provide satellite-based global internet access.

    A favorable tax environment, a streamlined permitting process, existing infrastructure and a robust talent pool made Florida a no-brainer when OneWeb wanted to further develop its aerospace and IT products. “The Space Coast has one of the highest densities of engineers of any place in the U.S., primarily from its large base of aerospace and other technology companies,” says Greg Wyler, OneWeb’s founder and executive chairman. “It’s a very well-run state that operates without debt and has affordable housing, so our dedicated engineers can also have a great quality of life.”

    Wyler has big plans for the future, centered on OneWeb’s proprietary orbital internet technology, which is built into the satellites that its aerospace and IT talent will manufacture and launch in Florida. “Our goal is to connect every school in the world by 2022 and to bridge the digital divide by 2027,” he says.

    Future: A Promising Flight Plan

    It’s clear Florida and the enterprises that choose to do business here have a lot of faith in aerospace and IT as economic drivers for the future.

    Business leaders like DiBello are even convinced that investing in aerospace will strengthen the American economy as a whole and make Florida the Silicon Valley of the Southeast. “Whenever a nation invests in doing difficult things, they advance technology,” says DiBello. “They create a culture of innovation that eventually permeates every aspect of their society and that lifts the nation up.”

    Aerospace and IT enterprises aren’t just part of Florida’s past, they are also its future. The state is open for business and more than ready to help firms at any stage have an impact on the globe — and even, perhaps, the galaxy.
