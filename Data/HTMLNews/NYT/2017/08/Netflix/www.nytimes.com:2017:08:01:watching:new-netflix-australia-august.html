The Best Movies and TV Shows New to Netflix Australia in August

by Noel Murray

Aug. 1, 2017

Clockwise from top left: “The Defenders,” “The Lego Movie,” “Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later” and “Mad Max: Fury Road.”Netflix; Warner Bros. Pictures

Welcome to Watching, The New York Times’s TV and movie recommendation site.

Every month, Netflix Australia adds a new batch of movies and TV shows to its library. Here are the titles we think are most interesting, broken down by release date. Netflix occasionally changes its schedule without giving notice. (Unfortunately, the streaming information provided in our Watchlist listings only applies to viewers in the United States.)

Netflix Original TV Series

‘Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later’
Starts streaming: Aug. 4

After turning their raunchy ’80s teen comedy homage, “Wet Hot American Summer,” into an equally hilarious “prequel” series, the writer-director-producer team of Michael Showalter and David Wain are jumping ahead a decade, to 1991, in order to show what happened to the Camp Firewood crew after their surreal, sexy adventures in the woods. Pitched as a parody of “class reunion” movies like “The Big Chill” and ’90s Gen-X “hangout” sitcoms like “Friends” — with a cast that includes the returning comedians Michael Ian Black, Janeane Garofalo, Paul Rudd and Amy Poehler — “Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later” is likely to be just as strange and silly as every other entry in this most unlikely franchise.
—

‘Atypical’
Starts streaming: Aug. 11

As more and more American families struggle with raising children on the autistic spectrum, movie and TV producers have looked for ways to make their stories both moving and amusing. This recent effort comes from Robia Rashid, a writer for “How I Met Your Mother” and “The Goldbergs,” who has Jennifer Jason Leigh and Michael Rapaport playing the parents of Sam (Keir Gilchrist), an 18-year-old making strides to live independently, even as he wrestles with his autism. It’s a situation that all too many moms and dads will recognize.
—

‘Marvel’s The Defenders’
Starts streaming: Aug. 18

From the moment Netflix announced a partnership with Marvel Television for a set of down-to-Earth superhero shows set in New York City, comics fans have looked forward to what the project has been building toward all along: a small-screen version of the offbeat team the Defenders, who since the ’70s have been the Marvel Universe’s freakier counterpart to the Avengers. This mini-series unites Daredevil, Luke Cage, Jessica Jones and Iron Fist in a battle royale against the mystically superpowered ninjas known as the Hand.

TV Series

‘Penn & Teller: Fool Us’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

The rare reality competition that’s as edifying as it is entertaining, this cutting-edge magic show asks two of the world’s most famous illusionists to figure out the secrets behind their colleagues’ tricks. Beyond showcasing some genuinely astonishing magic, “Fool Us” is an affectionate salute to one of showbiz’s oldest professions. Penn Jillette and his silent partner, Teller, put the acts on their Las Vegas stage into a larger historical context while praising the ways that hard-working prestidigitators keep finding new ways to perform the classic routines.—

Also of interest: “Grimm” Seasons 4 and 5 (Aug. 1), “The Royal House of Windsor” Season 1 (Aug. 1), “Stranger” Season 1 (Aug. 1), “White Gold” Season 1 (Aug. 11), “The Mist” Season 1 (Aug. 25), and “Vikings” Season 4 (Aug. 25).

Movies

‘Marvel’s Doctor Strange’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

While Marvel Studios has been constructing a vast, interconnected narrative across multiple movies, some of its best films have actually been set off in their own little worlds, away from the likes of Iron Man and the Hulk. “Doctor Strange” exists within the greater Marvel Cinematic Universe, but it inhabits its own funky little neighborhood, where the title sorcerer (Benedict Cumberbatch) protects the Earth from mystical nemeses from other dimensions. The mind-bending visual effects are reason enough to watch an action-adventure film that’s more concise than the usual products of the Marvel Cinematic Universe.
—

‘Edge of Tomorrow’
Starts streaming: Aug. 10

It may have been a box office disappointment in summer 2014, but “Edge of Tomorrow” (also known by its cooler alternate title, “Live. Die. Repeat.”) has been increasingly and rightly regarded as one of the best action pictures of the past decade. Thanks to a strong star turn by Tom Cruise as a cowardly military officer — and a clever script that has the hero stuck in a time loop while trying to save Earth from alien domination — this is the rare blockbuster that’s as packed with personality as plot twists.
—

‘John Wick’
Starts streaming: Aug. 10

What starts out as a taut little crime picture becomes something else entirely in the bloody, delightful “John Wick,” which stars Keanu Reeves as a retired assassin looking for revenge against the mobsters who killed his dog. The audience gets drawn into an increasingly elaborate criminal underworld, with its own bizarre codes and rituals.
—

‘Mad Max: Fury Road’
Starts streaming: Aug. 10

Oscar-prognosticating pundits seemed shocked in 2015 when “Mad Max: Fury Road” made so many critics’ best-of-the-year lists — and were even more surprised when the movie grabbed 10 Academy Award nominations, winning six. Movie buffs, though, knew immediately that this long-delayed fourth entry in director George Miller’s post-apocalyptic epic had the goods: from Tom Hardy’s quietly compelling performance in the title role, to Charlize Theron’s scene-stealing turn as a slavery-busting warrior, to elaborately staged car chases that look like an avant-garde circus on wheels.
—

‘The Lego Movie’
Starts streaming: Aug. 10

Everything is still awesome with the first entry in Lego’s big-screen franchise, a corporate brand-boosting exercise that turned out far smarter and more soulful than anyone could have expected. Writer-directors Phil Lord and Christopher Miller honor the limited movement and limitless imagination of Lego play sets in this animated adventure, creating something that looks charmingly handmade. It’s so full of funny ideas that it almost demands repeat viewing.
—

‘The Manchurian Candidate’
Starts streaming: Aug. 25

It’s hard to read the recent headlines about Russian interference in international politics without thinking of Richard Condon’s classic novel “The Manchurian Candidate,” which imagines what would happen if a foreign power manipulated an American hero who’s running for office. Director Jonathan Demme’s 2004 movie adaptation isn’t as acclaimed as John Frankenheimer’s 1962 version, but it feels even more vital, in a way, because it’s so close to the present-day. Special notice goes to Meryl Streep, who takes on Angela Lansbury’s role from the earlier film as the controlling mother of the brainwashed candidate, embodying the modern epitome of a tough-talking, media-savvy demagogue.
—

‘O Brother, Where Art Thou?’
Starts streaming: Aug. 25

One of the Coen brothers’ biggest box office hits, this picaresque period comedy benefits from a funny lead performance by George Clooney, who plays a dandyish convict who escapes from prison and travels across the Depression-era American South with two lunkheads (John Turturro and Tim Blake Nelson). The Coens throw in some terrific old bluegrass and gospel numbers in a movie that’s more good-natured than their usual darkly ironic pictures.
—

‘Moana’
Starts streaming: Aug. 29

Given that parents get stuck watching hit animated movies over and over with their kids for years, it’s always welcome when one is as spirited and tuneful as “Moana.” The story of an island princess who recruits a wayward demigod to save her people, “Moana” may not be as elaborate as some of Disney’s other classics, but Dwayne Johnson’s voice-performance and Lin-Manuel Miranda’s catchy songs make the film a pleasure to watch. And then watch again. And again.
—

Also of interest: “The Autopsy of Jane Doe” (Aug. 1), “Dear White People” (Aug. 1), “A Man Called Ove” (Aug. 1), “12 Angry Men” (Aug. 1), “The Machinist” (Aug. 4), “Prisoners” (Aug. 6), “Disney’s Queen of Katwe” (Aug. 8), “Justice League Dark” (Aug. 8), “Brick Mansions” (Aug. 10), “Paper Planes” (Aug. 10), “A Walk Among the Tombstones” (Aug. 10), “Zack and Miri Make a Porno” (Aug. 10), “Wedding Crashers” (Aug. 17), “Airplane!” (Aug. 18)

Netflix Original Movies

‘Icarus’
Starts streaming: Aug. 4

In this probing documentary, the filmmaker and amateur cyclist Bryan Fogel sets out to experiment with performance enhancing drugs and then stumbles deep into the world of Russian doping. A look at both widespread corruption in sports and the far-reaching tentacles of international criminal syndicates (some of which are covertly state-sponsored), “Icarus” exposes the darker realities of the modern world.
—

‘Death Note’
Starts streaming: Aug. 25

This adaptation of the violent, supernaturally tinged Japanese comic book series “Death Note” looks to be one of Netflix’s most ambitious original films yet. The popular young horror director Adam Wingard (best-known for “You’re Next,” “The Guest,” and the recent “Blair Witch” revamp) steers a cast that includes the former Nickelodeon phenom Nat Wolff as a teenage vigilante, Margaret Qualley (“The Leftovers”) as the girl he likes and the “Atlanta” oddball Keith Stanfield as a dogged detective on the antihero’s trail.
