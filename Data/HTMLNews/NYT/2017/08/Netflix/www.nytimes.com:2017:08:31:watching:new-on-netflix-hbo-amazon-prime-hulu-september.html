The Best TV Shows and Movies New to Netflix, Amazon and More in September

by Monica Castillo

Aug. 31, 2017UPDATED Sep. 8, 2017

Clockwise from top left: “Tron,” “Carol,” La La Land” and “Akeelah and the Bee.”Walt Disney; Weinstein Company; Lionsgate; Vestron Pictures

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation newsletter and website.

Every month, subscription streaming services add a new batch of movies and TV shows to their libraries. Here are the titles we think are most interesting for September, broken down by service and release date. Streaming services occasionally change schedules without giving notice.

New to Netflix

Gael García Bernal in “Amores Perros.”Lions Gate Films
‘Amores Perros’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

This intense drama introduced the world to both the director Alejandro González Iñárritu and the actor Gael García Bernal. Its complex narrative revolves around a violent car crash in Mexico City that brings together three strangers — a love-struck young man trying to run away with his sister-in-law, a supermodel in a troubled relationship and a vagrant who’s actually an assassin — jumping back and forth to before and after the accident. The movie earned Iñárritu international acclaim, and he moved to Hollywood to make “21 Grams” (which also features a car crash) and “Babel.”
—

Alexandre Rodrigues and Alice Braga in “City of God.”Miramax Films
‘City of God’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Set away from Rio de Janeiro’s sandy beaches and tourist destinations, “City of God” is about a Brazilian favela that gives this lively but disturbing movie its title. The events of this ferocious drama rush by over the course of two decades, as Rocket (Alexandre Rodrigues) seeks to escape the slums, gun-toting children and ruthless gangs of his neighborhood by becoming a photographer. The film operates in extremes — shot with vivid colors, quick edits and frenetic camera movements, containing brutal spurts of violence.
—

Tracy Camilla Johns and Tommy Redmond Hicks in “She’s Gotta Have It.”Island Pictures
‘She’s Gotta Have It’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Before Netflix’s series adaptation of this 1986 film from Spike Lee film debuts this Thanksgiving, catch the original in all its indie glory. In the movie, a sexually free-spirited young woman (Tracy Camilla Johns) looking for Mr. Right Now dates three men at once, one of whom is played by Lee. “I am not a one man woman,” she tells the camera frankly. Shot in black and white and set to a jazzy score by Lee’s father, Bill, this debut feature is a scrappy precursor to Lee’s breakout film, “Do the Right Thing.”
—

Rooney Mara, left, and Cate Blanchett in "Carol."Wilson Webb/Weinstein Company
‘Carol’
Starts streaming: Sept. 20

In ’50s New York City, the waifish shopgirl Therese (Rooney Mara) falls for a glamorous customer, Carol (Cate Blanchett), and the two begin a passionate affair that soon threatens the outcome of Carol’s pending divorce and custody fight for her daughter. Directed by Todd Haynes, this movie is a heartbreaking period piece, but don’t let that scare you off. Blanchett is stunning as the title character, who shares longing, half-hidden glances with a meek but emotional Therese. The film’s detailed costume and production design layers old-school Hollywood glamour atop a repressive and wintry city.
—

Also of interest: “Dead Poets Society” (Sept. 1), “Gangs of New York” (Sept. 1), “Gone Baby Gone” (Sept. 1), the “Jaws” series (Sept. 1), “Mulan” (Sept. 1), “Pulp Fiction” (Sept. 1), “The Squid and the Whale” (Sept. 1) and “Pocahontas” (Sept. 14).
—

Netflix Originals

Marc Maron in “Marc Maron: Too Real.”Adam Bettcher/Netflix
‘Marc Maron: Too Real’
Starts streaming: Sept. 5

Fresh off his career-best performance as a pro-wrestling visionary in the Netflix dramedy series “GLOW,” the comedian and podcast star Marc Maron gets back to his standup roots, tackling his own experiences as someone who’s trying to stay politically and culturally engaged after turning 50.
—

The title character in “BoJack Horseman,” voiced by Will Arnett.Netflix
‘BoJack Horseman’ Season 4
Starts streaming: Sept. 8

TV’s best animated series about a chronically depressed equine celebrity returns as the title character reacts to the success of his recent showbiz comeback by hitting the road, on a search for deeper meaning. This latest set of “BoJack Horseman” episodes promises to be the most ambitious yet, extending the show’s commentary on fame and materialism beyond Hollywood to consider politics, family and the state of the American dream.
—

A scene from “First They Killed My Father.”Netflix
‘First They Killed My Father’
Starts streaming: Sept. 15

Angelina Jolie’s latest directorial effort adapts the memoir from Loung Ung, about enduring the atrocities of the Pol Pot regime as a child in 1970s Cambodia. The film has already garnered a lot of attention because of Jolie’s unusual approach to the project, working directly with the non-English-speaking children of survivors to recreate a harrowing story.
—

Director Yance Ford’s mother, Barbara Dunmore, circa 1958.Yanceville Films, via Netflix
‘Strong Island’
Starts streaming: Sept. 15

This powerhouse documentary from Yance Ford reflects on how the murder of his older brother exposed the fragility of his family’s black middle-class existence in the New York suburbs. Filled with poignant home-movie footage and probing interviews with friends and family, “Strong Island” confronts the constricting realities of racism in what is supposed to be the land of opportunity.
—

Jerry Seinfeld in “Jerry Before Seinfeld.”Netflix
‘Jerry Before Seinfeld’
Starts streaming: Sept. 19

Netflix landed a real coup for its roster of stand-up specials when the company signed a deal with the comedian Jerry Seinfeld. He’ll be taking advantage of the creative freedom to do something a little different, mixing stage performance and memoir in a set that looks back on his earliest days in the business and revisits some of his best jokes from the ’70s.
—

Carla Gugino in “Gerald’s Game.”Netflix
‘Gerald’s Game’
Starts streaming: Sept. 29

The excellent young horror filmmaker Mike Flanagan goes through one of the genre’s rites of passage, helming his first Stephen King adaptation. Carla Gugino stars as a woman who has a bad reaction to her husband’s kinky erotic overtures and soon finds herself fighting for her life in their remote cabin, plagued by bad memories and physical threats.
—

Robert Redford and Jane Fonda in “Our Souls at Night.”Kerry Brown/Netflix
‘Our Souls at Night’
Starts streaming: Sept. 29

Based on the posthumously published novel by Kent Haruf, this low-key romance stars Jane Fonda and Robert Redford as widowed small-town neighbors who strike up a friendship that quickly turns intimate. Director Ritesh Batra (best-known for the superb Indian drama “The Lunchbox”) has some fine imagery at his disposal, from rocky Colorado landscapes to the lived-in faces of two cinematic legends.
—

New to Amazon

From left, Te Aho Eketone-Whitu, James Rolleston and Taika Waititi in “Boy.”Paladin
‘Boy’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Before the actor, writer and director Taika Waititi got sucked into the Marvel universe (he is directing “Thor: Ragnarok,” scheduled for release this fall) he made clever, idiosyncratic movies set in his native New Zealand. In this 2010 movie, a boy (James Rolleston) rails against his absentee father (played by Waititi, who also wrote the script), who returns after a long hiatus and doesn’t know how to be a parent. Despite the ominous setup, the film manages to be very funny and heartwarming thanks to the boy’s playful point-of-view and Waititi’s charming performance.
—

Wiley Wiggins, left, and Patrick Riester, center, in “Computer Chess.”Kino Lorber
‘Computer Chess’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

This black-and-white comedy from Andrew Bujalski is a riff on Christopher Guest-style mockumentaries. In it, a group of computer nerds gathers at a weekend tournament to write a program to beat a human chess player in one of the first man vs. machine competitions. With a visual style that looks as if the movie were filmed on old VHS tapes, this subdued comedy about oddballs and obsessives is a sendup of the tech world in its infancy.
—

Patrick Swayze and Jennifer Grey in “Dirty Dancing.”Artisan Entertainment
‘Dirty Dancing’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

One of the quintessential ’80s movies is celebrating its 30th anniversary this year. During her family’s trip to a summer resort in the Catskill Mountains, Baby (an adorable Jennifer Grey) quickly becomes enamored with the hotel’s moody dance instructor, Johnny (a swoon-worthy Patrick Swayze), who reluctantly trains her to be his partner on the dance floor (and, well, possibly other places too). People have been trying to copy this movie’s memorable dance moves ever since. Nobody puts this movie in a corner.
—

Marlene Clark and Duane Jones in “Ganja and Hess.”Kino Lorber
‘Ganja and Hess’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Released during the blaxploitation craze of the 1970s, this sexy genre movie does more than shock and titillate. “Ganja and Hess” mixes race with faith in a story about two vampire lovers drawn together by a thirst for blood who must then decide if they want to remain murderous bloodsuckers or repent and destroy themselves. Spike Lee would later put his own spin on the story with “Da Sweet Blood of Jesus” in 2015.
—

Ryan Gosling in “Lars and the Real Girl.”MGM
‘Lars and the Real Girl’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

A baby-faced Ryan Gosling finally put his Mickey Mouse Club days behind him with this strange drama, playing a young man with debilitating shyness who finally brings home a girlfriend — a sex doll. On the advice of a psychiatrist, everyone in town goes along with it. Gosling’s performance avoids casting his character’s mental illness as something to be ridiculed, and the movie is better for it.
—

Tom Holland, center left, and Charlie Hunnam, center right, in “The Lost City of Z.”Aidan Monaghan/Amazon Studios — Bleecker Street
‘The Lost City of Z’
Starts streaming: Sept. 15

Adapted from the book by the New Yorker writer David Grann, “The Lost City of Z” follows the British officer Percy Fawcett (Charlie Hunnam) on several expeditions in the early 20th century, first to map uncharted terrain between Bolivia and Brazil and then to find evidence of a possible lost city. This Amazon Original movie from James Gray — which also stars the new Spider-Man, Tom Holland; Sienna Miller; and Robert Pattinson in an almost unrecognizable role — isn’t an “Indiana Jones” kind of action-adventure. Drama springs from the characters’ personal struggles, the harsh elements they face and their cultural clashes with indigenous tribes.
—

Also of interest: “The Black Stallion” (Sept. 1), “Carrie” (Sept. 1) and “An American Werewolf in London” (Sept. 15).
—

New to Hulu

Laurence Fishburne and Keke Palmer in “Akeelah and the Bee.”Saeed Adyani/Lionsgate Films
‘Akeelah and the Bee’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

This endearing underdog story arrives just in time for the start of the school year. Akeelah (Keke Palmer) is a young spelling savant whose potential is recognized and supported by one of my favorite movie teachers (Laurence Fishburne). But the real lesson of the movie — that great teachers are everywhere — comes from Akeelah’s mom, played by Angela Bassett. It’s cute, good to watch with your family, and it may give you warm and fuzzy feelings.
—

Wilson and Jatnna in “Mad Hot Ballroom.”Claudia Raschke-Robinson/Paramount Classics
‘Mad Hot Ballroom’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Another good movie to watch with the family: This adorable documentary observes several young and rowdy New York City students during their after school lessons in fox trot, tango, merengue and more. The teachers’ goal is to get their pupils to work together at an age when cooties are still a legitimate concern, but the kids’ eyes are on the top prize: an oversized trophy and bragging rights.
—

Mary Tyler Moore and Timothy Hutton in “Ordinary People.”Paramount Pictures
‘Ordinary People’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

Robert Redford’s directorial debut is a bleak drama about a wealthy but troubled family. Dad (Donald Sutherland) doesn’t know how to help his suicidal son (Timothy Hutton), who is still traumatized by the death of his brother. Mom (Mary Tyler Moore, in an impressive break from her comedic persona) is more concerned with hiding everyone’s emotions from the neighbors. Redford captured amazing performances from his actors, and the movie won four Oscars, including best picture and best director.
—

Lina Leandersson in “Let the Right One In.”Magnet Releasing
‘Let the Right One In’
Starts streaming: Sept. 27

In this bloody Swedish thriller, a young boy, the target of cruel bullying, befriends a mysterious young girl who turns out to be a vampire. Well, don’t let that get in the way of enjoying this strangely sweet friendship. This movie isn’t conventionally frightening, but it delivers an intense level of tension and brooding.
—

Also of interest: “A River Runs Through It” (Sept. 1), “Batman” (Sept. 1), “The Black Stallion” (Sept. 1), “Carrie” (Sept. 1), “Contact” (Sept. 1), “Hitch” (Sept. 1), “Invasion of the Body Snatchers” (Sept. 1), “Lars and the Real Girl” (Sept. 1), “RoboCop” (Sept. 1), “Secretary” (Sept. 1), “The Silence of the Lambs” (Sept. 1), “Freaky Friday” (Sept. 2), “The Emperor’s New Groove” (Sept. 5) and “An American Werewolf in London” (Sept. 15).

New to HBO

From left, Yasiin Bey (formerly Mos Def), Martin Freeman and Sam Rockwell in “A Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy.”Touchstone Pictures
‘The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy’
Starts Streaming: Sept. 1

Admittedly, it’s not as good as the classic book by Douglas Adams, but could any adaptation do this odd sci-fi story justice? After the Earth’s destruction, the planet’s sole human survivor (Martin Freeman) ends up hitchhiking across space with some strange beings, including a suicidal robot and a face-changing alien. The movie is a who’s who of British actors — including Stephen Fry, Bill Nighy, Warwick Davis and Alan Rickman. A pre-”New Girl” Zooey Deschanel adds some doe-eyed calm to the craziness.
—

A scene from “Tron.”Walt Disney Productions
‘Tron’
Starts streaming: Sept. 1

In this cutting edge 1982 movie, Jeff Bridges plays a computer programmer who, after trying to hack into the files of the rival who stole his ideas, is transported into a gladiatorial computer game with two friends. There they must outplay the Master Control Program (M.C.P. for short) in order to escape. If you’ve seen only the sleeker sequel (which has its moments), you must go back to the original for some early ’80s computer graphics goodness.
—

From left, Octavia Spencer, Taraji P. Henson and Janelle Monáe in “Hidden Figures.”Hopper Stone/20th Century Fox
‘Hidden Figures’
Starts streaming: Sept. 9

Well, not so hidden anymore. The once little-known story about three NASA mathematicians, each of them accomplished black women, is finally enjoying the spotlight. The women persevered in the face of discrimination, rising through the ranks of the space program in the 1960s, smashing records and helping send the first astronauts to the moon. Each lead actress — Taraji P. Henson, Octavia Spencer and Janelle Monáe — gives a nuanced yet passionate performance.
—

Emma Stone and Ryan Gosling in “La La Land.”Summit Entertainment
‘La La Land’
Starts streaming: Sept. 16

Earlier this year, this candy-coated romantic dramedy about a struggling jazz musician (Ryan Gosling) and an aspiring actress (Emma Stone) tied the record for most Oscar nominations. Referencing classic musicals from “An American in Paris” to “The Young Girls of Rochefort,” “La La Land” is a catchy, song-filled tribute to the City of Angels and Hollywood — even to its endless traffic jams. The movie makes fabulous use of its surroundings with scenes like a fantastical waltz through the Griffith Observatory and a stroll through a Hollywood back lot not unlike something out of “Singin’ in the Rain.”
—
