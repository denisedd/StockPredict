Still Processing: We Care for Ourselves and Others in Trump’s America

By Wesley Morris and Jenna Wortham

  • Aug. 24, 2017

The events of the last few weeks have shown us that we are fully living in Trump’s America, with a president who is slow to condemn the actions of white supremacists. The realization has been exhausting. This week, we take a step back to think about what the political moment is doing to our emotional and physical states. We discuss our routines of self-care — and investigate the origins of the phrase and why it feels overused and even a little cliché. We also talk to Matthew Steinfeld, an assistant professor of psychiatry at the Yale University School of Medicine about our ailing country and get a few ideas for how to remedy ourselves, and perhaps one another.

I mean, that’s — like who is going to sit there for that?

WM: How do you get a person who won’t listen to you to talk to you?

MS: So, with regard to Charlottesville, I would imagine that among the people present there holding torches, the people assembled have a range of different investments in whatever agenda they were putting forth, from wanting to cause actual harm to perhaps — maybe this is too charitable — but not wanting to cause physical harm to anybody, but wanting to be seen and heard. For people that are oozing with — I’m not sure what the right term, but — white fragility, a sense of historical amnesia, and a sense of righteous indignation mixed with presentism, my sense is that the opening kinds of conversations would be quite contentious, but what would be necessary is some frame to hold people in relation to one another so they can maintain dialogue over time. There are debates in the psychoanalytic community about whether to take white racists’ suffering seriously or not. How useful that is in terms of ameliorating these impasses.

WM: I think you would have to.

MS: I often think of the 50 or so workers at the Fukushima nuclear plant from Japan who volunteered to stay inside so the thing didn’t melt down and hurt more people as sort of what these sort of conversations might feel like to some people. Like, I’m going to hang in there with somebody so that I could potentially help somebody have a positive experience with somebody who looks like me. Everybody’s got families, and there’s always somebody in somebody’s family whose thinking could use a little updating. And —

WM: Amen. Ain’t it the truth.

MS: And so this is where each of us — you know, it’s possible to love somebody who has views that are abhorrent, you know. Maybe starting there.

JW: I mean, this was my mom’s strategy would, we went on TV to talk about Charlottesville. She told all my white relatives that voted for Trump to watch. She didn’t say what we were going to be talking about. She just texted them and said, tune in now. And I said, Mom, what are you doing? And she said, maybe it’s time they heard it from someone they love.

MS: That’s as good an answer as I’ve heard.

WM: How do you treat this toxicity, and how do you get the unexamined person to examine his or herself? Like how do you treat this?

MS: Right. Let me offer one broad association and then a couple ideas. You just really nicely articulated sort of one way to think about what therapy is, which is the study of how people maintain not knowing what they know to be the case. Like, if you put your glasses on your head and you forget they’re there, and you’re searching all around, it’s quite frustrating. But you put them there, so how do you not know that they’re up there? And so, all of us, when we go into therapy, do so because we can’t quite put our finger on something that we want to change. Because if we could, we would have done it. And so, this is one of the really useful values of thinking about things outside of awareness that contribute to repetitive cycles of human suffering. In the American context, the dominant nationalist American narrative is very invested in not knowing what the body politick knows. Which is to say, if you read a high school textbook, it would gloss over the slaughter of tens of millions of indigenous people and the seizure of land. It would gloss of the middle passage, and the slaughter, rape, and murder, forced slavery of millions and millions and millions of fellow human beings of African ancestry. The American narrative is that shining castle on a hill.

JW: The medicine is also the poison, right? Like if so much of the sickness is tied up in the way as Americans we’ve come to view the American narrative, and we can’t unlearn, or we’re trying to unlearn what that is — we’re literally doing that right now with this, this argument over what to do about Confederate statues, right. We can’t even agree that we should rethink the narrative. How do we even start to think about what it means to try to cure that?

WM: Right, because I think so much of what we’re talking about is the ways in which identity are bound up in totems of, of nationhood, but then by extension, also selfhood.

MS: So, the question is how do we facilitate movement? So, I have a few thoughts. One is self care. I’ll quote the law professor and trans activist Dean Spade here in saying that self care during this time, in this country, is a form of resistance. And increases the likelihood of an equitable distribution of life chances for lots of different kinds of bodies. Many of these kinds of bodies are being explicitly targeted. In order to sustain the long marathon in the pursuit of justice, self care is crucial.

JW: How should we be thinking about the larger medical infrastructure that doesn’t facilitate that? Like what are people not getting from their regular medical routines or lifestyles that does imply that we should be taking better care of ourselves, if that makes sense? Or sorry, why are we not conditioned or taught earlier on that taking care of ourselves should be essential?

MS: Yeah. I mean, I think many of us, many people are, and then we grow up and the tempo increases and the seductions of materialism are like, rampant. You look at Instagram, you would think that human beings have no sadness, anger, they don’t mourn. They don’t remember. Instagram is — which is such a potent and useful platform in many instance — sort of implies that a large portion of the palate of human emotions are not useful, and that if you have them, you’re not on target or something like that. And so, I think there are just endless instances of social messaging that pull us away from our wholeness. And that’s kind of what we’re trying to talk about here, which is like, how are we all able to stay in relationships with one another and be able to be whole? After self care, number two would be other care. So, to quote Nobel Peace Prize recipient Aung San Suu Kyi, when you’re feeling helpless, help someone. If we can invert the ways in which we feel marginalized and reach out to a neighbor, that is a meaningful and substantive gesture that in the aggregate have real weight. And I would say number three would be to seek out elders.

JW: This is what I do all the time!

WM: Very Jenna.

MS: The psychotherapeutic community is a wisdom community. I, with regularity, seek out people who know more than I do because I don’t know what to do. I think it’s really useful to interact with people that have a longer time horizon. People don’t have to reinvent the wheel. We also live in a culture that discards wisdom, in a sense. We don’t venerate elders as we might, and with wisdom — not even wisdom, but like facts under such assault, which is antithetical to the practice of academic rigor or science or just staying rooted in reality, I think elders can afford some nourishment, and care, and also hugs, if nothing else. Number four, make and consume art and music.

JW: It’s perfect.

MS: The arts are the pulse of the body politick as I see it. The extent to which you can get a read on how healthy it is, how generative it is able to be with what means and creativity it’s reflecting on its own mind in some sense. And...there needs to transcend, organize, and orient people to new realities. I was thinking about 10,000 people standing around is a mob. One person stands up with a guitar and it’s a concert. And then people are listening. And once you have people’s attention deplored in a direction, the question is, where do you want to go? When we’re talking about how do we sustain a relationship with one another and curate difficult encounters, the arts are an extremely potent form of doing this.

JW: I kind of feel like the eclipse was that a little bit, like a very universal piece of art. Like, we were saying earlier, it felt like a ceasefire. People were looking at the crescent shaped shadows and taking pictures of them with a colander. There was so much about that that felt like for the first time in a while, a kind of moment of something greater than ourselves and our weird insignificance in all of it. I don’t know. It was really beautiful to me.

WM: I agree with that.

JW: I was very moved by that yesterday.

MS: That sounds like Mother Nature’s ability to still get us to stop and pause and slow down and just pay attention, which is at its most fundamental, elemental level what we need in some sense, and is always the point of departure for good psychotherapy, which is like, how can we find a place to really been seen as we see each other and to notice what that feels like? Number five — might be expected — is therapy’s a good idea.

JW: Yes! I tell everyone who asks. I’m like, get thee to therapy.

WM: But everyone’s — go on, sorry. I just, I feel like the barriers to people going have everything to do with the way in which culture depicts therapy. It is depicted as a very bougie thing to do rather than going to the doctor and working on your head and your feelings. And yourself. Your full self.

MS: Yeah, I mean, we live in a culture with a lot of barriers to engaging in mental health care. Let’s call it what it is. Mental health care. A major one is stigma, and I’m a big advocate of just destigmatizing as a really normal — we brush our teeth twice a day, we all eat some food every day, and there’s as many kinds of therapists as there are people, and as many kinds of therapeutic relationships as there are relationships. And as everybody knows, the good ones are life changing, and the bad ones...better to keep it moving.

JW: It’s just like dating people. But I will say, I mean, this is part of one of the reasons why we wanted to have you on today, I think if you are someone who is moving through the world and trying to deal with racial trauma, and trying to deal with anxiety or fear over persecution because of your citizenship or lack thereof, or your gender orientation, or your gender identity or your sexual orientation, it’s like, are you going to be able to talk to someone who understands that or has context for that, or are you going to go to someone who...doesn’t know how to talk to you about what a microaggression is, or what it feels like if someone calls you the N word on the subway? I mean, I feel like that — I know in the course of my having a relationship with lots of different therapists, there are things I selectively tell them and don’t tell them because the fear of a therapist of all people saying, you know, not that they would say this, but a therapist not understanding what it feels like to worry about your safety because you’re black looms large. I mean, that’s a big barrier to entry to, is like, what if I go to this therapist, and they’re like, this is not a real concern?

MS: Everything you just said I think is really, really important. When I’m teaching my students at Yale, when I’m working at a clinic, when I’m thinking with friends and colleagues, or when friends and family, people I don’t know, reach out to me for a referral, one of the things I always tell them is to ask about these questions. Hopefully, a therapist will set what’s called a therapeutic frame — kind of like setting the framework in which they bring the social, economic, political into the conversation. Psychotherapy is at its root — and a lot has been written about this lately — a first amendment practice. It’s about free speech. When free speech is possible, it can be a democratizing activity, given other amendments like the 4th amendment, like, you feel safe. So, 1st amendment practices with 4th amendment protections. Anything that gets in the way of being able to lay claim to one’s head and heart and find language to speak to that is going to get in the way of treatment. And so hopefully, good therapists find a way of referencing any kind of difference in the room between them and their clients, and just bringing it up as a topic of conversation that, you know, I notice I’m taller than you or you know, I’m younger than you and you’re older than me. How does this feel? And most of the time, people are like, yeah, I’m fine with it. But you’ve marked the moment, and you have opened the door to say, if at some point you notice you’re having difficulty saying what you want to say, just know that it’s okay — including if it’s about me, my body, how I’m constituted, and the way it’s impacting you — and a crucial recognition is that regardless of positive intentions, all of the social, political, and economic dynamics outside of the room may recapitulate in the room. And then, the question is how do we stay in relationship with one another long enough to speak to the truth of that, to bear the feelings that come up and to try to lay claim to our wholeness in one another’s sight? And that is a model of what we need to do nationally. And that can be very painful sometimes. But hopefully, with a good guide, it feels safe enough to go there. And each of us, regardless of whether it feels that way to us in the moment, has an innate capacity to recognize what is more true than not, to be able to utilize reality. And I would suggest there’s a difference between productive discomfort and not productive discomfort. So, being able to have those conversations can really be growth promoting, but a therapist that misses the mark or says something insensitive or leaves a patient feeling unseen, it can really hurt for quite a long time. So, my recommendations to friends, family, whatever, when they’re working with somebody is to call it out. If you feel something, say something.

JW: If you feel something, say something. I’m going to write that down.

MS: And then see what the therapist does. Therapists usually only run into problems when they feel the need to be right, and when they can’t yield. When they can’t say, oh, yes, the way I look has hurt you. I’m so sorry. That was not my intention. How I’m embodied in this lifetime has made you feel unable to join here with me. I’m so sorry. Let’s try and think about how that happened and in what ways we were collaboratively maintaining some way of not knowing what we were feeling together. How were we doing that together? Does this have any relevance in other relationships in your life? And so on and so forth. I mean, there’s this caricature of you know, the run on therapy that goes forever and ever and ever, but that’s sort of what the American experiment is. Like, how do we have a never ending conversation with people with different commitments and values, and how can we forge something decent together that provides enough for all? We’ll see what happens. I mean, it’s going to depend on who lays claim to it more. And in this context, self care is crucial, because we need to be feeling okay in order to be able to not just react, but act intentionally.

JW: That’s perfect. Matt.

WM: Thanks for coming on.

MS: It is my pleasure.

WM: We’re back on my stoop.

JW: Dropping you off after work.

WM: We have talked about self care, we have talked about seeing a therapist and the importance of therapy, and now I just, I want to put something on my body. I want a sort of bodily tonic or something. So, what can you give me?

JW: Okay. So, here’s what I got at the store. And I’ll show it to you. I picked up two new essential oils. I like working with essential oils because they’re very accessible. Obviously we went to a specialty store, but you can buy them anywhere. They have them in any health food store. Most drug stores now carry various types of essential oils, and they’re very simple to work with. Today, I got ginger and I got black pepper.

WM: Oooh.

JW: Which are unusual, fragrance wise, right? But both work to warm up the body, which will be really nice as we move into fall to kind of keep you warm, keep you focused, keep you active. They both are antibacterial, so if you’re putting it on your body, it’s good. It can help you keep from getting sick.

WM: I’d like to smell these in their premixed modes.

JW: This is the ginger.

WM: Mmm.

JW: Spicy, right?

WM: It’s very spicy.

JW: This is the black pepper oil.

WM: It’s very intensely pepper-y, but not in a food way. It does not remind me of anything I put on my food. It’s like...

JW: It’s iconic.

WM: It’s a separate class of black pepper fragrance.

JW: So, basically, what we’re going to do is we’re just going to take a little bit of the ginger essential oil and a little bit of the black pepper oil, and we’re going to add it to some grapeseed oil and make a little body lotion for you that you can then rub all over yourself. And I like grapeseed oil, but you can use coconut oil, you could use sesame oil, you could use...you know, whatever oil that you happen to have lying around. I just like grapeseed oil because it’s very light and it doesn’t clog my pores. And I’ll give you a little extra on the side, because you can add essential oils to a tea candle, you can add it to a bath, to a diffuser, you can put a little bit in a bottled water and spray it around as a mister. I mean, there are lots of ways you can use essential oils in your lives to imbue your life with whatever properties they have naturally.

WM: How should I be feeling? How would you like to be feeling with these on your body?

JW: I mean, the basic thing is that we can’t really control what’s happening in the world, and we can’t really control how it makes us feel, but we can control how we care for ourselves. And so, for me, just taking a few moments every morning to just put on a little bit of a body oil that I made, maybe I’m listening to some jazz, maybe I’m having a coffee, it’s just that 30 seconds to a minute of just literal care taking makes all the difference for me in terms of how I move through the world every day.

WM: Thank you.

JW: You’re welcome.

Wesley Morris is a critic at large for The New York Times and a staff writer for The New York Times Magazine. Jenna Wortham is a staff writer for the magazine.
