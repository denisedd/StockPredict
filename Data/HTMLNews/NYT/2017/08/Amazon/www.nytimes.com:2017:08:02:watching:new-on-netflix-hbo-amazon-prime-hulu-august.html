The Best TV Shows and Movies New to Netflix, Amazon and More in August

by Monica Castillo

Aug. 2, 2017

Clockwise from lower left: “Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later,” “The Tick,” “The Matrix,” “Ali” and “The Addams Family.”Netflix; Amazon Studios; Warner Bros; Columbia Pictures; Orion

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation newsletter and website.

At the beginning of every month, subscription streaming services add a new batch of movies and TV shows to their libraries. Here are the titles we think are most interesting, broken down by service and release date. Streaming services occasionally change schedules without giving notice.

New to Netflix

Pam Grier in “Jackie Brown.”Darren Michaels/Miramax Films
‘Jackie Brown’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

This isn’t your typical Quentin Tarantino film. In some ways, it’s a better character study than many of his more blood-splattery staples. Based on a novel by Elmore Leonard, “Jackie Brown” stars Pam Grier as an airline stewardess who gets mixed up in a smuggling operation gone wrong and Robert Forster as a bondsman who could be falling in love with her. The film follows Grier’s titular character through a complex hustle to outwit both a criminal (Samuel L. Jackson) and his right-hand man (Robert De Niro) while trying to turn a nice profit. Naturally, it’s all set to an incredible soundtrack.
—

From left, Naomi Watts, Michael Pitt and Brady Corbet in “Funny Games.”Nicole Rivelli/Warner Independent Pictures
‘Funny Games’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

The Austrian director Michael Haneke remade his 1997 home invasion thriller “Funny Games” in 2007 for American audiences. Starring Naomi Watts, Tim Roth and Michael Pitt, the film follows a family on vacation whose trip is ruined by unexpected guests who torture their hosts for sport. The movie is intended to be a critique of movie violence, but not everyone walks away from “Funny Games” feeling that way.
—

Anjelica Huston and Raul Julia in “The Addams Family.”Orion Pictures
‘The Addams Family’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

In the first film based on the cartoon family, the Addams clan is desperate to reconnect with a long-lost Uncle Fester (Christopher Lloyd), who fell out long before with his brother, Gomez (Raúl Juliá). The lovable, death-obsessed household is brought to life on the big screen with a cast that includes a poised Anjelica Huston as the matriarch Morticia and a scowling young Christina Ricci as Wednesday.
—

Keanu Reeves in '‘The Matrix.’'Warner Bros.

The ‘Matrix’ Trilogy
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

Craving more Keanu Reeves after the “John Wick” movies? Try any of the films in the “Matrix” series, in which machines have learned to use humans as living batteries; resistance is led by people who have broken out of the machine-manufactured dream world (our world, of course); and everyone looks amazing in sleek, long coats. The first one is by far the best, but the second and third have their fair share of great action sequences.
—

Shia LaBeouf, left, and Khleo Thomas in “Holes.”Tony Barbera/Walt Disney Pictures
‘Holes’
Starts streaming: Aug. 5

Remember when Shia LaBeouf was known more for the Disney Channel’s “Even Stevens” than for the “Transformers” movies, strange performance art exhibitions or unfortunate public displays? If not, take a trip back with this adorable adaptation of a popular young adult book about an accused shoe thief (LaBeouf) and his long cursed family. It’s a strange and comic movie about prison camp for kids.
—

Netflix Original Shows

Marguerite Moreau and Paul Rudd in “Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later.”Saeed Adyani/Netflix
‘Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later’
Starts streaming: Aug. 4

After turning their raunchy ’80s teen comedy homage, “Wet Hot American Summer,” into an equally hilarious “prequel” series, the writer-director-producer team of Michael Showalter and David Wain are jumping ahead a decade, to 1991, in order to show what happened to the Camp Firewood crew after their surreal, sexy adventures in the woods. Pitched as a parody of “class reunion” movies like “The Big Chill” and ’90s Gen-X “hangout” sitcoms like “Friends” — with a cast that includes the returning comedians Michael Ian Black, Janeane Garofalo, Paul Rudd and Amy Poehler — “Wet Hot American Summer: Ten Years Later” is likely to be just as strange and silly as every other entry in this most unlikely franchise.
—

Keir Gilchrist and Jenna Boyd in “Atypical.”Greg Gayne/Netflix
‘Atypical’
Starts streaming: Aug. 11

As more and more American families struggle with raising children on the autistic spectrum, movie and TV producers have looked for ways to make their stories both moving and amusing. This recent effort comes from Robia Rashid, a writer for “How I Met Your Mother” and “The Goldbergs,” who has Jennifer Jason Leigh and Michael Rapaport playing the parents of Sam (Keir Gilchrist), an 18-year-old making strides to live independently, even as he wrestles with his autism. It’s a situation that all too many moms and dads will recognize.
—

From left, Mike Colter, Krysten Ritter and Charlie Cox in “Marvel’s The Defenders.”Jessica Miglio/Netflix
‘Marvel’s The Defenders’
Starts streaming: Aug. 18

From the moment Netflix announced a partnership with Marvel Television for a set of down-to-Earth superhero shows set in New York City, comics fans have looked forward to what the project has been building toward all along: a small-screen version of the offbeat team the Defenders, who since the ’70s have been the Marvel Universe’s freakier counterpart to the Avengers. This mini-series unites Daredevil, Luke Cage, Jessica Jones and Iron Fist in a battle royale against the mystically superpowered ninjas known as the Hand.
—

Netflix Original Movies

Bryan Fogel in “Icarus.”Netflix
‘Icarus’
Starts streaming: Aug. 4

In this probing documentary, the filmmaker and amateur cyclist Bryan Fogel sets out to experiment with performance enhancing drugs and then stumbles deep into the world of Russian doping. A look at both widespread corruption in sports and the far-reaching tentacles of international criminal syndicates (some of which are covertly state-sponsored), “Icarus” exposes the darker realities of the modern world.
—

Lakeith Stanfield in “Death Note.”James Dittiger/Netflix
‘Death Note’
Starts streaming: Aug. 25

This adaptation of the violent, supernaturally tinged Japanese comic book series “Death Note” looks to be one of Netflix’s most ambitious original films yet. The popular young horror director Adam Wingard (best-known for “You’re Next,” “The Guest,” and the recent “Blair Witch” revamp) steers a cast that includes the former Nickelodeon phenom Nat Wolff as a teenage vigilante, Margaret Qualley (“The Leftovers”) as the girl he likes and the “Atlanta” oddball Lakeith Stanfield as a dogged detective on the antihero’s trail.
—

New to Hulu

Will Smith as Muhammad Ali in “Ali.”Frank Connor/Columbia Pictures
‘Ali’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

It’s been a minute and change since we last saw Will Smith really get into character. Which is a shame, because he is capable of delivering a fiery performance when he does, as he did here in this 2001 biopic of the boxing legend Muhammad Ali. Smith fully invests in the physicality of the role and the emotional turmoil that results from his failed marriages, the assassination of his friend Malcolm X and the loss of his boxing license after he protested the Vietnam War.
—

From left, William Hurt, Jeff Goldblum, JoBeth Williams, Tom Berenger, Meg Tilly and Kevin Kline in “The Big Chill.”Columbia Pictures
‘The Big Chill’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

After breaking through with his screenplay for “The Empire Strikes Back,” Lawrence Kasdan began to direct, making the steamy film noir “Body Heat” and the “The Big Chill,” a nostalgia-filled hangout movie that follows a group of friends that reunites after the death of one of its own. With a cast that includes Glenn Close, Jeff Goldblum, Kevin Kline and William Hurt, the reunion is bittersweet, as each shares regrets about the loss of their youthful idealism. But it’s not too mushy, being full of feel-good Motown and rock standards.
—

Eddie Murphy, left, and Arsenio Hall in “Coming to America.”Paramount Pictures
‘Coming to America’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

An African prince (Eddie Murphy) leaves home and an arranged marriage in order to seek true love in the only place he reasons he can find a royal bride: Queens. This was the first movie to feature Murphy in multiple roles under heavy makeup, a trick he would repeat many times in movies like “The Nutty Professor.” Joined by a cast that includes James Earl Jones, Arsenio Hall and Shari Headley, “Coming to America” stars Murphy at the top of his comedic prowess.
—

Anne Hathaway, left, and Rosemarie DeWitt in “Rachel Getting Married.”Bob Vergara/Sony Pictures Classics
‘Rachel Getting Married’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

Anne Hathaway haters need not apply. Jonathan Demme gave her one of the best roles of her career thus far as a recovering addict disrupting her sister’s wedding. Although this 2008 film is at times dark, Demme’s free-flowing direction and wonderful supporting cast — including Anna Deavere Smith, Rosemarie DeWitt, Bill Irwin and Debra Winger — ensure that the moments you will remember most are the emotional ones.
—

Diane Keaton and Warren Beatty in “Reds.”Paramount Pictures
‘Reds’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

In the midst of the Cold War and the start of the Reagan presidency, Warren Beatty wrote, directed and starred in a grand epic about the American socialist activist and journalist John Reed. Rather than make a dry biopic about the life of an American author who wrote extensively about the Russian Revolution, Beatty instead focuses on Reed’s personal life, including his troubled courtship and marriage to Louise Bryant (Diane Keaton) and the romantic competition between him and playwright Eugene O’Neill (Jack Nicholson) for her affection. As a wide-eyed Bryant is eagerly swept off her feet by Reed’s infectious enthusiasm and passion, so are we taken through this tumultuous era with left-wing intellectuals and dreamers.
—

Mel Gibson in “Hamlet.”Warner Bros.
‘Hamlet’
Starts streaming: Aug. 15

In this 1990 adaptation of the play by William Shakespeare, Mel Gibson plays the erratic Danish prince with a performance that was at odds with Gibson’s cool movie star persona of the ’80s. It’s a good thing his performance is so aggressive since he shares the screen with the likes of Glenn Close, Helena Bonham Carter, Ian Holm and Alan Bates.
—

Also of interest: “As Good as It Gets,” “Eve’s Bayou,” “Ghost,” “High Noon,” “Saving Private Ryan” and “Spider-Man.”

New to Amazon Prime

Christopher Walken in “The Dead Zone.”Paramount Pictures
‘The Dead Zone’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

A man (a reserved Christopher Walken) wakes from a coma with newly discovered psychic abilities in David Cronenberg’s adaptation of the novel by Stephen King. Of course, those psychic abilities are both a blessing and a curse, and the movie takes a political turn when the man’s abilities reveal that a presidential candidate (Martin Sheen) might be worse than your average politician. This is one of the more muted Stephen King movie adaptations and one of Walken’s most somber performances.
—

From left: Lynn Whitfield and Jurnee Smollett, now known as Jurnee Smollett-Bell, in “Eve’s Bayou.”Chris Helcermanas-Benge/Trimark Pictures
‘Eve’s Bayou’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

A young Jurnee Smollett (“Underground”; known today as Jurnee Smollett-Bell) stars in director Kasi Lemmons’s Southern Gothic story about a 10-year-old girl in a prominent black family in Louisiana and the day she killed her father (Samuel L. Jackson). Told in the past tense, the movie uses memories and premonitions to foreshadow the dark twists to come. Between the creative storytelling, the haunting setting and Smollett’s complex performance, “Eve’s Bayou” is an astonishing thriller from a first-time filmmaker.
—

Gary Cooper in “High Noon.”United Artists
‘High Noon’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

Gary Cooper often played the everyman’s champion in movies like “Meet John Doe,” “Sergeant York” and “The Pride of the Yankees.” But his character in this Western faces a different kind of challenge. In it, Cooper plays Will Kane, a local sheriff who plans to leave law enforcement behind after marrying a pacifist Quaker (Grace Kelly). But on the day of their wedding, an outlaw and his gang arrives on the noon train, seeking revenge. Kane asks the townspeople for help, but they turn their backs, leaving him to face the gunfire by himself. The film’s screenwriter, Carl Foreman, was himself blacklisted and forced to flee the United States shortly after the film’s debut — it was almost as if he’d known what was coming. Cooper won an Oscar for his performance.
—
Also of interest: “All Dogs Go to Heaven,” “Ghost,” “Save the Last Dance” and “Superbad.”

Amazon Prime Original Shows

From left, Florin Piersic Jr. and Corneliu Ulici in “Comrade Detective.”Alexandru Ionita/Amazon Prime Video
‘Comrade Detective’
Starts streaming: Aug. 4

With one of the weirder premises to hit the small screen in a while, this Amazon comedy presents a fake ’80s Romanian cop show — in which shaggy communists fight against capitalist crooks — with Channing Tatum, Joseph Gordon-Levitt, Nick Offerman, Jenny Slate and other well-known American actors and comedians dubbing in the English-language dialogue. So meta that it’s hard to tell what it’s actually parodying, “Comrade Detective” is first and foremost endearingly goofy. Any deeper sociopolitical messages are mostly incidental.
—

Peter Serafinowicz in “The Tick.”Amazon Studios
‘The Tick’
Starts streaming: Aug. 25

The Tick, the beefy, blue-clad superhero from cartoonist Ben Edlund, has been spoofing comic book clichés since the ’80s and has already anchored two cult TV series on Fox: a Saturday morning cartoon that ran for three seasons in the mid-90s and a short-lived but fiercely beloved live-action sitcom. In Amazon’s new version, Peter Serafinowicz plays the Tick, a childlike super-dope, who patrols the streets of “the City” and encounters some of the most ridiculously named villains and fellow heroes in the history of the genre.
—

New to HBO

Nicolas Cage as Donald Kaufman (left) and Charlie Kaufman in "Adaptation."Columbia Pictures
‘Adaptation’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

This clever movie by screenwriter Charlie Kaufman (“Being John Malkovich,” “Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind”) follows a successful but beleaguered screenwriter (a mopey Nicolas Cage), his clueless twin brother (a manic Nicolas Cage) a wild ensemble of Hollywood execs, a journalist (Meryl Streep) and a Floridian misfit (Chris Cooper) in this complicated effort to adapt a popular novel to the big screen. How they all fit into this inventive drama from Spike Jonze is fascinating to watch, and the performances and the script both hold many surprises for first-time and returning viewers.
—

Frank Langella as Richard M. Nixon, with Michael Sheen as David Frost at the right, in “Frost/Nixon.”Ralph Nelson/Universal Pictures
‘Frost/Nixon’
Starts streaming: Aug. 1

Ron Howard’s adaptation of the titular play about a set of interviews between the showy British TV host David Frost (Michael Sheen) and a post-resignation President Richard Nixon (a fantastically gruff Frank Langella), is a tense and wordy face-off. The main attraction does not disappoint as Sheen and Langella recreate the showdown between two great egos desperate to salvage their careers.
—

From left, Warren Beatty and Steve Coogan in “Rules Don’t Apply.”François Duhamel/20th Century Fox
‘Rules Don’t Apply’
Starts streaming: Aug. 14

Warren Beatty’s recent film “Rules Don’t Apply” is an unusual mix of Hollywood, religion and screwball comedy. It’s a film in which an ambitious chauffeur (Alden Ehrenreich, who is set to star as a young Han Solo in a coming “Star Wars” movie), working for one of the richest men in 1950s Hollywood, falls for an ambitious young actress (Lily Collins) who is deeply religious. Their boss is no ordinary executive: He’s Howard Hughes (Beatty), who forces them to hide their feelings because he doesn’t approve of his actresses dating. Beatty, who also wrote and directed the movie, plays Hughes charmingly, rather than disturbingly eccentric. Annette Bening, Matthew Broderick, Martin Sheen and Alec Baldwin round out the cast.
—

Natalie Portman as Jacqueline Kennedy in “Jackie.”Fox Searchlight Pictures
‘Jackie’
Starts streaming: Aug. 26

The Chilean director Pablo Larraín made his English-language film debut with a bold take on the former first lady Jackie Kennedy during the days after her husband’s assassination. With Mica Levi’s haunting score and Natalie Portman’s performance as Mrs. Kennedy, Larraín’s unorthodox biopic offers more than a historical play-by-play, becoming surprisingly affecting and raw as it proceeds.
—
