A Guide to the Different Scotlands You’ll Find on Film

by Judy Berman

Oct. 19, 2017UPDATED Oct. 20, 2017

Clockwise from top left: “Breaking the Waves,” “Braveheart” and “Trainspotting.”October Films; Paramount Pictures; Miramax Films

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation newsletter and website.

The steamy Starz drama “Outlander” offers ample fuel for romantic fantasies, but what if you’re more smitten with its Scottish Highland setting than with its leads? Thankfully, those dramatic vistas have made plenty of memorable appearances on film. But not all big-screen depictions of Scotland have focused on that rugged countryside. Through love and war, across its countryside and in its cities, the many Scotlands depicted throughout cinema history are as rich as they are varied. Here’s an introductory guide:

Romantic Scotland

A scene from “I Know Where I’m Going!”The Archers

If Claire and Jamie have you craving more kilts, ruins and star-crossed love affairs between headstrong Englishwomen and rugged Scots, then “I Know Where I’m Going!” is sure to satisfy. Directed by Michael Powell and Emeric Pressburger, the team behind classics like “The Red Shoes” and “Black Narcissus,” this drama from 1945 follows a young social climber (Wendy Hiller) who’s traveling to an island in the Hebrides to marry a rich businessman. Waylaid by a storm, she meets a naval officer (Roger Livesey) and gets a taste of true love. As in “Outlander,” rural Scotland’s wild beauty only intensifies the film’s frisson of forbidden romance.

‘I Know Where I’m Going!’
Where to watch: Amazon

For a lighter love story, check out “Gregory’s Girl,” a quirky 1980s rom-com about teens in the Glasgow suburbs. In this film, the awkward Gregory (John Gordon Sinclair) loses his position on the high-school soccer team to a girl (Dee Hepburn) — but instead of resenting his new teammate, he falls for her. Bill Forsyth, who wrote and directed the movie, is one of Scotland’s best-known filmmakers, and his other films are worth seeking out, too. Two of his other offbeat Glaswegian comedies, “That Sinking Feeling” and “Comfort and Joy,” are harder to find online but just as enjoyable.

‘Gregory’s Girl’
Where to watch: Amazon

Valiant, Historical Scotland

Mel Gibson in “Braveheart.”Andrew Cooper/Paramount Pictures

Perhaps your ideal Scottish hero is more fighter than lover. In that case, consider starting with “Braveheart.” Mel Gibson directs and stars in that big-budget biopic of the medieval warrior William Wallace, a leader and martyr of the First Scottish War of Independence. Elaborate battle scenes, leather armor and rousing speeches on the sanctity of freedom abound. Squeamish viewers, be warned: Gibson also throws in a pretty excruciating torture sequence.

‘Braveheart’
Where to watch: Netflix, Hulu, Amazon, iTunes

Fast-forward four centuries for “Rob Roy,” the 1995 film adaptation of Sir Walter Scott’s classic novel about an 18th-century folk hero who is essentially a Scottish Robin Hood. This adventure story is perfectly cast, starring Liam Neeson as the titular swashbuckler, Jessica Lange as his loyal wife and Tim Roth as the villainous aristocrat who steals their money. Neeson and Lange have wonderful chemistry, but the real draw is the movie’s elegantly choreographed sword fighting.

‘Rob Roy’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

For a historical epic that’s suitable for the whole family — or for a medieval Scottish warrior who’s also a teenage girl — try the animated film “Brave.” Kelly Macdonald gives voice to Pixar’s first female protagonist, a spunky, flame-haired highlands princess who uses her archery skills in an attempt to escape an arranged marriage. Beneath all the girl-power affirmations, there’s a heartwarming message about the relationships between mothers and daughters.

‘Brave’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Weird Scotland

Emily Watson in “Breaking the Waves.”October Films

Scotland’s rebellious spirit, colorful folklore and ample wilderness have inspired more than their share of eccentric tales. In fact, the word “weird” makes an early appearance in William Shakespeare’s Scottish play “Macbeth.” Although its meaning at the time had more to do with fate than with strangeness, it’s a fitting description of a tragedy that hinges on the prophecy of three witchy sisters who mix up a brew of newt eyes and frog toes. Of the countless big-screen adaptations, Orson Welles’s expressionistic “Macbeth” from 1948 and Roman Polanski’s chilling interpretation from 1971 are among the best.

Set amid an isolated, fervently religious community in the 1970s, “Breaking the Waves,” by the Danish filmmaker Lars von Trier, conjures a very different kind of Weird Scotland. The film’s heroine, Bess (Emily Watson), is an innocent oddball who believes she speaks directly with God. When her outsider husband (Stellan Skarsgard) is paralyzed in an oil rig accident, her compliance with his lurid requests raise the question of whether she’s a fool, a saint or both. As is generally the case with von Trier’s films, there’s a lot of suffering onscreen, but also a measure of transcendence.

‘Breaking the Waves’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

The Scottish auteur Lynne Ramsay is best known stateside for her 2011 thriller, “We Need to Talk About Kevin,” set in the United States. But her early film “Morvern Callar” is set in her home country, and its premise is bizarre. A woman (Samantha Morton) wakes up on Christmas, finds that her boyfriend has killed himself, signs her name to the completed manuscript of his first novel and then uses the funeral money he left to go on vacation with her best friend (Kathleen McDermott). That the character is still lovable is a testament to Morton’s charisma.

‘Morvern Callar’
Where to watch: Amazon

Gritty Scotland

A scene from “Trainspotting.”Liam Longman/Miramax Films

Scotland’s filmography offers plenty of urban grit along with all the rural eccentricity. Danny Boyle’s breakthrough feature, the dark comedy “Trainspotting,” follows a 20-something heroin addict (Ewan McGregor) and his motley troupe of friends as they hustle to avoid straight jobs and maintain their hedonistic lifestyles in Edinburgh. It’s a coming-of-age story of the “better late than never” variety, with a soundtrack that helped make it a quintessential British Gen-X film.

‘Trainspotting’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Peter Mullan, the Scottish actor and director who played a drug dealer in “Trainspotting,” went on to make “Neds,” his own film about delinquent youth. Set in 1970s Glasgow, that film traces a working-class kid’s devolution from star student to gangbanger. One critic described it as “a Scottish ‘400 Blows’ plus a few razor slashings and a head butt or two.”

Terrifying Scotland

Robert Donat and Madeleine Carroll in “The 39 Steps.”Gaumont British

Of course, the land of the Loch Ness Monster also has a scary side. Alfred Hitchcock’s “The 39 Steps” centers upon a regular guy (Robert Donat) who stumbles into high-stakes espionage. After sheltering a female spy who’s just killed a foreign agent at a London music hall, he learns that his own life is in danger and catches a train to the Scottish Highlands. The stone bridges and rolling hills make the perfect backdrop for a thriller that mixes nail-biting suspense with swooning romance.

‘The 39 Steps’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Scotland’s pagan past plays a crucial role in “The Wicker Man,” first released in 1973. (Unless you’re a connoisseur of unhinged Nicolas Cage performances, you’ll probably want to skip the ridiculous 2006 remake.) That classic midnight movie follows a committed Christian cop (Edward Woodward) whose search for a missing girl takes him to the remote, fictional island of Summerisle, where a cult inspired by the ancient Celts runs wild. His investigation grows creepier by the minute, culminating in one of the craziest set pieces in the history of cinema.

‘The Wicker Man’
Where to watch: iTunes

You can find a more contemporary Scary Scotland in “Under the Skin,” director Jonathan Glazer’s visually stunning sci-fi thriller about an alien seductress (Scarlett Johansson) who preys on lonely guys. Shot in Glasgow, with a breathtaking climactic sequence that takes place in the highlands, the film includes footage of real, unscripted encounters between Johansson’s nameless protagonist and local men who don’t know that cameras are rolling.

‘Under the Skin’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes
