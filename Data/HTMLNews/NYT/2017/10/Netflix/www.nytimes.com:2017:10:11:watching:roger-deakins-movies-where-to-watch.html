Stream These Beautiful Movies From Cinematographer Roger Deakins

by Charles Bramesco

Oct. 11, 2017

A scene from “The Shawshank Redemption.”Castle Rock Entertainment

Watching is The New York Times’s TV and film recommendation newsletter and website.

Although “Blade Runner 2049” underperformed at the box office last weekend, it’s hard to deny the virtuosic work done by its cinematographer, Roger Deakins. For more than 40 years, he has proved himself one of the foremost talents in his field, but has yet to win an Oscar despite 13 nominations. Below is a sampling of his most distinctive achievements, from films about America’s bloody past to dark projections of its future:

John Hurt, foreground, and Bob Flag in “1984.”20th Century Fox
‘1984’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Deakins did right by one of the great novels of the 20th century in his second feature as a cinematographer, bringing George Orwell’s chilling totalitarian nightmare to life in a dank, dystopian London. Deakins imagines a world almost completely devoid of hope, where a heavy, dictatorial fist pounds every last shred of individuality to dust. Deakins silently communicates the oppression of totalitarianism by emphasizing brutalist straight lines and symmetry, as if even the world itself has submitted to conformity.
—

John Turturro in “Barton Fink.”20th Century Fox
‘Barton Fink’
Where to watch: Showtime, Starz, Amazon, iTunes

The first of Deakins’s 12 collaborations with Joel and Ethan Coen was an odd, allusive period piece about a well-intentioned but pretentious writer (John Turturro) making the transition from Broadway to Hollywood. Deakins realized the 1940s setting with worn-looking photography while putting Barton’s gradual descent into madness in stunning visual terms. Perhaps the most cerebral of the Coens’ films, this enigma continues to beguile viewers with ambiguous symbols and allegorical teases. Deakins sets up shots of a weeping, infected ear and sticky adhesive on peeling wallpaper as parallel images — he’s the one cluing the audience in on this film’s many secrets.
—

Morgan Freeman in “The Shawshank Redemption.”Castle Rock Entertainment
‘The Shawshank Redemption’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Deakins is responsible for so many of the indelible images that stick in the brain between late-night cable rewatches: a baffled warden peering through a mini-tunnel, an idyllic siesta on a newly-laid roof, a freed prisoner (Tim Robbins) casting his arms to heaven in a cleansing rain. So much of this adaptation of Stephen King’s novella about an extraordinary jailbird has been absorbed into the popular canon, from the eternal wisdom of the inmate Red (Morgan Freeman) to the final shot on the shores of paradise. Comfort-food cinema at its most comforting, it’s a colorful tale bolstered by Deakins’s lively lens work.
—

Steve Buscemi in “Fargo.”MGM
‘Fargo’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime, Epix, iTunes

Deakins’s work usually has a painterly quality to it, but the homey Midwestern tableaux in this criminal tragicomedy more closely resemble the needlepoints that the promotional poster stylized literally. In “Fargo,” the typically Coen-esque schemer Jerry Lundegaard (William H. Macy) hatches a flimsy plot to extort a little dough from some well-off relatives, the first in a series of bad decisions that scatter corpses across the barren snowy plains of North Dakota, alerting the razor-sharp and very pregnant policewoman Marge Gunderson (Frances McDormand). Through Deakins’ chilly camera, those endless, numbing expanses of white transform into a personal hell for Jerry as he gets in deeper and deeper over his head.
—

A scene from the Coen Brothers film “The Man Who Wasn’t There.”USA Films
‘The Man Who Wasn’t There’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

Widely considered a minor work for the Coens, this noir contains some of Deakins’s most moody and evocative work with them. As the foolhardy but stoic barber Ed (Billy Bob Thornton) lies, embezzles and murders his way into a moral hole six feet deep, Deakins captures it all in gorgeously shaded black-and-white photography, harkening to the chiaroscuro classics that influenced this reverent homage. This film plays it a bit closer to the vest than most Coen output does, ratcheting up the inscrutability and going light on the dry comedy. But Deakins’s stills could be isolated and hung in museums.
—

Brad Pitt in “The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford.”Warner Bros.
‘The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

No film genre invites passages of natural splendor quite like the Western, and Deakins’s foray into the tradition of American mythmaking stands comparison to the best. For this story of the wannabe bandit Bob Ford (Casey Affleck) and his evolving relationship with the larger-than-life gunslinger Jesse James (Brad Pitt), director Andrew Dominik spins a yarn of pastoral brotherhood and betrayal. Deakins broke ground in his field as well, pioneering a new technique of layering lenses over one another to imitate the look of an old photograph. The result of his innovation and Dominik’s unconventional approach to biography is one of the most widely respected Westerns of the modern era.
—

Joaquin Phoenix in “The Village.”Touchstone Pictures
‘The Village’
Where to watch: Netflix, Amazon, iTunes

This film from M. Night Shyamalan still draws a polarized response for its twist ending, but even the film’s detractors agree that the cinematographer handled the unique setting — a Pennsylvania hamlet living in total isolation in 1897 — with an old pro’s aplomb. Deakins summoned an appropriate sense of ambient terror, solidifying the premise by communicating just how frightening the unknown beyond their boundaries could be. He carefully withholds visual information about the mysterious monsters, showing them in fleeting glimpses that preserve the enigma while suggesting their scariness.
—

Daniel Craig in “Skyfall.”Columbia Pictures — MGM
‘Skyfall’
Where to watch: Amazon, iTunes

With this 23rd James Bond film, Deakins; its director, Sam Mendes; and its star, Daniel Craig, ushered in a new era for the British spy — a darker and edgier phase for a tougher, more brooding 007. Widely regarded as the high point of the Craig years, this installment of the long-running espionage franchise has Bond faking his own death and getting back to his roots with a visit to his family estate. Although it’s not without the series’ signature action sequences, “Skyfall” is a more expressive and downbeat take on Bond than usual, fraught with delicate beauty uncommon in the action movie canon. A neon-laced interlude in Shanghai allows Deakins some of his wildest experiments with color.
—

A scene from “Sicario.”Lionsgate
‘Sicario’
Where to watch: Amazon Prime, Epix, Hulu, iTunes

Teaming with the director of “Blade Runner 2049,” Denis Villeneuve, for this drug-war saga brought Deakins fascinating new challenges, which he surmounts with ingenuity and technical sophistication. Villeneuve and Deakins drop principled the F.B.I. agent Kate Macer (Emily Blunt) into carefully constructed high-pressure situations — a night-vision raid, a tense showdown on a rush-hour freeway — and defy her to escape with her life. But her greatest conflicts are moral, as she slowly comes to realize that her latest mission may be more than she signed up for. Director, cinematographer and star all work in perfect synthesis for this unbearably tense thriller, which asks all manner of troubling questions about the ethics of America’s anti-narcotics strategy south of the border.
