Love Scary Movies? Shudder Lets You Stream Them Year-Round

by Monica Castillo

Oct. 18, 2017UPDATED Oct. 22, 2017

Clockwise: “The Tingler,” “Prevenge, ” “Nosferatu” and “Ms. 45.”Columbia Pictures; Shudder Films; Prana Film; Drafthouse Films

As the old saying goes, ’tis the season for screamin’. But what if you like your scares year-round? Lucky for you, we’re in an age of niche streaming services like Shudder, which specializes in suspense, thrillers and horror movies and TV shows.

Shudder, from AMC Networks, provides movies that range from gruesome to campy, from silent to chain-saw loud, from low budget to no budget. There’s also a healthy collection of foreign horror films and TV, like the cruel and torturous “Audition” from Japan, the terrifying Spanish zombie movie “[Rec],” the Japanese series “Penance” and the Swedish series “Jordskott.” If you’re daunted by the sheer number of titles, then look into Shudder's easy-to-browse collections, like their films about the apocalypse or women-led horror movies. That should also help you weed out some of the more lurid and trashy movies on Shudder, which may not be of interest.

Shudder subscriptions are available in the United States for $4.99 a month or $47.88 a year, and in Canada, Britain and Ireland at locally adjusted prices. A Shudder app is available on all major streaming devices. And as with HBO, Showtime and Starz, subscribers to Amazon Prime can add Shudder through Amazon Channels, which allows you to pay for multiple streaming services through Amazon, rather than have multiple separate bills.

Not sure where to start? Here are a few suggestions:

I’m Interested in Classic Creepers

Claude Rains and Gloria Stuart in “The Invisible Man.”Universal Pictures
‘The Invisible Man’

Shudder treated its subscribers to a collection of classic Universal Monsters movies: “Dracula” (1931), “Frankenstein” (1931), “Bride of Frankenstein” (1935), “The Mummy” (1932), “The Wolf Man” (1941) and my personal favorite, “The Invisible Man” (1933). You don’t even see the Invisible Man (Claude Rains) for most of the movie. Rains’ smooth voice is your only guide through the tragic downfall of an overambitious scientist and his insistence on injecting himself with a serum that makes him both invisible and unstable.
—

Vincent Price in “The Tingler.”Columbia Pictures
‘The Tingler’

In the ’50s and ’60s, the director, producer and all-around showman William Castle frequently packaged his horror movies with some kind of a theatrical gimmick to help sell tickets. For his 1959 monster movie, “The Tingler,” he added devices that would shake theater seats in an attempt to scare audience members into believing the monster was loose. Although that experience can’t be replicated at home, you can still get a sense of Castle’s prankish B-movie spirit. The movie’s titular Tingler binds itself to the spine of its victims, who can only get rid of it if they scream. So when star Vincent Price urges you to scream, you’d better do what he says.
—

The shadow of Max Schreck in “Nosferatu.”Prana Film
‘Nosferatu’

This early adaptation of Bram Stoker’s “Dracula” changed the names of the book’s main characters and features a vampire who looks closer to a bat than a charming count. But this German silent film, first released abroad in 1922, is one of cinema’s most beautiful horror movies. The film’s director, F.W. Murnau, uses the exaggerated shadows of German Expressionism and creative camera techniques like double exposure to compose an elegant and frightening story. If you’re not tired of vampires yet, Werner Herzog’s 1979 remake, “Nosferatu, the Vampyre,” is also available on Shudder.
—

I’m Interested in International Scares

Lina Leandersson in “Let the Right One In.”Magnet Releasing
‘Let the Right One in’

In this 2008 Swedish horror movie, a bullied adolescent befriends a bloodthirsty, youthful-looking vampire, who in turn defends him against his tormentors. This film focuses less on the monster’s feeding habits than on the friendship between two social outcasts. “Let the Right One in” has its gory moments, yet it manages to retain some brief moments of childlike sweetness.
—

Junio Valverde in “The Devil’s Backbone.”Miguel Bracho/Sony Pictures Classics
‘The Devil’s Backbone’

Guillermo del Toro seems to have affection for misunderstood monsters — from his gothic ghost romance “Crimson Peak” to his coming film “The Shape of Water,” a tender riff on “The Creature from the Black Lagoon.” That sense of empathy is also present in his earlier movies, like “The Devil’s Backbone.” In that film, a boy is sent to an oppressive Catholic orphanage during the Spanish Civil War, where he discovers the supernatural presence of a lost soul. This lonely story is not as scary as it is sad.
—

A scene from “Battle Royale.”Anchor Bay
‘Battle Royale’

Years before “The Hunger Games,” there was this berserk Japanese movie, first released overseas in 2000. To punish society’s ever unruly adolescents, the government sends an armed group of students into an arena to kill one another until only one child remains A dark sense of humor drips from “Battle Royale,” especially during the preposterously violent death scenes.
—

I’m Interested in Lesser-Known Horror Movies

Alice Lowe in “Prevenge.”Shudder Films
‘Prevenge’

It’s a difficult pregnancy in a rather gruesome sense: A mother-to-be waits for her baby’s instructions on whom to kill next. This is no “Rosemary’s Baby”; it’s a horror comedy. The expecting mother is a reluctant and sometimes clumsy serial killer, exacting revenge on the people who mistreat her.
—

Elissa Dowling in “We Are Still Here.”Snowfort Pictures
‘We Are Still Here’

This 2015 film injects a few surprises into the overly familiar haunted-house genre. In it, a couple moves to a pastoral New England town for some peace after their son’s death. Unfortunately, they move into a former funeral home that demands human sacrifices in the basement. This film calls for patience, but the bloody finale is worth the slow climb.
—

Zoe Lund in “Ms. 45.”Drafthouse Films
‘Ms. 45’

This exploitation thriller is not for everyone. Made in 1981, just as slasher movies began to trend, “Ms. 45” portrays a rape survivor who’s bent on revenge, killing other cruel men. This movie belongs to the rape-revenge subgenre, a harrowingly violent set of movies that includes “I Spit on Your Grave” and “The Last House on the Left.” “Ms. 45” stands apart, however, because its heroine has an emotional nuance that’s missing from most retribution movies.
