    France is the idea

    Who are the new movers and shakers of French creativity?

    Ideas – the spark of creativity and innovation – are the touchstone of France, a country long known for its groundbreaking philosophers, writers, designers and researchers. Today, a new generation of French innovators is changing the face of industries in France and around the world.

    Creativity and achievement are baked into French culture, beginning with the capacity to imagine. As the writer and aviator Antoine de Saint-Exupéry said: “A pile of rocks ceases to be a rock pile when somebody contemplates it with the idea of a cathedral in mind.” And, like the builders of France’s storied cathedrals, today’s inventors, researchers, entrepreneurs and business developers are reimagining the landscape of our world.

    France is known for the rigor of its education system, with its emphasis on math and science, and its high output of top-flight engineers. The country has produced 68 Nobel laureates and 13 winners of the prestigious Fields Medal in mathematics. But its creative minds are active in many fields, from the prodigious international product designer Philippe Starck to Hélène Darroze, voted World’s Best Female Chef 2015.

    Additionally, French Tech is investing €200 million ($212 million) in private start-up accelerators. Station F, conceived as the world’s biggest business incubator, is opening on Paris’s Left Bank this year. It is the brainchild of Xavier Niel, founder of 42, the tuition-free computer programming school. “France has always been very proactive in terms of new technology and services adoption,” says Niel. “We also have the talents, the engineers and more and more capital available to fund them.” These and other initiatives, such as Créative Next, a competition to identify key game-changers and innovators in each field of French industry, continue to raise the profile of French innovation.

    Industry ofthe future

    In light of these developments, a host of new industries is taking shape. For these, France provides an excellent ecosystem for innovation. Its internationally renowned universities, exceptional infrastructure, location in the heart of Europe and excellent quality of life make it an attractive location for companies of all sizes.

    An estimated2 billion new connected deviceswill be sold in France in the next five years

    This infrastructure includes initiatives like Orange’s move into public cloud services and the smart cities sector. According to Thierry Bonhomme, CEO of Orange’s enterprise arm, Orange’s recently awarded contract to transform the center of Doha, Qatar, into a digital district is an initiative that can be extended elsewhere in the Middle East and in France.

    As a top-ranking tourist destination, France has long been a pioneer in transport. Little wonder, then, that it is home to BlaBlaCar, a leader in ridesharing and the product of an innovative, disruptive idea. BlaBlaCar, in addition to linking riders with ride-sharers, allows users to choose traveling companions according to their preferences.

    “The start-up landscape has been booming over the past few years, and hopefully it will unlock the potential of our French talents and innovation.”

    Xavier NielFounder of 42

    France’s contributions to art and culture hardly need reiterating. But French creativity is alive and thriving in many newer areas, from animation to digital technology. As director and co-director, the French animator Pierre Coffin helped to create the “Despicable Me” film franchise, including voicing the famous Minion characters. Also well known in the animation world is Paris’s Ecole de l’Image Gobelins, which in 2016 was voted the top international animation school for the second year in a row.

    “We have lots of different specialties in design schools – most of them are very high quality and innovative. That’s lucky for young designers.”

    Patrick Jouin Industrial designer

    Another product by French entrepreneurs is DJiT’s Edjing, the top free mobile app for D.J.s, now used in 182 countries. Created by Jean-Baptiste Hironde and Nicolas Dupré, Edjing was named innovation of the year by Google in 2013, and currently boasts more than 20 million users.

    Emblematic of France’s strength in math is the flamboyant Cédric Villani, 2010 Fields Medal winner and head of the Sorbonne’s Institut Henri Poincaré. Math represents 15 percent of France’s gross domestic product, or €285 billion, supporting a wide range of industries, including Silicon Valley, where the French talent for conceptualization is in high demand.

    “France has a long tradition of innovation and creativity. These things last for a long time. Both our institutions and culture favor this.”

    Cédric VillaniMathematician

    France is also known for its scientific research. Créative Next, which launched its competition in eight countries (France, the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, China, Singapore, South Korea and India), chose as a U.S. 2016 winner the French start-up Hemarina technology, whose R&D focuses on therapeutic applications of oxygen carrier molecules. Another Créative Next winner is Rosa, the first robotic assistant approved for neurosurgical procedures. Developed by Medtech, a French start-up founded by Bertin Nahum, Rosa is described by its creators as a GPS for surgeons.

    Says Jean-Baptiste Hironde, CEO of DJiT: “Being French has obviously helped me in creating my own business. In France, we have some of the world’s best engineering schools. It naturally gives students all the keys to create their business. Moreover, French education pushes us to think outside the box. The start-up way of thinking is more and more promoted.”
