Margaret’s 5 TV Shows to Watch This Week

by Margaret Lyons

April 10, 2017

This is a slow week for new shows, but it’s also the right week to check in with “Sesame Street” and to welcome two other great series back into your life.

If you find yourself with a little room in your viewing schedule, we have hundreds of recommendations here on Watching, The New York Times’s TV and movie recommendation site. In the mood for some British comedy? Or an excellent mini-series? Your next great TV obsession is out there somewhere. See you Friday for your weekend recommendations.

Monday

Alan Muraoka with Julia, a new muppet character with autism, on “Sesame Street.Zach Hyman/Sesame Workshop
‘Sesame Street’
When to watch: Streaming now, on HBO.

This is the episode that introduces the new Muppet Julia, who is autistic. “Meet Julia” isn’t about autism, though; the theme of the episode is friendship, with Elmo, Abby and Alan helping Big Bird learn about the ways Julia likes, and does not like, to play. If you haven’t seen an episode of “Sesame Street” yet this season, note that Elmo now has a smartphone named Smartie.
—

‘Better Call Saul’
When to watch: 10 p.m., on AMC (season premiere).

If you’ve never watched “Breaking Bad” or “Better Call Saul,” this is a tough place to start — there’s a lot of depth and detail that won’t read to a new viewer. You will get much, much more out of this show by starting at the beginning, or at least at the beginning of “Saul” if you feel that you must forgo “Breaking Bad.”But … if there’s a part of you that thinks you would like a slightly oddball character drama, just start watching. (Do yourself a favor and at least read some recaps before starting cold.) It will be more confusing and less rich, but you’ll live, and you’ll live even better because the joy and intrigue of “Better Call Saul” will form a part of your routine. Season 3 picks up shortly after the events of Season 2; Jimmy (Bob Odenkirk), our panicky lawyer hero, remains perched on the edge of the dark side.

Tuesday

From left, Melissa Fumero, Andy Samberg, Stephanie Beatriz, Joe Lo Truglio and Kyle Bornheimer in “Brooklyn Nine-Nine.”John P Fleenor/Fox
‘Brooklyn Nine-Nine’
When to watch: 8 p.m., on Fox.

Oh thank God, “Brooklyn Nine-Nine” is finally back. It’s been gone since Jan. 1, and our world has been sadder for its absence. The Nine-Nine is getting audited, which means everyone is on high alert, especially Amy, since her dullsville ex, Teddy, is doing the auditing. “Brooklyn” is at its best like this, when its characters are on the same side, teaming up against an outsider.

Wednesday

From left, Fred Armisen, Hector Elias and Luis Fernando Hoyos in “Documentary, Now!”Rhys Thomas/IFC
‘Documentary Now!’
When to watch: Now, on Netflix.

“Documentary Now!” lives and dies by how familiar you are with whichever documentary an episode is parodying. If you’ve never seen “The Kid Stays in the Picture,” for example, the two-part season finale, “Mr. Runner Up: My Life as an Oscar Bridesmaid,” is not much of a draw.One of the episodes this season, however, is among the best half-hours of TV from 2016: the episode “Juan Likes Rice and Chicken.” It’s a spoof of fawning docs like “Jiro Dreams of Sushi” and shows like “Chef’s Table” — a big enough part of the culture that one can enjoy the episode even without seeing the exact source material.

Thursday

Darren Carlton, a deckhand, in “Discovery Impact: Sacred Cod.”Steve Liss/Discovery

‘Discovery Impact: Sacred Cod’
When to watch: 9 p.m., on Discovery Channel.

The ocean is getting warmer (the Gulf of Maine is warming especially fast), fishing regulations have increased, and the fishing industry in New England is in colossal decline. This documentary captures a community in distress: “I had to lay off my son,” one fisherman says. Oof.

If you like documentaries that make you feel for people on every side of the story — regulators, scientists, fishermen, local government officials, the guy who runs the ice factory — “Sacred Cod” is for you. It’s also for you if you like descending into a panic about how something as vast as our ecosystem can still be so fragile.
